Other disinfectants currently in use include carbolic acid. Pouring this disinfectant into waste containers should be done not only in areas where cholera is epidemic, but also in places where there are no signs of outbreak yet. This is because even when there are no patients in the household, visitors and others who have come into contact with cholera patients or interacted with people in the incubation period of cholera disease may carry the poison without knowing it since they have not yet developed symptoms. When they use the toilet, the poison remains in that toilet. When household members unknowingly come into contact with this, they will clearly be infected with the poison. Therefore, even in areas where there is no outbreak yet, one must not neglect to carefully pour disinfectants such as green vitriol and carbolic acid into waste containers.

Those who have died from cholera should preferably be cremated rather than buried. At this time, clothing, bedding, etc. should also be burned together.

There are various ways cholera poison spreads. The following are one or two examples: When excretions from cholera patients are scattered on the ground, they pass through the ground due to rainfall and reach nearby wells and water supplies, mixing into that water. Those who drink this will undoubtedly be infected with cholera poison. Also, if cattle are given water containing cholera poison to drink, while the cattle are not infected, they absorb it into their bodies and secrete it into their milk.
