The disease toxins generated in this manner invade the human body through drinking water (due to disease toxins penetrating water sources or well water in the vicinity where "cholera" patients' waste has been buried), food (through the meat of fish, shellfish, and livestock that have consumed "cholera" patients' excrement, or vegetables that used such excrement as fertilizer, or through disease toxins present in the air that contaminate food, or through small insects carrying filth and depositing it, etc.), or through using toilets where disinfection methods are not practiced. Moreover, human bodies and equipment also serve as effective intermediaries for transmitting disease toxins.

For this reason, the attempt to completely reject this disease through maritime and land quarantine regulations makes achieving that objective extremely difficult. The method of disinfection has recently become the most urgent matter among "cholera" disease prevention methods, and since its proven effectiveness in disease prevention has been widely demonstrated by various countries, implementing this method should not be left merely to the individual attention of the people. It is a requirement of protection that it must be encouraged or enforced with strict implementation.

This is because this method provides a means to immediately eradicate disease toxins, so when implemented with precise attention, it can effectively control the spread and permanently eliminate the source of disease in that area. Recently, European countries generally implement this during times of infectious disease outbreaks, requiring enormous national expenditures for this purpose.
