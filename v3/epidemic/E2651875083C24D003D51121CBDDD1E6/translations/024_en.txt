　Day 12: The red halo greatly diminishes and disappears, the thin fluid inside the vesicles becomes thick, and small scabs form at the center of the pox. This condition sometimes appears on the eleventh day.
　
　Days 13-14: The central scab completely covers and extends to the outer edges, and the vesicle fluid also completely dries and solidifies.
　
　Days 15-16: The pox scabs become tea-brown in color, and the scabs fall off between the twentieth and thirtieth days.
　
　Those cases that progress in the orderly sequence described above represent the symptoms of so-called true pox, and there is no doubt that they will never again contract smallpox.

Article 24: For transplanting and inoculating pox matter, direct transmission from arm to arm is considered best. This is because in most cases it avoids errors in technique. However, in remote areas, it is not possible to maintain continuous inoculation schedules, so whenever good-natured pox is available, the matter should be stored for use when supplies are lacking. There are various methods of storage, which are presented below:

Method 1: Take two glass plates about one inch square, apply pox fluid to the inner surface of each, join them together, seal the joint tightly with paste, or apply melted pitch to seal the outside.
