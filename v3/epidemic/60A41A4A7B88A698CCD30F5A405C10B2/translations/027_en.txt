(7) When notification of an infectious disease patient is received, the principle is to admit the patient to an infectious disease hospital or isolation ward as quickly as possible, and home treatment should not be allowed unless sufficient conditions are met.

(8) After sending the patient to an infectious disease hospital or isolation ward, the patient's home, especially the sickroom, as well as the dishes used by the patient and family members, clothing, bedding, and other items must be disinfected. Particular attention must be paid to disinfecting wells suspected of contamination and toilets used by the patient (including doors, handles, interior walls, floors, toilet bowls, washbasins, ladles, hand towels, sandals, etc.). Toilets can be used immediately after disinfection is completed, and feces and urine may be used as fertilizer one week after disinfection without problem.

(9) For typhoid and paratyphoid, there is of course no recognized need for traffic restrictions and isolation of healthy persons, but when dysentery occurs in rural villages, it is necessary to implement traffic restrictions and isolation. However, even in urban areas, this may be implemented when deemed necessary.

(10) After patient discovery, for ten days for dysentery and twenty days for typhoid and paratyphoid, family members of the patient or even non-family members who are contaminated or suspected of contamination with pathogens should add lime powder after each bowel movement. Furthermore, after patient recovery, for twenty days for dysentery and preferably one month or longer for typhoid, lime powder should be added after each bowel movement, and hands should be disinfected with carbolic acid solution. It is also advisable to periodically disinfect loincloths and similar items. Moreover, those recovering from typhoid and paratyphoid still have [bacteria] in their urine for a certain period
