One, ten, hundred (meaning ten tens), thousand (meaning ten hundreds), ten thousand (meaning ten thousands), hundred thousand,
million, ten million, oku/hundred million (meaning ten thousand times ten thousand), billion, ten billion, hundred billion,
cho/trillion (meaning ten thousand oku), ten trillion, hundred trillion, thousand trillion, kei/quadrillion (meaning ten thousand cho), ten quadrillion,
hundred quadrillion, thousand quadrillion, gai/quintillion (meaning ten thousand kei), ten quintillion, hundred quintillion, thousand quintillion.
Beyond these: shi, jo, ko, kan, sei, zai, kyoku, etc., all of which can be understood by extension. These are called the positions of the great vehicle (daijo), which are commonly used today. In addition, there are names such as gōgasha (Ganges sands) and asōgi (incalculable), but most of these appear in Buddhist teachings.

Decimal Numbers　Numbers below one
Fun (meaning ten rin), rin (meaning ten mō), mō (meaning ten shi), shi (meaning ten kotsu, others follow this pattern), kotsu, bi,
sen, sha, jin, ai, byō, haku.
Beyond these: moko, shunjun, suyu, etc., exist without limit, all appearing in Buddhist teachings.

【Left page】
In general, the extreme of small numbers becomes emptiness (kū), and the extreme of large numbers becomes void (kyo). When reaching these extremes, they are beyond what each person's vision can comprehend. Also, in mathematical precision (sūri sei'on), "fatsu" is recorded after "mō." Therefore, calendar books and such often use the character "fatsu."

Linear Measurements
Jō (meaning ten shaku), shaku, sun, fun, rin, mō.
Tan (from 2 jō 5 shaku or 2 jō 6 shaku to 3 jō of whale-scale measurement, with variations in length depending on quality, all terms for silk cloth), hiki (meaning two tan).
Shi (meaning eight sun), jin (meaning four shaku), hiro (meaning two jin, which is eight shaku), jō (meaning two hiro), in (meaning ten jō).
Curved ruler/carpenter's square (equivalent to eight sun of whale-scale measurement) - This name comes from the curved, right-angled tool used by craftsmen. The reverse scale divides 1 shaku 4 sun 1 bu 4 rin and more into one shaku, and its origin comes from square root extraction. The diagonal of a one-shaku square is 1 shaku 4 sun 1 bu 4 rin and more. Therefore it is called sumi-[kane].
