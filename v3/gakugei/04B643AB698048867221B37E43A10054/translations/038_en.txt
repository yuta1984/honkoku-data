Upon receiving a pardon and returning to Edo, he first changed his name to Hanabusa Itchō and took the pen name Hokusō-ō (North Window Elder). From that time on, people competed to acquire the silk paintings and paper works he created, treasuring them as precious objects. On the thirteenth day of the first month of Kyōhō 9 (1724), he died at the age of seventy-three. The works he created during his lifetime, such as "Asazuma Boat Picture with Poem" and "Asa Shimizu Record," have been transmitted to the world and are greatly admired. He was a contemporary and friend of the haiku masters Bashō and Kikaku.

Hōshin-sai Kikaku-ō's Grave: Located in the precincts of Jōgyō-ji Temple, a Nichiren sect temple on the opposite side. Kikaku's family name was Takeshita, and his father was called Tōjun 《a man from Katata in Ōmi Province who practiced medicine》. Enomoto was his mother's family name. He studied Confucianism under Master Kansai and poetry under the monk Taiten. In calligraphy, he received instruction from Sasaki Kenryū and developed his own distinctive style. In medicine, he learned the art from a certain Mr. Kusakari, and in painting he followed his friend Hanabusa Itchō. In the early Enpō period, he entered the school of Master Bashō to study haikai poetry and eventually made his name. He had numerous pen names including Raichūshi, Kyōraidō, Yūchikukyo, Rokuzōan, Zensaian, Bun'an, as well as Raisha and Shōsen. Shinji was probably his playful pen name.
