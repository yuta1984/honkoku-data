○Saka (slope) refers to steep inclines. These are high and precipitous places in mountains. Small slopes are called tō. Tō (stone steps) is the same.
○Dake refers to steep high mountains, such as Nyoigadake in Yamashiro and Hiragadake in Ōmi.
○Tani (valley) is flowing water between two mountains. Kei and kei are the same. Water flowing into a ravine is called tani. Water between mountains is called kan, read as "tanigawa" (valley river).
○Oka (hill) refers to high ground. It also refers to areas that are high on four sides with a broad center. Fu is the same. When foxes die, they use hills as pillows.
○Ban refers to large stones. Also called banjaku (bedrock). The common expression "daibanjaku" (great bedrock) is redundant language.

【Left page, upper section】
○Gan refers to rock formations. These are what is sung of as "pebbles becoming boulders." Stone caves are called gan. It refers to stones that are sharp and tower high. The Book of Songs states "these stones are towering." Gan (rock) is the same.
○Gake (cliff) refers to mountainsides. It describes one side of a mountain that rises steeply and precipitously. Gai is the same. It is also called kengai (hanging cliff). Commonly called "gake."
○Baku is also written as taki (waterfall). Because the falling water appears white like cloth being bleached, it is also called bakufu (waterfall). Japan also has what is called Nunobiki Falls. China has famous waterfalls at Mount Lu. Waterfalls are also called hisen (flying springs).
