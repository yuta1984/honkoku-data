Upon seeing (the woman's poem), the man found his feelings unbearable.

*Toeba iu / towaneba uramu / Musashi abumi / kakaru ori ni ya / hito wa shinuran*
(If I visit, she speaks her mind; if I do not visit, she holds a grudge — like the word *kakaru* in "Musashi stirrup," is this the kind of predicament in which a man must die?)

【Section 14】

Long ago, a man wandered his way to the province of Michinoku. A woman there must have found someone from the capital a rare and wonderful thing, for she had feelings of deep longing for him. And so she composed:

*Nakanaka ni / koi ni shinazuba / kuwako ni zo / naru beka ri keru / tama no o bakari*
(Rather than linger on like this, unable to die of love, I should perhaps have been a silkworm — living out just a thin thread of a life upon the mulberry leaves.)

Even her poem had a rustic air about it. Still, perhaps finding a certain charm in it, he went to her and spent the night. As he left while the night was still deep, the woman said:

*Yomo akeba / kitsuni wa menate / kudakake no / madaki ni naite / sena wo yaritsuru*
(I had hoped to show you properly once dawn came, but the crowing cock called out too early and sent my beloved away.)

The man, saying he was returning to the capital, composed:

*Kurihara no / aneha no matsu no / hito naraba / miyako no tsuto ni / iza to iwamashi*
(If the pine of Aneha in Kurihara were a person, how gladly I would say, "Come, let me take you as a gift to the capital!")

And so it was said that the woman rejoiced and continued to think of him.

【Section 15】

Long ago, in the province of Michinoku, a man was visiting the wife of a man of no particular distinction. Finding it strange that, in such a situation, she seemed not quite the woman she ought to be, he composed:

*Shinobu yama / shinobite kayou / michi mo gana / hito no kokoro no / oku mo miru beku*
(If only there were a path to thread secretly through Mount Shinobu — so that I might see into the very depths of that person's heart.)

【Left Page】

The woman thought the poem utterly wonderful without measure, but seeing the savage, barbarian-like heart of such a rough husband, what was there she could do?

【Section 16】

Long ago, there was a man named Ki no Aritsune. He had served three successive emperors and had flourished in his time, but later the world changed and the times shifted, so that he could not live as an ordinary man would. Yet his nature was beautiful of heart, refined and graceful in his tastes, and unlike other people. Even living in poverty, he retained the spirit of his better days of old, and remained ignorant of the ways of the ordinary world. A wife with whom he had grown accustomed over many years gradually drifted away from him, and at last became a nun, going to join a place where her elder sister had already taken vows ahead of her. The man, though their bond had not truly been a deeply intimate one, found it deeply moving that she was now departing for good. Yet being poor, there was nothing at all he could do. Overcome with grief, he wrote to a friend with whom he had long been on intimate terms of mutual visits and conversation: "As things stand, my wife is now leaving for good, yet I am unable to do even the smallest thing for her — I can only send her off," and at the end he wrote:

*Te wo orite / ai mishi koto wo / kazofureba / tō to iitsutsu / yottsu wa heni keri*
(Counting on my fingers the years we have spent together, I find that I have said "ten" again and again — and forty years have passed.)
