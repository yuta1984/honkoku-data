*(Continued from the previous page — A Method for Preparing Pine-Needle Tobacco)*

Soak [the prepared pine needles] again in sake for approximately two hours (*futotoki*), steam them in a steaming basket (*seirō*), then dry them in the shade. Once more, pound them on a stone to soften them, and pinch them by hand until they resemble finely cut tobacco. Place the result in a jar for storage. By doing this, one obtains a pine-needle tobacco of exceedingly fine flavor.

…[in former times, at corners, they used to] demolish houses [to get the timber through]. Nowadays, the front end of the timber is raised on a pillow block (*makura*) placed on a wheeled cart (*kuruma*) to a height that clears the roofline, so that the timber stands upright. The cart and the rear end of the timber are then swung around using a wooden lever (*tekko*).

**A Method for Preventing the Light of an *Ariake* Lamp from Being Seen Outside**

- The way to prevent the light of a lamp from being visible to others without using a lamp shade (*kashiya* / *hoya*) is as follows: place a thin piece of wood horizontally across the top of the oil dish (*aburatuki*), and lay an abacus rod (*kesan*) on top of it, directed upward toward the flame.

**A Method for Cooking Rice While Walking Along a Night Road**

---

**[Left Page — Lower Section]**

- Cut a medium-sized piece of bamboo, leaving the nodes intact at both the top and bottom. Make a small hole through one of the nodes, and pack rice inside. Then pour an appropriate amount of water in through the hole, and seal the hole tightly with a twisted paper cord (*koyori*). Carry this alongside a torch (*taimatsu*) as you walk. The heat from the torch will cook the rice to an exceedingly fine flavor.
  
  *(Marginal note in smaller print):* The moisture within prevents the bamboo from burning or cracking; the cooked rice fills the inside of the bamboo completely. If one stands a knife blade against this bamboo [after cooking], it will split open cleanly.
