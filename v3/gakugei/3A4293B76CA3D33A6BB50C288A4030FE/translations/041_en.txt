…When (Sanekazura, believing she had found Misako in her dream) was on the verge of enjoying a tender reunion, his figure vanished before her eyes. Realizing then that Misako had truly died, she threw herself into the deep pool nearby and perished. It is said that from this time on, the place came to be called *Ōsaka* ("Meeting Slope").

(A small slope to the west of Kagurazaka is called *Yūrei-zaka* ["Ghost Slope"] by the local people — this is likely a confusion with *Ōsaka*. One should also be aware that linking the place name *Ōsaka* ["Meeting Slope"] with the woman's name *Sanekazura* is almost certainly the fanciful invention of some enthusiastic storyteller. Nevertheless, as the tale has been handed down for so long, we include it here despite our reservations.)

---

**Kagura-zaka** — This refers to the slope outside the Ushigome Gate in the same district. Halfway up the slope on the right side is the *tabisho* (temporary resting place for a portable shrine) of Takada Ana Hachiman. During the festival, the sacred palanquin (*mikoshi*) is carried in procession to this spot, and *kagura* (sacred Shinto music and dance) is performed there — hence the name.

(According to one account, when the Tsukuto Myōjin deity was relocated from Tayasu to its present site, *kagura* was performed on this slope, and thus the slope received its name. Another tradition holds that the Wakamiya Hachiman Shrine is nearby, and the sounds of *kagura* music could always be heard as far as this slope — and hence the name.)

---

**Wakamiya Hachiman Shrine** — Located in the same district, at the top of Wakamiya-zaka in Wakamiya-chō (also referred to as Wakamiya Kōji). Its administrative temple (*bettō*) is the Tendai sect's Fumon'in. According to tradition, in the autumn of the fifth year of Bunji (1189), the Right General Lord Yoritomo set out to subjugate Fujiwara no Yasuhira of Ōshū (northern Honshu). He had made a vow that after the pacification of Ōshū, he would establish this shrine and enshrine therein the Wakamiya Hachiman deity from Tsurugaoka in Kamakura.

(The *wakamiya* ["young shrine"] deity is Emperor Nintoku; later, it is said, worship was changed to Emperor Ōjin.)

During the Bunmei era, Ōta Dōkan rebuilt the shrine for the protection of Edo Castle,

**Gozusan Gyōganji Temple** — Also called Senjuin. Located in the same district, above Kagura-zaka, to the right of the temple-town road (*teramachi michi*). It belongs to the Tendai sect, affiliated with Tōeizan (Kan'eiji Temple). The principal image — a statue of Thousand-Armed Kannon (Senju Kannon Daishi) — was carved by Eshin Sōzu (Genshin). (It is venerated under the name "Yoritomo's Collar-Hanging Statue.") The temple claims Jikaku Daishi (Ennin) as its founder.

(Local tradition holds that this temple was once a great monastery, with its main gate (*sōmon*) standing around the present-day Ushigome Gate, and that Kagura-zaka itself was the site of the middle gate (*chūmon*). The halls and pagodas were destroyed in the military disturbances of the Daiei era (around 1521). An ancient copy of the *Dai Hannya-kyō* (the Great Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra) from that period is said to be kept here as a treasure. As there were once many nandina (*nanten*) trees growing on both sides of the gate, the common people still refer to the temple as *Nantenji* ["Nandina Temple"].)

The temple's account of the principal image reads as follows: After the Battle of Ishibashiyama, Lord Yoritomo passed through Awa and Kazusa provinces and was making his way from Shimōsa Province into this province (Musashi). He spent a night in vigil (*tsuya*) before this sacred image. That night, he dreamed that he himself placed the sacred image around his own collar and thereby opened up the martial fortunes of the Minamoto clan. It later came to pass exactly so — he unified the realm under his rule — and therefore the image has ever since been venerated as "The Sacred Image That Yoritomo Wore at His Collar."

---

**Site of Ushigome Castle** — It is traditionally said that the area above *Waratana* (the straw goods market) in the same district is the former site of the castle. It is said to be the remains of a fortification where Ushigome Kunai-no-shō Katsuyuki resided during the Tenbun era (1532–1555).
