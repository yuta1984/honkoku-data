**Site of the Nō Stage (Nōbutai no Ato)**
This is located to the left of the main shrine; today only the foundation stones remain. It is said to be the site where the Kanze Tayū performed a "lifetime Nō" (ichidai nō) performance in the third month of the third year of Kan'en (1750), the year of Kōgo.

It is noteworthy that the bettō-ji (administrative Buddhist temple) of this shrine is named Kōshōzan, a name derived from the wondrous virtues of the sacred tree. The poem composed in praise reads:
*"The way of the gods and the sovereign is straight and true; the reign is at peace without murkiness — like the clear oath of Iwashimizu."*
This divine virtue is held in the deepest reverence. Especially since the shrine's restoration during the Genroku era, the divine grace (wakō) has manifested day by day with brilliant clarity.

**Takata Inari Myōjin Shrine (髙田稲荷明神社)**

This shrine is located in the same area, across the road to the right of the Hachiman Shrine. It is called the tutelary deity (ubusuna-gami) of Totsuka Village, and is therefore also known as Totsuka Inari. The honji-butsu (original Buddhist form) of the deity — the Sacred Kannon (Shō Kanzeon) — is said to be the work of Tokuichi Daishi of Nanto (Nara).

According to tradition, the origins of this shrine are extremely ancient. However, in the first year of Bunki (1501), the year of Kanoto-Tori, Uesugi Jibu-shōyu Nyūdō Tomoyoshi (recorded in the Nankōtei as "Tomooki") rebuilt the shrine's sacred precincts following a divine dream (reimu) and granted the lands of Totsuka Village as shrine domains.

*(The shrine preserves an old ridge plaque (munafuda) whose inscription reads: "Rebuilt on the 29th day of the 2nd month of the 19th year of Tenbun [1550], by Ushigomi Shuzen Tokikuni; bettō: Hōsen-bō Shūhō; carpenters: Yozaemon and Saemon-Gorō." Upon investigation, the name of Ushigomi Shuzen Tokikuni has yet to be fully verified. It is recorded in the family genealogy and the records of Sōzan-ji temple in Ushigomi that the descendants of the Ōgo clan of Jōshū (Kōzuke Province), having settled in Ushigomi in Musashi Province, changed their clan name to Ushigomi in the 24th year of Tenbun [1555]. Reconciling the dates, the Ōgo clan would not yet have changed their name to Ushigomi at the time of Tenbun 19. Therefore, the "Tokikuni" mentioned here must presumably have been a different individual altogether. This point requires further correction at a later date.)*

**Sacred Spring (Shinsen)**
This refers to the water that trickles out from the hollow of the hackberry tree before the shrine. It is said to possess miraculous powers.

**Bishamondō (Hall of Bishamonten)**

This is situated on a small elevated hill within the same precincts. The principal sacred image of Bishamonten (the Heavenly King of the North) is said to have been carved by Jikaku Daishi and to have served as the personal devotional image (nenjibutsu) of Musashi no Kami Fujiwara no Hidesato.

According to tradition, Jikaku Daishi arrived at the shore of Karasaki in Gōshū (Ōmi Province), where he happened to find a flute. Inside it was a sacred image of Tamonten (another name for Bishamonten), measuring one sun and eight bu (approximately 5.5 cm) in height. Deeply moved with joy, the Daishi took this as his own devotional image. During the Ninju era (851–854), he traveled down to his home province of Shimotsuke, entered the Daiji-ji temple in Sano, and carved a new image of Tamonten measuring two shaku and five sun (approximately 75 cm) in height, enshrining the earlier small sacred image within its body cavity (taichū), and installed it at Daiji-ji. It is said that during the Tengyō era (938–947), after Musashi no Kami Hidesato's subjugation of Taira no Masakado, the image was relocated to this present location.

*(A book called "Murasaki Hitomoto" (A Single Stalk of Murasaki) states that when Hidesato was subduing Masakado, he prayed deeply to Bishamonten, who then appeared atop his helmet, and Hidesato himself modeled and carved the image from this vision — a tradition that differs from the temple's own account.)*
