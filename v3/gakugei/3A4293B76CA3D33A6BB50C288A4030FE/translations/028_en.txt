(Continuation from previous text) They revered this place, enclosed the land, established a shrine, and invited the kami of Shinmei-gū.

Around the Kenkyū period (1190-1199), there was a local farmer named Yokoi Hyōbu. (The distant descendants of this person still live in this area today, with the lineage continuing unbroken through generations. Long ago, when Minamoto no Yoriyoshi went on his campaign to pacify the northeast, he arrived at this place. The ancestor of the Yokoi family, a man called Hyōbu, had joined as one of the accompanying soldiers, but suddenly fell ill and could not proceed to the battlefield, so he remained here and eventually became a farmer. This origin story has been passed down in that family.)

Having a prayer to fulfill, he intended to make a pilgrimage to Ise Taijingū and stayed at an inn at Nohono station in Seishū. That night, he received a divine revelation from Taijingū, and the next day he found a sacred stone in the waters of the Miya River. Following the divine will, he carried it back to his hometown and enshrined it in the aforementioned Shinmei-gū as the sacred object. Later, a monk named Gikai received a divine message and moved the shrine to its present location. (The old site is seven or eight chō to the east, which the local people call Moto-Ise.)

Nichien-zan Myōhō-ji Temple: Located in Horinouchi village. It is a temple of the Nichiren sect, Icchi school, and is quite a prosperous temple. The sacred image of the sect founder Nichiren Daishi is known in the world as the yakuyoke (evil-averting) sacred image. It was carved by Saint Nichirō and was previously at Myōhokke-ji Temple in Himonya, but around the Genroku period (1688-1704), due to certain circumstances when Hokke-ji was converted to the Tendai sect, this sacred image was moved to this temple. (This was during the time of the temple's resident priest, Master Nichishō.)

According to tradition, in the first year of Kōchō, Kanoto-tori (1261), Saint Nichiren (age forty) was exiled to Itō in Izu. Master Nichirō tried to accompany him to that place, but this was not permitted.

Therefore, at that time, by the saint's command, Master Nichirō remained at Yui Beach in Kamakura, praying day and night for his master's pardon. One evening, in the same coastal waters, he miraculously obtained a sacred piece of wood and hand-carved a true image of Saint Nichiren, serving it constantly without neglect. (This sacred image was the first image ever made of the sect founder.)

When the time of divine response arrived, in the fifth month of the third year of Kōchō, Mizunoto-i (1263), a pardon was granted and Saint Nichiren returned to Kamakura. At that time, seeing this sacred statue, he was deeply moved and said, "My spirit has now transferred into this wooden image. Until the far future, the benefits of protecting sentient beings will be infinite. Since I have already received salvation at age forty-two, this wooden image shall be called by the name of yakuyoke (evil-averting)," and he performed the eye-opening ceremony himself.

Protective Talismans: (Faithful people who chant the title and recite sutras with right mindfulness before this talisman for twenty-one days will have their prayers fulfilled, so many people receive them. Those suffering from illness paste them on the walls near their sickbeds or on house pillars. Therefore, common people call them "pasted protective talismans." According to tradition, when Saint Nichiren was at his place of exile in Itō, Izu, a man named Hachirō Uemon suffered from a serious illness. The saint granted him this miraculous talisman and there was a divine response. Later, Master Nichirō transmitted this practice, and from then on it has been passed down through generations.)

Although this temple is located far from the capital, because of its remarkable spiritual efficacy, people do not mind the distance.
