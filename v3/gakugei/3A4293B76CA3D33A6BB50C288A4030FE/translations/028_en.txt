Around the Kenkyū era, a farmer in this area named Yokoi Hyōbu [Note: The distant descendants of this man are said to still live here today, with the family line continuing unbroken. Long ago, when Minamoto no Yoriyoshi, the Imperial Advisor, came to this land during his campaign to subjugate Ōshū, the ancestor of the Yokoi clan, a man named Hyōbu, had joined his forces, but was suddenly struck by illness and could not proceed to the battlefield. He therefore remained here and eventually became a farmer—so it is transmitted in that family.] had a wish to fulfill, and set out to make a pilgrimage to the Grand Shrine of Ise. He lodged at a post station at Nohono in Seishū (Ise Province). That night, he received a divine revelation from the Grand Shrine, and the following day obtained a sacred stone from the waters of the Miyagawa River. Entrusting himself to the divine will, he carried it back to his home village and enshrined it in the said Shinmei-gū shrine, where it became the sacred object of worship. Afterward, a Buddhist monk named Gikai received a divine revelation and moved the shrine to its present location. [Note: The former site is located seven or eight chō to the east. The local people call it "Moto-Ise" (the Original Ise).]

Nichienji Myōhōji Temple [Furigana: Located in Horinouichi Village] — This is a temple of the Itchi school of the Nichiren sect, and a particularly prosperous establishment. The sacred image of the sect's founder, the Great Teacher Nichiren, is known to the world as the "Yakuyoke no Miei" (the protective portrait against evil and misfortune). It was carved by the Venerable Nichirō, and was originally kept at Myōhōkkeji in Himonya. Around the Genroku era, for certain reasons, when Hokkkeji was converted to the Tendai sect, this sacred image was transferred to the present temple. [Note: This occurred during the abbacy of Nissē, a resident monk of this temple.] According to tradition, in the first year of Kōchō, the year of Kanoto-Tori, the Venerable Nichiren [age forty] was exiled to Itō in Izu.

The Venerable Nichirō attempted to accompany him to that place, but was unable to do so. Therefore, by order of the Master at that time, Nichirō remained at Yuigahama in Kamakura and prayed day and night for the Master's pardon. One evening, from the same sea, he obtained a sacred piece of driftwood by divine grace, and with his own hands carved a true likeness of the Venerable Nichiren, which he served devotedly without ever slackening. [Note: This sacred image is considered to be the very first carved image of the founding Great Teacher.] When the time of the response from the heavenly deities arrived, a pardon was granted in the fifth month of the third year of Kōchō, the year of Mizunoto-I, and the Venerable Nichiren returned to Kamakura. Around that time, upon viewing this sacred image, he was deeply moved and declared: "My spirit shall henceforth dwell within this wooden image. For all time to come, its merit in protecting and saving sentient beings shall be without end. Since I myself received salvation at the age of forty-two, this wooden image shall be given the title of 'Yakuyoke' (protection against evil)." With these words, he personally performed the eye-opening ceremony (kaigen) for the image.

Kaji-no-fu (Talismanic Amulets) [Note: Those with sincere faith who, over the course of twenty-one days, face this talisman and recite the daimoku and sutras with right mindfulness are said to have their prayers fulfilled; thus people receive these. Those suffering from illness affix it to the wall near their sickbed or to a pillar of the house, which is why common people call it the "Hari-Mifū" (pasted sacred talisman). According to tradition, when the Venerable Nichiren was at his place of exile in Itō in Izu, a man named Hachirozaemon suffered from a serious illness; the Venerable accordingly granted him this wondrous talisman, and miraculous efficacy was witnessed. Since the Venerable Nichirō later inherited this practice, it has been transmitted from generation to generation.]
