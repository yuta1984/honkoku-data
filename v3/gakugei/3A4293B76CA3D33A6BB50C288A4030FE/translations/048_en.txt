According to tradition, during the Enryaku era (782-806), Master Dengyō received an imperial edict from Emperor Kanmu and carved this sacred statue at Mount Hiei for the protection of the nation, disaster prevention, and longevity. However, in the 2nd year of Genki (1571), when Lord Oda Nobunaga set fire to Mount Hiei, all Buddhist temples and monks' quarters were reduced to ashes. At that time, there was someone who protected it, and only this principal image was safely removed. Emperor Go-Mizunoo deeply devoted himself to Buddhism, so he worshipped it and also bestowed an imperial inscription adding the title "Shakyamuni Buddha." Master Nichii obtained this principal image through divine inspiration, opened this temple, and enshrined it.

Unkosan Sōsanji Temple - Located in Benzaiten-machi in the same area (this place is called "todomeki" by the local people). It is a Sōtō Zen temple belonging to Kichijōji Temple in Komagome. The principal image is Shakyamuni Tathāgata, with Manjusri and Samantabhadra as attendant bodhisattvas. The founding priest is called Kan'ei Rin'etsu Oshō. The plaque "Daiichigi" (First Principle) on the main gate was written by Zen Master Shinetsu, the plaque "Unkosan" on the middle gate was written by Oka Ryōhitsu, the three characters "Sōsanji" on the Buddha hall were written by Sakiyō Dōei, and the plaque on the Zen hall is said to be by Ōbaku Etsuzan. According to tradition, the temple's founder is called Ushigome Kunaishōyū Fujiwara no Katsuyuki (appointed to Junior Fifth Rank Lower in the 1st year of Kōji. His Buddhist name was Sanshūin-den Shinge Seiun Anju).
