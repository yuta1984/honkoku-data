On the way back from selling an emaciated horse kept at the western market for one kan mon (1000 mon coins), he arrived at Asakusa and untied the string of coins he had received, only to find that they were all Taikan coins. Kuro, having thoughts in his heart, immediately visited Kannon Hall and offered those coins before the sacred altar, returning home empty-handed. From that time forward, he received unexpected good fortune, and his household became greatly wealthy. Therefore, in the 10th year of Oei, year of the Metal Boar (1403), he rebuilt the shrine and newly invited the twelve deities to be enshrined, donating various rice fields and gardens. After several generations, it fell into ruin, the sacred lanterns grew dim, and regular rituals were often neglected. However, because divine responses remained swift, the villagers were filled with awe, and finally during the Kyoho era (1716-1736), they petitioned the government to make Jogansji Temple the shrine's custodian. From that time onward, divine offerings have been strictly maintained, and rituals are never neglected. September 21st is designated as the day of ritual observance.

Tahozan Joganzen Temple - Same location. Across the Josui River to the west, facing the same riverbank in Hongo Village. It is a Zen temple of the Soto sect, belonging to Koun Temple in Tahara Village, Sagami Province. This temple serves as the betto (administrator) of Tsunohazu Junisho Gongen Shrine.
