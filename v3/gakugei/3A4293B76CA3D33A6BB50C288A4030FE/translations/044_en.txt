Enma Hall - Located on the left side of Teramachi Street at the same location, enshrined in the Tendai sect temple Yōzen-in. The statue of Enma-ō (King of Hell) is said to be the work of the Buddhist sculptor Unkei. On the 16th day of the first and seventh months, worshippers gather in crowds. It is said to have been located in the Hirakawa area within the castle grounds in ancient times, and as evidence of this, it is still called Heisen-ji. The temple's revival is attributed to the monk Chidō Hōin.

Sōryūzan Shōgen-ji Temple - Located on the opposite side of the same area, this is a Zen temple of the Kyoto Myōshin-ji sect and one of the four head temples (furegashira) of Edo. It enshrines a statue of Shakyamuni Buddha as its principal image. The founding priest was called Zen Master Reikan Fushō. The master's posthumous name was Sōkyū, with the courtesy name Hōzan (commonly called Naginata Hōzan. In ancient times, monkeys were kept tied up on the temple grounds, so it is still popularly known as Saru-dera [Monkey Temple]. The former location is said to have been in Banchō. The Kannon Hall's principal image is Shō Kannon, created by Kōbō Daishi).

Yakuryūzan Shōzō-in Temple - Located south of the same area in Yokoteramachi, belonging to the Tendai sect Tōeizan. The founding priest was Priest Enkan. The sacred image of Yakushi Buddha is said to be the work of Dengyō Daishi, carved with one knife and three prostrations (popularly called Kusakari Yakushi Nyorai [Grass-cutting Yakushi Buddha]). According to tradition, when this temple was formerly located at Bairinzaka (within the castle grounds), a grass cutter came and presented this sacred image of Yakushi to the founding priest Enkan and departed. During the Chōroku era, Ōta Sakinko Nyūdō Dōkan founded the temple and made this the

principal image. Later, Uesugi Tomooki's devotion was particularly deep, and he donated gōō hōin (sacred seals) and other items, which are still preserved today. This temple was formerly located near Hirakawa Bairinzaka, later moved to the Tayasu area, and during the Genna era was relocated to its present location.

Akagi Myōjin Shrine - Located on the northern back street of the same area, serving as the guardian shrine of Ushigome, with the Tendai sect temple Tōkaku-ji as its bettō (administrator). The deity is the same as that of Mount Akagi in Kōzuke Province, and its honji-butsu (Buddhist manifestation) is called Shōgun Jizō-son. In ancient times, the Ōkō clan deeply revered this deity, initially enshrining it in their domain and calling it Chikato Myōjin. Their descendant Shigeyasu moved to this province and resided in Ushigome. He also changed his name from Ōkō to Ushigome (his residence was in the Waradana area of Ushigome, mentioned earlier), and following his ancestors' intentions, enshrined this deity here. The festival is held on September 19th (the original location where this shrine was first established was in the rice fields of Sekiguchi territory below Mejiro, and even now there remains a small grove called Akagi no Mori [Akagi Forest]).
