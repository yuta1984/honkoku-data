**The Emma-dō (Hall of Emma, King of Hell)**　On the left side of the Tera-machi (Temple Street) in the same area, within the Tendai-sect temple Yōzen-in, there is enshrined a statue of Emma-ō (Yama, King of Hell), said to have been carved by the master Buddhist sculptor Unkei. On the sixteenth day of the first and seventh months, crowds of worshippers gather here. It is said that in ancient times, the hall stood within the castle grounds in the area of Hirakawa, and as proof of this, it still carries the name "Heisenji." The restorer of the temple is known as Chitō Hōin.

**Sōryūzan Shōgenji (Temple)**　Located across the street in the same area. It is a Zen temple of the Myōshinji branch of the Rinzai sect in Kyoto, and serves as one of the four head liaison temples (*furekashira*) for Edo. The principal image is a statue of Shakyamuni Buddha. The founder is known as Reikan Fushō Zenji. The Zenji's personal name (*imina*) was Sōkyū, and his courtesy name (*azana*) was Hōzan. (Commonly called "Naginata Hōzan." In former times, a monkey was kept tethered within the temple grounds, and to this day it is popularly known as "Saru-dera" [Monkey Temple]. Its former site was said to be in Banchō.) The principal image of the Kannon Hall is the Shō-Kannon (Aryāvalokiteśvara), said to be the work of Kōbō Daishi (Kūkai).

**Yakuryūzan Shōzōin (Temple)**　Located to the south of the same area, in Yoko-tera-machi. It belongs to the Tendai sect under Tōeizan (Kan'eiji Temple). The founder is Enkan Risshi. The sacred image of Yakushi Nyorai (the Healing Buddha), the principal object of worship, is said to have been carved by Dengyō Daishi (Saichō) with one stroke per three prostrations (*ittō sanrai*). (It is commonly known as the "Kusakari Yakushi Nyorai" [Grass-cutter's Healing Buddha].) According to tradition, when this temple was formerly located near Bairin-zaka slope (within the castle grounds), a grass-cutter appeared and presented this sacred image of Yakushi to the founder, Priest Enkan, and then departed. During the Chōroku era, Ōta Sakinogo Nyūdō Dōkan founded this temple and made the image

**Akagi Myōjin Shrine**　Located along the back street to the north of the same area. It is the guardian shrine (*chinju*) of Ushigome, and the administrative priest's temple (*bettō*) is the Tendai-sect Tōkakuji. The enshrined deity is the same god as that of Mt. Akagi in Kōzuke Province (present-day Gunma Prefecture), and the *honji butsu* (Buddhist counterpart deity) is said to be the Shōgun Jizō Bosattva. In ancient times, the Ōgo clan deeply revered this deity, initially enshrining it in their domain and calling it "Chikato Myōjin." Their descendant, Shigeyasu, moved to this province (Musashi) and settled in Ushigome. He then changed his surname from Ōgo to Ushigome (his place of residence was in the area of Ushigome Wara-dana, as mentioned previously), and following the wishes of his ancestors, enshrined this deity here. The festival is held on the nineteenth day of the ninth month. (The site of the original enshrinement of this shrine was in a rice field in the Sekiguchi domain, below Mejiro; even today a small grove of trees remains there, which is called "Akagi no Mori" [Akagi Forest].)
