Inryōzan Saishōji Temple: Located in Enokimachi in the same area, it is a Zen temple of the Kyoto Myōshinji sect (formerly, abbots were dispatched from Myōshinji in rotation). It enshrines Shakyamuni Buddha as its principal image. The founding priest was Zen Master Shin'in Shōden, and the founding patron was the nun Soshin. This nun was the daughter of Makino Hyōbu Shōyū Masaharu and served as a lady-in-waiting close to the Shogun along with Kasuga no Tsubone. The temple has a Buddha hall, with Hōshin'in serving as its administrator (this temple was founded by the nun Hōshin). The pond in front of the Buddha hall is called Hōōchi (Phoenix Pond). The Reiki Water is located on the grounds of Hōshin'in, and during the Kan'ei era, it was used for the Shogun's tea water.

The founder's memorial pagoda is maintained by Yōshun'in. All together, there are six monks' quarters, a sutra hall, bell tower, kitchen, bathhouse, and other buildings that majestically line up in splendid array (the plaque on the Sanbutsu Hall reading "Tenka Inryō" (Universal Cool Shade) is the calligraphy of Imperial Prince Kōkei Hōshin'ō, First Rank Quasi-Empress).

Site of the Former Residence of Bungo Koshijū Ōtomo Yoshinobu: The temple grounds are traditionally said to mark this historic site. In the second year of Bunroku, Ōtomo Yoshinobu participated in the Korean campaign, but due to negligence in military preparations,

Toyotomi Taikō punished him by exiling him to this province and confining him to this location. This place is said to be that very site (According to "Nankō Sawa," Ōtomo Saemon no Kami Yoshisumi neglected his duties during the Korean campaign in the Bunroku years, had his domain confiscated, and later died in Hitachi Province. His heir, Sōgorō Yoshinobu, lived at this location. Yoshinobu was appointed to the Fourth Court Rank and became a chamberlain, hence he was called Bungo Koshijū. After the Battle of Sekigahara in the fifth year of Keichō, he was granted land worth 3,500 koku in Tsukuba District, Jōshū, but died young. Also, "Edo Kanoko," a popular publication, mistakenly records the name as Yoshinori instead of Yoshinobu). Later, Ōhashi Ryūkei resided at this location (In a work called "Bōkai Maidan," recording events of the 17th year of Kan'ei, it mentions that the Shogun's secretary Ōhashi Ryūkei was granted the Ōtomo residence in Takada, where there was a Tenman shrine. Details are given under Takada Tenmangu).

The Ōtomo Pine: Located on the grounds of the Ontachitsutsu-gumi Takano family, east of Tenjinmachi in the same area. This was formerly a pine in the garden of Ōtomo Yoshinobu's villa, but was later lost in a fire. The landowner lamented the loss of this historic site and planted a young tree (According to one account from Ōtomo family tradition, this was the pine in front of a sukiya tea house built by the retainer Kira Denzaemon, who followed Ōtomo Sōgorō Yoshinobu when he moved to Bushū. The name Inryōzan Saishōji is said to derive from this pine).

Ōtomo Inari Shrine: Located in the same area, this is also said to have been established by Yoshinobu's patronage.

Ichijuzan Sōhakuji Temple: Located in the side street facing Saishōji, it belongs to the Nichiren sect and is affiliated with Chōmyōji in Kyoto. The founding priest was called Nichii Shōnin. The image of Shakyamuni Buddha as the principal deity was made by Dengyō Daishi.
