In the third year, Fujiwara no Hidesato led his military forces to Nakano in this province to destroy him (Taira no Masakado). At that time, he had an illness in his right arm, and there was no medicine in the army, causing him great worry. That night, receiving a divine revelation, he prayed to the principal Buddhist image of this temple, and his illness was immediately cured. At that time, he also offered a petition for the subjugation of Masakado. Indeed, he succeeded in killing Masakado, and after his triumphant return, he built temple buildings and named it Ensho-ji. Later, in the second year of Kennin (1202), Edo Minbu-no-Taifu Yorisuke carried out repairs, but in the eighth year of Koan (1285), it was caught in war fires and the Buddhist buildings burned down. Later, in the first year of Einin (1293), High Priest Raiyu restored the thatched buildings and repaired the old records, but during the Tensho era when Uesugi Kagetora fought in this area, it was again destroyed by war fires. In the eighteenth year of Kanei (1641), Kasuga no Tsubone sought official permission and had it restored once again.

Uemon Cherry Tree {{Located in front of the temple hall, it is a single-petaled variety with exceptionally fine fragrance, an incomparable famous tree. According to local folklore, long ago a person named Takeda Uemon lived here and loved this cherry tree. Being a famous cherry tree in Kashiwagi Village, it was named after Kashiwagi Uemon from The Tale of Genji. In the domain registry of the Hojo family of Odawara, it states "Ayabe Soshiro's domain: Kashiwagi Tsunohazu"}}

Yoroi Myojin Shrine - Located to the northeast of Ensho-ji and managed by Ensho-ji. According to tradition, Fujiwara no Hidesato

killed Masakado, and after his triumphant return, buried Masakado's armor in this place, built a small shrine above it, and called it Yoroi Myojin (Armor Deity). In front of the shrine stands an old pine tree called Kabuto-matsu (Helmet Pine), which is said to mark where the helmet was buried.

Yodobashi Bridge - Spans between Naruko-juku and Nakano Village. There are two bridges, large and small, with water wheels on this side of the bridge. Long ago, when the Tokugawa shogun family held falconry in this area, there was an imperial command to name this bridge Yodobashi after Yodo in Yamashiro Province. {{Some say that Yodobashi might be Yoto-bashi. In the Wamyo-sho, there is a village called Yoto in Toshima District of Musashi Province. Since this place is between Toshima and Tama districts and was an ancient yoto (administrative unit), it might have been called Yoto-bashi. However, we do not know if this is correct}}

The old names were also Omokage-no-hashi (Reflection Bridge) and Sugatami-no-hashi (Mirror Bridge).

Junisho Gongen Shrine - Located in Tsunohazu Village south of Yodobashi Bridge. The deity enshrined is the same as Kumano Gongen of Kii Province. It is a shrine served by Joganzn-ji Temple in Hongo Village. According to the shrine records, during the Oei era, there was a person named Suzuki Kuro, a descendant of Suzuki Shoji Shigekuni, who lived in Fujishiro in Kii Province but wandered and moved to this area of Nakano.
