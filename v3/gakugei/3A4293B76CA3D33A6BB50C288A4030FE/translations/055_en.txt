【Right Page】
It is said that this mountain originally had two old pine trees, and at that time mountain doves would come and play daily on the branches of these pines. This was considered auspicious, so a small shrine to Hachiman Daijin was temporarily built, and those pine trees were made sacred trees. {{Annotation: According to Nankōtei, this place was formerly part of Waseda Village called Nakashima. Here lived a wealthy man named Aoyagitsu Rokubei, who had served the Hōjō family in ancient times as a samurai, and this was the mountain forest he had inherited.}} This place was formerly called Amida Mountain, but no one knew the reason why. In the eighteenth year, summer of the year of Kanoto-Mi, among the disciples of Priest Shūyū Hōin of Nakano Hōsen-ji was a monk named Ishōin Ryōshō. He was from Suō Province and was a parishioner of Yamaguchi Hachiman. {{Annotation: As a child he served a certain Enomoto of the Mōri family retainers, but after Enomoto's death, he became a monk at age nineteen, climbed Mount Kōya, became a disciple of Priest Shunzan of Hōshōin, completed twelve years of religious training, and from age thirty-one began traveling the provinces for religious practice, during which he is said to have performed various miracles.}} Therefore this monk was welcomed as the shrine priest. In the autumn of the same year, on the third day of the eighth month, when clearing the hillside to build a hermitage, a sacred cave was discovered. Inside this cave, on a stone, stood a bronze statue of Amida Buddha. {{Annotation: About three inches tall}} This was the honji (original Buddhist form) of Hachiman Shrine and corresponded to the mountain's name, so it was considered miraculous. {{Annotation: The name "Ana-Hachiman" (Cave Hachiman) originated here. The old site still remains beside the slope today.}} Also on this day, the Shogun's heir {{Annotation: Lord Tsunayoshi}} was born, so people increasingly recognized its divine power. {{Annotation: The Edo Meisho-ki states it was the ninth day of the eighth month of the same year.}}

【Left Page】
{{Annotation: The area around the shrine was cordoned off in a square of one chō, the land was cleared, the main shrine was moved to the base of the sacred pine tree, and protective fences were built around it. At that time, the Lord of Kaga Province sent several hundred workers to fortify the ground, so it was completed without delay.}} {{Annotation: On the fourteenth day of the same month, the shrine relocation ceremony was performed. Matsudaira Shingo-zaemon brought his retainers to set up curtains around Matoyama and erected formal small targets. Since the sacred archery followed proper shooting methods, a twelve-year-old child of someone named Koike performed this duty.}} Later, during the Genroku period, the shrine buildings were constructed as they are today and became magnificently equipped. {{Annotation: According to Nankōtei Sawaī, Lord Tsunayoshi especially revered this shrine, and after his vows were fulfilled, he had the shrine built. The back gate was donated by Naitō Buzen-no-kami, Fugen Hall by Matsudaira Sakon-shōgen, the hand-washing enclosure by Masuyama Hyōbu-shōyū, and others. Also, books such as Kōfu Jinja Ryakki and Wakan Sansai Zue state that Keishōin-den rebuilt it during the Genroku period.}}

Wakamiya Hachiman Shrine {{Annotation: Located to the left in front of the main shrine}}
Tōshō Daigongen {{Annotation: Enshrined alongside at the same location. Many people visit on April 17th each year}}
Himuro Myōjin Shrine {{Annotation: Facing the main shrine. A plaque carved with the two characters "Seitoku" is hung. The deity enshrined is Ōnamuchi-no-mikoto. On December 22nd of Genroku 2, Makimura Naoyoshi, aged sixteen, suffered from smallpox. On January 2nd of Genroku 3, Watanabe Koreyoshi, a resident of Kanazawa, received a divine dream response and worshipped this deity. Naoyoshi prayed to this deity and recovered. It is said that the deity was first enshrined here around Genroku 7.}}
Hikari-matsu (Light Pine) {{Annotation: Located on a side path of the slope between the temple administrator's quarters and the main shrine. The old pine is said to have withered during the Enkyō period, so what exists now is a young tree planted later. According to Nankōtei, this place was formerly a mountain forest where pine trees flourished, and among them was one pine tree that would sometimes emit miraculous light on dark nights, hence the tree was called "Hikari-matsu" (Light Pine). Also, when Hachiman Shrine was first established here in Kan'ei 13, mountain doves came to play in this tree.}}
Hōshō-chi (Life-Release Pond) {{Annotation: Located below the stone steps. Pure spring water drips down from the hillside. This can truly be said to be a wonder that lives up to the name of Iwashimizu (Pure Rock Water).}}
Shutsugen-sho (Manifestation Site) {{Annotation: Located along the cliff on the mid-slope. This is the old site of the ancient sacred cave. Until recently, there was a hall called Shutsugen-dō where a statue of Amida Nyorai of the lower grade, upper birth of the Nine Grades of Buddha was enshrined, but it is no longer visible.}}
