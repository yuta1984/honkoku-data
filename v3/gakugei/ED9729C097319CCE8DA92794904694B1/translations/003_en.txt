Muryōzan Dentsū-in, also called Jukyō-ji, is located about two chō northwest (inu-i direction) of Koishikawa Ushi Tenjin. It is one of the eighteen seminaries (danrin) of the Jōdo sect. The principal image of Amida Nyorai was created by Eshin Sōzu, and the temple was founded by priest Ryōyo during the Meitoku era (1390-1394). 《There are over one hundred dormitory buildings》

Mausoleum 《This is the mausoleum of Dentsū-in-den. According to her last will, a tomb was built, and her posthumous Buddhist name was taken as the temple's name. Her remains are enshrined at Sōkei-ji in the same location》
《According to the Wakan Sansai Zue, she passed away on the 29th day of the 8th month of Keichō 7 (1602), year of the metal tiger》

Founder's Hall 《Located to the right of the main hall》
Hachiman Shrine 《Located in the same place. During the Tenshō era, an old man obtained a stone plaque with the characters for Hachiman Shrine through a divine dream. The custodian temple is called Keikyū-in》
Benzaiten Shrine 《This shrine was originally located on the grounds of Hakusan Palace alongside Hakusan Hikawa, but the Nyotai Gongen shrine was moved to this location and dedicated to Benzaiten》
Taku Zōsu Inari Shrine 《Located near the rear gate within the precinct. In ancient times, a fox transformed into a monk, calling itself Taku Zōsu and coming to the seminary nightly to discuss Buddhist law. Later it was dedicated as Inari and made a guardian deity of the temple》
Jōnenbutsu Hall 《Belongs to Shinju-in, one of the sub-temples》
Shin-nenbutsu Hall 《Similarly belongs to Zuishin-in》

Daikokuten 《Located in Fukuju-in within the temple complex. According to Kikuoka Senryō, when they first dug a well, this sacred image was found in the earth, so the well was named Ofuku-no-i (Fortune Well). It was once within Dentsū-in's precinct, but now the well is within Lord Matsudaira Harima's estate. It may have been confused with Gokuraku water. That well is also within the same estate, and having two springs in such a small area suggests it's probably one of them》
《According to the temple's origin story, the Daikokuten enshrined at this temple is a miraculous image transmitted through three countries, being a sacred image combining three deities: Daikoku, Tamon, and Benzaiten. During Emperor Kōtoku's reign, a person named Toko from minister Rokurai of Goryeo brought it to Japan, and it was kept in Gamō District of Ōmi Province until priest Hōyo Reiō obtained it through divine inspiration during the Meiwa era and enshrined it here. On kinoe-ne days, worshippers gather in crowds. The hall's plaque reads "Fukuju-den" and was written by Nyokū, the 23rd generation master of this temple》

Sutra Hall 《A plaque reading "Shōkyō-kutsu" hangs at the front, and inside houses the complete Ming edition of the Buddhist canon. For special reasons, it is revered and treasured as a dharma treasure of this temple》
Unclaimed Graves Mound 《Kyōhō 6, year of the metal ox (1721)》
