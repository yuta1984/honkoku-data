Since these were not swords that distant relatives would commonly wear, growing ever more suspicious, he opened the body amulet to examine it, and found it written in the imperial hand of the Emperor of Yoshino: "Regarding the subjugation of the imperial enemies, where the imperial will is directed lies entirely in Yoshisada's military achievements. Having chosen him, we seek no other. Swift strategies should be employed." Thus it was certain that this was indeed Yoshisada's head, so the corpse was placed on a palanquin, carried by eight time-monks (ji-shū) and sent to Ōjō-in temple for funeral rites, while the head was placed in a vermillion Chinese chest and, accompanied by Ujie Nakatsukasa, was immediately sent up to Kyoto.

Nitta-san Jōju-in: Also called Shō-fudō-ji. Located about one chō south of the same place, on the same side. This is the head temple (bettō-ji) of Nitta Daimyōjin, belonging to the Shingi school of Shingon Buddhism under Hōtō-in of Rokugō. The principal image, Fudō Myō-ō, is said to be carved by Kōbō Daishi and was a sacred image protected by Lord Yoshisada. 《Marginal note: Now a separate hall has been built called Inu-dō where it is enshrined, located on the left side within the gate》According to tradition, when Lord Yoshisada established his camp at Iruma River, two divine children appeared at his bedside, saying that if he had a sincere wish to defeat Kamakura, he should enshrine them at the place in Watada village...
