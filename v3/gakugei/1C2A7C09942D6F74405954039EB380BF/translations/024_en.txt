【Right Page Upper Section】
○Mari (kemari ball) is kicked in imitation of the head of Chiyū. The Asukai family is the head family of this art. Among the commoners, there is one called Sakon.

○Kenbō are screens placed behind the inkstone. They prevent the ink on the inkstone from drying in the wind and also serve as dust protection.

○Shochin are paperweights used when the wind blows. They are also called bunchin or atsusho.

○Atsushaku are kesan (calculation tools). They are called kesan because they resemble the kusazuri (skirt plates) of armor, which are called kesan.

○Suiteki (water droppers) are water containers for inkstones. They are also called gyokuzenjo. They are water containers made in the shape of zenjo (toads).

【Left Page Upper Section】
They are also called kenteki.

○Tsumezue are also called kakizue. They are called "Mako's hand" after the immortal woman Mako, whose hands were like bird claws.

○Hitsuka are brush rests. They are also called hitsukaku, hitsuhō, or hitsusan.

○Kaihō are what we now call hijoegi (rulers).

○Gankyō are eyeglasses. They are also called aitai.

○Shokudai are candlesticks. They are also called shokuka, and come in various shapes.

○Shokudo refers to candlesticks that have human figures.
