**The Grave of Master Kyūsō Muro**　Located in the same area, slightly up a hill behind the north side of Sakashita-machi. Beside it is also the grave of his son Chūzaburō Hiromo.

《The master's family name was Muro; his personal name was Naokiyo; his courtesy name was Shirei; he took the literary name Kyūsō. His common name was Shinsuke. His studio was named Seiken (Quiet Frugality). His ancestor was a descendant of Kumagai Naozane, and the family originated from Aga-gun in Bitchū Province. His father's name was Genboku, who took the literary name Sōan. His mother was of the Hirano family. He was born in the village of Yanaka in Edo in Manji 1 (1658), the year of Tsuchinoe-inu. He was of extraordinary talent, and his keen intelligence surpassed all others. He entered service in the Kaga domain, studied under Master Kinoshita Jun'an, and resided as a guest in Kyoto. In his spare time from discussions and debates, he composed the "Daigaku Shinsho" (New Commentary on the Great Learning), thereby elucidating the deep meanings of the text's chapters and phrases. In Shōtoku 1 (1711), in response to a summons from the Edo Shogunate, he came to Edo, and his poems and writings of exchange and correspondence accumulated into volumes. His manner of response flowed like a stream; he caused the glory of Eastern civilization (Japan) to shine as never before in all antiquity, proclaimed the flourishing peace of the nation, spread his influence across the seas, and rendered it worthy of proclamation for all eternity. After Lord Yūtoku (Tokugawa Yoshimune) succeeded to the position of Shōgun, he specially selected this master and appointed him as Lecturer-in-Attendance at the castle. The establishment of this post is said to have》

【Left page】

《begun with this very master. He once received the Shōgun's august instructions and composed an explanatory record of the names and meanings of the Five Relationships and Five Virtues in native Japanese script (kana), and the completed work was presented for the Shōgun's perusal. He also composed the "Rokuyū Engi Taigi" (Essentials of the Extended Meaning of the Six Maxims), which was published and distributed throughout the realm by official order. Before this, he had written commentaries on the Analects and Mencius, the Doctrine of the Mean, and a broad interpretation of the Book of Changes. Before the collation and revision were complete, they were lost in a disaster. The master happened to fall ill with an unknown ailment and was unable to continue working on the manuscript. The illness worsened day by day, and in the end he did not recover. He repeatedly submitted requests citing illness and asking to retire, but received gracious responses urging him to remain in service. He continued to bear his official title while residing at home, devoting himself to recuperation. During his illness he composed "Shundai Zatsuwa" (Miscellaneous Conversations at Shundai), which was recognized as significant and requested by the Shōgun for perusal. He also composed "Taikyoku-zu Jutsu" (Explication of the Diagram of the Supreme Ultimate), completing one volume, which broadly expounded the millennium-old secrets of Lianxi (Zhou Dunyi) and the Fujian school (Zhu Xi), leaving it for future scholars in generations to come. This was the master's final work. On the twelfth day of the eighth month of Kyōhō 19 (1734), the year of Kinoe-tora, he passed away at his Shogunate-granted residence at Shundai. He was seventy-eight years of age. He was buried at Ōtsuka-no-sato in Toshima-gun, Musashi Province.》

**Tsukubasan Goji-in Temple**　Located to the north of Otowa-machi. It belongs to the Shingon sect and is a branch of the Hase school of Yamato Province. It is granted a temple estate of over one thousand five hundred koku.

**Main Hall**　The principal image is Fudō Myōō (Acala, the Immovable Wisdom King). 《The maker is unknown. It is also said that in former times, a Shakyamuni Buddha was enshrined as the principal image.》

**Kangiten (Vinayaka)**《Enshrined to the right alongside (the principal image).》　[Kani-ga-ike Pond] 《This refers to the pond in front of the temple. Before the temple was built, this location was called "Seriseri" or "Seresere." It is also said that in ancient times, an image of Yakushi Nyorai (Bhaiṣajyaguru) appeared from this very spot.》

**Gongen-yama (Gongen Hill)**《This refers to a slightly elevated hill in the rear garden. The true likeness and sacred image of Tōshō Daijin-kun (Tokugawa Ieyasu) is enshrined here. It is said that he personally planted his own whiskers (into the image).》
