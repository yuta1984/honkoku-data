The Great Master (Nichiren) is regarded as the founding patriarch. In Bunpō 1, year of the Fire Snake (1317), Nichirō Shōnin, second among the six elder disciples, renovated this temple and made it a great monastery. Thereupon all the disciples unanimously honored him as the founder.
《Marginal note: Nichirō Shōnin was called Chikugo Kō Shōbōin Taikoku Ajari. His surname was Minamoto, and his father was Jirō Moriyoshi of Hirakaga in Nansō, a descendant of Nitta Yoshimitsu (Shinra Saburō). He was the son of Hirakaga Arikuni, fourth-generation descendant. At age ten he became a monk. In Bun'ei 8 (1271) he followed the Great Master and was imprisoned in the earthen dungeon at Tatsunokuchi. In the same year plus one he went to Sado Province, and in Kōan 1 (1278) became a senior disciple. In Bunpō 2 (1318) he received orders from Hōjō Tokimune and together with Nichiin debated doctrine with followers of various sects successfully. In Gen'ō 1》
《Marginal note: On January 21st he died at Ikegami at age seventy-eight. The Bokkaen Manroku states that genkan (entrance halls) did not exist in ancient times. During the Ashikaga period, Zen Buddhism established them with the meaning of "entering into the profound mystery." In ancient times people wore wooden clogs and sandals even up to the corridors above the genkan. This was the case throughout Kyoto until recently, according to elderly people's accounts. This old custom still remains at Ikegami Honmon-ji Temple today}}

Soshi-dō (Founder's Hall) {{Marginal note: According to Sōkanoko, it burned down on October 13th, 7th year of Hōei, year of the Tiger (1710). The Watanabe Kōan Rōjin Taiwa-ki records this fire as occurring on the 10th at the hour of the Boar. The Kōgen Bukan records that in November of Kōji 1 (1555), when Kaji Umanokamii Akisue under the Hōjō family banner raised a rebellion, the various halls of this Honmon-ji Temple were also consumed by fire. Nittō Shōnin rebuilt it}}
