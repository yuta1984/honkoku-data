The private houses in this area plant plum trees throughout their front gardens and back yards. In May, they harvest the fruit to sell in Edo, so in February when the flowers are in full bloom, many people come to enjoy the fragrance. {{The garden of a farmer named Sanemon has particularly old trees with the most excellent flower fragrance}}

Site of Namekata Danjō-chū Akitsura's Residence: This likely refers to the area around Rokugō Hachiman-zuka. There is a place called Misono Village in this area, which is said to be named so because it was the former site of Akitsura's flower garden.

Seikōzan Endon-ji Temple: Located in Kamata Village, it belongs to the Nichiren sect under Ikegami Honmon-ji. The principal images are wooden statues of Shakyamuni, Tahō, and others. The founding priest was the elderly monk Nichishō {{who was also the founder of Ikegami Taibō}}, and the temple was revived by Nichikei {{who died on the first day of the second month of Kan'ei 20 (1643)}}. This temple is on the former site of the residence of Namekata Danjō-chū Naokiyo, a retainer of the Odawara Hōjō family and lord of Rokugō {{The Odawara Hōjō family's domain register shows that Namekata Yojirō held territories including Rokugō, Taishi-gawara, Kasai, and Terashima. Yojirō may have been a member of the Danjō clan or perhaps an earlier name}}, and he was the founding patron of this temple.

The temple's death register records Naokiyo's posthumous Buddhist name as Seikōin-den Enan-gyōton-nichihō-daikoji {{His father was called Shurinōsuke Yasuchika. On March 18, Tenshō 18 (1590), Naokiyo was killed in action at the Battle of Odawara}}. His gravestone remains on the left side in front of the main hall. The moat that exists in front of the temple is said to be used as it was from the time when Naokiyo resided here, serving as the

outer moat of his fortification. The Odawara-ki records that in Eiroku 9, Takeda Shingen watched for an opportunity when Odawara had few troops and attacked from an unexpected direction. It states that since Namekata Danjō was stationed in Rokugō, he fortified Hachiman near his residence and, leading Inage's Tajima {{Tajima Hyōbu-zaemon Yukifusa}}, Yokota {{likely referring to Yokoyama Shikibu Hironari}}, Komabayashi {{referring to Komabayashi Tosho Sadatomo}}, and others, burned down bridges to prevent the Kōshū forces from passing. Shingen scattered Shinagawa's Utakawa Iwami-no-kami, Suzuki, and others, and when Rokugō Bridge fell, he proceeded to Ikegami, pursued Ikegami Temple {{Ikegami Temple likely refers to Honmon-ji}}, used monks from this temple as guides, crossed the Yaguchi ferry by boat, reached a place called Hirama in Inage, and pursued the sixteen villages of Inage.

Kamata Hachiman Shrine: Located in the same area, beside the road. The Odawara-ki text mentioned earlier, which states that Namekata Danjō fortified Hachiman near his residence, likely refers to this shrine. {{The Sandai Jitsuroku records that on August 14, Jōgan 6 (864), an imperial decree ranked Kamata-no-kami of Musashi Province, Junior Fifth Rank Lower Grade, among the official shrines, which may also refer to this shrine}}
