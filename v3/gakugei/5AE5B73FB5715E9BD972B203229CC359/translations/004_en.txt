Now, the crushed grains are stripped of their essence by the millstone, their substance transformed by the filtering water, and with vapors rising like clouds, suddenly a white, lustrous, square jewel emerges. Is this not wondrous? In the work by Master He, it says "one jewel yielding a hundred delicacies." Alas, this humble food item merely satisfies human appetite, yet the so-called culinary arts produce endless transformations, with myriad forms and variations - how could this be the limit? This would not be false even if Yi Ya were to reappear and present it to the Prince of Huainan. When I prepare it and others season it, and we transmit and spread this to the world, it becomes ever more varied and ever more

precious, ever more transformed and ever more wondrous. Does it transform and transform again, change and change again? Or not? Transformation ultimately knows no bounds. When the book was completed and Kō asked me for a preface, I playfully composed this quatrain: "Huainan's legacy, a hundred delicacies achieved; Well-fed wealthy men find pure sustenance; All this, so subtle, worthy of savoring the Way; Even pedantic scholars gladly gain unified fame."

Written in the Hekkō Pavilion by Sō Teishi Kyū in the auspicious first year of Tenmei, year of the Metal Ox, in peaceful times.
