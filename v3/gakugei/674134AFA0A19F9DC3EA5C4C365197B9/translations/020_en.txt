【Right Page】
In the lands of Michinoku, the shinobu fern-pattern dyeing—
for whose sake has my heart
begun to be disturbed, when it was not I who caused it?

This is also a love poem. Long ago, from Shinobu District in Ōshū Province, they produced something called mojizuri (rubbed-pattern dyeing). This was silk dyed with indigo and purple in patterns, and the disordered appearance of these patterns was likened to a heart disordered by love in composing this poem. The meaning is: "My heart becoming disordered by love is not due to someone else—it has become disordered because of you. Yet why do you remain so cold and refuse to open your heart to me?" expressing some resentment. The part "Michinoku no shinobu mojizuri" is what is called a jo-kotoba (prefatory phrase), and up to the word "mojizuri" there is no particular meaning.

Kawara no Sadaijin (Minister of the Left Kawara)

【Left Page】
For your sake I went out to the spring fields
to pick young herbs—
upon my sleeves the snow
keeps falling and falling.

This was composed when the poet had not yet ascended to the imperial throne but was still a prince, and was sent along with young herbs he gave to someone. The meaning is: "I went out to the spring meadows to pick these young herbs to give to you, but it was extremely cold, with snow falling on my sleeves and such—I truly went through hardship to pick these young herbs for you." To give something prepared through such hardship shows the depth of one's sincerity, and in ancient times everyone was like this.

Emperor Kōkō
