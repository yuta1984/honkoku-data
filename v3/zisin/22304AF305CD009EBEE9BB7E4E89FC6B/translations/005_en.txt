...lighting horse lanterns (bachōchin) to visit nearby friends and relatives to inquire after their wellbeing — this was something everyone did for one another. It was also a rather amusing sight to see dogs crawling up to the pillows of people sleeping where they had tumbled down. The water in all the wells had turned muddy. Those who had recovered somewhat in spirit boiled tea and cooked rice on the main streets, drew water, and at last managed to get food down their throats. Even so, their hearts remained unsettled, as the low rumbling continued without cease.

Within each neighborhood, iron rods and split bamboo were clapped and rattled as people went around strictly making fire-prevention announcements. From somewhere or other, an old person came along distributing talismanic charms. The verse of the charm read:

　　Though it may loosen, surely it shall not slip —
　　　　the keystone (kaname-ishi),
　　　as long as the god of Kashima endures.

Everyone copied this down and pasted it on the doorpost pillars or on the central pillar (daikoku-bashira) of their homes. With great reverence, people placed the sacred purification amulets (ohārai) of Amaterasu Ōmikami upon their heads, and tucked talismanic secret writings (himon) into their topknots. Those caring for the elderly, the sick, or small children were beyond description in their anxiety. Among them, there were those who had been crushed and pinned beneath collapsed houses, and others who were caught between objects or pushed against walls... (continues)
