...laying out tatami mats, everyone made their way out onto the main streets. As for old houses and old earthen storehouses (dozō), all had collapsed. Furthermore, shrines and temples, stone torii gates, stone lanterns, mud-plastered walls (tsuiji-bei), and tall fences fell in tremendous numbers. Even the sturdiest of storehouses was not spared cracks. Shelved goods fell and were damaged, kitchen stoves toppled, and sliding doors (fusuma), wooden doors, and paper screens (shōji) needless to say suffered damage. It was universally the case that houses warped and doors no longer shut properly. Among all this, there was a curious irony: old houses that had already been warped before the earthquake sometimes found their doors fitting better than before — and this too was a strange and amusing thing. The sounds of destruction echoed throughout heaven and earth, and clouds of dust darkened the air.

All the while, the low rumbling continued without pause; minds were thrown into disarray, souls seemed stolen away, and people were chilled to the bone with fear — those who panicked and fell into a frenzy were hardly to be blamed. On the night of the 2nd, people intended to keep watch through the night on the main streets, but thinking of how they might suffer from the cold night air, they used boards to fashion makeshift roofs, stretched ropes across and covered them with straw mats, and the entire city of Kyoto spent the night outdoors. In each neighborhood, tall lanterns (takaharichiochin) were solemnly erected, hanging lanterns (andō) were strung along the rows of houses, people donned warrior's sedge helmets (jinkasa) and chest armor...
