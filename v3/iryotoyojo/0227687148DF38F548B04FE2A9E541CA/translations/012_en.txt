Detoxification
Generally, when intoxicated by tobacco, one detoxifies with sugar water. Alternatively, with licorice root, or miso soup, or salt water, or even cold water can provide detoxification. One prescription states:

　　Ophiopogon, perilla seeds, trichosanthes seeds, loquat leaves, licorice root
　　Take the above five ingredients in equal portions, decoct as usual, remove the dregs, add one ryō of sugar and take - this is most effective. When I tried this myself, it was difficult to drink.

According to a certain book, when tar from inside tobacco pipes sticks to clothing and won't wash out, simply add watermelon seeds and rub, and it will come off cleanly.

　　　　Medicinal Uses
Ryōan says that in Nanban-style surgical blue plasters, adding the juice of young tobacco leaves effectively stops pain, opens abscesses, stops bleeding, and kills insects. Generally, for indigo and various grasses whose leaves develop insects, pouring tobacco stem juice on them

is good. Bundling stems and placing them in the recesses under the eaves prevents rats from passing through.
　Chopping stems into small pieces and scattering them over privy waste prevents insects from breeding.
　Placing tobacco between the pages of books removes stains. Boiling stems and dyeing paper with that liquid works excellently as a substitute for phellodendron bark.
　For cataracts or night blindness, occasionally inserting tar about the size of a poppy seed is good. When the inside of the eyes becomes cloudy for no particular reason and tears flow, inserting tobacco tar about the size of a poppy seed immediately clears the vision.
