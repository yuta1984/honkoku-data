In the "Mahā-śamatha-vipaśyanā" it states: "If one focuses the mind in the dantian, then the qi and breath become harmonized. Therefore, one can effectively heal illnesses. The dantian is located two and a half inches from the navel." This all refers to making the center between the thighs below the navel the pivot of the entire body. For example, it is like a door having hinges or a fan having a rivet. (Dantian is originally a vulgar term, but we temporarily follow what people can easily understand. At this dantian below the navel, at the space between the eyebrows on the face, at the tips of the feet, and at the tip of a sword held in hand - at these points that align with the center of the straight line where heavenly qi reaches the earth's core, an enemy's striking sword can never penetrate. This matter has been discussed in detail in the sword technique section of volume four.)

Mencius calls this the "qi of vast expansiveness" (hao-ran zhi qi). "Hao" means broad, or great, or abundant like water, referring to water that is wide and deep and greatly overflowing.

[Left page]
Therefore, "hao-ran" is annotated as the appearance of magnificent flowing, compared to a great river's flow without stagnation or obstruction, meaning it fills the space between heaven and earth with nothing left unpenetrated. This is precisely what humans receive from the combination of the yin-yang dual qi of heaven and earth, what the "Yi Jing" calls "Supreme indeed is the Kun principle! All things receive life from it, and it obediently receives from Heaven." Its virtue is described as "containing, magnanimous, luminous, and great; all things achieve fulfillment." "Containing" means embracing, "magnanimous" means abundant, "luminous and great" means clear and expansive, and "all achieve fulfillment" means this great qi enables all varieties and multitudes of things to realize their nature.
