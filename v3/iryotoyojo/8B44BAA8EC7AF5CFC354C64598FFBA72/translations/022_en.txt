The *Mahāśamathavipaśyanā* states: "If one stops the mind in the dantian, then the qi-breath becomes harmonized. Therefore it can heal illnesses. The dantian is located two and a half inches below the navel." This also [refers to] making the center between the thighs below the navel the pivot of the entire body. For example, it is like a door having hinges, or a fan having a central pin. (Dantian is originally a vulgar term, but I temporarily follow what people can easily understand. When this dantian below the navel, the space between the eyebrows on the face, the tips of the feet, and the tip of a sword held in hand are all aligned with the center of the straight line where this heavenly qi reaches to the earth's center, an enemy's striking sword can never penetrate. This matter has been discussed in detail in the section on swordsmanship in volume four.)

Mencius calls this "haoran qi" (vast qi). "Hao" means vast, great, or abundant like water, referring to water that is broad and deep and greatly overflowing.

[Left page]
Therefore, haoran is annotated as the appearance of grand flow, compared to a great river's flow without stagnation or obstruction, meaning it fills heaven and earth completely, reaching everywhere without exception. This is precisely what humans receive from the unity of the two qi of heaven and earth, yin and yang, what the *Book of Changes* calls "Supreme indeed is the Kun principle! All things receive life from it, and it obediently follows Heaven." Its virtue is described as "embracing and magnanimous, brilliant and great, all things achieve fulfillment." "Embracing" means encompassing, "magnanimous" means abundant, "brilliant and great" means clearly and broadly allowing all varieties of myriad things to achieve their nature through fulfillment—this is the great qi.
