The origin of colors begins with three: red, yellow, and blue. Among these, yellow and blue each have dark and light variations, which when added give five colors. To these, the intermediate colors of moss green (stone blue) and purple are added, yielding seven colors. Beyond these, there are no other colors. Therefore, since colors stop at exactly these seven, musical tones too reach seven and stop there. This is due to the natural principles of numbers.

In a previous year, a man named Someya Kichigoro Tomonobu deeply immersed himself in the principles of pitch and musical tuning (ritsuryō), and discovered from the colors that appear in a rainbow that color and sound share the same origin, and that colors number only these seven. He argued that the conventional view of five colors—blue, yellow, red, white, and black—was in error. However, at that time there was no one in the world who would accept this idea.

Then, a book by the Westerner (Dutch) "Marutin" (Martin) arrived in Japan, which described projecting sunlight onto paper to demonstrate that color and sound share the same origin, and compared the seven colors to the seven modes of musical tuning to determine their proportional measurements. Not only was it remarkable that Tomonobu's theory, separated by ten thousand leagues, turned out to accord perfectly with this work, but it is also wonderful to note that the translator, upon hearing this, was at last able to read (and understand) it.
