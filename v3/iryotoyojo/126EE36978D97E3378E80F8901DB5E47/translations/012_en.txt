In our country as well, since the time of Kamakura when tea seeds were transmitted from the Song dynasty of China, tea has continued to spread throughout society and flourishes ever more to this day. Regarding the varieties of tea and the customs of the tea ceremony, these are well known to all, and there is no need to go into detail here. Its flavor is bitter, sweet, and slightly cold, with no toxicity. It treats scrofula (*ruso*, lumpy sores), clears phlegm-heat, stops thirst, lessens the need for sleep while imparting energy and cheering the spirit, aids digestion, dispels heat, removes miasmic pestilence (*shōki*), promotes urination and bowel movements, and refreshes the head and eyes. It treats the stuporous, unawakening state caused by stroke (*chūfū*), and dispels heatstroke. When brewed strong, it causes one to expectorate phlegm and saliva associated with wind-heat conditions. Excessive consumption reduces the body's fat, causing emaciation, and makes it impossible to sleep. It is best drunk hot; drinking it cold causes phlegm to accumulate. For tea

**Tobacco (*Tabako*)** 　In Japan, around the Tenshō and Keichō eras (with some accounts saying the Genki era, and others saying the tenth year of Keichō, i.e., 1605), traders from southern barbarian lands (*Nanban*) brought the seeds here. In China, it is said to have been first introduced by Westerners (the Dutch) at the end of the Ming dynasty. According to Western accounts, this plant originally comes from an island called "Tabako" (*Tabakorojima*) within the continent of "America" (*Amerrika*).
