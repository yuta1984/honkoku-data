**Sugar (*Satō*)** 　Neither Japan nor China had sugar in ancient times. It is said that in China it first arrived as tribute from foreign lands during the Tang dynasty. In Japan, until recent times only imported sugar was prized. During the Kyōhō era (1716–1736), sugarcane from the Ryūkyū Islands was introduced via Satsuma (present-day Kagoshima Prefecture), but the method of manufacture was not yet well understood. Later, around the Hōreki era (1751–1764), the process gradually became better known, and at present, domestically produced sugar has come to surpass imported varieties. An overview of sugar varieties: sugarcane (*kōbi*), molasses (*satō mitsu*), refined white sugar (*taihaku*), medium-grade white sugar (*chūhaku*), frost sugar (*shimi*), brown sugar (*kuro satō*), rock sugar (*kōri zatō*), *aruheiro* candy, konpeitō (star-shaped hard candy), *karumeira* candy, and candied preserves (*satōzuke*). Its flavor is sweet and cold, with cooling and laxative properties; it has no toxicity. It treats heat and bloating in the chest and abdomen,

**Tea (*Chamei*)** 　It is said that in China tea existed as early as the Three Kingdoms period (early 3rd century), and in Japan it already existed during the reign of Emperor Saga (r. 809–823).
