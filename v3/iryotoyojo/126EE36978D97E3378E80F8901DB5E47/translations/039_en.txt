This is, in other words, mustard powder. In pharmacology, it is known as *hakugaishi* (白芥子 / white mustard seed) and has both medicinal benefits and some toxicity.

● **Flavor and Nature (*kimi*):** Acrid and warm, with no toxicity. It acts upon the nose and benefits the nine orifices (*kyūkyō* / 九竅: two eyes, two ears, two nostrils, the mouth, and the two lower orifices). It warms the interior of the body, stops coughing and upward surging of *ki* (*nobose* / 上気), removes cold *ki*, dispels wind affecting the head and face, opens the lungs, cuts through phlegm, promotes the free flow of the diaphragm (*kaku* / 膈), and opens the stomach. However, if consumed over a long period, the warm *ki* accumulates and turns to heat. Since its acrid, dispersing nature (*shinsan* / 辛散) is very vigorous, it depletes a person's *shin'gen* (真元 / fundamental vitality / primordial *ki*), causes the liver to take on illness, and leads to dimming of the eyesight. Those with sores and abscesses (*sōyō* / 瘡瘍), hemorrhoids, bloody stools, or eye ailments, as well as those who are pregnant, postpartum, or suffering from blood loss, and also children and the elderly, are forbidden from consuming it carelessly. It is popularly transmitted that eating it together with *tanishi* (田螺 / freshwater snails / *Cipangopaludina*) can kill a person — though whether this is true or not is uncertain.

**Mansei (蔓菁) / *Kaburana* / Turnip (*Brassica rapa*)**

The stems and leaves may be either reddish-purple or white in color. It resembles *shō* (菘) and belongs to the same family, but is a distinct variety. The root may be round like a taro (*imo kashira*), or long like a *daikon* radish. It is said that if the poor eat these two varieties during years of crop failure, they can avoid *saishoku* (菜色 / the sallow, pallid complexion that comes from malnutrition), owing to the nourishment (*uruoi* / moisture and richness) found in its roots and stems. Therefore, it should be sown abundantly in lean years. What is called *Shōkatsusai* (諸葛菜 / Zhuge vegetable) is the same plant. In ancient times, Zhuge Kongming (諸葛孔明 / Zhuge Liang) reportedly had his soldiers plant it in large quantities wherever he encamped, and he also spoke of it having six benefits. *Kukitachi* (茎立) and *suguki* (酸茎) are also the same plant. When made into *kakena* (懸菜 / hung-dried greens) or *hoshina* (乾菜 / dried greens), the flavor is excellent and it keeps well for a long time. For the elderly and the infirm, it is better than fresh greens. It is particularly effective at stopping diarrhea.
