*(Continuing from the previous page: Black Soybean)*

When ground up raw and applied to swellings and sores, or when the cooking liquid is drunk, it eliminates strange and harmful poisons (*kido* / 鬼毒), relieves pain, and removes water swelling (edema) and heat-induced numbness (*netsu-bi*) within the stomach. It is beneficial for abdominal pain and urinary disorders (*rin-byō* / strangury), promotes the circulation of stagnant blood (*o-ketsu*), and disperses accumulations and stagnation (*shaku*) within the viscera.

It is an old saying that "black beans cooked with salt nourish the kidneys" — and indeed, this refers to the *nimame* (simmered beans) that people commonly enjoy. Today in the streets of Edo, there are many shops that prepare and sell these beans, and every conceivable manner of preparation for delicious flavor has been explored. However, for the purposes of health cultivation (*yōjō*), it is considered best not to make the preparation overly elaborate or exotic.

**Horse-grain Bean (*Maryūzu* / 馬粒豆)** — Also commonly known as *Gan'yaku-mame* ("pill bean"). In antiquity, people would swallow fourteen (2×7) of these beans each morning with freshly drawn water. This practice was called "*Gozō-koku*" (五蔵穀, "the grain of the five viscera"). It is said that this harmonizes the intestines and stomach (viscera), promotes the smooth passage of both urine and stool, and prevents the body from deteriorating even in old age.

**Adzuki Bean (*Azuki* / 赤小豆)** — [*Azuki* is the general name; its proper name is *aka-azuki* (red adzuki).] There are early-ripening and late-ripening varieties. In form, they are compact and small, and red in color; there are also pale red, kite-brown (*tobi*), black, white, and blue-green varieties. The slightly larger, deep crimson variety is of the finest quality and the most delicious in flavor. This is commonly called "*Dainagon*." They are prepared as rice cooked with beans, or as a porridge, and are also processed into sweet bean paste (*an*), and used in making *yōkan* (sweet bean jelly) and confections (*kashi*). Furthermore, when ground to a fine powder and soaked in water, the mixture is used to wash clothing and to remove oily grime (*aka*) from the face and body, for which purpose it is colloquially called "*Shabon*" (soap). This is no doubt because its use resembles that of the Western-imported soap (*shabon* / savão, from Portuguese).

Its flavor and nature are sweet, salty (*kan*), and neutral (*hei*), with no toxicity. It reduces water swelling (edema), drains the pus and blood of sores and swellings, dispels heat toxins, promotes urination, removes abdominal distension, alleviates *shōkatsu* (消渇, a condition resembling diabetes mellitus), and promotes the production and flow of breast milk. Eating it over a long period, however, will deplete the body's vital energy (*kyo*), leading to emaciation and fatigue. Those with a condition of deficiency-cold (*kyokan* / 虚寒, a weakened constitution prone to coldness) should not eat it. Those who are already thin and emaciated should not eat it in large quantities.
