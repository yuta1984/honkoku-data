*(Continuing from the previous page: Wild Pea / 野豌豆)*

The seeds are small and not suitable for eating. One can only boil the young shoots as a vegetable. It is also known as *kyōyōsai* (翹揺菜).

· Its flavor and nature are sweet and neutral (*hei*), with no toxicity. When cooked in lightly salted water and eaten, it treats *shōkatsu* (消渇, a wasting-thirst condition resembling diabetes), stops vomiting and nausea, relieves diarrhea, and is also effective for abdominal pain and tenesmus (*shiburi-bara*). Furthermore, it regulates the *ei* and *e* (营衛, the body's nutritive and defensive *qi*), nourishes the interior of the body, calms the *qi*, and promotes the flow of breast milk. It also has many uses in external medicine (*geka*).

**Broad Bean (*Sora-mame* / 蚕豆; also *Natsu-mame*)** — It is so named because the pods face skyward (*sora*, the sky). Since it originally came from foreign lands, it is also called *tō-mame* (唐豆, "Chinese/foreign bean"). It grows and bears fruit well even in shaded areas, beneath trees, along ridge-paths (*aze*), and in poor, lean soil. It is well-suited for those of humble station to cultivate, and should be kept in readiness as a famine-relief food (*kyūkō*) to help alleviate hunger.

· Its flavor and nature are sweet, salty (*kan*), pungent (*shin*), and neutral (*hei*), with no toxicity. It eases and pleases the stomach and harmonizes the viscera and bowels. Whether roasted or

**Cowpea / Long Bean (*Sasage* / 豇豆)** — There are many varieties. The *kaki-sasage* ("hedge sasage") has long trailing vines, while the *chi-sasage* ("ground sasage") has short vines. The flowers come in red and white; the pods come in red, white, purple, red, green, mottled (*madara*), and mixed colors, and the longest pods can reach approximately two *shaku* (about 60 cm) in length. Both the leaves and the pods, while still young and tender, are boiled and prepared as dressed salads (*aemono*), and from summer through autumn, they rank as a premium vegetable, second to none — not even eggplant (*nasu*). The dried seeds, when bleached and sun-dried, surpass adzuki beans in quality. The finest variety is called "*Jūroku Sasage*" (十六ささげ, "Sixteen-seed Sasage"), so named because its pods are long and plump, and each pod typically contains sixteen seeds. The individual seeds approach the size of soybeans, and the flavor, too, is sweet and delicious. There is also a variety that can be harvested twice in a single year.
