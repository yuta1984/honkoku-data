● ***Afuyō* (阿芙蓉 / Opium)** *(the terms "Ahen" [阿片] and "Ahen" [鴉片] refer to the same thing)* — This is prepared from the poppy (*keshi*) plant while the fruit is still young. Its flavor and nature are acrid (*kara* / 辛), bitter (*niga* / 苦), and warm (*on* / 温), with great toxicity. Both its benefits and its harms are extreme. Taken internally, it stops all manner of pain, induces sweating, brings on sleep, stops diarrhea, and reduces urination. It renders a person insensible (anaesthetized), and if used excessively, causes one to die in one's sleep. Applied externally, it effectively causes one to forget pain and itching, but thereafter induces numbness (*maboku* / 麻木) and loss of sensation (*fujin* / 不仁). It also suppresses and restrains the vital essence (*seiki* / 精気) of men.

**Vegetables (*Sairui* / 菜類)**

All herbaceous plants that can be cooked and eaten are called *sai* (菜 / vegetables). The five grains (*gokoku* / 五穀) are that which nourishes all living beings, while the five vegetables (*gosai* / 五菜) are that which replenishes the stomach *ki* (*i no ki* / 胃気). This is the reason they supplement and assist the *ki* of grains, and promote the free flow through any stagnation and obstruction. Vegetables are certainly not limited to five kinds; as the ages have progressed, their varieties have grown ever more numerous. The nourishing benefit that vegetables offer to people is by no means small. However, the quality — whether beneficial or harmful — of the five *ki* (五気) is not the same for each, and where the five flavors (五味) enter the body, there are imbalances and predominances. As things that people use daily, one must not remain ignorant of this. Vegetables in general are divided into five categories: *kunshinn* (葷辛 / pungent and acrid), *jūkatsu* (柔滑 / soft and smooth), *ra* (蓏 / fruits of herbaceous plants), *sui* (水 / aquatic plants), and *shiji* (芝栭 / mushrooms and fungi).
