**Kansai [Wasabi]** (*Wasabia japonica* / Japanese horseradish)

Its leaves resemble those of *Kamo-aoi* (hollyhock mallow, *Asarum caulescens*), and the shape and flavor of its root are similar to ginger. For this reason, in Japan it goes by the names *sanki* ("mountain hollyhock") and *sankyō* ("mountain ginger"). It grows in cold, high-mountain locations; when cultivated in villages, shaded ground and the edges of riverbanks are said to be suitable. It dislikes direct sunlight. The root is bluish-black in color, covered with many knobby protuberances, and has short root-hairs. The flesh is glutinous (mucilaginous) and fragrant.

●Flavor: acrid; nature: warm; non-toxic. It disperses stagnation and depression (*fusagi*), promotes perspiration, drains dampness, dissolves accumulations (*shaku*, congealed masses in the body), brings down blockages (*tsukae*), and is beneficial for *senki* (hernial/abdominal pain). It resolves the toxins of fish and fowl, and neutralizes the toxicity of buckwheat.

**Ryō [Tade]** (*Polygonum* spp. / water pepper / smartweed)

There are many varieties, growing in both open fields and wetlands/marshes. Their forms also differ from one to another.

●Properties of seedlings, leaves, and seeds: acrid, warm, non-toxic. Brightens the eyes, warms the middle (*uchi*, the digestive organs), provides resistance to wind-cold (*fūkan*) ailments,

**Kin [Seri]** (*Oenanthe javanica* / Japanese parsley / water dropwort)

That which grows in water has many roots; that growing on flat ground has fewer roots. Red *seri* has an unpleasant flavor and is unsuitable for use. White *seri* has a fine flavor and may be used regularly.

When people say that *seri* is poisonous, this is because leeches (*biru*) leave their eggs on it, and snakes and lizards leave their droppings clinging to the stems and leaves. People, however, are unable to detect this and encounter the residual toxins left behind, thereby suffering harm. One must always wash it carefully and thoroughly before eating.

In our country, it has been one of the seven spring herbs (*nanakusa*) since ancient times.
