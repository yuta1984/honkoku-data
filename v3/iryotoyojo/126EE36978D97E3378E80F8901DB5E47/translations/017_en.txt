It sharpens hearing, brightens eyesight, relieves heat and irritation, and quenches thirst. This grain (rice) alone receives the harmonious qi of heaven and earth, and is in accord with the life-giving power of nature; therefore, it cannot be compared to other foods.

New rice stirs the qi, and therefore those with a weak spleen and stomach, or those suffering from digestive stagnation (*shakutai*), should not eat it. Roasted rice offers no benefit to people; patients and young children should be prohibited from consuming it.

Since ancient times, there have been no more than three methods of cooking rice: *takiboshi* (cook-dry method), *yutori* (draining method), and *nidohan* (double-cook method). One should choose appropriately according to the strength or weakness of a person's constitution and the severity of their illness.

The cooking water from rice (rice gruel water / starchy water) rinses the molars and tongue, clears the chest, aids digestion, harmonizes food intake, and promotes thorough digestion and absorption. It also quenches thirst after meals or after drinking sake, and its benefits throughout all four seasons are not small.

It is something that must be used every day. The various ways of making vegetable rice, leaf rice, tea rice, fish and fowl rice, and sushi are too numerous to count. Each also has its own harmful properties (*doku*), which will be recorded one by one.

As for porridge (*kayu*), the methods of making it are likewise too many to enumerate. The ancients said: if upon rising each morning one eats one large bowl of porridge, the grain's qi will immediately arise to fill an empty, deficient stomach, and its restorative benefit is not small. Moreover, because it is extremely soft and smooth, it agrees well with the intestines and stomach and generates bodily fluids (*津液, shineki*).

However, depending on a person's constitution, eating porridge can generate dampness (*shitsu*) in the stomach, making one prone to diarrhea, frequent urination, and lack of energy.
