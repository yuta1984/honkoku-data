**Soybean (*mame* / 大豆)** — There are many varieties: yellow, white, red, black, brown (persimmon-colored), speckled, and others too numerous to list. For the preparation of miso, soy sauce (*shōyu*), natto, and tofu, yellow or white soybeans are invariably used. It is considered a product of great benefit to the nation. Its flavor and nature are sweet and warm, with no toxicity. It descends the qi, relaxes the interior of the abdomen, and promotes the smooth functioning of the intestines and stomach.

**Roasted Soybean Flour (*kinako* / 炒豆粉)** — [New beans are green in color; old beans are reddish-yellow.] The traditional confection known as *suhama* is made by kneading kinako with soft candy (*mizuame* / starch syrup), then wrapping it in bamboo sheath. Eating it in excess stirs up digestive stagnation (*shaku*) and produces phlegm.

**Miso and Soy Sauce (*shōyu*)** — [Already treated in detail under the category of condiments/seasonings.]

**Fermented Soybeans (*nattō* / 豆豉)** — Many people enjoy eating this as a soup (*shiru* / 羹), but it should be withheld from the ill and from young children with care. It creates stagnation in the chest and diaphragm (causing a clogged, heavy feeling), and causes abdominal bloating. Even healthy individuals should not eat it in excess.

**Salted Temple Natto (*tera-nattō* / 鹹豉)** — No toxicity. It regulates the interior of the abdomen and replenishes qi. It is harmless even for sick people to eat.

**Tofu (豆腐)** — Has slight toxicity. It obstructs qi, causes digestive stagnation (*shakutai*), and may sometimes trigger sores, skin eruptions, and headaches. On the other hand, it can relax the interior of the abdomen and replenish qi,

**Okara (*karakitazu* / *unohana* / 雪花菜)** — Mild in flavor, with no toxicity. This too effectively dispels the toxic effects of alcohol. Eating it in excess obstructs the qi. It is advisable to eat it together with pungent (*karai*) foods.

**Yuba (腐皮 / *uba*)** — No toxicity. Eating it in excess obstructs the qi.

**Black Soybean (*kuromame* / 黒大豆)** — There are also quite a number of varieties. The flavor and nature are broadly similar to those of the soybean. There are large, medium, and small-grained types. Among them, the smallest and most perfectly round variety is known in Chinese (*kara-na*) as *baりゅうzu* (馬粒豆). The large, flat variety is commonly called *gankui-mame* (雁喰豆, "goose-eating bean"). Beans that are white on all four sides with the center entirely black are called *kurakake-mame* (鞍懸豆, "saddle-draped bean") in Kyoto parlance. Those that are large-grained, round, and purplish-brown (chestnut-colored) are called *kuri-mame* ("chestnut bean"), and the *Sendai-mame* also belongs to this category. Its flavor and nature are sweet and neutral (*hei*), with no toxicity. It activates the blood, promotes the discharge of water (diuretic effect), descends the qi, eliminates various kinds of wind-heat, and counteracts the toxicity of all manner of foods and medicines (*hyaku-yaku* / the hundred medicines).
