It repels cold qi, resolves drug toxicity, removes high fever (*sōnetsu*), and treats phlegm-rattling wheezing (*tanzen*), abdominal bloating and fullness (*chōman*), cold-type diarrhea (*hie-kudari*), abdominal cramps and spasms (*korogari-suji*/*転筋*), and oppressive fullness in the chest (*shinman*). It kills the foul-smelling belching from the chest, as well as intestinal roundworms (*naganushi*) in the abdomen. It strengthens the spleen and stomach, and resolves mushroom poisoning and the toxins of various other substances.

●When raw, it has a dispersing effect; when well-cooked, it harmonizes the middle (the digestive organs). Beyond these, its merits are many.

●*Kankyō* (dried processed ginger) is hot in nature (processed according to the proper method). *Hoshi-shōga* (simply dried ginger) is greatly warming in nature (dried as-is). Fresh ginger (*nama-shōga*) is warming in nature.

When traveling and encountering a change of water (local water that may cause digestive upset), one should always take dried ginger. When blood has stained books, clothing, or similar items, grind fresh ginger, mix it with water, hold the mixture in the mouth, and wash the stain—it will come out. In general, ginger should be used regularly, but one should not use it in excess.

**Myōga** (*Zingiber mioga*; anciently called *mega*, now called *myōga*)

It grows abundantly beneath trees and in shaded spots. It is also cultivated in gardens.

●**Leaves and stems**: The *Honkyō* (*Shennong Bencao Jing*, the Divine Husbandman's Classic of Materia Medica) classifies them as bitter and cold (*kukan*), but this is doubtful. Today, boiling both the leaves and stems and using the liquid to wash chilblains has proven effective; therefore, the nature should likely be warming.

●**Flower buds** (*myōga no ko*, the edible flower spike): Flavor—bitter and sweet; nature—slightly warm; non-toxic. It opens up depression and emotional stagnation (*utsu*), stimulates the appetite, removes pathogenic qi, and wards off misfortune and inauspiciousness.

●**Root**: Flavor—acrid; nature—warm; slight toxicity. When an awn (the bristle-like tip) from rice or wheat straw enters the eye and cannot be removed, instilling the squeezed juice will immediately cause it to come out. For red, inflamed eyes (*shakugan*), astringent pain (*shūtsū*), and cases of accidental injury to the eye, instilling the squeezed juice produces remarkable results.
