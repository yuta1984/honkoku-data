**Sansan (山蒜) / *Nehiru* (archaic) / *Nobiru* (modern)** (*Allium macrostemon* / wild scallion / *nobiru*)

Also called *takusan* (沢蒜 / marsh garlic), *sekisan* (石蒜 / rock garlic), and *yasan* (野蒜 / field garlic)—all of these are likely the same *nobiru*. It is simply that they have acquired different names depending on the place where they grow.

● **Flavor and Nature (*kimi*):** Acrid and warm, with no toxicity. It is good for accumulations (*shaku* / 積), lumps (*katamari* / 塊), and blood lumps (*ketsuka* / 血瘕) in women. It descends *ki* and smooths the source of water (promotes urination / fluid metabolism).

● Regarding ***sekisan* (石蒜):** There is another substance that shares this name but is a different thing (*dōmei ibutsu* / 同名異物). It is also known as *rō'asan* (老鴉蒜 / "old crow garlic") and has various names in both Japanese and Chinese traditions. This is in fact the *manjushage* (蔓珠莎華 / red spider lily / *Lycoris radiata*), which can be found everywhere in the fields and meadows at the end of autumn. One wonders how it came to have such a beautiful name. It also has an ill-omened name that people detest and avoid. Nevertheless, it is something that can serve as one means of relieving hunger in times of famine. It must not be confused with *nobiru*.

**Shō (菘) / *Na* / *Hatakena*** (菜 / field greens / *Brassica rapa* / turnip greens / field mustard)

By nature it endures winter and is *hancho* (晩凋 / slow to wilt / late-fading). It can be seen throughout all four seasons. Because it has the steadfast character (*misao*) of the pine tree, the character for pine (松) was given a grass radical (艹) on top to form the character 菘 (*shō*), or so it is said. There are many varieties. It was incorrect of people of old to read it as *horiiri-na* or *koōbone* and to classify it as a variety of *daikon* (莱菔 / *Raphanus sativus*). *Shō* belongs to the same family as *kaburana* (蕪菁 / turnip / *Brassica rapa* var. *rapa*) and is a separate thing from *daikon*. When it blooms in spring, the whole area turns gold like [a field of] gold. Oil can be extracted from its seeds. *Daikon* (莱菔) has pale purple flowers, and its fruits also differ. One should know the distinction between *na* (菘), *kabura* (蔓菁 / turnip), and *daikon* (蘿匐 / 莱菔). *Na* (*shō*) is a vegetable found abundantly everywhere and is indispensable to the people. However, since there are several varieties within the same category, their flavors, properties, and levels of toxicity are each likely to differ somewhat from one another.
