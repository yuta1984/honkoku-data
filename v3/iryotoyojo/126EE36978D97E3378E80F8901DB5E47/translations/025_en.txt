**Mung Bean (*Buntō* / 緑豆; also *Yaenari*)** — Ancient people called this "an excellent grain that saves the world." There are not many varieties; the differences lie only in the size of the grain and in color, which ranges from *moegi* (bright yellow-green) to *onando-cha* (a dark grayish-green). Its uses are wide-ranging: it is prepared as cooked rice (mixed), porridge, sake (fermented), roasted and eaten (*sū-shoku*), ground into a roasted flour and eaten (*shō-shoku*), milled into noodles (*men*), refined by water-levigation (*suihi*) to produce fine starch powder, made into dumplings (*dango*), rice cakes (*mochi*), and thin noodles (*sōmen*) — it is truly a fine and excellent food item. Furthermore, the "Kitō Shinpō San-zu-san" (稀痘神方三豆散, a three-bean formula for preventing smallpox) is something well known to the general public.

Its flavor and nature are sweet and neutral (*hei*) — also said to be cooling (*kanryō*) — with no toxicity. It disperses swellings, descends *qi* (vital energy), and counteracts the toxins of smallpox (*hōsō*), various medicinal drugs, and mushroom poisons,

It is said that there is no prohibition against long-term consumption causing the body to wither, yet since its nature is cooling (*ryō*), those with a deficient and cold spleen and stomach (*hi-i kyokan*) should simply moderate their intake accordingly.

**Garden Pea (*Endō* / 豌豆)** — [Common name: *nora-mame*; colloquially *entō*] Seeds are sown in the eighth and ninth months; seedlings emerge in the first and second months; flowers open in the third and fourth months, and pods (*saya*) form shortly thereafter. The pods are approximately one *sun* (approx. 3 cm) in length, and the seeds are round, resembling medicinal pills (*gan'yaku*). Though a humble and modest plant, it is the very earliest of all the hundred grains to ripen. When farming households raise their rice seedlings, they collect the pea plants and plow them into the paddies as green manure (*koe*), and it is said to be excellent for fertilizing and nourishing the soil. Among the common people, the peas are eaten as tea-snacks (*chagashi*), or ground into flour and made into noodles, and also made into a washing powder (*arai-ko* / *sō-zu*) — mixed with buckwheat (*soba*) lees — said to be excellent for brightening the complexion and giving the skin a lustrous glow. There is also the wild pea (*no-endō*).
