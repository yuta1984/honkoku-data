**Sōmen noodles (*sōmen*)** — Aged sōmen has fewer harmful effects. Whether to eat cold sōmen or hot sōmen in broth (*nyūmen*) simply depends on one's preference according to the season.

**Glutinous millet (*mochi-kibi*) and non-glutinous millet (*uru-kibi*)** — Both have many varieties, including small millet (*ko-kibi*), black millet (*kuro-kibi*), black-tipped millet (*tsumaguro-kibi*), and sorghum (*morokoshi*), among others. Their flavor and nature are sweet and warm, with no toxicity. They supplement the lungs, benefit the qi, and relieve pain. Eating them in excess or over a long period will generate vexing heat (*han'netsu*), loosen the sinews and bones, trigger old and lingering ailments (*koshitsu*), and cause a person to sleep excessively.

**Corn / Maize (*nanbankibi*, *tōmorokoshi*)** — Its flavor and nature are sweet and neutral, with no toxicity. It opens the stomach, regulates the interior of the body, and stops diarrhea. Eating it in excess makes it difficult to digest. For this reason, people commonly say it is toxic and triggers chronic ailments (*koshitsu*), but this is not necessarily so—it is simply the harm caused by eating it greedily and to excess.

**Foxtail millet (*awa*)** — There are two types: non-glutinous (*ura-awa* / *ryō*) and glutinous (*mochi-awa* / *shuku*). Within each type there are many varieties, which are numerous and varied: large, small, yellow, white, red, black, and so on. There are also early-, mid-, and late-ripening varieties. The very latest of them is harvested after being touched by frost, and is therefore called "frost millet" (*shimo-awa*). Non-glutinous millet (*ura-awa*) is made into cooked rice or porridge, but it is only the food of common folk and peasants. Glutinous millet (*mochi-awa*) is made into rice cakes and can even be offered as a refined dish (*o-agari*) to persons of high rank. For this reason, farming households regard glutinous millet as the finest.

**Non-glutinous millet (*ryō*)** — Its flavor and nature are salty and slightly cold, with no toxicity. It nourishes kidney qi and opens the intestines and stomach. It is good for *shōkatsu* (a condition resembling diabetes, characterized by excessive thirst and urination) and difficulty urinating. It treats diarrhea caused by heat and abdominal pain, and is also good for *hon'i* (counterflow of stomach qi, i.e., vomiting/regurgitation) and nosebleeds.

**Glutinous millet (*shuku* / *mochi-awa*)** — Its flavor and nature are sweet and warm, with no toxicity. It treats conditions where yang is in excess and yin is deficient, causing an inability to sleep. It also treats excessive vaginal discharge (*orimono*) during pregnancy. Furthermore, it is generally good for use in lung ailments.
