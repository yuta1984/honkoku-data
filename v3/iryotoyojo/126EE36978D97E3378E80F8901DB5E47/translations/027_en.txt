Such varieties also exist; their flavor is excellent, their pods are tender, and both the pods and seeds are of fine quality.

● Its flavor and nature are sweet, salty (*kan*), and neutral (*hei*), with no toxicity. It regulates the body's interior, augments the *qi*, tonifies the kidneys, strengthens the stomach, harmonizes the five viscera (*gozō*), balances the nutritive and defensive *qi* (*ei-ei* / 営衛), and generates vital essence and marrow. It stops *shōkatsu* (消渇, a wasting-thirst condition resembling diabetes), vomiting, and counterflow *qi*; and it is beneficial for diarrhea and frequent urination. It has no particular contraindications for general ailments; however, it should be avoided in cases of water-swelling (*suishu* / edema). It is effective in dispelling the poison of rats and snakes.

● White *sasage* (*shiro-sasage*) is particularly effective in treating blood dizziness (*chi no memai* / 血暈) in women and various blood-related ailments.

**Sword Bean (*Nata-mame* / 刀豆; also *Tatewaki*)** — Its alternative name is *Kyōkenzu* (挟剣豆, "sword-gripping bean"). It is a vine-type plant that grows to a height of one to two *jō* (approximately 3–6 meters). The pods, when long, approach one *shaku* (approx. 30 cm) in length, and generally resemble the pods of the honey locust (*saikachi* / 皂莢), being flat and having three ridges like the back of a sword blade. The color is a deep green. Inside the pod are beans approximately the size of the tip of a thumb,

**Sesame (*Goma* / 胡麻)** — *Shima* (芝麻), *shima* (脂麻), and *koshōshi* (巨勝子) are all alternative names. Zhang Qian (張騫) of the Han dynasty first obtained sesame seeds (*abura-ma* / 油麻) from the kingdom of Dayuan (大宛国) and brought them back to be cultivated in China. It is said that when the plant existed in the western foreign lands (*ebisunochu* / 胡地), the seeds were very large, but gradually became smaller thereafter. Among the five grains (*gokoku*), what is called "*ma*" (麻) refers to the seeds of hemp (*asa*); this sesame, however, surpasses all others among the eight grains (*hakkoku*), which is why it is called *Koshō* (巨勝, "the great victor")
