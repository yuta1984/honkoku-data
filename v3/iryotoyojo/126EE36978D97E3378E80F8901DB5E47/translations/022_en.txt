It can be made into cooked rice or porridge, but the taste is not particularly good. It is best mixed together with rice or foxtail millet (*awa*) before it becomes reasonably edible. **Buckwheat (*soba*)** — In medieval Japan, provincial governors (*kokushi*) were ordered to encourage the cultivation of buckwheat as a provision against crop failure and famine. It was made into pounded cakes (*kaimochi*) and grilled cakes to satisfy hunger, and it was by no means enjoyed in the manner of modern refined buckwheat noodles (*soba-kiri*). The name *karō* (thin noodles of buckwheat) is an ancient one, yet buckwheat noodles never surpassed udon in prestige. It is said that the manufacture and sale of buckwheat noodles (*soba-kiri*) in the shops of Edo began after the Kanbun era (1661–1673). Although buckwheat is produced in various provinces,

**[Left Page]**

the product of the northeastern regions is superior, while that from the southwestern regions is not good. Above all, it is commonly said that nothing rivals what is produced in Shinano Province — a fact well known among people. Its flavor and nature are sweet and mildly cold, with no toxicity. It descends the qi, relaxes the intestines, and effectively breaks down waste, impurities, and stagnation (*shakutai*) accumulated in the intestines and stomach. It also treats water swelling (*suishu* / edema), turbid urine (*hakudaku*), vaginal discharge (*hakutai* / *koshike*), diarrhea (*setsuri*), abdominal pain, and flushing (*jōki* / heat rising to the head). It is suitable for those in whom qi is exuberant and damp-heat is present. It also treats convulsions (*hikitsuke*) in children and *rekisetsu-fū* (painful wandering joint disease, resembling rheumatic or arthritic conditions). Furthermore, for *tandoku* (erysipelas), red swelling, and heat sores (*netsu-sō*), buckwheat flour mixed with vinegar and applied topically is effective.

However, if a person whose spleen and stomach are deficient and cold (*kyokan*) eats buckwheat, it will greatly deplete their vital energy (*genki*), and if they continue eating it excessively without restraint, they may ultimately suffer serious bodily harm.

That said, what is commonly said in society — namely, that buckwheat "stirs wind qi, causes the beard and eyebrows to fall out, triggers *sen* (hernia-like abdominal pain), causes abscesses and ulcers (*yōyō*) to erupt, and that bathing after eating buckwheat will cause a sudden stroke (*sotchū-fū*)" — is not necessarily true.
