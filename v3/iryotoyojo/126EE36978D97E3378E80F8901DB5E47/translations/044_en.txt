**[Right folio]**

*(Continuing from previous page: Seri [Japanese Parsley])*

It is placed in the rice gruel (*kayu*) eaten on the seventh day of the first month, to ward off evil influences (*jaki*) for the year ahead. When poets compose verses on "root parsley" (*neseri*), this is the plant they refer to.

●Flavor: sweet; nature: (balanced); non-toxic. It preserves the blood and pulse, nourishes the vital essence (*sei*), increases the qi, and makes people well-nourished and healthy. It dispels latent heat (*fukuetsu*, heat lodged within the body), heat following alcohol consumption, sudden fever in children, and wind-heat in the head. It is beneficial for the mouth and teeth, promotes the proper functioning of the large and small intestines, and is good for vexatious thirst (*hankatsu*), uterine bleeding and vaginal discharge (*崩中・帯下*, flooding and leaking conditions in women), and what is commonly called jaundice (*kinaru yamai*, "the yellowing disease").

●Incidental note: The custom of eating the seven herbs on *Jinjitsu* (the seventh day of the first month, "Human Day") originally derives from a tradition in China, but no one knows the specific names of the plants used there. In our country, the names of the seven herbs have been established since ancient times, though there are minor differences in the actual plants included. These will be recorded and presented in turn, one by one.

**Yashokuki [Mitsuba-zeri]** (*Cryptotaenia japonica* / Japanese honewort / mitsuba)

It grows in moist ground near water. It is also cultivated in paddy fields.

●Properties: The stems and leaves are slightly acrid, bitter, astringent, and sweet; nature is cold; non-toxic. Principal uses: opens the stomach and stimulates appetite. It is beneficial, not harmful, to the spleen and stomach. [Note on what those who are ill should avoid is unclear/missing in the text.] In popular usage, it is said to be poisonous, but I have yet to see anyone who has actually suffered harm from it. On reflection, I believe the confusion arose in the past from the popular practice of reading *tagarashi* (石竜苪, *Ranunculus sceleratus*, the cursed crowfoot—a genuinely toxic plant) with the reading *mitsuba-zeri*, thus causing the misattribution of toxicity to mitsuba.

*(Japanese name: norae; now widely known by the common name shiso)*

It grows abundantly everywhere. Seeds should be sown in the second or third month. That which grows in fertile soil has leaves that are purple on both the upper and lower surfaces.
