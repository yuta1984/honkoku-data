If food is not properly cut, do not eat it. If the appropriate seasoning for a dish cannot be obtained, do not eat it. Even if there is much meat available, it should not exceed the amount of staple food. This represents Confucius's prudent approach to food and drink. He also said, "Only for wine there is no set measure, but one must not reach the point of disorder." While people have individual preferences in food and drink, there is generally an appropriate amount for most things. Only wine has no fixed appropriate amount for the drinker. Some may drink three gō, five gō, one shō, or two shō according to their individual constitution's capacity. "Disorder" means disturbing one's dignity and deportment. The teaching is that even when intoxicated, one should drink only to the extent that it does not disturb one's dignity. Wine is called "the thing that makes one forget worries" and "the chief of a hundred medicines," so there are countless ancient people who loved wine. When people measure their own capacity and drink only to the extent that disorder is not reached, they nourish their bodies well and preserve their longevity well. When they exceed their own capacity and reach the point of disturbing their dignity, they harm their bodies and damage their lives.

The way people enjoy wine differs from person to person, and there are very many who exceed their own capacity. Some love the taste of wine. Some do not care for the taste but enjoy the state of intoxication. Some love the aesthetic pleasure of wine. Some cannot get through a single day without daily wine. Some can drink much without disturbing their dignity, while others become like mud and collapse on the road after drinking only a little. They laugh randomly, cry randomly, become angry randomly - their drunken behavior manifests in a hundred ways, and very many disturb their dignity. Afterwards, they inevitably develop various illnesses. One should measure one's own capacity and exercise restraint. Kenkō said "it is better for a man not to be a teetotaler," but being a teetotaler is also unrefined. If one is labeled as a heavy drinker, one tends to exceed proper limits. Food and drink are fundamentally not things with which one should indulge the desires of mouth and stomach as one pleases.
