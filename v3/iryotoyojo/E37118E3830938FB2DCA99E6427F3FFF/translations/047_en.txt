○Japanese spikenard (udo)　　　No harm in using, but should not be used raw in large quantities. Removes wind (cold).
○Solomon's seal (ōsei)　　　Cold in nature and damages the stomach. No harm for those with strong spleen-stomach.
○Polygonum multiflorum (kashū)　　Those with weak spleen-stomach should not eat it. Loosens bowel movements.
○Perilla (shiso)　　　Non-toxic. Not troublesome even for patients. Neutralizes fish poison. However, if eaten with carp, causes malignant sores.
○Kudzu powder (kuzu powder)　　　Those with spleen-stomach deficiency-cold should be cautious.
○Coltsfoot (fuki)　　　Harmless. Regulates heart and lungs, treats phlegm and cough.
○Kochia (hōkigusa/ground cypress)　　　Promotes urination. Should not be used for diarrhea conditions.
○Water pepper (tade)　　　Drains water qi and benefits the eyes. Patients should be careful.
○Chrysanthemum leaves (kiku no ha)　　　Neither flowers nor leaves cause harm or benefit.
○Yomena (wild chrysanthemum)　　　Opens the chest and promotes appetite. Not good for those with weak spleen-stomach. Causes abdominal pain if eaten with skewered persimmons. Harms the eyes if eaten with vinegar.
○Field horsetail (tsukushi)　　Patients should be cautious. Causes diarrhea if eaten together with poppy seeds.
○Cedar greens (sugi-na)　　Not good even for healthy people.
○Mugwort (yomogi)　　　Harmless to both patients and healthy people. Used in dumplings and rice cakes.
○Cockscomb seedlings (keitō no nae)　　Young leaves are used in soups and dressed dishes, but they are toxic.
○Burdock (gobō)　　　Always beneficial when used regularly. Good for various diseases.
○Day lily (kanzō)　　　Calms heart heat and is good for various blood disorders.
○Saltwort (matsuna)　　　Warm in nature, harmonizes the stomach and nourishes kidney water.
○Kidney beans (ingen-mame)　　　Regulates intestines and stomach, strengthens kidneys.
○Sword bean (nata-mame)　　　Descends middle qi and effectively stops hiccups.
