Thus, during the Tang and Song dynasties, there was the Ming Suan (Bright Mathematics) examination for selecting scholars. In the Tang Six Codes it states: "Students of the Ten Classics of Mathematical Learning under the doctoral instructors complete their studies in five years." Among Song scholars like Shao Kangjie, Sima Wenzheng, Zhu Wengong, and Cai Xeishan, there were none who were not excellent at precise calculation. Particularly during the Daiguan era of the Song dynasty, sixty-six mathematicians from ancient times were ennobled and divided into four ranks: Duke, Earl, Viscount, and Baron - nine were made Dukes, twenty-eight Earls, twenty Viscounts, and nine Barons. Zu Chongzhi was selected and received the rank of Viscount. This person's precision in discussing mathematics was unparalleled in ancient and modern times. This is detailed in the Sui Dynasty's Treatise on Pitch-pipes and the Calendar. In our country, Emperor Kōtoku issued an edict on the first day of the first month of the second year of Taika, saying: "Select those who are intelligent and skilled in writing and mathematics to serve as chief administrators and chief clerks."

【Left Page】
In the first year of Taihō under Emperor Monmu, regulations were established and the position of mathematical instructor was created. On the fifteenth day of the fourth month of the fourth year of Jōgan under Emperor Seiwa, Iehara Sukune Ujinushi, who held the positions of Assistant Director of the Bureau of Audit, Junior Fifth Rank Lower, and Acting Mathematical Instructor, was appointed as Acting Assistant Governor of Mimasaka. Around the Hōgen era, it is said that Michinori, Governor of Hyūga, transmitted the "stepchild mathematics." The families that excelled in this way were the Ozuki and Miyoshi clans. When the medieval period reached the age of warring states, the learning of the Nine Chapters consequently fell to the ground. Scholars labored with military affairs and the people suffered from displacement, finding division troublesome and not using it, performing only multiplication. This was called Shōkei mathematics, similar to today's Kamei mathematics.
