He wrote "Sanso" (Mathematical Treatise) and "Sanpō Chokkai" (Direct Solutions in Mathematics). Later he served Lord Asano Takumi-no-kami, the lord of Akō Castle in Harima Province, and received a stipend. His two sons followed their lord in the vendetta.

Among Yoshida Kōyū's disciples was one called Yokogawa Gen'etsu, who later created the art of abacus级聚 (kyūshū). He was the founder of this technique. Among Takahara's disciples was Isomura Kihei Yoshinori, who wrote "Sanpō Ketsugi-shō" and "Dō Tōsho." He served Lord Niwa Sakyō-dayū of Nihonmatsu Castle. Naitō Jihei served Lord Ishikawa Mimasaka-no-kami. The master (Araki Muraei) first took this Ichigen as his teacher. Up to this point, all followed the old-style mathematical methods, specializing in the techniques of ketsugi, jūgai, jinki, and so forth.

Yokogawa Gen'etsu, a disciple of Kōyū, invented the art of kyūshū based on "Sangaku Keimō" (Mathematical Enlightenment). His disciple Hoshino Sukeuemon wrote "Keimō Chūkai" (Commentary on Mathematical Enlightenment). Sumida Kōun's disciple Satō Rizaemon wrote "Kongen-ki" (Record of Origins). Kogōri Hikozaemon created "Jōjo Ōrai" (Primer on Multiplication and Division). Later he changed his name to Ikeda Masaoki and lived in front of Saishō-ji Temple in Shiba. This Hikozaemon collected the Shoushi Calendar and made it conform to the current calendar. His disciple created the Jōkyō Calendar and submitted it to the government, but later Masaoki sued the government claiming all the works were his own creation. However, since the matter had already been decided above, it was not taken up again. One called Kobori Ichirōemon was also said to be Kōun's disciple. Among Isomura Yoshinori's disciples was Murase Shozaemon Yoshimasu, a resident of Sekiyado in Katsushika District, Shimōsa Province, who wrote "Buttan-kai." 

Nakanishi Jūtayū Masayoshi was initially called Tokoi Bunzaemon and studied under the elder Inoue Gentetsu.
