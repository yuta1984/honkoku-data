Hana-ayame　Grows wild in mountain valleys. When transplanted to dry land, it flourishes well. Widely
　cultivated in residential gardens for flower appreciation. In spring, it produces shoots from old roots. The leaf shape resembles Kakitsubata
　leaves but narrower, one to two shaku in length, with ridges on the back but not on the front surface. Like the central
　vein of leaves. Many grow in clusters. In April, it separately sends up round stems and bears
　two to three flowers at the tips. When one flower wilts, another flower opens in succession. Six petals of various sizes,
　the shape resembles Hanashōbu but smaller, with narrow petals. Purple-blue color with dozens of varieties in different sizes.
　There are also white-flowered varieties. All produce pods after flowering. The shoots wither in autumn. Seeds fall and
　germinate easily. One variety that is very small is called Hime-ayame, also known as Ko-a-
　yame. Leaves are about five sun long, flowers are about eight bu in size.

　　　　　Main properties
　　△Sweet taste, no toxicity. Repels fleas and lice.
