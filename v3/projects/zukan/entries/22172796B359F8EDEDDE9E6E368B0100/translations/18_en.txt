The "Zōho Tashikihen" (Supplementary Multi-Knowledge Compilation), Shrubs section states:
　　　Mulberry　Japanese name: Kuwa　Alternative name: The fruit is called Jin (kuwa-no-mi, sawaraki)


The "Wamyō Ruijushō" states:
　　　Mulberry (kuwa)　The "Gyokuhen" states: Mulberry (pronounced "sō", the character is also written as 桒)
　　　　　　　　Japanese name: kuwa
　　　　　Food for silkworms


　　　　The "Honzō" states there are several varieties. Now
　　　　upon examination, there are two types: one type has leaves without lobes, used for
　　　　feeding silkworms and considered superior grade, while those with lobes are inferior grade.
　　　　Kuwa refers to the leaves that silkworms eat.


The "Kyūkō Honzō" states:
　　　Mulberry fruit (kuwa-ichigo)

The "Honzō Kōmoku" states:
The mulberry is formed by the accumulation of the Kei constellation, thus it is the
sacred tree of leaves that silkworms consume


　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　On the 17th day of early Koshō in the year Kinoto-Tori,
　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　drawn from life after breaking off a branch

　　　　　　　　　celery-leaf, chrysanthemum-leaf. One variety, male mulberry, is called takusha
