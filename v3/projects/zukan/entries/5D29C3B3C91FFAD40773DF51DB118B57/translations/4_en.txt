I had earnestly declined, saying I was unfit for the task, but when you (Kurimoto Zuiken) also examined my work, following the precedent of Eijuin Hōin's "Saikyūhō" (Emergency Medical Prescriptions), I found it difficult to refuse such an earnest request, and so I have again taken up my inadequate brush here.

Autumn day of Bunka 8 (1811)
Written by Junior Fifth Rank Lower, Echizen no Kami, Fujiwara no Kiyofumi

[Left folio]
In spring gardens, butterflies carry the fragrance of blue pollen; in autumn eaves, the dew is cold and crickets sing. They can well provide material for poets. In distant mountains ten ri away, in the music quarters of blue forests, in the several acres of garden steps and grasslands, drums and flutes appropriately evoke quiet and secluded interest. Though creatures are minute, they form nations among locust branches; though their kinds are numerous, they protect kings on terraces. Like those who understand proper order, they spin cocoons to clothe the common people and weave nets to assist in imperial enterprises. Even with wings that bear mountains while avoiding the shame of green gauze, they do not fail to display the courage of arms that resist chariots. Their species are abundant and varied, changing form according to location and taking different names according to region. How could they number merely several tens of millions? I previously wrote one volume of "Insect Records," merely searching for reliable precedents. Recently, my uncle-in-law Kurimoto Zuiken Hōgen painted their forms, applied colors, titled it "Thousand Insects Illustrated," and requested a preface from me, saying: "The multitude of insect forms—how could one possibly illustrate them all?"
