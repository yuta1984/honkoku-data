○In the first year of Kōshō under Emperor Go-Hanazono 《Note: Year of the Metal Boar》, on the 14th day of the 8th month 《Note: Day of the Fire Boar》, the Seidian ceremony was performed.

On the 10th day of the 2nd month of Kan'ei 10, year of the Water Rooster, the Seisai ceremony was first performed at the Confucian temple at Shinobugaoka. The shogunate's Seisai ceremonies began here. ○The old ritual was called Seisai, but in Kansei Hinoe-tatsu (1796) it was renamed Seidian. The Shōhei-shi states: "The old Seidian ritual was based on Ming regulations 《Note: etc.》. The current ritual 《Note: established in Kansei Kōshin》, although it primarily follows Tang ceremonies, still uses Ming regulations for the temple hall 《Note: etc.》. In essence, these are all creations of one era. What we call the old ritual refers only to those from before Kansei Kōshin. Things like the various rituals of the Kanbun and Genroku eras are old upon old." ○The current ritual exclusively uses the Engi-shiki. However, from posthumous titles and associated worship to prayer texts and offerings, many still follow contemporary customs and maintain old practices. The current ritual refers to the ceremony first performed in the 2nd month of Kansei Kōshin, which has not yet been further revised. ○The history of schools and Seidian ceremonies is detailed in the Shōhei-shi.

○The Daigaku-ryō (Imperial University Bureau) was the imperial capital's center of learning, modeled after Tang China's Guozijian (Imperial Academy). Students from far and near gathered here, with food, firewood, and other necessities provided by the court. Within the bureau were two divisions, east and west: the Eastern Division followed the scholarly tradition of Minister Kan (Sugawara no Michizane), while the Western Division followed the tradition of Ōe no Koretoki.
