On the 15th, when Ōta Dōkan, a retainer of the Uesugi, attacked Usui Castle, it was first established as a fortress. Yoshiaki also brought out his horse and waited, saying "The enemy is slow." Meanwhile, Ujitsuna departed from Odawara on the 4th day of the 10th month of Tenbun 6 (1537), arrived at Edo Castle, and when he conducted the arrival inspection, large forces rushed to join from various directions, totaling over twenty thousand horsemen as recorded (omitted). Thus, it was decided that battle would take place on the morning of the 5th day. The vanguard stealthily approached the riverbank during the night, planning to cross Matsudo at daybreak, and waited at the base of the embankment. When night had already broken, the Odawara forces faced the riverbank. The first formation, led by Lord Hakone and including Matsuda, Shimizu, Kano, and Kasahara, and the second formation with Tōyama, Yamanaka, Tame, Arakawa, Kinsekisai and other troops pressed forward like clouds and mist. The vanguard of the Oyumi forces - Shiizu, Murakami, Horie, Karashima and others - were waiting in position at the riverbank when a reconnaissance soldier came to the main camp and reported: "The enemy has already crossed the river. Their force is like clouds and mist, appearing to be twenty to thirty thousand. With our allied forces, a conventional equal battle cannot be achieved. It is difficult to strike a large army with a small force. Right now, quickly move the banner and either decide the battle in the middle of the river, or pretend that our allies are retreating, and when the enemy's vanguard is about half-way across..."
