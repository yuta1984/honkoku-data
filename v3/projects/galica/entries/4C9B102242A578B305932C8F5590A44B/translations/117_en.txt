in recent times, Inaba Michikuni made reproduced woodblock prints that are widely circulated), Kuchi-yami (by Minamoto no Tamenori, copied on the 5th day of the second month of Kōchō 3; in recent years, reproduced woodblock prints are widely circulated), Amida-kyō-sho (copied in Kahō 2), Shinsen-rōei-shū (bears a postscript from Kenji 1), Shūbun-in-ryaku (a woodblock printed edition from Kyōroku-kōin), Ruijū-jinji-hongen (bears a postscript from Ōan 5), Kojiki (the postscript states "Shakusei Chikatada-ason ippon Yoshida-dainagon Sadafusa-kyō hisho-bō no aida ie-no-kimi go-mei ni yori shosya susume ori mata ippon shosya kore o tomuru"; this is the famous Shinpuku-ji manuscript of the Kojiki cited by Motoori Norinaga in his Kojiki-den and by Hirata Atsutane in his Koshi-chō-kaidai and other works), Fusō-ryakki (ancient handwritten scroll), Jinji-hiki (transmission of the three sacred treasures), Gunka-sōron-saiyō (the cover bears the inscription "Honchō-musō Genchin-sho"), Dai-Nihon-koku-genpō-zen'aku-rei-i-ki (incomplete scroll manuscript, with middle and lower volumes available; this is what is referred to as the Shinpuku-ji manuscript in Gunsho-ruijū), Tōdaiji-komonjo (several collections exist), Honchō-jinja-ki (records the origins of shrines), Ruijū-kiken-shō (records factual accounts of shrines), Tenshō-kō-daijin-senkō-jidai-shō (scroll, likely the original text of Gengen-shū), Atsuta-hishaku-kenbun (scroll, included in Zoku-gunsho-ruijū), Atsuta-kōshiki (scroll, included in Zoku-gunsho-ruijū), Shinkō-shōtō-roku (included in Zoku-gunsho-ruijū), Ko-shōmon-kijin-jō (beginning with the certificates of land donations by Owari Toshimura in Kangen 3 and 4, there are several dozen certificates from 500 and 200 years past).
