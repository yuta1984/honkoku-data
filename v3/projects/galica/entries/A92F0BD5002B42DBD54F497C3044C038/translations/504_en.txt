Only about three men including one named Bango were spared and told to "report these events," then sent back to their country. From Rokuhara, Utsunomiya Sadatsuna was appointed as general, gathered forces from the Chūgoku region, and headed for Kyushu. Although they heard around Bingo that "the Mongols have already been defeated," Sadatsuna proceeded to Kyushu, made preparations for future invasions by foreign enemies, and returned. This great wind was considered a manifestation of divine protection from the various gods, and the Kaze-no-yashiro shrine at Ise was renamed Kaze-no-miya. The divine wind (kamikaze) of our country blowing apart the Mongol ships refers to this event. The common expression "mokori gotari" (meaning "enough of this" or expressing something terrifying) probably derives from the Mongols having learned their lesson. The three men including Gan Shang escaped and returned to report these events to the Mongol ruler. The Mongol ruler was Emperor Shizu of the Yuan dynasty.

In the tenth month of the fifth year of Kōan (1282), the warrior monks of Kōfuku-ji Temple, harboring resentment against the imperial court, carried the sacred tree of Kasuga Shrine into the capital. In the twelfth month, Middle Counselor Minamoto no Tomofusa was accused and exiled to Aki Province.
