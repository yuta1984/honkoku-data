Nevertheless, since he was Yoritsugu's grandfather, the Kanto (Kamakura bakufu) may have left him alone. Now, since he died at the time when Ryogyō's affair came to light, it is not without suspicion. Perhaps the military government (bakufu) secretly arranged it - it is doubtful. Dōke's sons and his grandson Tadaie were either exiled or dismissed from office. Only Lord Nijō Yoshizane was unaffected. This was because he was on bad terms with Dōke. According to the family account of the Nijō house, Yoshizane constantly lamented Dōke's plot to resent the Hōjō and disturb the realm, and frequently remonstrated with him, which made Dōke angry and caused the father and son relationship to become strained. Since Tokiyori knew this, no punishment was imposed on him. In the third month, First Rank Minister of Central Affairs Prince Munetaka left Kyoto. In the fourth month, he arrived in Kamakura and was appointed Sei-i Taishōgun (Barbarian-Subduing Generalissimo). He was thirteen years old at the time, though some say eleven. Hōjō Sagami-no-kami Tokiyori became shikken (regent). Hōjō Mutsu-no-kami Shigétoki became rensho (co-signatory). In the same month, former shōgun Fujiwara no Yoritsugu lost his position
