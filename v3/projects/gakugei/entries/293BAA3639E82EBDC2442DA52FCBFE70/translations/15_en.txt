Fū (wind) refers to supplementary verse. It is when one thing is added to another thing in verse. It is said to enlighten that person without directly stating the matter.
　At Naniwa bay, do these flowers bloom? After winter's seclusion, now that spring has come, do these flowers bloom?
Fu (exposition) is enumerative verse. It is expressed without comparing to anything.
　My heart drawn to blooming flowers, restlessly unaware of the weariness entering my being
Hi (comparison) is likening verse. It likens to something. For example, "comparing" means the same thing.
　If I were to leave you this morning like frost at dawn, would I fade away with longing?
Kyō (evocative image) is metaphorical verse.
　Though one may count my love, it will never be exhausted, even if one could count all the grains of sand on Ariso beach
Ga (elegance) is called straightforward verse.

Shō (hymn) is celebratory verse.
　This mansion has indeed prospered, built with three-leaf and four-leaf flourishing grass
The Yakumo Goshō states that Tsurayuki said it would be impossible to divide poetry broadly into six types. Indeed, would it be difficult to distinguish the variations within the six principles? However, this should depend on the style and circumstances.
The six forms (rokutai) are: long poem (chōka), short poem (tanka), sedōka, konpon'uta, acrostic (oriku), and crown and base (kutsukaburi). These are called the six forms.
Long poems (chōka) string together syllable counts of 5-7-5-7-5-7-5-7-7-7.
