12. If a patient is isolated in a separate room, it is necessary to properly disinfect not only the previous sickroom but also rooms with possible pathogenic contamination, utensils used by the patient, and family tableware, etc.

13. Wells, toilets, sewers, waste pits, etc. should be disinfected only when they have been contaminated by eating and drinking utensils, clothing, toys, etc. used by the patient, or when such contamination is suspected.

14. During illness, the eating utensils of the patient and caregiver should be boiled and disinfected each time.

15. Those engaged in nursing must not touch healthy children unless they have disinfected their clothing and hands.

16. It goes without saying that after the patient's complete recovery, the sickroom and all items related to the patient should be thoroughly disinfected. However, there is almost no need to disinfect areas such as under the floor.

17. Although symptoms completely recover, diphtheria bacteria remain in the throat for several days to three weeks to three months afterward, and infectivity persists. Therefore, during this period, disinfection of eating utensils should be continued as much as possible, and school attendance should be prohibited for at least four weeks after recovery.

(Note) Refer to "Degree of Disinfection by Disease Type" listed later.

Scarlet Fever Prevention Methods

1. Those who have traffic connections with scarlet fever epidemic areas should be monitored during the incubation period of the disease to see whether they develop the illness.

2. When there are suspicious symptoms such as chills, fever, vomiting, throat pain, etc., it is necessary to receive a physician's examination promptly.

3. When notification of a patient's occurrence is received, the patient should be [transferred to] an infectious disease [hospital/facility] as quickly as possible.
