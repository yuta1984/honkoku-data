　Cases with recorded considerably effective results were: eustachian tube abscess, oral abscess, alveolar pyorrhea, mastoiditis, erysipelas, empyema, proctitis, chancroid, and venous ulcer.
　As mentioned above, the appropriate cases for subcutaneous vaccine therapy with antivirus are extremely diverse. These cases belong to surgery, obstetrics, gynecology, urology, dermatology, ophthalmology, otorhinolaryngology, and oral surgery.
　Important areas of activity are still reserved for antivirus: we aim for local vaccine therapy before and after surgery.
　Since formation of local immunity is extremely rapid, it appears rational that certain important surgical procedures should involve washing with large amounts of antivirus or be performed later as necessary. This measure should particularly be taken when operating on urogenital organs or gastrointestinal organs. For example, operations such as gastrectomy or gastroenterostomy often cast doubt on even the most rigorous disinfection by the most skilled surgeons. In this case, why not operate on tissues that have been given local immunity? Why not preliminarily irrigate tissues to be incised with antivirus, soak the surgical site, surgeon's hands, tampons, and sutures in antivirus to obtain the opportunity to prevent complications? By specifically strengthening the natural immunity of tissues, it would defend against the growth of bacteria coming from outside. Of course, it would act similarly not only against bacteria existing in the body in a latent state, but also against bacteria that are distant and about to leave their nests.
