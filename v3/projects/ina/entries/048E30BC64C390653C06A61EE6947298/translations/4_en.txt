The fifth son of Lord Shingen, Lord Gorō Harukiyo, inherited the prestigious Nishina family of this province, changed his name to Nobumori, and when he became lord of Takato Castle, he had Hōdō-in perform prayers for protection. In particular, because Lord Nobumori deeply revered the great deity Suwa enshrined at a place called Okinotaira in Itamachi village, when he moved the shrine into the castle, he built a shrine within the compound of Hōdō-in and had the resident monks of Hōdō-in conduct the festivals for the great deity. Thus, from the 29th day of the 2nd month of Tenshō 10, Mizunoe-Uma (1582), when forces of the Oda clan from Owari Province came and besieged Takato Castle, Lord Nobumori defended the castle fortifications firmly, but ultimately could not hold out,

[Left page]

and on the 1st day of the 3rd month, he committed seppuku and passed away. At this time, the resident monks of Hōdō-in held solemn memorial services to console the lord's spirit, and his memorial tablet was preserved in the temple for posterity. What is still preserved at our temple today and posthumously honored as Sōryū-in-dono truly refers to this lord.

Thereafter, during the reign of Emperor Go-Yōzei, on the 29th day of the 2nd month of Bunroku 1, Mizunoe-Tatsu (1592), the monk Keishitsu Kōrin from Daisen-zan Hoda-ji temple of the Sōtō sect in Matsuida, Kōzuke Province, left that temple and came to reside at Hōdō-in. At this time, the lord of Takato Castle was a lord named Naitō Shurisuke Shōgetsu, formerly the lord of Minowa Castle in this district, and son of Naitō Shuri Nobutoy, a senior retainer of the Takeda family.
