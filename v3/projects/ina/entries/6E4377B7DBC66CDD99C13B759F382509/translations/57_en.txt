He was granted Takahashi in Miike District of Chikugo Province and was ennobled and appointed to the position of Shuzen-no-shō. When the Korean campaign began, he crossed over to that country and fought in various places. During the rebellion of Ishida Mitsunari and others, he attacked and captured Fushimi Castle and also attacked Ōtsu Castle. His brother-in-law Tachibana Sakon-no-shōgen Muneshige also sided with the western forces, so the brothers alike had their domains confiscated. In the spring of Keichō 18, the Shogun summoned the brothers Muneshige and Naotsugi and granted them territorial lands. Naotsugi was given the land of Kakioka in Tsukuba District of Hitachi Province (5,000 koku). Furthermore, [the Shogun] declared, "Since you two are brothers, you should adopt the same family name." From this point, Naotsugi took the Tachibana name through the Genji lineage. He served the Shogun in the battles before and after Osaka. In the autumn of Genna 6,

when Muneshige received back his original domain, Naotsugi was likewise summoned. However, his illness was already severe and he could not attend, dying shortly thereafter. In the spring of the following 7th year, his eldest son Tanetsugi was summoned and granted the original domain, again ruling Miike District in Chikugo Province (10,000 koku). He was ennobled and appointed Shuzen-no-shō, and died at the age of twenty-seven (the exact date is not yet known). His son, Izumi-no-kami Tanenaga, retired following his father's example, became a Buddhist monk taking the name Dōun, and died at seventy-seven years of age. His son, Shuzen-no-shō Taneaki, inherited the house.
