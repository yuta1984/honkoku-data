　Festival for the kitchen god Sanpō Kōjin.
　A. Soot from the hearth is collected and put in a measuring box (masu) together with rice, then offered to the earth god. (Osaki)

　　　　　　　　141　Shichi-Go-San Celebration　(15th day)
　This celebration, which first began among the nobility, is said to have become widely practiced among common people probably from the Edo period onward. This was called hakama-gi, kamitoki, obi-toki, etc., where children aged three, five, and seven were dressed in celebratory formal wear and taken to visit their tutelary deity. In old merchant districts, after the shrine visit was completed, they would continue to visit relatives and acquaintances' homes.
　A. An auspicious day is chosen in this month, relatives are invited, and a celebration banquet is held. Those invited respond to the invitation with celebratory gifts. (Fukuoka)
　　　　　　　　142　Akiba Festival　(16th day)　Old Calendar
　Festival for Akiba Mountain in Tōtōmi and Akiba Shrine.
　A. There is hi-machi (night vigil). (Isobe, Osaki)
　B. Two people from each district serve as proxy pilgrims to Akiba Mountain, holding night vigil with mochi-throwing. (Takashi)
　C. Each group sends proxy pilgrims to Akiba Mountain. (Ueda)

　　　　　　　　143　Imperial Review Memorial Day　(21st day)
　On the occasion of the grand army maneuvers held in the fields of Bishū in Showa 2, 
His Majesty the Emperor graciously reviewed the people of the Eastern Mikawa region at the 18th Infantry Regiment drill ground. To commemorate that day, a memorial ceremony is held annually at the same location.
　　　　　　　　144　Niiname-sai (Harvest Festival)　(23rd day)
　This festival is a ceremony where the first fruits of that year's new grain are offered to the heavenly and earthly deities at the Shinkaden in the imperial palace, and His Majesty also partakes of them and bestows them upon court officials. Its origins date back to the age of gods, and it was established by the Taihō Code. After being suspended for a long time following the Ōnin War, it was restored by imperial proclamation on November 15, Meiji 1, and after the calendar reform of Meiji 6, it was set for November 23.
　Furthermore, the first Niiname-sai after an emperor's enthronement is called Daijō-e, and it was stipulated in the Imperial House Law promulgated in Meiji 22 that it should be conducted in Kyoto together with the enthronement ceremony.
　　　　　　　　145　Daishi-kō (Great Master Lecture)　(24th day)　Old Calendar
　As this day is the death anniversary of Tendai Chisha Daishi, dharma assemblies are conducted at Tendai sect temples, beginning with Jingūji in Konyachō. In private homes, azuki bean porridge called "O-Daishi-gayu" is eaten on this morning.
