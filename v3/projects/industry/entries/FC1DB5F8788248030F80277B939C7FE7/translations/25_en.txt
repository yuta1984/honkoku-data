Chapter 10: About How to Make Silkworm Eggs Turn Blue
When the eggs are slow to turn blue, it is bad to place them in sunny spots, put them in one's breast pocket, or place them near braziers or fire. Simply fold the eggs in half, wrap them in wide paper, or wrap them during the day in unlined garments, at night in padded clothing, hang them in high places in warm houses, light small fires, and five or six times during the day

spread them out to let in air, then rewrap and hang them in the aforementioned place, allowing them to naturally develop their blue color before hatching. However, eggs that have turned blue should be hung along the lintel. But when the eggs reach the season of turning blue, they must not be put in water. Silkworms that emerge in two days will take four or five days longer to develop properly. This is very bad.

Chapter 11: About How to Hatch Silkworms
Before hatching silkworms, for every two sheets of eggs, prepare about one bale's worth of non-glutinous rice chaff, dry it in the sun, remove debris with a sieve, and set aside the coarser portions on top. Also, on the day of beginning to hatch, for each sheet of eggs, prepare mulberry seeds
