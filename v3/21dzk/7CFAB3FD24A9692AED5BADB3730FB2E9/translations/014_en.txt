…because those with suitable faculties are rare (though it encompasses both the specially gifted and those incidentally suited, this is reckoned by overall numbers and also discusses those of proper capacity); because the Dharma is difficult to understand (since the doctrinal principles are intricate and extremely profound, those of timid and weak character do not believe and do not study); because there is much envy and resentment (since the doctrinal path differs greatly from all other schools, those still caught in biased attachment uniformly slander it)—for these reasons it has not spread abroad. That being so, one should be ashamed of one's own shortcomings, not treat it as a shortcoming of the Dharma. Moreover, if one were to take widespread dissemination as a mark of superiority, there would be many objections one could raise regarding both doctrine and analogy. This is evident, and one should think it through and understand it for oneself. Stepping back and examining the increases and decreases in transmission across the three countries (India, China, Japan): in the western heavens (India) there is focused admiration (setting aside the biased views of the various outsiders and Hīnayāna practitioners); in the eastern Han (China) there is gradual hesitation and doubt; in Japan there is widespread slander and disparagement. What is the reason for this? Furthermore, I have heard that in the era of Taizong and Gaozong (of the Tang dynasty), (this teaching) spread throughout the nine provinces (all of China). I have also heard that in the Tang state even unto the present time, it is rare in the southern regions but flourishing greatly in the northern regions (and so forth). Furthermore, in our court (Japan), although people do not believe and create obstructive enmity, many deities (kami) believe and take refuge (in this teaching) and have issued vows of protection (such as Kasuga, Hachiman, and Kinpusen, and so on). What is the reason for this as well? Can it be that the Buddha-land (India) is base and the mlecchas (unbelievers) are noble, that the ancient was inferior and the present superior, that the deities are foolish and human beings wise? Comparing (things) in various ways, one cannot enumerate them all. For now, let one set aside narrow-minded thinking and consider them one by one.

Question: When it is said that those with suitable faculties are rare, what faculties are meant?

Answer: The original scripture (the Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra) says: "The ādāna-consciousness (ālayavijñāna) is extremely deep and subtle. All the seeds (flow) like a torrent. I do not expound this to ordinary foolish beings, fearing they would discriminate and cling to it as a self." (text) The Abhidharma-sūtra says: "Because of the consciousness that contains and stores all dharmas and all seeds, it is called the ālaya. I reveal this to the superior ones." (text) The nature of the ordinary-and-foolish and the superior ones is as stated in the Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi-śāstra. Therefore, those whose faculties of sudden or gradual awakening within the Mahāyāna have ripened are the intended audience for this (teaching). One should know: all persons who believe in and study (this teaching) all possess Buddha-nature and will all certainly become Buddhas. One should have absolutely no doubt about oneself. Therefore, the fundamental commentary (the Vijñaptimātratā-siddhi commentary) says: "If even those of fixed lineage (niyatarāśi), Mahāyāna ordinary beings, are not taught this, then who would construct and practice the true vijñaptimātratā-contemplation? How could people like us ever come to hear of this consciousness?" (text) Those who hear

and believe (this teaching) have no reason to doubt—this is made clear in the commentary's explanation. How much more so (is this true) for those who contemplate and practice! In general, faith has many levels and its depth and shallowness are immeasurable. One must diligently apply the mind (to this study) and pray to sacred images and the like, to attain a pure and steadfast faith-and-forbearance (śraddhā-kṣānti). What benefit is there in doubting? What harm is there in believing? For one who believes and practices, there will certainly be immeasurable and innumerable merits. For one who doubts and is negligent, there will certainly be innumerable losses and decline. Who of wisdom would abandon benefit and choose harm?

Question: What has been clarified above is thorough and detailed, but I do not yet know whether the transmitted teachings of our school are entirely exhausted here, with nothing else remaining.

Answer: It is not yet exhausted. The reason is: what has been discussed above is merely the general gist of the Dharma-characteristics (Yogācāra) gate. Beyond this, there is further the dharma-gate of entering the principle (入理). That is to say, among the five treatises of the Venerable Maitreya (慈尊), there is the Prajñā-treatise. The Vajracchedikā-sūtra (Diamond Sūtra) is its root scripture. This is the contemplation of the empty (śūnyatā) of the non-dual middle way of the Mahāyāna that Asaṅga (阿僧) requested of Maitreya (慈氏) and which Maitreya expounded in response to that request. Asaṅga received the teaching and transmitted it to Vasubandhu. Both teacher and disciple composed explanatory treatises. The school's founding master (Kuiji/Kuiji 窺基) propagated it and also composed a doctrinal commentary—namely, the laudatory annotations and the reconciling explanations and so on. Bodhisattva Dharmapāla likewise composed the Śataśāstra-vivṛti (廣百論) based on this gate, clearly elucidating its purport. However, these two gates (the Dharma-characteristics gate and the Prajñā/entering-principle gate) do not conflict with each other. Although they return to one gate, they also differ. This is because the doctrinal path of the dharma-gates has many major divisions at multiple levels. The several levels of correlations (相対) presented in the essentials of the Mādhyamika school (中宗) are precisely this intention. Now, examining its purport: within the Dharma-characteristics gate there are further the two gates of "entering the principle" and "Dharma-characteristics," and by pushing inward through one after another, one at last arrives at the transcendence of speech (絶言). All dharma-gates are here entirely exhausted. One should know: our school is the root-mother of all schools. The future suffering arising from the sin of slander—by inference, one can measure it. The causes and effects of saṃsāra do not differ from a sealed document (one cannot escape them). Who can prevent the person who, entrusting themselves to karma, does not repent?

Question: The purport that is excerpted in this (work) strongly favors harmony and reconciliation (和会) and differs from ordinary discourse. What is the intention?

Answer: Because the Dharma is actually thus. Because this is the true gate. Because of fear of the sin of slander. Because of accommodating others' views. Because of subduing others' attachments. Because the merits are many. Because it allows the Dharma to abide long. Because of longing for the true Dharma. Because of compassion for the (degenerate) image-and-final (末法) period. Because this is the great vow. If it were not so, it would contradict (our) school. Just as one subdues those of the two vehicles (二乗) and practices the practices of the two vehicles (such as the sixteen minds in the path of the preparatory vision 相見道), and likewise subdues the outsiders (外道) and practices the outsider practices (such as the six years of ascetic practice, and so on)—how much more so (is this true) for the different gates within the Mahāyāna? How much more so, when the Dharma is originally one? Therefore, the Tripiṭaka-master (Xuanzang 玄奘) said: "A sage in composing a treatise no longer engages in contradictions and disputes. It is only that students have their agreements and disagreements." And (Xuanzang) composed the Huizong-lun (会宗論, Treatise on the Reconciliation of the Schools) in three thousand lines of verse—worthy masters all praised and submitted to it, and divergent attachments immediately ceased. Furthermore, opening the "Chapter on Admonishing Disciples" of the Śataśāstra-vivṛti, (Dharmapāla) deeply admonishes against contention and discord and encourages harmony and reconciliation, saying: "People of this sort, each seeing things differently, divide the holy words and make them into many parts; they engage in mutual disputation and each clings to one extreme. Since they are unable to remove the dust and filth of evil views, who can match the profound and wondrous purport of the great Mahāyāna expounded by the Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones? Without having awakened to the true principle, they follow their own attachments and biases, deeming themselves right and others wrong. This is deeply to be feared. One should abandon attachment to the two extremes of emptiness and existence, and awaken to the non-dual middle way of the Mahāyāna." (and so forth) This is none other than the bodhisattva's pāramitā of skillful means (upāyakauśalya-pāramitā) of compliant harmonizing and reconciling (随順会通). Though (this work) is poor (in quality), what I aspire to is solely this doctrinal import.

Now, the final age of the semblance Dharma presses at the throat, and the life of the Dharma has reached the chest (i.e., it is in mortal danger). Obstacles and difficulties grow more intense by the day, and study and practice decline with each passing year. I fear that the confused utterances of those in delusion may exceed even those of ages past. Hearing this, I could not bear it and have hastily written down my shallow thoughts. Both their doctrinal content and their expression—I am deeply ashamed that they will be seen by posterity. I fervently hope that all wise and virtuous (scholars) will thoroughly rectify the errors (□謬). If there is anything that meets with approval, let this serve as a starting point, each

(Colophon:)
The monk Ryōben, whose dharma-name was Shingwan (信願), first resided at Shōgan-in within Kōfuku-ji, then moved to the separate hermitage of Chisoku at Tōdai-ji, and finally secluded himself at Daishō Chikurin-ji on Mount Ikoma. The details of his life and deeds are recorded in a separate account. He was truly a dragon-elephant (a great master) of the revival of the Dharma-characteristics school. The commentaries and treatises he composed are quite numerous, and the "tigers of doctrine" (doctrinal experts) of the various schools compete to treasure and enjoy them. I think that because the world has already fallen into the latter age of the semblance Dharma and the study of vijñaptimātratā (consciousness-only) has been declining more and more with each passing year, (the author) could not bear this sorrow—and this is why he composed (this work). Among the followers of the same gate (our school), who would not share the same aspiration? For this reason, (I) wish to have it carved on woodblocks and cause it to circulate throughout the world, and I hope that all later students will each examine their own minds by means of this (work). Thus is written by the one who undertakes its publication.
