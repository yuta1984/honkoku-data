Namely, among the hundred dharmas, these are all the remaining mental factors corresponding to the eight consciousnesses except sensation and perception, plus the twenty-four non-associated formations. "Consciousness aggregate" means gathering all mind-kings—the eight consciousnesses among the hundred dharmas. "Aggregate" means collection. Because there are five collections, they are called the five aggregates. Since these are conditioned dharmas, this classification does not include unconditioned dharmas. This is precisely what all Buddhas proclaimed to enable sentient beings to eliminate self-attachment. One should observe one's own body regarding the parts of one's physical form: from head to foot, from skin to bone, large intestine and small intestine, the six organs and five viscera—all these things are form aggregate. Using this as support, that which can serve as support and can perceive—mind and mental factors—and among the various meaningful divisions, the latter four aggregates are distinguished. Beyond this there is nothing more. What could be self or person? Those various bodily implements are form aggregate. Matters of suffering and pleasure arise from sensation aggregate. The causes of verbal expression are called perception aggregate. The fabrication of dharmic and non-dharmic activities lies in formations aggregate. All foundational supports are consciousness aggregate. Therefore, knowing the five aggregates means knowing the wonderful medicine for eliminating self-attachment. To eliminate this dharma-attachment, one relies on consciousness-only contemplation, as in the original commentary notes.

Characteristics of the Twelve Sense-Fields

Among the hundred dharmas are six faculties (the five faculties of eye, etc., plus the mental faculty, which is the manas) and six objects (the five dusts of form, etc., plus dharma-dust, which comprises all dharmas that are objects of mental consciousness). These constitute the twelve sense-fields: eye-field through dharma-field. These twelve dharmas are so named because they are the successive gates for the arising of the six consciousnesses, hence called "fields." The mental faculty also includes the ālaya-consciousness. The dharma-objects definitely include unconditioned dharmas.

Characteristics of the Eighteen Realms

【Lower Section】
Among the hundred dharmas are six faculties, six objects, and six consciousnesses: eye-realm, form-realm, eye-consciousness-realm, up to mind-realm, dharma-realm, mind-consciousness-realm. These are called the eighteen realms. These eighteen dharmas are called "realms" because of their tribal distinctions. The mind-realm here is the same as the mental-faculty field. The dharma-realm is also the same as the dharma-field. These twelve fields and eighteen realms are also for eliminating self-attachment and thing-attachment. However, the differences among these three approaches are: the aggregate approach is taught for those with dull faculties regarding mental factors who generally cling to self; the twelve fields are taught for those with dull faculties regarding material dharmas who generally cling to self; the eighteen realms are taught for those with dull faculties regarding both matter and mind who generally cling to self. Or alternatively, for sequential superior, medium, and inferior capacities, brief, medium, and extensive teachings are given. All have their reasons. These dharma-attachments are all eliminated through reliance on consciousness-only contemplation.

Principles of the Four Noble Truths

Suffering, origin, cessation, and path are called the Four Noble Truths. Generally, this is the principle of cause-effect-cessation. Within cause-effect principle are two gates of defilement and purity. Suffering and origin are precisely the cause and effect of defilement. Cessation and path are precisely the cause and effect of purity—sequential contaminated and uncontaminated dharmas. "Suffering" means being tormented by the three sufferings (suffering of suffering, suffering of change, and suffering of conditioned existence) and eight sufferings (birth, old age, sickness, death, separation from loved ones, meeting with hatred, not obtaining what is sought—encompassing all sufferings; details omitted) of contaminated karmic results in the three realms and six paths. "Origin" is the cause that brings about this suffering-result—namely all afflictions and all contaminated good and evil karma. Thus contemplating the principle of cause and effect, one becomes weary of suffering, cuts off origin, and escapes birth-and-death. "Cessation" means analytical cessation, which is unconditioned. Through the power of wisdom's analytical discrimination, one extinguishes all defiled dharmas of afflictions and suffering, ultimately realizing and attaining this as the fruit. "Path" is the cause for attaining this cessation-fruit—namely all uncontaminated conventional and ultimate wisdoms. Thus

The Two Vehicles also contemplate these Four Truths: fragmentary results are called suffering truth, afflictions and deluded karma are called origin truth, the cessation of suffering and origin is called cessation truth, giving rise to emptiness-wisdom is called path truth. The Non-Established Four Noble Truths are called Unconditioned Four Truths in old translations. Within this are three levels of contemplation. First are the transformational Four Noble Truths: transformational results are called suffering truth, knowledge-obstructions etc. are called origin truth, non-abiding nirvana is called cessation truth, dharma-emptiness wisdom is called path truth. Second, contemplating the two-emptiness true-thusness without making separate contemplations is called non-established. Third, abandoning conceptual expression for the one ultimate principle—since mind and speech are cut off, this is called non-established. These latter three levels are exclusively bodhisattva contemplations, not accessible to the Two Vehicles. The non-established described in the Yogācāra treatises is precisely the one ultimate reality. The Śrīmālā Sūtra also contains this principle. Six Characteristics of Seeds

The second volume of the [Consciousness-Only] treatise states: "First, momentary cessation—meaning the essence barely arises then inevitably ceases without interval, possessing superior efficacy to become a seed. This excludes permanent dharmas, which, being permanent without transformation, cannot be said to have generative function. Second, simultaneous with effect—meaning appearing simultaneously and harmoniously with the produced manifest effect-dharmas to become a seed. This excludes temporal separation and definite separation... establishing the name 'seed' based on producing manifest results, not calling it 'seed' based on generating its own type. Therefore, one should only speak of simultaneity with effects. Third, continuous transformation—meaning necessarily continuing in one type for a long time until the ultimate stage to become a seed. This excludes the evolving consciousnesses, which transform and are interrupted, not corresponding to seed-dharmas, thus revealing seeds' self-type mutual generation. Fourth, determined nature—meaning the capacity to produce good, evil, etc. is determined according to causal power to become a seed. This excludes other schools' position that heterogeneous causes having causal conditions with heterogeneous effects. Fifth, awaiting multiple conditions—meaning necessarily awaiting the convergence of its own multiple conditions with superior capacity to become a seed. This excludes externalists' position that natural causes constantly produce effects all at once without awaiting multiple conditions, or excludes other schools' position that conditions are never non-existent, revealing that awaited conditions do not have permanently existing nature, so seeds do not constantly produce effects all at once regarding fruits. Sixth, drawing forth its own effects—meaning each separately draws forth respective form, mind, and other effects to become a seed. This excludes externalists' position that only one cause produces all effects, or excludes other schools' position that form, mind, etc. mutually serve as causal conditions. Only the functional differences within the fundamental consciousness, possessing these six characteristics, constitute seeds and not others."
