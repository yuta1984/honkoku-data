## Right Page (Zoku Hossō-shū Shoshin Ryakuyō, Lower Volume — Section 6)

### [Upper Section]

Among the *indeterminate mental factors* (*aniyata-caitasika*, 不定心所), the two factors of *kaukṛtya* ("regret," 悔) and *middha* ("torpor/sleep," 眠) [Note: "眼" in the text is likely a scribal error for "眠"] are absent in the upper realms. Of the two factors *vitarka* ("applied thought," 尋) and *vicāra* ("sustained thought," 伺): the factor *vitarka* extends through the desire realm (*kāmadhātu*) and the first *dhyāna*. The factor *vicāra* extends through the intermediate *dhyāna* (*chūkanzен*, 中間禅). The "intermediate *dhyāna*" refers to the third heaven within the first *dhyāna*. The mental factor *vitarka* is absent in that heaven. All remaining mental factors extend universally throughout the three realms. Among the five sense consciousnesses, the nose consciousness and the tongue consciousness exist only in the desire realm. The eye, ear, and body consciousnesses extend into the first *dhyāna* as well. In the second *dhyāna* and above, all five sense consciousnesses are entirely absent. Regarding the universal applicability and limitation [Note: "屈" is likely a scribal error for "局"] of their associated mental factors, one should follow the analogies given above.

### On the Obstruction to Omniscience (*jñeyāvaraṇa*, 所知障)

The obstruction to omniscience (*jñeyāvaraṇa*) does not consist of a separate set of dharmas apart from the afflictive obstruction (*kleśāvaraṇa*, 煩悩障). At the base of each and every affliction (*kleśa*, 煩悩) there is a subtle portion that confuses one regarding the nature (*svabhāva*, 体) of dharmas. This is called the obstruction to omniscience. Thus, the afflictive obstruction confuses one regarding the *function* (*yō*, 用) of dharmas, while the obstruction to omniscience confuses one regarding the *nature* (*tai*, 体) of dharmas.

These two portions differ in what they obstruct. The portion that confuses regarding function obstructs *nirvāṇa*, agitates and distresses sentient beings, and causes them to remain in *saṃsāra*. The portion that confuses regarding nature obstructs *bodhi*, covers over the objects of cognition (*jñeya*, 所知境), and prevents wisdom from arising. Therefore, the path for cutting them off also has its respective limitations [Note: "眼" is likely a scribal error for "限"], and for this reason (the obstructions) are divided into two.

In ultimate truth (*riji*, 理実), the two obstructions are of the same substance (*dōtai*, 同体). For example, when a single *kleśa* of greed (*lobha*, 貪) arises as love/attachment toward a single sentient being, at the base of that greedy attachment there must always be a portion that confuses regarding the nature (*svabhāva*) of the five aggregates (*skandha*, 五蘊). If one awakens to the fact that form (*rūpa*, 色) and so forth are like illusions, how could one generate attachment toward what is merely a composite fiction of color and so on? It is thus clearly understood that this greedy love arises with the "portion that confuses regarding the nature of the five aggregates" as its support (*āśraya*, 所依). This "portion that confuses regarding nature" is precisely the obstruction to omniscience contained within the *kleśa* of greed. All other (afflictions) should likewise be understood by analogy.

In general, one must distinguish between *sōen* ("associative cognition," 想縁) and *betsuen* ("distinct cognition," 別縁). By "*sōen*" is meant conceiving and grasping the composite of the five aggregates as "self." By "*betsuen*" is meant separately grasping the ālayavijñāna (*raiya*, 賴耶) itself as "self."

### [Lower Section]

Grasping the function (*yō*) constitutes the grasping of self (*ātmagrāha*, 我執); grasping the nature (*tai*) constitutes the grasping of dharmas (*dharmagrāha*, 法執). Repeated contemplation of this will make it clear. The afflictive obstruction takes the view of self (*ātmadṛṣṭi*, 我見) as its chief element; the obstruction to omniscience takes the view of the dharma-self (*dharmaātmadṛṣṭi*, 法我見) as its chief element. However, since the ignorance (*avidyā*, 無明) component of the obstruction to omniscience is dominant, the sutras refer to it by the name "the ground of ignorance-dwelling" (*avidyāvāsabhūmi*, 無明住地). Strictly speaking, this does not mean there are no other views and so forth (within it).

This obstruction to omniscience, when viewed from the perspective of the two vehicles (*dviyāna*, 二乗), is "unobscured neutral" (*anivṛtāvyākṛta*, 無覆無記) — because it does not constitute an obstruction for them. When viewed from the perspective of the bodhisattva, it is "obscured neutral" (*nivṛtāvyākṛta*, 有覆無記) — because it obstructs the bodhisattva's *bodhi*.

Now, (the statement that) the afflictive obstruction and the obstruction to omniscience are, respectively, obstructions to *nirvāṇa* and *bodhi* — this is a first-pass explanation. The statement that the afflictive obstruction obstructs the emptiness of self (*jīvaśūnyatā*, 生空) and the obstruction to omniscience obstructs the emptiness of dharmas (*dharmaśūnyatā*, 法空) — this too is a first-pass explanation. Both of these describe the "primary function" (*shōyō*, 正用) and "superior function" (*shōyō*, 勝用). If one argues exhaustively through principle: the afflictive obstruction also obstructs *bodhi* and the emptiness of dharmas; the obstruction to omniscience also obstructs *nirvāṇa* and the emptiness of self. These constitute "concurrent function" (*kenyō*, 兼用) and "inferior function" (*retsuyō*, 劣用). The reason for this is: the affliction is the "depending" (*nōe*, 能依) element, and the obstruction to omniscience is the "depended-upon" (*shoe*, 所依) element. Afflictions are coarse; the obstruction to omniscience is subtle. The power of the depended-upon necessarily reaches the depending; coarse power necessarily reaches the subtle. Hence (this mutual influence).

### On the Three Classes of Objects (*sanruikyō*, 三類境)

The verse of the Tripiṭaka Master (Xuanzang, 三蔵) says: *"The nature-object does not follow the mind; the shadow-only (object) follows solely the percept; the quality-bearing (object) extends through both the perceiving subject and the original (object); nature, seed, and so on follow accordingly."*

A "**nature-object**" (*seikkyō*, 性境) is an object that has real function (*jitsuyō*, 実用), does not follow the perceiving mind (*nōen*, 能縁), and maintains its own nature — hence it is called "nature-object." There are three ways in which it "does not follow": (1) *Nature does not follow*: it does not necessarily follow the three natures (wholesome, unwholesome, neutral) of the perceiving mind (i.e., good, evil, etc.); (2) *Seed does not follow*: its seeds are different from those of the perceiving mind; (3) *Binding does not follow*: it does not necessarily follow the realm-binding (*kai-ke*, 界繋) of the perceiving mind. In terms of individual consciousnesses: the individual clusters of the five sense consciousnesses all perceive exclusively nature-objects. The mind-king (*citta*, 心王) of the eighth consciousness likewise perceives exclusively nature-objects. The cluster of the sixth consciousness at times perceives nature-objects. The cluster of the defiled seventh consciousness (*kliṣṭamanas*, 有漏第七識) cannot at all perceive nature-objects.

### [Upper Section]

A "**shadow-only object**" (*dokuyō-kyō*, 独影境) is an object that has no real function, entirely follows the perceiving mind, and arises alone without an original basis (*honjichi*, 本質) — hence it is called "shadow-only." There are three ways in which it "follows the mind": (1) *Nature follows mind*: its nature is the same as that of the perceiving mind; (2) *Seed follows mind*: it shares the same seeds as the perceiving mind; (3) *Binding follows mind*: it belongs to the same realm-binding as the perceiving mind. In terms of individual consciousnesses: the cluster of the sixth consciousness at times perceives shadow-only objects. The mental factors associated with the eighth consciousness also constantly perceive shadow-only objects. The defiled five sense consciousnesses and the seventh consciousness never perceive shadow-only objects.

A "**quality-bearing object**" (*taishitsu-kyō*, 帯質境) is neither like the nature-object (which does not follow the perceiving mind) nor necessarily like the shadow-only object (which follows the perceiving mind); it necessarily "bears" an original basis (*honjichi*, 本質) and has a portion that follows that basis — hence it is called "quality-bearing." There are three ways in which it "extends through both perceiving subject and original basis" (*tsū-jō-hon*, 通情本): (1) *Nature extends through perceiving subject and original basis*: it follows both the three natures of the perceiving mind and the three natures of the original basis; (2) *Seed extends through perceiving subject and original basis*: it follows both the seeds of the perceiving mind and the seeds of the original basis; (3) *Binding extends through perceiving subject and original basis*: it belongs to both the realm-binding of the perceiving mind and the realm-binding of the original basis. In terms of individual consciousnesses: the cluster of the seventh consciousness constantly perceives this type of object. The cluster of the sixth consciousness at times also perceives it. Other consciousnesses do not ordinarily perceive it.

Regarding the above three objects — when pointing to their substance: the nature-object consists of real form (*jisshiki*, 実色) and real mind (*jisshin*, 実心). Therefore the *Sūyo* (*Chéngwéishì lùn sūyào*, 成唯識論掌中枢要) commentary explains: "Form is true form; mind is real mind" (and so forth). By "real form" is meant the five sense faculties (*pañcendriya*, 五根) and the five sense objects (*pañcaviṣaya*, 五境). The real form included within the *dharmadhātu* (*hossho*, 法処) also belongs here — specifically, the "real form produced by *samādhi*" (*jōka-jisshiki*, 定果実色), the gold, silver, and other things transformed through the meritorious *samādhi* (*itoku-jō*, 威徳定). By "real mind" is meant: the self-witnessing division (*svasaṃvittibhāga*, 自証分) perceiving the perceiving division (*darśanabhāga*, 見分), and the latter two divisions cognizing each other, and so forth. With respect to such objects, the perceiving mind obtains the individual characteristic (*svalakṣaṇa*, 自相) of the object — clearly manifesting and transforming the image division (*nimittabhāga*, 相分).

### [Lower Section]

Therefore the *Sūyo* commentary explains: "Arising from real seeds, there is real substance and real function; the perceiving mind obtains that (object's) individual characteristic — this is called the nature-object" (end of quote).

The "shadow-only object" refers to things like the image-division (*nimittabhāga*) that appears in the mind when perceiving things like turtle hair or rabbit horns. The perceiving mind is confused and deluded; the object also has no original basis; it is borne entirely upon deluded mental projection and is through and through a fictitious dharma.

The "quality-bearing object": when the seventh consciousness perceives (*ālambana*-izes) the perceiving division (*darśanabhāga*) of the eighth consciousness, the transformed image division (*nimittabhāga*) constitutes the primary example of a quality-bearing object. This image division does have an original basis — the real mind (the eighth consciousness) — yet the perceiving mind cannot obtain the individual characteristic (*svalakṣaṇa*) of the object; it is through and through a non-standard cognition (*pramāṇa-vyatikrama*, 非量). Nevertheless, the persistent grasping of "self" and "mine" does not cease. Therefore, the nature, seeds, etc. of the transformed image division extend through both the perceiving subject (*jō*, 情) and the original basis (*hon*, 本).

(Why this object is classified as quality-bearing:) Since there is already a real basis, it does not entirely follow the perceiving mind. Therefore its nature is judged to follow the original basis — the eighth consciousness — and it can also be said to be "included within the unobscured neutral" (*anivṛtāvyākṛta*). This is why it differs from the shadow-only object. On the other hand, since there is already the perceiving mind (the seventh consciousness), it cannot obtain the self-characteristic of the (object's) substance. Therefore it does not entirely follow the original basis. Thus its nature is judged to follow the perceiving mind — the seventh consciousness — and it can also be said to be "included within the obscured neutral" (*nivṛtāvyākṛta*). This is why it differs from the nature-object. In a single moment of a single thought, both these aspects are present — this is called "nature extending through perceiving subject and original basis" (*sei tsū jō hon*, 性通情本).

The seeds that produce (this image division) are in reality separate seeds; but since the manifest activity (*gengyō*, 現行) is already classified as extending through both perceiving subject and original basis in this manner, the seeds too can, by way of meaning, be described as either "the seeds of the perceiving mind" or "the seeds of the original basis." They may be called "arisen from the seeds of the perceiving mind" and also "arisen from the seeds of the original basis." This is called "seed extending through perceiving subject and original basis" (*shu tsū jō hon*, 種通情本).

As for "binding extending through perceiving subject and original basis" (*ke tsū jō hon*, 繫通情本): the manifest activity of the seventh and eighth consciousnesses necessarily belongs to the same realm-binding. Therefore, though the bindings of perceiving subject and original basis are not different, looking from each side separately — it is said that (the image-division) is "the same realm-binding as the perceiving mind" and equally said to be "the same realm-binding as the original basis." This meaning too can be understood. [Note: However, the matter of *binding extending through perceiving subject and original basis* is not entirely clear; one should consult the original texts.]
