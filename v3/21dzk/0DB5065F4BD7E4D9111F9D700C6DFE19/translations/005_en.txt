### [Upper Section]

The fourth phrase, "nature, seed, and so on follow accordingly" (*seishu tō zuiō*, 性種等随応), means that the above-mentioned three categories of nature, seed, and realm-binding, and so on, should each be understood as following accordingly in (each of) the three matters. Within this phrase, there are various combinations of "single" and "overlapping" (*fuku*, 覆) instances: the three objects may each occur individually without combining, or two may overlap (nature-object combined with shadow-only; nature-object combined with quality-bearing; shadow-only combined with quality-bearing), or all three may overlap (all three objects combined). One commentary states: "When these two consciousnesses (the sixth and seventh) cognize the five aggregates of the past and future, the resulting object can be described as a shadow-only object, and it can also be said to be a quality-bearing object, since (those past and future dharmas) perfume and form seeds that give rise to the original basis" (end of quote). This is therefore a combined object of shadow-only and quality-bearing. The remaining cases can be understood by analogy with this. The detailed purport should be sought in the original text (e.g., the *Chéngwéishì lùn*).

**Question:** The cluster of the eighth consciousness transforms objects naturally according to the force of karmic causes, and none of these objects have an original basis (*honjichi*, 本質). As for things like body and land (*shindo*, 身土), even if the transformation of another sentient being may serve as an *indirectly perceived condition* (*soshoen-en*, 疎所縁縁), this is not a true original basis. If this is the case, then all the image-divisions (*nimitta-bhāga*, 相分) of the cluster of the ālayavijñāna should be shadow-only objects. On the other hand, if (we say it is) a nature-object because the perceiving mind obtains the individual characteristic (*svalakṣaṇa*, 自相) of the object through direct perception (*pratyakṣa*, 現量), then the entire cluster should be nature-objects. Yet the present explanation says the mind-king (*citta-rāja*, 心王) perceives nature-objects while the mental factors (*caitasika*, 心所) transform shadow-only objects. The reasoning behind this is not yet clear. How should this be understood?

**Answer:** In general, the ālayavijñāna is the root of all conditioned existence (*saṃskṛta*, 有為) and relies exclusively upon the force of prior karma to transform its sphere of objects. All (other) consciousnesses take it as their basis and depend upon it as their original basis. The cluster of the eighth consciousness is different from other consciousnesses, and since it already obtains the individual characteristic of its object, its objects are real objects (*jikkyō*, 実境) with real substance and real function — hence they are called nature-objects. However, regarding the difference between the mind-king and mental factors: as stated earlier (in the *Kennyō*, 顯要), since the mind-king is already the root of conditioned existence, (the mental factors) cannot attain to it. If (the mental factors) were equally to transform real objects, then a single sentient being would have six body-sense faculties and so on, and various contradictions would arise. For this reason, the mental factors of the eighth consciousness necessarily transform shadow-only objects — that is, fictitious seeds, fictitious five faculties, and the physical world-container (*kikai*, 器界).

The *Yuishiki-so* (*Chéngwéishì lùn shū*, 唯識疏) [Note: "疎" is likely a scribal error for "疏"] states: "The five (caitasika-) groups in the causal stage rely solely upon what the mind-king has transformed as their original basis" (end of quote). According to this commentary, the mental factors of the eighth consciousness take the mind-king's objects as their original basis — so how can they be called shadow-only?

**Answer:** That commentary has a different intent. The transformation of the three kinds of objects is no simple matter. Seeds and various dharmas as direct causes (*pratītyasamutpāda-hetu*, 親因縁) — unless one is the appropriate recipient of the dharma's influence through habituation, who can possibly transform them? [Note: "the appropriate recipient of the dharma's influence" necessarily refers to the mind-king of the eighth consciousness; mental factors are not so.] The form-body (*rūpa-kāya*, 五根) of sentient beings — unless one is the true master of the retributive body (*shinhōshu*, 眞報主), who can possibly transform it? The physical world-container where sentient beings dwell extends immeasurably across boundless realms. Without the ālayavijñāna, who could transform it? But the mental factors are companions (of the mind-king); since their power is insufficient, they necessarily rely upon the power and force of the mind-king's objects, and the dharmic appearances manifest as if the mental factors had transformed them themselves. This is what is meant by "relying upon what the mind-king has transformed as the original basis" — it is not like the quality-bearing object, where the image genuinely relies upon and depends on the original object. Since the image-division (of the mental factors) is transformed in this three-stage manner (i.e., is a derivative appearance), it does not in any way contradict the meaning of shadow-only.

**Question:** The notion that the image-division (*nimittabhāga*) produced when the sixth consciousness cognizes the five aggregates of the past and future can perfume (*vāsanā*, 熏) the seeds of the original basis (*honjichi*) is highly doubtful. In the first place, the image-division transformed when cognizing a dharma that is altogether nonexistent is itself merely a fictitious image-division (*nimitta-bhāga*) of false dharmic appearances; it cannot even perfume its own seeds. How then could it perfume the seeds of the nonexistent dharma that is the original basis?

**Answer:** In general, what is capable of producing real dharmas has a substance different from turtle hair (i.e., what is utterly nonexistent). Even when cognizing a dharma that has not yet arisen or has already ceased, since its appearance (*lakṣaṇa*, 相) already takes the form of dharmas such as color and blue, although it is fictitious and not the same as (presently existing) things like a pot, it is nonetheless capable of perfuming seeds. As for saying that the original basis having no substance does not permit (such perfuming): even though the original basis (i.e., a past or future dharma) has no present image-division (*nimittabhāga*), it already has its substance (*tai*, 体), and therefore the capacity to perfume the seeds of the basis (*jisshushi*, 質種) lies originally in the power of the image-division (of the cognizing mind). Why then raise the absence (of the present original basis) as an objection?

**Question:** According to the doctrine of the Yogācāra (*Yuishiki*, 唯識) school, objects necessarily follow the mind. If they do not follow the mind, how can they be called transformations of the mind? Yet the nature-object being discussed here possesses the three non-followings, which seems greatly to contradict the principle of mind-only (*vijñaptimātratā*, 唯識). How should this be understood?

Since they are fictitious, they do not lose their fictitious substance and function. Therefore there exists "non-following as-if-illusory," and there exists "extension-through-perceiving-subject-and-original-basis as-if-illusory." One must not grasp these as real. This arises from the discriminations of one's own mind; the distinctions of the three objects follow the modes of cognition (*pramāṇa*, 量) of the perceiving mind; and the three classes of image-divisions are like illusions and dreams. This is precisely the meaning of transformation in mind-only (*vijñaptimātratā-pariṇāma*, 唯識転変).

To elaborate further: when the perceiving mind and the original basis confront each other, the object of the original basis is not something transformed by the present perceiving mind — thus it is necessarily the case that the object does not follow the present perceiving mind. With respect to such a "non-following dharma," the clear and pure mind of direct perception (*pratyakṣa*, 現量) obtains it just as it is. Therefore, the shadow-image (*nimitta-bhāga*, 相分) manifested by this mind (as a nature-object) is extremely similar to the object of the original basis. How could it fail to possess the quality of "non-following"? This means precisely that because the object follows the mind (i.e., is a mind-transformation), there is in turn the quality of "non-following." Reflect deeply on this and it will become clear.

### On the Matter of the Object-Only (*kyō-yuishiki*, 境唯識)

With respect to the objects transformed by the eighth consciousness, there are four distinct categories (*shiku*, 四句). The first is "common among the common" (*kyō-chū-kyō*, 共中共), referring to things such as mountains, rivers, and the earth. All sentient beings transform these together and use them together, and there is no sense in which they belong to any particular party. The second is "uncommon among the common" (*kyō-chū-fukyō*, 共中不共), referring to things such as one's own fields and dwelling. Although all sentient beings transform these in common, they are not used in common; they belong to their respective owners, and so differ from the previous category. The third is "common among the uncommon" (*fukyō-chū-kyō*, 不共中共), referring to the assisting-root form (*fukon*, 扶根, i.e., the gross physical body that supports the sense faculties). This is the dependent body of each sentient being, and each being transforms it individually. One's own body's assisting root — though others also transform it,

**Question:** Consider the water at a single location: humans see it as water, heavenly beings see it as lapis lazuli, animals (*bōshō*, 傍生) see it as a dwelling or path, hungry ghosts (*preta*, 鬼) see it as raging flames. Now, are these various transformations experienced by each of them merely *parikalpita* (遍計所執, the imagined and attached-to)? Or is this difference also present within the dependent nature (*paratantra*, 依他起) dharma-body itself? Is this only a transformation of the sixth consciousness? Or does this difference also exist within the objects of the eighth consciousness? And to which of the four categories above does it belong?

**Answer:** In the *Abhidharmasamuccaya-sūtra* (*Abidatsuma-kyō*, 阿毘達磨経), it is explained that four kinds of wisdom (*jñāna*, 智) accomplish *bodhi* and enable one to awaken to the meaning of mind-only. The first — the "wisdom of the characteristic of mutually contradictory perceptions" (*sōii-shikisō-chi*, 相違識相智) — takes the different perceptions of those (four classes of) beings in the four destinies (*yonshu*, 四趣) as its basis for awakening to mind-only. If the *paratantra* image-division had no such difference, how could this serve as the meaning of mind-only transformation? In this regard: whenever all the minds and mental factors of all sentient beings arise, they invariably bear an *adhyātmaviṣaya* (*shinshoen-en*, 親所縁縁, directly perceived condition). Except for the perception-division (*darśanabhāga*, 見分) of correct wisdom (*samyagjñāna*, 正智), all (minds) bear an inner dependent-nature object (*eta-nai-kyō*, 依他内境). Never has there been a case of a mind arising bearing only the *parikalpita*. This is the invariable and correct nature (of all perception). The *parikalpita* consists of non-existing dharmas. Non-existing dharmas cannot be an *ālambana-pratyaya* (*shoen-en*, 所縁縁, object-condition). Can the mind arise without an *ālambana-pratyaya*? Even when cognizing things like turtle hair, there must necessarily be an inner dependent-nature object appearing in the mind that serves as the *ālambana-pratyaya*. This inner object (i.e., the image-division) is called a "shadow-only object" (*dokuyō-kyō*, 独影境). Since the original basis is absent, it does not constitute an *ālambana-pratyaya*; (the imagined object itself) is not subsumed under the three classes of objects. The present question is not asking about this.
