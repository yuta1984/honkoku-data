## Right Page (Zoku Hossō-shū Shoshin Ryakuyō, Lower Volume — Section 10)

### [Upper Section]

At that moment (when a hungry ghost sees raging fire), the appearance (*lakṣaṇa*, 相) of the *parikalpita* (遍計所執, imagined-and-attached-to) raging fire manifests before one's awareness (*tōjō*, 当情). That there is a reason for this is likewise necessarily so. However, whether this appearance actually exists within the objects transformed by the eighth consciousness, which serve as the original basis (*honjichi*, 本質), is something that seems at first difficult to determine. Upon reflection, however, it may be concluded that it does in fact exist. This is brought about by the force of karma (*gōriki*, 業力). When the hungry ghost's eighth consciousness transforms water at a certain location and (the ghost) attempts to make use of it, that (water) suddenly transforms into fire. That is, the present-activity (*gengyō*, 現行) of water ceases and the present-activity of fire arises in that same place. [Note: Strictly speaking, seawater is composed of the four great elements, and raging fire is likewise composed of the four great elements. The real elemental natures (*daishū*, 大種) are all mutually present, but here, speaking in terms of the dominant appearance (*zōsō*, 増相), we say that the water ceases and fire appears.] At that same time, the eighth consciousness of a human continues to transform water in that same place, just as before. The water and fire respectively transformed by the eighth consciousnesses of the ghost and the human are different from each other, yet they coexist in the same moment and place. This is because the substance of all dharmas is like an illusion and ultimately returns to the one mind. Therefore, what is impressed (*kanzuru*, 感) by the force of karma is inconceivable in this way. The inconceivability of karmic force is exactly like the domain of supernatural powers (*jintsū-kyōkai*, 神通境界), and the evidence for this is not limited to one case — there is no need to begin debating it anew. Therefore, in the objects transformed by the eighth consciousness, there is this very difference (that is, one place appearing as water to humans and as fire to hungry ghosts). The six consciousnesses of humans and hungry ghosts each rely upon their own eighth consciousness's transformations and each transform their own image-divisions (*nimittabhāga*, 相分): the former see water, the latter see fire. If this were not so, the reasoning would be extremely difficult to establish. Consider: the hungry ghost directly sees raging fire right before its eyes. At that moment, how could the eye-consciousness (*cakṣur-vijñāna*, 眼識) not arise? If the eye-consciousness arises, how could it not rely upon what the eighth consciousness has transformed? — since it is determined that "the five sense-consciousnesses rely solely upon the image-division (*nimitta-bhāga*) of the eighth consciousness." Alternatively, (the view that) the image-division arises from an external basis (*geshichi*, 外質) transformed by the sixth consciousness is not the case during the operation of karmic force. Furthermore, at that time, even the mental consciousness (*manovijñāna*, 意識) sees the color of the raging fire through the sense-gate (*konmon*, 根門, i.e., the sense-faculty). If there were no original basis, how could it see this through the sense-gate? This would contradict the proper state of affairs (*shōsō*, 正相) in various ways. The eighth consciousness already cognizes (its object) through the sense-gate. If the eye-consciousness does not arise, this too contradicts the proper state of affairs. The notion that the mental consciousness alone relies upon the sense-gate without the five sense-consciousnesses arising is exceedingly untenable. Therefore, if the eye-consciousness arises, there must necessarily be an original basis constituted by the object transformed by the eighth consciousness — as stated before. In this way, when carefully investigated, it becomes necessarily clear that the differences in perception among the four classes of beings (*shishu*, 四趣) arise from the image-divisions of the eighth consciousness's objects.

### [Lower Section]

As for the four categories of "common" and so forth: this (case of water/fire) belongs to the category of "uncommon among the common" (*kyō-chū-fukyō*, 共中不共). Things such as raging fire and so on are originally external-container (*geiki*, 外器) dharmas that are commonly transformed, yet because their use and enjoyment (*juyō*, 受用) varies according to individual karma (*betsugō*, 別業), they properly belong to that category.

In general, the physical world-container (*kikai*, 器界) is transformed by self and other sentient beings, and these transformations merge to appear as a single thing; what is accommodated and what accommodates mutually constitute causes and effects. Dharmas of cause-and-effect are necessarily neither identical (*soku*, 即) nor separate (*ri*, 離). It is necessarily said: "If (cause and effect) were definitively different, they could not have the nature of cause and effect"; and it is determined: "Because the natural cause and effect (*hōni-inga*, 法爾因果) are neither identical nor separate." Therefore, taking a single tree as an example: all the transformations of the eighth consciousnesses of all sentient beings within the same realm are all present within this single tree — because this is how it is in the desire realm among desire-realm beings, and likewise up through the higher realms. In this way, when we measure the transformations of sentient beings, although they merge to appear as a single thing, there are differences in each being's individual transformation. Though different, they are not definitively different; yet neither are they definitively one. The correlation between the objects transformed by the eighth consciousnesses of self and others is already like this. How much more so is it the case that within one's own object-sphere (*jisō*, 自相), the original basis and the shadow-image (*nimitta-bhāga*) are neither identical nor separate; and how much more so that within a single mind, the perceiving subject (*nōen*, 能緣) and the perceived object (*shoen*, 所縁) are neither identical nor separate. It is mutually so in all cases. In this way, within a single cluster (*isshu*, 一聚), the mind-king and mental factors stand in correlation; within a single body, the eight consciousnesses stand in correlation — all are without exception neither identical nor separate. There is not a single dharma that has a fixed, real characteristic (*jōjitsu no sō*, 定実の相). Why is this? Because every dharma arises from the discriminations of one's own mind. [Note: That the principles of habituation (*kunshū*, 熏習) and so on are necessarily so has been set forth in detail above.] It is like the objects encountered in a dream.

Moreover, as each such pair stands in correlation — all mutually serving as cause and effect for one another, all without exception neither identical nor separate — according to the event and the characteristic, there are degrees of proximity (*shinso*, 親疎) and of principal and derivative (*honmatsu*, 本末). The correlation between self and other sentient beings is the most remote — this is the coarsest condition (*araken*, 麤縁) among the dominant conditions (*zōjōen*, 増上緣). Next, when dharmas within a single body stand in correlation, there are, as appropriate, differences among the four conditions (*shien*, 四縁), among which the cause-and-effect of the direct cause (*shininen*, 親因縁) is the most proximate. The remaining layers of correlations, one by one, can be understood by analogy. This is because, even though (all things are) fictitious (*koke*, 虚仮), the positions and stages of the fictitious are not disordered, and the ten thousand dharmas (*banpō*, 万法) exist in vivid clarity.

## Left Page (Zoku Hossō-shū Shoshin Ryakuyō, Lower Volume — Section 11)

### [Upper Section]

This too is because (everything) arises from the discriminations of one's own mind. To state it in essence: because (everything) arises from the discriminations of one's own mind, there are no fixed characteristics (*jōsō*, 定相); and because (everything) arises from the discriminations of one's own mind, (things) do not fall into mutual confusion. If there were not such layered and manifold differences — if all things were simply and uniformly identical (*sōsoku*, 相即) — then there would conversely be fixed characteristics. How then could one speak of "all dharmas being like a dream"? The principle of mind-only (*vijñaptimātratā-yukti*, 唯識道理) is truly deep and wondrous.

In this way, like an illusion (*nyogē*, 如幻), like a dream (*nyomu*, 如夢): existence and nonexistence, identity and difference, simultaneous and non-simultaneous — all such manifold characteristics (*shūsō*, 衆相) are entirely without any fixed, real nature (*jōjitsu*, 定実). The nature (*shō*, 性) of all this is precisely *tathatā* (真如, suchness/true-thusness). When this *tathatā* is explained through verbal expression (*shōsen*, 詮), it is the natural principle (*hōni no dōri*, 法爾道理) of all dharmas arising dependently and being generated through dependent origination — a fictitious reality like an illusion. [Note: Regarding the relationship between *tathatā* as principle and as phenomenon, see what follows below.] Now, this ultimate truth (*shinri*, 真理) and the phenomenal characteristics of conditioned existence (*ue no jisō*, 有為事相) are mutually neither identical nor separate. And within that ultimate truth itself, there is also the quality of being neither identical nor separate.

This being so, in the *paramārtha* (勝義, ultimate) gate of our school (Hossō-shū), within all instances of "neither identical nor separate": where phenomena (*ji*, 事) stand in correlation and are neither identical nor separate, "non-identity" (*fusoku*, 不即) is primary — because phenomena are many. Where principles (*ri*, 理) stand in correlation and are neither identical nor separate, "non-separation" (*furi*, 不離) is primary — because principle is of one flavor (*ichimi*, 一味). Where phenomena and principle stand in correlation and are neither identical nor separate, they are equal with no primary or derivative — because characteristic (*sō*, 相) and nature (*shō*, 性) are mutually dependent.

The nature (of all dharmas) is unconditioned (*muī*, 無為); the characteristic (of all dharmas) has no fixed form. Though there is arising and ceasing, there is no real arising and ceasing; though there is no real arising and ceasing, it is not the case that there is no fictitious arising and ceasing. "Fictitious" means having no fixed reality; having no fixed reality means being without (fixed) characteristic (*musō*, 無相). Being without characteristic in nature is precisely the ultimate truth. When differentiated, arising-and-ceasing and non-arising-non-ceasing are distinct. When seen as interpenetrating, being without characteristic and "having no fixed characteristic" ultimately return to one. What wise person could, with respect to this, definitively assert either identity or separation?

Therefore, in the *Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra* (*Gejinmikkyō*, 解深密経), the Buddha himself, regarding the views of "absolute mutual identity" (*ikkō sōsoku*, 一向相即) and "absolute mutual separateness" (*ikkō kakubetsu*, 一向各別), raised the faults of the seed (*shūji*, 種子) [Note: The commentary suggests "子" may be a scribal error for "種"] and rejected both. He took "not one, not different" (*fu'ichi fu'i*, 不一不異) as the true and wondrous principle.

In this way, for one who, by means of the principle that "the mind is like a dream" (*yuishin nyomu no dōri*, 唯心如夢道理), has come fully to know that self and other, mind and object, cause and effect, and so on are all neither identical nor separate:

### [Lower Section]

"Mind, Buddha, and sentient beings — all are without distinction"; "The three realms are only one mind; outside the mind there is no separate dharma; mind, Buddha, and sentient beings — these three are without distinction" — this is precisely what is meant. This is because one has learned that there is no fixed, real distinction (*jōjitsu no sabetsu*, 定実差別).

"Establishing the two kinds of no-self (*futari no muga*, 二無我)" is likewise the same doctrinal gateway. Because there is no real self or other (*jitsu no ga ta*, 実我他), there are no fixed, real dharmas.

"Establishing the one vehicle (*ichijō*, 一乗)" is likewise the same gateway. All the noble ones (*ārya*, 聖者) of the three vehicles are all within this one wondrous purity. Because (they) attain the fruit at different depths within this (one principle), (the teaching of the five natures (*gosei*, 五性) is established,) and since all sentient beings of the five natures are nothing but fictitious distinctions within dharmas that are like an illusion, one should reflect deeply upon this.

For one who has fully come to understand the principle that the objects commonly transformed by self and other sentient beings are neither identical nor separate: the meaning that among the things that the transforming mind (*nōhen-shin*, 能変心) fictitiously produces, there is no fixed distinction between self and other (*hishi ni jōjitsu no betsu nashi*, 彼此無定別) — this naturally becomes evident. For one who has fully come to understand the meaning that self and other, mind and object, are without fixed distinction: the wondrous meaning of "the three without distinction" (*san-mu-sabetsu*, 三無差別) naturally becomes evident. For one who has fully manifested the meaning of "the three without distinction": the one vehicle and the five natures are seen to be in no conflict whatsoever. Know truly: among all the gates of the wondrous principle of mind-only (*vijñaptimātratā*, 唯識妙理), this is foremost. Through universal contemplation and careful examination, mind-only is supreme. (The teaching that) gradual awakening and sudden awakening, the one vehicle and the Mahāyāna, exist because they do not rely upon explaining this deep principle — this may well be the intent (of this passage).

Furthermore, the *Chéngwéishì lùn* (*Vijñaptimātratāsiddhi-śāstra*, 唯識論) employs the metaphor of lamps (*tōmyō no tatoe*, 燈明の喩え) to illustrate the commonly transformed objects. That is: when many lamps are placed within a single room, each lamp's light pervades and fills the entire room; it is difficult to distinguish which light comes from which lamp, they do not obstruct one another, and together they appear as a single light. Yet they are not definitively one (since they each arise from separate lamps), nor are they definitively separate (since they illuminate the room together). The mountains, seas, grasses, trees, and so forth transformed by self and other sentient beings can be understood by means of this metaphor.
