### The Two Truths (*Nigi*, 二諦)

Our school (the Hossō school) establishes the **Four-Layered Two Truths** (*shijū-nigi*, 四重二諦). These are the Fourfold Conventional Truth (*sezoku-shijū*, 世俗四重) and the Fourfold Ultimate Truth (*shōgi-shijū*, 勝義四重).

The **Fourfold Conventional Truth** is as follows: (1) "Mundane conventional truth" (*seken-sezoku*, 世間世俗) — things such as jars and bowls; (2) "Rational conventional truth" (*dōri-sezoku*, 道理世俗) — things such as the five aggregates (*skandha*, 五蘊), twelve bases (*āyatana*, 十二処), and eighteen elements (*dhātu*, 十八界); (3) "Attained-through-realization conventional truth" (*shōtoku-sezoku*, 証得世俗) — the Four Noble Truths (*catvāry āryasatyāni*, 四諦) of suffering, origination, cessation, and the path; (4) "Ultimate conventional truth" (*shōgi-sezoku*, 勝義世俗) — the two-fold emptiness-suchness (*ni-kū-shinnyo*, 二空真如).

The **Fourfold Ultimate Truth** is as follows: (1) "Mundane ultimate truth" (*seken-shōgi*, 世間勝義) — the three categories (*sankа*, 三科) such as the five aggregates; (2) "Rational ultimate truth" (*dōri-shōgi*, 道理勝義) — the Four Noble Truths such as suffering; (3) "Attained-through-realization ultimate truth" (*shōtoku-shōgi*, 証得勝義) — the two-fold emptiness-suchness; (4) "Ultimate-of-the-ultimate truth" (*shōgi-shōgi*, 勝義勝義) — the principle of the one true *dharmadhātu* (*ichijin-hōkai*, 一真法界).

Now this establishment and dissolution [of the two truths] is explained by combining all three vehicles (*triyāna*, 三乗). (One should consult the commentaries.) If we speak only with regard to the bodhisattva: (1) Real self, real dharmas, jars, armies, forests, etc. constitute the first conventional truth; (2) The ten skillful means (*daśakuśalāni*, 十善巧) such as the three categories and the Four Noble Truths constitute the second conventional / first ultimate; (3) The principles of the three natures (*trisvabhāva*, 三性), the three non-natures (*trinaihsvābhāvya*, 無性), and the Vijñaptimātratā (*yuishiki*, 唯識) constitute the third conventional / second ultimate; (4) The two-fold emptiness-suchness constitutes the fourth conventional / third ultimate; (5) The principle of the one true *dharmadhātu* of the twofold emptiness constitutes the fourth ultimate. Therefore, when dharmas are discussed, there are five layers.

Since the ultimate and the conventional mutually depend upon each other [Note: "形" (*katachi*) may be a scribal error for "依" (*yoru*, "depend upon")], when they are laid out in relation to one another, this amounts to eight layers. That is to say: the ultimate is ultimate only in dependence upon the conventional; the conventional is conventional only in dependence upon the ultimate. The "ultimate" is the inner reality (*jitsu*, 實) of a thing. If there were no "thing," whose reality would it be? Therefore, the ultimate is an ultimate that has the conventional as its home. The "conventional" is a conventional fiction (*ke*, 仮) upon reality. If there were no "reality" [Note: "者" (*mono*) may be missing after "実" in the text], whose fiction would it be? Therefore, the conventional is a conventional that has the ultimate as its home. Thus, within conventional phenomena (*zokujи*, 俗事) there is invariably ultimate principle (*shingiri*, 真理), and within ultimate principle there is invariably conventional phenomena. If one is lacking, the other is necessarily lost as well. No dharma can stand alone independently. This is called the "gate of the mutual dependence of the ultimate and the conventional" (*shinjoku-sōe-no-hōmon*, 真俗相依法門).

### [Lower Section]

In this way, taking the first conventional as a basis, the second ultimate is established; taking the first ultimate as a basis, the first conventional is established. That is to say: the three categories, the Four Noble Truths, etc., in relation to the imagined constructs of armies and forests, have a somewhat deeper principle — and even the first ultimate is like this; how much more so the other two layers! Therefore, taking the first conventional as a basis, four ultimates are established in total.

Next, taking the second conventional as a basis, the second ultimate is established; and taking the second ultimate as a basis, the second conventional is established. That is, the three natures etc., in relation to the three categories etc., have a progressively deeper principle — and even the second ultimate is like this; how much more so the latter two layers! Therefore, taking the second conventional as a basis, three ultimates are established in total.

Next, taking the third conventional as a basis, the third ultimate is established; and taking the third ultimate as a basis, the third conventional is established. That is, the two-fold emptiness-suchness, in relation to the three natures etc., has a still deeper principle — and even the third ultimate is like this; how much more so the latter one layer! Therefore, taking the third conventional as a basis, two ultimates are established in total.

Next, taking the fourth conventional as a basis, the fourth ultimate is established; and taking the fourth ultimate as a basis, the fourth conventional is established. That is, the principle of the one true *dharmadhātu*, being of the deepest principle in relation to the two-fold emptiness-suchness, — taking the fourth conventional as a basis, the last one ultimate is established.

Now among these five layers of dharmas: the first layer is solely conventional and not ultimate; the fourth layer is solely ultimate (and also conventional); the three intermediate layers are both ultimate and conventional. The above is the discussion proceeding from shallow to deep. If one proceeds from deep to shallow: taking the fourth ultimate as a basis, four conventionals are established in total; taking the four conventionals as a basis, only one ultimate is established. The formula "four conventionals, one ultimate" (*shisoku-ichishin*, 四俗一真) found in the root text belongs to this approach. The rest should be understood by analogy.

Now these eight layers are bundled into **four-layered mutual contrasts** (*shijū-sōtai*, 四重相対):
(1) **Contrast of existence and non-existence** (*umu-sōtai*, 有無相対): the first conventional corresponds to "non-existence," since real self and real dharmas have no substantial nature. From the three categories up to the "one reality" (*ichijitsu*, 一実) — these are called "phenomenally existent" (*sōu*, 相有), since the dependent and perfected natures (*paratantra* and *pariniṣpanna*, 依円二性) have substance.
(2) **Contrast of phenomena and principle** (*jiri-sōtai*, 事理相対): this is discussed within the foregoing "existent." The five aggregates etc. are "phenomena" (*ji*, 事) — as they are the phenomena that constitute the substance of dharmas. From the Four Noble Truths etc.

...up to "one reality" are "nature/principle" (*shō*, 性) — as they are the deep principle of dharmas.
(3) **Contrast of shallow and deep** (*senshindepth-sōtai*, 浅深相対): this is opened within the foregoing "principle." The Four Noble Truths etc. are "shallow" — their principle is somewhat shallow. The twofold emptiness and one reality are "deep" — their principle is still deeper.
(4) **Contrast of expression and import** (*senshu-sōtai*, 詮旨相対) [Note: "自" may be a scribal error for "旨"]: this is opened within the foregoing (deep). The two-fold emptiness-suchness is "expression" (*sen*, 詮) — because it reveals the nature of dharmas (*dharmatā*, 法性) by way of the gate of the two forms of selflessness. The one reality is "import" (*shi*, 旨) — because both mental activity and verbal expression are exhausted, and it directly points to the nature of dharmas.

To take the example of a single "blue color" and pursue its ultimate reality: there are four layers. Taking it as a real existent — this is the appearance arising from one's subjective projection, and this is the first conventional. (Since its substantial nature is entirely absent, we will not discuss it here.) That blue color [seen] as illusory is the second conventional / first ultimate. The principle of causation within that color is the third conventional / second ultimate. The *tathatā* (suchness) of the twofold selflessness within that principle is the fourth conventional / third ultimate. Within that suchness, setting aside expression and speaking of import — this is the fourth ultimate. All other dharmas follow the same pattern.

Turning toward a single sentient being: we take the apparent one to be real; taking that person's form, scent, etc. to be real corresponds respectively to real self and real dharmas. This is the first conventional. That illusory collection of five aggregates is the second conventional / first ultimate. The remaining layers follow the same pattern.

These five layers, when bundled together, can be mapped onto the **three natures** (*trisvabhāva*): the first conventional is *parikalpita* (the imagined/imputedly-constructed nature, 遍計所執). The three categories, the Four Noble Truths, etc. are *paratantra* (the dependent nature, 依他起性) [*Note:* unconditioned dharmas and the two-fold emptiness-suchness are provisionally taken as *paratantra* in terms of consciousness-transformation]. The one reality-principle is *pariniṣpanna* (the perfected nature, 円成実性). Alternatively, they can be bundled into three: **phenomena** (*ji*), **principle** (*ri*), and **suchness** (*nyo*). The five aggregates etc. are "phenomena"; suffering and origination etc. are "principle"; one reality is "suchness." As for the two-fold emptiness-suchness: when discussing it in terms of consciousness-transformation (*shikihен*, 識変), it is "principle"; when speaking of its original substance (*honjichi*, 本質), it is "suchness."

### The Three Natures of the Clusters (*isshu*, 聚) of the Eight Consciousnesses

The single cluster of the eighth consciousness, in the causal stage (*imii*, 因位), is entirely and solely neutral and unobscured (*anivṛtāvyākṛta*, 無覆無記) — specifically, the neutral maturation (*vipāka*, 異熟無記). It does not extend to any other nature (good or evil). The single cluster of the seventh consciousness, in the causal stage, is entirely and solely obscured-neutral (*nivṛtāvyākṛta*, 有覆無記). It does not extend to any other nature. The single cluster of the sixth consciousness extends through all three natures (wholesome, unwholesome, and neutral).

### [Lower Section]

At times the entire cluster [of the sixth consciousness] is obscured; at other times the entire cluster is unobscured. Among the unobscured, the four types of neutral [karma] should also be understood by analogy. Each individual cluster of the five sense consciousnesses likewise extends through the three natures and should be understood by analogy with the sixth consciousness. However, within the unobscured, minds of deportment (*igirojin*, 威儀路心) and the like probably cannot exist [in the five sense consciousnesses].

In general, the mind (*citta*) and mental factors (*caitasika*) of a single cluster must invariably be of the same nature. The statement "all associated dharmas must be of the same nature" and the expression "actions and functions must invariably be the same" refer to precisely this. All unobscured (*anāsrava*) minds and mental factors are entirely wholesome.

---

### Correspondence of the Eight Consciousnesses' Clusters with Attachment

The clusters of the five [sense] consciousnesses and the eighth consciousness are entirely free from attachment (*grāha*, 執) at all times. The single cluster of the seventh consciousness invariably has attachment at all times — except in the unobscured (*anāsrava*) [state]. The sixth consciousness sometimes has attachment and sometimes does not. All unobscured [states] are free from attachment.

---

### Presence and Absence of the Eight Consciousnesses Across the Nine Temporal Stages (*kyūji*, 九時) and Grounds (*chii*, 地)

The seventh and eighth consciousnesses are present in sentient beings of all nine grounds of the three realms (*triloka*, 三界). Their associated mental factors are likewise invariant. The sixth consciousness is also present in sentient beings of all nine grounds of the three realms. However, regarding the associated mental factors, there are differences according to location. Among the eleven wholesome mental factors, the one factor of *pīti* / "mental pliancy" (*kyōan*, 軽安) is absent in the desire realm (*kāmadhātu*, 欲界). Among the six afflictions (*mūlakleśa*, 六煩悩), the affliction of "aversion" (*pratigha*, 瞋) is absent in the upper realms (the form realm and formless realm). Among the secondary afflictions (*upakleśa*, 随煩悩), the seven minor secondary [afflictions] [Note: excluding *śāṭhya* (dissimulation, 諂), *māyā* (deception, 誑), and *mada* (conceit, 憍)] and the two intermediate secondary [afflictions] are absent in all three realms. Of the minor secondary [afflictions], *māyā* (誑, deception) and *śāṭhya* (諂, dissimulation) are absent in the formless realm (*ārūpyadhātu*, 無色界). [Note: *māyā* and *śāṭhya* extend into the form realm — perhaps up through the first *dhyāna*.]
