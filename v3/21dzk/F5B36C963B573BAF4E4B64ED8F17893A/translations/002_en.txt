The commentary's "regarding conventional and ultimate truth... successively generating acceptance and rejection" refers to different masters of Mahāyāna. The conventional gate teaches existence; the ultimate gate teaches complete emptiness, etc. There are also teachings such as "existing objects, no mind," etc.

The commentary's "causing correct dharma meaning... briefly praising the middle way" expresses the commentator's vow to make the dharma endure long and benefit sentient beings. If one does not know the truth and falsehood of dharma, correct dharma will be hidden and disappear, which is not dharma remaining. By clearly distinguishing and abandoning the false to take the true, correct dharma endures long. Benefiting sentient beings—this meaning is clear.

The commentary's "first according to what was received" refers to what was received from the former master Xuanzang Tripiṭaka, hence "predecessor." "Briefly praising the middle way": ordinary beings cling to the extreme of desire and pleasure; non-Buddhists cling to the extreme of ascetic practices; Hīnayāna clings to dharma-existence; Mahāyāna clings to complete emptiness. Therefore it describes abandoning the extremes of existence-bias and emptiness-bias, and praising the middle way of neither-empty-nor-existent.

"As in the Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra... not a foothold for various debates" is to praise the middle way by citing texts that explain the middle way, making known the aspects of middle and extremes.

"The Buddha, based on parikalpita... the three non-natures" refers to the Buddha explaining the three non-natures based on the three natures. Because it blocks parikalpita, it says "based on parikalpita." Parikalpita has absolutely no substantial nature, hence "naturelessness of characteristics." Paratantra has no natural arising, hence "naturelessness of arising." The ultimate reality of pariniṣpanna has nothing to reveal regarding self and dharmas, hence "naturelessness of ultimate meaning." Having thus explained the three non-natures, this presents the origin of explaining the three-period teaching and revealing middle and extremes.

"At that time Paramārthasamutpāda... foothold for debates" refers to the first-period teaching. The Buddha first explained the doctrine of no-self but existing dharmas at Deer Park. This is the teachings of the Āgamas, etc.—the Hīnayāna teaching. The two truths of suffering and origination are worldly cause and effect. The two truths of cessation and path are transcendental cause and effect. Being previously unheard dharma, it is called "wonderful." Because it does not reveal that self and dharmas are non-existent and that emptiness and consciousness are non-void, it is called "provisional meaning." Though dharmas are neither empty nor existent, because it teaches existence one-sidedly, debates about emptiness arise. Hence it is called "a foothold for debates." "Foothold" means a place where debates do not depart but establish footing.

"Next, again solely for... foothold for debates" refers to the second-period teaching. The Buddha at Vulture Peak explained the doctrine of complete emptiness for those heading toward Mahāyāna. This is the Prajñāpāramitā sūtras, etc. Because it teaches dharma-emptiness, it is Mahāyāna teaching. However, because it does not yet explain what dharmas this emptiness empties, it is called "concealed wheel" and "provisional meaning." The words "further," "still," and "also" can be understood in meaning.

"Now to inspire... not a foothold for debates" refers to the third-period teaching. In the Buddha's third period, he explained the ultimate Mahāyāna definitive teachings such as the Saṃdhinirmocana and Lotus sūtras. "All vehicles" means the five vehicles. Empty parikalpita is called "non-existent"; existing paratantra and pariniṣpanna are called "non-empty." Thus there is nothing concealed, hence "manifest wheel." In the first period's existence-teaching, there is the superiority of the second period's emptiness-teaching, so there is something above existence. In today's third-period teaching, there is no dharma that can surpass this, hence "unsurpassed." The first two period teachings are incomplete so have room for more. "The Golden Light Sūtra... named Turning, Illuminating, Upholding": Generally regarding the teachings of one Buddha-lifetime, the different explanations of various schools are not uniform. Some speak of one period; some establish four or five periods. However, there are no texts for one, two, three, four, or five-period teachings. Our school's three periods is not merely the ultimate definitive teaching of the Saṃdhinirmocana Sūtra. Because the Golden Light Sūtra's explanation presents the doctrine of three periods, it is cited again as proof. The three dharmas are the three of existence, emptiness, and middle. The first period turns the wheel of four truths, hence "turning." The second period illuminates existence based on emptiness, hence "illuminating." The third period upholds both existence and emptiness, hence "upholding." Therefore in this text, turning and illuminating are extremes; upholding is middle. Hence to block extremes and reveal the middle, these texts are cited.

"The wheel is named for crushing and destroying, having both negation and affirmation, therefore the dharmas of three periods are all called wheels": The first period affirms the substantial existence of paratantra and pariniṣpanna; the second period negates the non-substantial parikalpita; the third has both negation and affirmation complete. Therefore, singly and doubly having both negation and affirmation complete, it is named "turning (wheel)." That wheel-king's wheel-treasure, when the great king travels, levels the road to make the great king's passage easy. That is, crushing and destroying mountains and stones, leveling and governing roads. Leaving things that should exist—this is called affirmation. Removing things that should not exist—this is called negation. This is a metaphor for the meaning of negating the conceived and affirming the dependent and perfect.

they become confused about objects and give rise to delusion, create karma and receive suffering results. Therefore the Buddha realized suchness and awakened to objects being like illusions. Observing the faculties and capacities of sentient beings, within dharma beyond language, he manifestly explains using verbal explanations suited to capacities. Making sentient beings attain middle way true characteristics like myself—this is the Buddha's fundamental intention.

"Therefore there is a verse... no-self, not-self" is text from the Madhyamaka (third chapter). This text reveals the doctrine of manifestly explaining dharma beyond language through language. This is text explaining the middle way. The third line raises the two natures of dependent and perfect; the fourth line explains the meaning of these two natures being beyond language. The first two lines mean establishing verbal explanations according to capacities. In the long prose of that treatise it says: "Thus all Buddhas, observing sentient beings with omniscience, explain variously for them. They also explain the existence of self, or explain no-self. For those whose minds are not yet mature, who do not yet have nirvana-potential and do not know to fear sin, for these they explain the existence of self. Also there are those who attain the path, who know all dharmas are empty and nominally designate the existence of self—for these, explaining self is without fault."

"Other sūtras again explain... each attaining understanding, etc." is text from the first fascicle of the Vimalakīrti Sūtra. The Buddha's virtuous powers are sovereign—with one sound he expounds limitless meanings. Though making sentient beings understand according to their types, it is not that in reality there is only one sound without giving rise to other words. The "one-sound teaching" establishes this principle, which is called "incorrect doctrine." Citing this text is to reveal the meaning of all dharmas being beyond language. If dharmas were definitively either empty or existent, how could they be explained with one sound? Because dharmas are neither empty nor existent, speaking of existence does not contradict the dharma-essence; speaking of emptiness does not contradict the dharma-essence. Therefore, to prove the middle way, this text is cited.
