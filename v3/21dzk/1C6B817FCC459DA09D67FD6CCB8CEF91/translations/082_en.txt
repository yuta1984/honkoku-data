He took tonsure under Vinaya Master Honnyoei. His nature was virtuous and he loved learning. He went to the Southern Capital (Nara) and received the Bodhisattva precepts from National Master Jūtatsu and received full ordination from Vinaya Master Myōchi. Subsequently he resided at Kaidan-in and made the propagation of Vinaya his mission. He also expounded Kegon teachings. Seekers of the Way from the four directions gathered, rubbing shoulders and linking sleeves. He demonstrated extinction at Shōmyō-ji Temple on August 15th of the third year of Eiwa (1377). His writings include "Ritsu Kōyō-den" in ten volumes, "Kaidan Keizu Tsūshōki" in five volumes, "Gokyōshō-shō" in eight volumes, "Gokyō Dan'waku Bunsai-shō" in two volumes, "Gokyōgi Kaishū" in thirty-two volumes, and "Kishinron-shō" in twelve volumes.

Biography of Three Vinaya Masters: Kakuryō, Sesshin, and Shōchi
Vinaya Master Kakuryō's religious name was Fujō. He presided over Gokuraku-ji Temple and spread transformative teachings. He passed away on November 28th of the first year of Kōryaku (1379). Vinaya Master Sesshin's religious name was Sōyū, with the courtesy name Tsūshiki. He received instruction from Master Shōtsū-ha. His talent and knowledge were brilliant and keen, and he well understood Vinaya. He resided at both Ryūge and Kaidan-in temples, and his reputation spread throughout the world. He entered extinction on April 21st of the third year of Shitoku (1386). His lifetime writings were very extensive. Vinaya Master Shōchi's religious name was Sōshin. He was also a disciple of Master Ha. He first emerged into the world at Kaidan-in, later presiding over Kumeda-ji Temple. His transformative influence extended comprehensively to the four directions. He passed away on February 21st of a certain year.

Biography of Two Vinaya Masters: Kakujō and Tsūshō
Vinaya Master Kakujō's religious name was Yūzon. Vinaya Master Tsūshō's religious name was Reiken. Both were disciples of Vinaya Master Sesshin-yū. Jō presided over both Kaidan and Ryūge temples and passed away on May 11th of the 29th year of Ōei (1422). Shō resided at Kumeda-ji Temple and later moved to Kaidan-in. He departed for the divine Pure Land on March 21st of the 30th year of Ōei (1423).

Biography of National Master Fūichi of Kaidan-in
The National Master's religious name was Shigyoku, with the courtesy name Sōen. He was a descendant of imperial lineage. At age seven, he took tonsure under Vinaya Master Sesshin-yū at Shōmyō-ji Temple. He also followed Vinaya Master Kakujō to receive full ordination. Hearing lectures on the Three Great Works, he was able to comprehend the principles of what is permitted and prohibited. He also studied Kegon and extended to other schools. In the 24th year of Ōei (1417), he sailed across the sea and entered China. This corresponded to the 15th year of Yongle of the Great Ming. The following year, Emperor Chengzu of the Great Ming summoned him into the imperial palace to lecture on the Avatamsaka Sutra. When the master moved his lips once, it was like toppling the Three Gorges. The imperial mood was greatly pleased, and he was granted the title National Master Fūichi. At that time, there was no one who surpassed the master in Kegon studies. Therefore even veteran masters and old monks were in awe of him. The master remained in China for five years. Later he returned to his home country, bringing back many sutras and dharma implements. He resided at Kaidan-in and constantly lectured on Kegon in the Birushana Hall. Scholars yearned for him and flowed abundantly like water rushing toward marshland. Emperor Shōkō esteemed his religious style and again granted him the title of National Master. Everyone considered it an honor. Later he resided at Shōmyō-ji and transferred to Gokuraku-ji. He also served as abbot at various temples including Mida-ji, Kegon-ji, Takayama-ji, and Yashima-ji. Because of this, monastics and laypeople, men and women, looked up to him like a true dharma banner. The master once received a request to offer incense for the deceased. When the ceremony was finished and he was returning, he visited the Kasuga Shrine. Suddenly thunder roared with lightning, and fierce winds broke trees. The shrine guardians all fled in fear. At that time, the deity emerged from the hall and said to the master: "I most abhor the defilement of the dead. Since you have already been involved in such matters, why do you come here?" The master replied: "I have just realized that all sentient beings are originally enlightened. Birth, death, and nirvana are like yesterday's dream. Does the deity not contemplate this?" The deity appreciated his words, composed a waka poem, and disappeared. On September 6th of the fourth year of Kanshō (1463), he demonstrated extinction at Takayama-ji Temple in Umeo. He lived eighty-one years with an unspecified number of years as a monk. His disciples included Fubun and several others. Zen Master Zuikei-hō also once received instruction from the master.

Praise: The National Master emerged from the imperial bloodline and ascended to the dharma king's field. The light of precepts blazed like candles, and the water of concentration was clear and pure. His thoughts entered profound clarity, and his conduct transcended the ordinary. Therefore, from court ministers above to common people below, none failed to turn toward his style with delight. Even the Chinese emperor granted him an honorific title. Alas, one like the National Master - how often could such be seen in an age?

Biography of Vinaya Master Kinkai of Zen'nō-ji Temple
The Vinaya Master's name was Kinkai, a person of the capital. His family name is not recorded. His mother worried about having no heir and vowed to visit Inari Myōjin Shrine for one hundred days of prayer. She did not cease even in wind and rain. On the night when the hundred days were completed, she dreamed that the deity told her: "I grant you a wise son." Thus the master was born. When the master grew up, he went to Zen'nō-ji Temple, met Vinaya Master Gekkai-jō, and cut his hair. However, he was dissolute and lazy, not following the precept standards. One day his mother grasped his hand and wept, saying: "I hate your rough conduct and can do nothing about it. I only fear that people will say the deity spoke falsely." The master hung his head and sweated. From this point he suddenly reformed himself, maintained precepts with severe precision, and did not eat after noon. He practiced ascetic disciplines with firm determination that could not be moved. Because of this, near and far praised his virtue, and his reputation reached the imperial court. Later Emperor Go-Nara issued an edict summoning him to the imperial palace to receive precept teachings. By special imperial command, he opened dharma teachings at Senyū-ji. He entered extinction on April 12th of the 24th year of Tenbun (1555).

Biography of Vinaya Master Zōji-sō of Denkō-ji Temple in the Southern Capital
Vinaya Master Zōji was from the Imagawa clan of Suruga Province. His religious name was Sensō. In his youth he left lay life at Mount Kezō. He received full ordination from Vinaya Master Kōhan at Shōdai-ji Temple. He devoted his heart to Vinaya studies while also engaging with esoteric teachings. During certain years, he received orders to preside over Senyū-ji Temple. Emperor Eiroku summoned him to the imperial quarters to receive the great Bodhisattva precepts. From then on, he was constantly summoned to lecture in the imperial palace. The great patron, Yamashiro Province Governor Tsutsui Junkei, founded Denkō-ji Temple and invited the master to preside over it. During breaks from military duties, the lord would frequently enter the temple to inquire about the Way, increasingly growing in delight. In his later years he responded to an invitation to the seat at Shōdai-ji Temple and lectured on the Nanzan commentaries and sub-commentaries. Students gathered like ants. The master's appearance was ancient and elegant, his nature simple and unpretentious. He governed himself very strictly and did not eat past midday. He constantly chanted the sacred name of Maitreya, hoping to be born in the Inner Court. On May 18th of the 16th year of Tenshō (1588), he chanted his extinction at Senyū-ji Temple at age seventy-one. His chief disciple was Vinaya Master Shōchin.

Biography of Vinaya Master Jisen-shō of Raigō-in
The Vinaya Master's name was Myōnenshō, with the courtesy name Shunpo and the title Jisen. His clan and village are not detailed. Both his understanding and practice were elevated, and his reputation was well known. He once presided over Senyū-ji and also resided at Raigō-in. Many monastics and laypeople turned to him in reverence. The imperial court esteemed his virtuous reputation and once issued an edict conferring precept teachings. Generalissimo Nobunaga also turned to him and frequently came to inquire about dharma. His being valued by royalty and ministers was like this. The master's advancing cultivation was extremely diligent - for forty years he practiced esoteric methods daily. Even during seasonal changes and invasions of illness, he never once became lax or lazy. On New Year's Day of the 13th year of Keichō (1608), after receiving the ceremonial greetings of the great assembly, he suddenly passed away.
