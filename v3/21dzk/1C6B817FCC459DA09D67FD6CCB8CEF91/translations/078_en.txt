**Biography of Vinaya Master Dōgetsu Nen**
The vinaya master's religious name was Shōnen, his courtesy name Dōgetsu. He left home and received full ordination under Vinaya Master Shingan Hen. He mastered vinaya under Vinaya Master Enritsu Gen and received esoteric teachings from Vinaya Master Chūdō Shu. Extending to the Hossō, Sanron, and Tendai schools, he achieved mastery to the very roots. Through this his reputation stirred court and countryside. When Vinaya Master Hen renovated Yakushi-ji in Yamato, he followed to assist. During the Shōō era, when Vinaya Master Shu passed away, the master received invitation to succeed at Shingon-in. He constantly expounded the profound teachings, and scholars came to him. He passed away on the 15th day of the 8th month of the 1st year of Shōwa (1312).

The encomium states: He received full ordination from Shingan Hen, studied the permitted and prohibited from Enritsu Gen, and received esoteric teachings from Chūdō Shu. All three were persons of high virtue and excellent conduct, extraordinary people. That this master was able to serve all of them personally - is this not remarkable?

**Biography of the Two Vinaya Masters Ryōjaku and Ritsuju**
Vinaya Master Ryōjaku's religious name was Enshō. Vinaya Master Ritsuju's religious name was Shinjō. Both emerged from the gate of Vinaya Master Enritsu and were truly Dharma grandsons of Great Compassion Bodhisattva (Kōshō Bodhisattva). They were proficient in the precepts and vinaya, savored various schools comprehensively, and their reputations blazed brightly. Shō resided at Shōdai-ji as the fifth generation. He was praised in the world for his lecturing ability. Scholars of the time all joyfully came to rely on him. After Shō, Jō succeeded to his position.

**Biography of National Master Jikan of Nanto Tōdai-ji**
The national master's religious name was Gyōnen, Jikan was his title. He was a son of the Fujiwara clan of Takahashi in Iyo Province. His mother was of a certain family. The master was born on the 6th day of the 3rd month of the 1st year of Ninji (1240). His natural endowments were brilliant and extraordinary, his spirit and bearing like that of a deity. When he was exactly three years old, he could recite any Buddhist scripture upon hearing it once. Was this not his natural disposition? He left home at eighteen, never being careless in his ordinary conduct and discipline. His study and diligent practice surpassed his peers. At Kōfuku-ji he received full ordination from Vinaya Master Jissōshō. He relied on Tōdai-ji for residence. He went out to study vinaya under Vinaya Master Enritsu Gen of Shōdai-ji. Among all the texts possessed by the school, there was none whose principles he did not master. He also became close to Master Chūdō Shu of Shingon-in and received both Tendai and Shingon esoteric teachings. Suddenly hearing that scholars were transmitting the renowned reputation of Priest-Prince Shūshō, he went directly to rely on him. Shūshō was a great master of the Kegon (Huayan) school. The master received his personal guidance and exhausted his profound teachings. Extending to Zen, Hossō, Sanron and various other schools, he left none unexplored. He also extended to the books of various philosophical schools and the hundred schools of thought. In the 2nd year of Kenji (1276) he lectured on the Huayan at the Rushana Hall of Tōdai-ji. His eloquent commentary was like a flying spring, his voice clear and resonant like heavenly drums. The assembly of listeners was magnificent - nothing could surpass this prosperity. Soon after he succeeded to the seat of Vinaya Master Shō and resided at the Precept Platform, greatly proclaiming vinaya while also propagating the Huayan school. That this school flourished in the world was largely due to the master's efforts. Therefore, those who spoke of Huayan at that time all took the master as their

The encomium states: The National Master's innate nature was pure and excellent.
