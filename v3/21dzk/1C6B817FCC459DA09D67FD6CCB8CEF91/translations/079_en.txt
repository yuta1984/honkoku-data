Biography of Vinaya Master Jūzen
Vinaya Master Jūzen's place of origin is not clearly known. He was a disciple of Vinaya Master Enshō. His will and strength were firm and clear, his intelligence sharp and outstanding among his peers. He once received the full precepts from Bodhisattva Kōshō and studied under Vinaya Master Zenne. Not only was he proficient in the Vinaya, but regarding various schools such as Shingon and Sanron, there was none whose profound teachings he did not master. Later he established himself at Buddhist temples and greatly opened dharma assemblies. His reputation spread in all four directions, and both monastics and laypeople came to revere him.

Biography of Vinaya Master Enkai-ni of Kumeda-ji Temple in Izumi Province
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Zen-ni, with the courtesy name Enkai. He received full ordination from Vinaya Master Enshō. Brilliant and wise, excelling in intelligence, he studied the Vinaya section and thoroughly investigated its ultimate meaning. He became close to National Master Jikan and received the profound teachings of Kegon. His reputation grew day by day. At the request of others, he lectured on the Avatamsaka Sutra and the Three Great Works at the Ordination Platform. The audience was packed. With one wave of his fly-whisk, words and meaning perfectly harmonized. The National Master greatly praised him. In a certain year, he went to Izumi Province and founded a temple compound called Kumeda. He constructed dormitories to house scholars who came carrying their books. He greatly proclaimed the two schools of Vinaya and Kegon. At this point his reputation became even more prominent, like the sun rising from the east. People from the four directions and distant places all looked up to him with reverence. When National Master Kan retired from the Ordination Platform, the master was raised up to preside over that position. He performed the formal procedures seven times and greatly wielded his transformative authority. One day he showed slight illness, but continued his religious practices as usual. Looking at his disciples, he said, "My departure from this human world is not far off." After three days and nights, he passed away peacefully. This was actually on January 8th of the second year of Shōchū (1325). His worldly age was seventy-three, with fifty years in the dharma. Zen Master Genmyō Kyokushun once composed praise extolling his virtue.

Biography of Vinaya Master Dōshō-kai, Founding Master of Kakuon-ji Temple in Sagami Province
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Chikai, courtesy name Shin'e, with the alternate title Dōshō. He studied the Vinaya under Vinaya Master Sōtō and also received esoteric teachings, obtaining the profound teachings of various lineages including Ono-ryū and Hirosawa-ryū. He also once received transmission of the universal reception method from Bodhisattva Ninshō. In the fourth year of Einin (1296), great patron Hōjō Sadatoki founded Kakuon-ji Temple on Mount Shubu and invited the master to serve as its founding patriarch. Monks with begging bowls gathered in great numbers, covering the area like a vast furnace. The master's reputation in the Way spread like thunder and rose like a whirlwind. He once painted an image of the Sacred Immovable One with his own hand and performed the Eight Thousand Tablet Ritual for over fifty sessions. That image still exists today.

Biography of Vinaya Master Kaku'ichi-a of Senyū-ji Temple
The Vinaya Master's name was Kaku-a, with the title Kaku'ichi. His hometown and family are unknown. He severed the worldly net and directly entered the monastic garden. After receiving the precepts, he studied under Vinaya Masters Chikyō and Jōin. His studies in the sectarian teachings were extensive. He also received transmission of the universal reception method from Bodhisattva Ninshō. Elegantly admiring the Pure Land teachings, he went to Nishi-Santani in Sanuki and met Dharma Master Kakushin. Shin was a dharma-grandson of Master Genkū of Kurodani. The master personally served under him and mastered the Pure Land teachings and Abhidharmakośa. Later he succeeded to Vinaya Master Sōtō's position and resided at Senyū-ji. His transformative influence spread widely and scholars increased daily. He departed this world on August 11th of a certain year. His lifespan is not detailed. Vinaya Master Kotsukotsu-gen succeeded in residing at the main temple, being accomplished in both Vinaya and Yogācāra schools.

Biography of Vinaya Master Ryōshin-mu
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Honmu, courtesy name Ryōshin. He served Master Kūchi and was a dharma-grandson of Vinaya Master Jissō-shō. His learning and practice were mutually integrated, and his reputation flew in all four directions on wings. He emerged at the Ordination Platform and lectured on the Vinaya. Scholars heard of his reputation and came, overflowing the hall. On October 3rd of the first year of Gentoku (1329), he passed away at Daishō Chikurin-ji Temple. His lifespan has not been investigated. His disciples included Kakugyō-gen and several others.

Biography of National Master Jūtatsu of the Ordination Platform
The National Master's religious name was Shunsai, with Jūtatsu as his courtesy name. He was a dharma successor of National Master Jikan. He left home while young, and his intelligence surpassed his peers. In his twentieth year, he ascended the platform and received full ordination. Regarding the two schools of Vinaya and Kegon, he thoroughly explored their profundities. He also studied under Vinaya Master Dōgetsu-nen and gained understanding of the three mysteries. Initially he opened teachings at Shingon-in, then succeeded at Daitsū-ji in the capital. His reputation spread to the four corners and his name reached the nine-fold palace. Emperor Go-Daigo observed the courtesy of a disciple, received the Great Bodhisattva Precepts, and bestowed upon him the title of National Master. In a certain year he emerged at the Ordination Platform of the Southern Capital. Both monastics and laypeople all revered his virtue. Later he traveled teaching in the eastern provinces and finally announced his passing at Shōmyōritsu-ji Temple. This was on October 2nd of the second year of Bunwa (1353). He wore the cut-and-sewn robes for seventy-six years and enjoyed a bodily lifespan of ninety-five years. His ordained disciples included Enjō and several others. The master once wrote the "Gokyō-shō Essential Text Collection" in thirty-two volumes.

Biographies of Vinaya Masters Gyōkai and Nyokū
Vinaya Master Gyōkai's religious name was Jōsen, and he was a disciple of Priest Jishin of Saidai-ji. He received full ordination in the fifth year of Shōō (1292) at age twenty. He was accomplished in understanding the meaning of the Vinaya. In early Shōwa, when he heard Jishin lecture on the "Bonmō Ancient Traces Record," he immediately gave review lectures for the assembly. Master Nyokū-shin recorded as he listened and thus completed the "Supplementary Notes" in ten volumes. The master's writings include "Sanju Jōkai Tsūju Sange Sanju Jōkai Yoji-shō" and several other volumes. He passed away on September 21st of a certain year. Vinaya Master Nyokū's religious name was Eishin, and he was a dharma-brother of Master Gyōkai. In the second year of Enkyō (1309), at age twenty-one, he received the full precepts. Through the excellence of his Vinaya scholarship, he achieved high reputation in the world. His writings include "Gyōji-shō Shiran-ketsu," "Hyōmuhyō-shō Kengyō-shō," "Bosatsukai Dōgi-shō" and several other volumes. He departed this world on October 6th of a certain year.

Praise: These two masters emerged from the school of Priest Jishin like autumn chrysanthemums and spring orchids, each excelling in their own beauty. People praise them to this day. The only regret is that their biographical details are not fully known. However, seeing their written works is sufficient to know the profound learning of these two masters. Ah, having such a furnace, one has such dharma vessels!

Biography of Vinaya Master Hon'nyo-ei
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Tan'ei, with the title Hon'nyo. From his youth he possessed extraordinary mind and far-reaching insight. He left home and entered the school of Vinaya Master Enkai-ni at the Ordination Platform, studying Vinaya and Kegon. Among Ni's disciples, those who could obtain the profound teachings were only the master and Seiyō. He emerged at Shōmyōritsu-ji Temple in Kamakura, lecturing and promoting the sectarian teachings, never showing weariness in the assemblies. Those who came to his gate to receive instruction were extremely numerous, all magnificent dragons and elephants. The master's later death is unknown as to time period. His writings include "Kyōri-shō" and "Sanshaku" among several volumes.
