Biography of Vinaya Master Shōen of Shōdai-ji Temple
The Vinaya Master's name was Shōen. His family lineage and birthplace are not detailed. His character was brilliant and keen, and he never tired of learning. He had a distinguished reputation among monastics and laypeople. In the second year of Genkō (1322), he received full ordination according to the Tsūju-hō method. During the Karyaku period (1326-1329), he studied the Brahmā Net Sutra under the two great virtuous masters Kakuya and Kakue, especially achieving subtle understanding and reaching profound depths in the ancient commentaries of Daiken. There were no other Vinaya texts that he could not masterfully interpret. He constantly grieved that after the two great masters Daiji and Kōshō, Vinaya studies had become dormant, and he secretly harbored aspirations for their revival. Therefore he made writing his work, greatly supplementing the sectarian principles. He composed commentaries on the "Three Great Works" called "Shigyō," "Keiī," and "Kenen," totaling sixty-five volumes. He began writing in the eighth month of the second year of Rekiō (1339) and completed the manuscript in the eighth month of the fifth year of Jōwa (1349). It took approximately eleven years to complete. During this period, despite fierce wind and snow and scorching sunlight, he never slackened even slightly. He also wrote the "Bonmō Koseki Jutsuji-shō" in five volumes, all of which became works studied by scholars. During the Kōan years (1361-1362), he opened lecture seats at Shōdai-ji Temple, and monastics and laypeople flocked there. The master's final whereabouts are unknown.

Biography of Vinaya Master Enjō
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Shōi, with the courtesy name Enjō. He took tonsure under National Master Jūtatsu. Rich in learning and elevated in the Way, he succeeded Vinaya Master Kakugyō and resided at both Gokuraku-ji and Kaidan-in temples. He constantly lectured on Kegon and the Three Great Works. Because of this, his reputation flowed to the four distant regions and he was admired by the worthies of his time. He returned to truth at Gokuraku-ji Temple on August 22nd of the first year of Ōan (1368).

Biography of Vinaya Master Jōshin-e
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Shōe, with the courtesy name Jōshin. He served Vinaya Master Kakugyō and possessed keen talents. He mastered sutras and Vinaya. During the Bunwa years (1352-1356), he traveled to various regions and extensively visited teachers. Upon returning to his country, he relied on Vinaya Master Seiyū at Kumeda-ji Temple. Not long after, Yū passed away. The master administered temple affairs and also resided at Kaidan-in. He promoted the two schools of Vinaya and Kegon while also spreading esoteric teachings. He passed away at Zenpōritsu-ji Temple in Yawata on November 2nd of the fourth year of Ōan (1371). His lifespan is not detailed.

Biography of Vinaya Master Seirender of Anrakukō-in
The Vinaya Master's name was Seirender. He was a distant descendant of Left Minister Fuyutsugu. His lay name was Toshitsune. His innate nature was pure and clean. Though his position was among the court ranks, his aspirations yearned for the Buddha Dharma. Finally, together with his younger brother Toshiari, though remaining laymen, they became monks. With virtue jar, ceremonial bowl, they deeply protected the roots of precepts. Subsequently they traveled like clouds to various places, extensively knocked on famous virtuous masters, and explored the profundities of all schools as easily as pointing to their palms. Because of this, their reputation emerged brilliantly. During the Enbun years (1356-1361), Anrakukō-in suffered greatly from the Maiba Rebellion and became severely ruined. The master, receiving orders from Kōgimon-in, strove to restore it. Before long, it surpassed its former state. The master resided there and widely proclaimed the sectarian teachings, with monastics and laypeople turning toward him. In his later years he retired from affairs and earnestly cultivated Pure Land practices without negligence. At the time of death his spirit and thoughts were not confused, and he passed away sitting upright. This was truly on May 25th of the first year of Eiwa (1375). His age and years as a monk are not specified. The temple was initially in the capital, but during the Kan'ei years (1624-1644), Master Chōgen moved it to Higashiyama.

Praise: I have heard that all worldly affairs can be falsified, but only in the transformation of death and life is no falsification tolerated. Now when Vinaya Master Ren faced his departure, he was calm and composed like this - thus what he had cultivated throughout his life can be known.

Biography of Vinaya Master Kōenshu of Gokuraku-in in the Southern Capital
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Dōshu, with the courtesy name Kōen. His place of origin is unknown. He studied the precepts and Vinaya, comprehensively grasping their profound mysteries. His fragrant style and excellent virtue were revered by people of his time. At the beginning of Ōan (1368), he presided over Gokuraku-in in the Southern Capital, spreading the Vinaya style and shaking the court and countryside. All Vinaya practitioners at Gokuraku-ji attributed their achievements to the master. His final whereabouts are not detailed. After the master, Jūnen, Dōki, Genshō, Inkai, Genjun, and Junen all successively resided at the main temple and carried on his work through generations.

Biography of Vinaya Master Chikugan-kō, Founding Master of Unryū-in
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Shōkō, with the courtesy name Chikugan. He was dharma heir to Vinaya Master Setsu[■]chin. A person of the capital, he came from the official Fujiwara clan. He was born in the first year of Shōchū, year of the Wood Rat (1324). From childhood he was intelligent and wise, with aspirations beyond worldly things. He took tonsure at seventeen and received full ordination at twenty-three. He devoted his attention to Vinaya, thoroughly mastering what is permitted and prohibited. He also studied esoteric teachings, investigating the sources of the Ono and Hirosawa lineages. He emerged into the world at Senyū-ji in the capital, and the gate's style greatly flourished. The entire community changed their perspective. Retired Emperor Bunwa esteemed his virtue and issued an edict conferring precept teachings. Because of this, his reputation shone brilliantly throughout China and Japan. He once selected land and founded Vinaya temples called Ryūge and Unryū, thus becoming the founding ancestor of both temples. He greatly bestowed dharma rain, and regardless of whether plants were tall or short, all received nourishment. Later Emperor Goen'yū also added deep reverence and respect. He once invited the master to receive precepts, maintaining the etiquette of a disciple. He also personally visited the temple to inquire about the dharma. The imperial favor was generous, and the mountains and rivers increased their beauty because of it. In the first year of Kōō (1389), receiving imperial edict, he used mineral and plant-based brushes to copy the Lotus Sutra and also performed repentance rituals. The copied sutras were stored in a pagoda - the so-called Dharma-according Sutras. The emperor granted several hectares of estates to fund the sutra copying. The imperial edict briefly stated: "The Dharma-according Sutras have extremely deep merit that cannot be conceived. Therefore I command Superior Shōkō to practice according to the dharma every year. After my death, this should be performed on my memorial day to benefit my posthumous fortune." Thus he granted Ikawa Estate in Harima Province to fund these expenses. Therefore, to this day, this is performed every year in the fourth month at the main temple. The emperor later requested the master to complete a portrait. Emperor Go-Komatsu, because the master was revered by both emperors, especially added imperial favor, proclaiming him precept master and bestowing imperial calligraphy. The flourishing of the master's virtue and the Way can thus be understood. On June 27th of the ninth year of Ōei (1402), he passed away without illness. His worldly age was seventy-nine, with fifty-six years as a monk. The master once composed a self-eulogy saying: "Original nature is the enlightened moon, fundamentally perfect from the beginning. Tiger skin, sheep substance - who can manifest true sincerity?" His writings include "Nanzan Kyōkan Myōmoku" in two volumes, circulated among scholars.

Praise: The elder Shōkō, over a hundred years after National Master Shōbō, was able to revive the traditional regulations without losing the family dharma. His reputation was heard among people, his Way illuminated his generation. He became imperial teacher to three courts and founding ancestor of two temples. Ah, he was truly a thunderingly great person of our sectarian gate!
