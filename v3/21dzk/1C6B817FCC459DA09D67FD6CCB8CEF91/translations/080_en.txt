Biography of Vinaya Master Mujin-dō
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Nyodō, courtesy name Kenren, with the title Mujin. His family name was Fujiwara, and he was the son of the governor of Hōki Province. Born extraordinary, his bearing and appearance were imposing. During his prime years, he set a period of one thousand days, and every day went barefoot to worship at the Kitano Tenman Daijizai Shrine, praying for divine protection. As the period was about to end, a heavenly child appeared and gave him pine needles, telling him: "Take these and cultivate them throughout the sixty-odd provinces." When the master returned and told this to a monk, the monk said: "Ah, you are fortunate! Could this not be a sign that in the future you will be able to provide shelter for all living beings?" Soon he went to Chion-in Temple and took tonsure. At nineteen, while traveling and crossing the Ōi River, he said with deep emotion: "This world is an impure land. This body is an impure vessel. There is nothing to enjoy even for an instant. It would be better to quickly abandon body and land and seek rebirth in a pure place." So he embraced a stone and threw himself into the water, but felt something under his feet lifting him out. The master exerted all his strength to jump down but still could not drown. So he wept and returned to his father's house, staying awake all night. He traveled to the Kansai region and stayed at Anraku-ji Temple, practicing intensively for three years. He then received novice precepts at Anyō-ji Temple in Chikugo Province. He was exactly twenty-one years old at the time. After residing there for seven years, he returned to the capital. The master elegantly admired the virtue of Great Master Chōshō and constantly said: "I should devote myself to the moksha." He received full ordination from Priest Kotsukotsu-gen of Senyū-ji Temple in the east, and studied under Vinaya Master Ryōchi for eight years with devoted reverence. There was no profound teaching in the Vinaya section that he did not thoroughly understand. He also transmitted the Tendai Perfect and Sudden Great Precepts and extended to Pure Land teachings. Master Chi exclaimed: "I never expected to obtain this 'Vinaya tiger' as I approach old age!" Later he went to Hiden-in Temple and received platform initiation under Elder Myōgen, studying esoteric teachings. He especially reached the profoundest depths in the Ono lineage. After a long time, he retreated to Kintai-ji Temple and cultivated nembutsu samadhi, not resting his side against his mat for several years. In his later years, he opened lectures at Zenkō-ji Temple in Shinano Province. With one wave of his speaking staff, the four assemblies followed like clouds, like the return of Pūrṇa. As he approached old age, he returned to the capital and resided at Eien-ji Temple. Jōrin-en Kannon-ji Temple and Chōnichi-zan Shōgon-in Temple were also places he presided over. Once he went to Tenryū-ji Temple and met National Master Musō, first presenting his understanding, then inquiring about the meaning beyond teachings. The National Master confirmed this, saying: "Outside teachings there is no Zen; outside Zen there are no teachings. As you see it, what more can I say?" Because of this, his reputation became even more far-reaching. Later he went to Aki Province and founded Kōsetsu-in Temple, residing there. After six years he returned to the capital and preached dharma for his mother at Hongan-ji Temple for forty-eight days. Monastics and laypeople rushed to the hall, and those who came late had no space even to place their knees. Suddenly the time arrived for his transformative affinities in other realms, and he passed away at Kannon-ji Temple. This was on May 27th of the second year of Enbun (1357). His worldly lifespan was seventy-four, with forty-eight years as a monk. The master usually resided at over ten temples and founded or restored fifteen temples. He also pitied women whose karmic bonds of sin were deep and heavy, establishing several dozen nunneries and ordaining many disciples. The number of monastics, laypeople, men and women who heard the dharma and returned to sincerity is also unknown. During the Ōei period, Vinaya Master Rennin of Kannon-ji Temple, fearing that the master's excellent conduct would gradually fade into obscurity, requested Zen Master Kaizō-jō to compose a biographical account and transmit it to the world.

Praise: The Way of the Great Hero has myriad differences, but reaching the ultimate, it is one. How can one exclusively guard one gate and claim to have exhausted all good and beauty? Someone like this master not only thoroughly understood his own school, but resolved Zen questions with Musō, transmitted esoteric teachings through Myōgen, and researched all schools including Tendai and Pure Land without omission. He completely swept away the crude practice where each school sectarianizes its school and each faction partisanizes its faction, mutually contradicting each other—was he not one who exhausted both beauty and goodness?

Biography of Vinaya Master Hikoshō-san of Byakugō-ji Temple in the Southern Capital
The Vinaya Master's name was Seisan, courtesy name Hikoshō. His place of origin is unknown. His ocean of learning was vast, and his reputation arose with wings in all four directions. He opened dharma teachings at Byakugō-ji Temple in the Southern Capital and was revered by monastics and laypeople from all directions. In the spring of the second month of the second year of Karyaku (1327), he was repairing the Southern Round Hall. While making ornamental implements, he gathered much red gold, and there was one lump of copper. When smelting it as before, the master found it strange and had the metalworkers break it open. Inside was a copper lotus flower. Within the flower were over a thousand relics, whose brilliant light dazzled people's eyes. Everyone considered this a miraculous event and reported it to the court. The emperor was moved to endless admiration. During the Enbun period, he presided over Saidai-ji Temple, residing for four years and greatly promoting Vinaya teachings. He passed away on November 14th of the first year of Jōji (1362). He was seventy-five years old. Throughout his life he loved writing and assisted the sectarian teachings. His works including "Sanshū Kōgi," "Sanbō Kōgi," "Bonmōkyō Koseki Kōgi," "Bosatsukai Kōgi," "Sanju Kōgi," "Jihan Kōgi," "Shiyaku Kōgi," "Kaitai-shō Kōgi" and several other volumes are circulated in the world.

Biography of Vinaya Master Bokuai-jun of Senyū-ji Temple
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Shijun, courtesy name Bokuai. He received dharma from Vinaya Master Daitō-chi. His innate nature was intelligent and perceptive, his eloquence sharp and quick—truly a fierce dragon of the Vinaya ocean. He studied the Vinaya Pitaka and thoroughly understood its profound meaning. He also obtained the profundities of esoteric teachings. Initially he opened transformative teachings at Seijō Kongō-ji Temple on Kegon-zan in Sagami, also presided over Kakuon-ji Temple on Shubu-zan, and later presided over Senyū-ji Temple in the capital. He raised the red banner of Daitō, and those who came to receive Vinaya teachings rushed like clouds and gathered like flowing rivers. The beauty of his reputation reached the imperial court. He received imperial edict to enter the palace and lecture on the precept sutras. The imperial heart was greatly pleased. The master passed away on August 16th of a certain year. His lifespan was eighty-six. He once lectured on the Three Great Works several times and composed several volumes of commentaries, making the doubtful passages and profound meanings of ancient masters clearly evident. He also wrote "Jōshin Kaikan-shō," which together serve as models for scholars.

Biography of Vinaya Master Kakugyō-gen
The Vinaya Master's religious name was Shōgen, courtesy name Kakugyō. He was a disciple of Vinaya Master Honmu. He studied the precepts and Vinaya under National Master Jūtatsu and deeply obtained their meaning. He also received esoteric and Yogācāra teachings and studied Kegon on the side. In the fourth year of Kōei (1345), he gathered supporters and built the Fragrant Kitchen at Tōdai-ji Temple. Later he received an invitation to preside over Gokuraku-ji Temple in Kamakura and also received orders from the Ordination Platform. After residing for two years, the sectarian style greatly flourished. Finally he departed at Daitsū-ji Temple in the capital. This was on June 5th of the third year of Enbun (1358). His reported age was fifty-eight.
