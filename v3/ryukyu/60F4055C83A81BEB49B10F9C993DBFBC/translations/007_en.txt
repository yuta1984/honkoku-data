All graves are made by digging holes in mountains. After burial, they pile up stones. Noble families polish stones into square shapes. They also build worship platforms and tomb gates. Viewed from afar, they look like bridge gates. Furthermore, some dig holes in mountains for burial, located on top of layered cliffs.

When sweeping graves, they do not set out sacrificial dishes and vegetables. They use wooden plates, incense, and fruits, hang banana fans, and set up three boards beside the grave. Sometimes they pick flowers to offer in front of the tomb.

Men and women never use the same eating utensils. Each sets up separate utensils and eats separately. Leftover food is discarded. When drinking with guests, they do not set up separate utensils for each person. They drink in turn from one cup. One pair of chopsticks is placed between the vessels for common use. Now when their high officials receive guests, they also imitate China's method of using the same vessels while dividing chopsticks for eating and drinking. However, in their daily life they still follow the old customs. The Record of Xia Ziyang states: When residing in office and discussing matters, one must prepare two jugs of wine and go to that person's house, kneel and pour it. After pouring, one announces and considers the matter concluded.

Shaving the Hair on Top of the Head

During the former Ming dynasty, Ryukyu people did not shave their hair. They simply did not use hair nets. During the Wanli period, the investiture envoy Xie Xingren Jie was from Changle in Fujian. A certain maternal uncle followed the mission, carrying several hundred hair nets. When they arrived, there was nothing they could sell. Envoy Xie delayed the investiture ceremony, and the ritual was not performed for a long time. He said that since this country already wore Chinese caps and sashes, if on the day of investiture any of the attending ministers did not wear hair nets, the investiture ceremony would not be conducted. The Ryukyu people competed to buy them, clearing out the entire market. In Fujian, even today when mocking those who force sales in the market, they say "Ryukyu people wearing hair nets."

When it came to this dynasty, they began to shave the hair on top of the head. From the king on down, all follow the current system. They leave the outer hair in a circle and tie a small topknot in the exact center of the crown. There is no difference between people from Shuri and Kume. The Record of Xia Ziyang states that Shuri people wore their topknots to one side, while Kume people wore their topknots in the center. Now this is not the case.
