Writing Brushes
Brushes are made using deer hair. The short shafts are relatively shorter compared to Chinese brushes. They are barely over four sun in length. The bamboo shafts resemble reeds. They value their lightness and fineness. At the end of the shaft there is a small tag with characters. All brush caps have small openings. When the people of this country write, they never lean on desks. They stand or sit, roll up the end of the paper backwards, clamp and hold it in their left palm, and write with their wrist suspended in the air. When dipping the brush in ink, they lay the brush horizontally and rotate it.

Paper
Paper is made from cocoons. It has fiber, and the firm white type is extremely good quality. The yellow, coarse-textured type is called "jiyi paper." All are cut into square sheets for use. It is exactly the same as Korean cocoon paper. The thick type can be dyed purple and made into clothing. It is called "neiyong paper." Those with printed patterns are like brocade and extremely lovely.

Agricultural Tools
Hoes and plows all follow Chinese models. However, they are made lighter and smaller. High fields rely only on rainwater. Lower fields are arranged in terraces with springs channeled down for irrigation. Those whose rivers and lakes connect with tidal flows are all saline and cannot be used for irrigation. Therefore, there are no tools such as shadoofs or water scoops.

Weaving Tools
The loom's shape has a narrow seat with a wide exterior. It is one shaku, five or six sun in height. The legs are low, only about three or four sun. A bamboo pole is erected in front of the loom and hangs down, pulling the reed up and down. The shuttle is over four sun long, shaped like a soap pod. The tools are light and small, convenient for sitting on the ground to work. Every household has them. They twist banana fiber and mix it with various threads to weave.
