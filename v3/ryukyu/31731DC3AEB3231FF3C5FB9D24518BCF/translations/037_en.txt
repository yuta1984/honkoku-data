　Children of hereditary officials are called "satonoko." This means "prince." The local name is Satodo-nushi
　《Annotation: In local pronunciation, the two characters Satodo are read like "sato," the character nu like "no," and the character shi like "ko"》. Young ones become naishi▢rō, named "sato-
　noko kōkyū naieki shin-jijū." When they pass fifteen years of age and reach eighteen, they shave the top of their heads,
　tie their hair in small topknots, no longer enter the palace, are granted land and become satonoko-pechin, gradually advancing to
　ōyakata.
　　This is the beginning of bureaucratic family children entering government service.
　　Kume village consists entirely of the thirty-six surname families granted registration from Fujian Province. Among their excellent children,
　three or four aged fifteen or sixteen are selected to become scholars (shūsai). The thirteen or fourteen who are not selected are
　named "young scholars" (waka-shūsai) and engage in reading and learning characters. These scholars are tested every year in the twelfth month,
　given topics from the Four Books and made to compose one poem of either eight or four lines. Those who succeed are recorded in the register and
　promoted to vice-interpreter (fuku-tsūji). From this they gradually advance to Shikin-taifu《Annotation: Shikin-taifu are also called ōyakata》.
　　This is the beginning of Kume children entering government service.
　　Fiefs《Annotation: Stipends》
　Royal uncles, grandsons of kings, and meritorious former ministers are all granted one or two fu as fiefs. Taifu
　and below who have merit, and those of the thirty-six surnames with hereditary positions, are all granted one county as a fief. Initially
