Preface
Ryukyu is merely a small island nation in the distant South Seas. Although it combines several dozen large and small islands into one domain, it is fundamentally insufficient to constitute an independent state and has traditionally been a tributary of our Imperial nation. Since the Ming Dynasty, it has sent envoys to the Chinese mainland, received investiture from China, and assumed the title of King of Chūzan. When sending envoys to China, it followed the Chinese calendar; when paying tribute to our country, it used our era names. This situation of one nation belonging to two countries leaves unclear which nation it truly serves as a tributary state. For this reason, there are often those who question the propriety of its titles and appellations. I wish to present ten pieces of evidence to clarify this matter. Regarding the nation of Ryukyu: if we examine its topography, it is clearly an extension of our Kyushu mountain range that undulates and extends into the South Seas.
