○The Beginning of Travel to Japan
The "Ryukyu Jiryaku" states: In the third year of Hōtoku during the reign of Emperor Go-Hanazono, in the seventh month, Ryukyu people came and presented one thousand kan of coins and local products (hōbutsu) to Shogun Yoshimasa. From this time, the people of that country came to Hyōgo Bay to trade. 《marginal note: as stated》Upon consideration, this was during the time when the fifteenth king, Shō Kinpuku, was on the throne. From then, four imperial reigns passed 《marginal note: Go-Hanazono, Go-Tsuchimikado, Go-Kashiwabara, Go-Nara》spanning one hundred twenty-three years《marginal note: n》, until during the reign of Emperor Ōgimachi, in the eleventh year of Genki, Ryukyu people came and presented local products.
Since Satsuma Province was a neighboring country, they maintained deep friendly relations, and using ships called "aya-bune," they sent gifts annually. However, during the Keichō era, a Sanshikan (high official) of that country named Jana conspired with the Ming Dynasty and persuaded the king to stop travel to Japan. Therefore, the lord of Satsuma, Shimazu Mutsu-no-kami Iehisa, sent an envoy to inquire about the reason. When Jana showed various forms of disrespect toward the envoy, Yoshihisa became greatly enraged. In the same thirteenth year, he went to Sunpu, had an audience with the Divine Ruler (Tokugawa Ieyasu), and requested permission to send troops for punitive action.
The Divine Ruler issued an imperial command that he would leave the matter to Yoshihisa's discretion. In the following year, February, he sent several hundred warships to attack. The various warriors distinguished themselves in the assault, and in April of the same year, they stormed into Shuri, captured King Shō Nei, and returned in triumph.
