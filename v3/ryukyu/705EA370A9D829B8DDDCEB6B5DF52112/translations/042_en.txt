For example, the village name "tomari" and the "tomari" in "moored boat" are both read as "tsuchi-ma-i," making one character with three sounds. The village name "Kyan" is read as the character "kou," which is three characters with one sound. The national language has many such examples. The people's speech also very often reads five or six characters as one or two characters. When they obtain many Chinese books, they mostly use hooks, marks, and marginal notes to read each phrase in reverse, placing substantive words above and function words below, reading backwards. Speech is also like this. In domestic official documents, they also mix in one or two Chinese characters, with national characters above and below. At the end of the forty-seven characters, there is one character that makes the two-dot sound "ma." This is a separate character used as a symbol to connect various sounds. Together they make forty-eight characters.

Yuan dynasty scholar Tao Zongyi said: When the Ryukyu Kingdom presented tribute to China, the memorials they submitted used wood tablets about eight inches high, three bu thick, and five bu wide, decorated with lacquer, fastened with tin, threaded with leather, and characters were carved horizontally on them. The character style was tadpole script. He also said: Within Japan there are native characters, with forty-seven character elements, and if one can understand these thoroughly, one can comprehend their sounds and meanings. Where they connect to form characters resembles Mongolian script methods. When their native script is used to copy Chinese poetry and prose, though it cannot be read [by Chinese], the brush movement is dynamic in all directions like flying dragons and snakes, solemnly preserving the legacy of Dian and Su. Now Ryukyu Kingdom's memorials and documents all use Chinese writing. The horizontal carved characters in tadpole script that Tao mentioned may have been their character style before they mastered Chinese, but this cannot be verified today.
