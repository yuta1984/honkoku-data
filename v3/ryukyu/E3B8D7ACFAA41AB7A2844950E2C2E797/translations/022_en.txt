Writing Brushes
Brushes are made using deer hair, with short handles that are considerably shorter compared to Chinese brushes, measuring only about four inches in length. The bamboo shaft resembles reed and is chosen for its lightness. At the end of the shaft, there is a small tag with local characters. All brush caps have small openings. When the people of this country write, they do not lean on desks; whether standing or sitting, they roll up the end of the paper, clamp it with their left hand, hold it steady in their palm, and write with a suspended wrist. When dipping the brush in ink, they hold the brush horizontally and roll it while lying flat.

Paper
Paper is made from cocoons. Those with fine texture that are firm and white are of excellent quality. The yellow, loose-textured ones are called "shigi paper." All are cut into square sheets for use, exactly the same as Korean cocoon paper. The thick-quality ones are dyed purple and can be made into clothing, called "naiyō paper." Those with printed patterns are extremely beautiful, like brocade.

Farming Tools
Hoes and plows all imitate Chinese designs but are made lighter and smaller. High fields rely only on rainwater, while lower fields are arranged in tiers with spring water channeled down for irrigation. Where rivers and lakes connect to tidal waters, all are saline and cannot be used for irrigation, so there are no shadoof or water-lifting tools.

Weaving Tools
The loom is shaped with a narrow sitting area that widens outward, standing one foot five to six inches high, with low legs of only three to four inches. In front of the loom stands a single bamboo pole that hangs down and is pulled to move up and down. The shuttle is over four inches long, shaped like a soap pod. The equipment is light and small, convenient for sitting on the ground. Every household has these, spinning banana fiber and weaving it together in various combinations.
