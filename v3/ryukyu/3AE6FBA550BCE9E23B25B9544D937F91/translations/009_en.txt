Only the Kerama Islands can be seen in the waves.

Naminoue is located on the seashore of Wakasa village. There is a cliff several dozen fathoms high, which poets call Takenoko-gai (Bamboo Shoot Cliff) because of its resemblance to that shape. The top of the cliff is flat, with fine grass and moss spread like a blue carpet. There is a shrine hall where three deities are enshrined, surrounded by stone walls. When one climbs this cliff, to the west the Kerama Islands extend, to the north one can see Zampa Point in Yomitan-zan, and to the southeast the mountain ranges of Nakagami and Shimajiri stretch continuously, providing an absolutely magnificent view. It has been famous as a scenic spot since ancient times.

Tomari Port is located at the northeastern corner of Naha. Although the harbor is narrow and the water shallow, it can still moor several dozen small vessels. In Tomari village there is a large temple called Sōgen-ji, which is the family temple of the Shō clan.

Mankohara is a salt production site, measuring 240 ken from north to south and 80 ken from east to west. The northwestern corner area faces the sea. It is a place where samurai and commoners regularly gather to practice horsemanship.

Local products include kasuri (ikat textiles), salt, lacquerware, tin crafts, cycads, pigs, and so forth.

Shuri is located one ri and eleven chō east of Naha. It was the capital before the establishment of the prefecture. Its area extends to one ri square. To the northeast it intermingles with Urasoe and Nishihara magiri, to the southwest it borders Mawashi magiri, and at the southeastern corner it diagonally adjoins Haebaru magiri.
