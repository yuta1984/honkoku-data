If there is sound but no character, two characters are combined and written using the fanqie method. For example, the village name "tomari" (泊) and "tomari" (泊) for anchoring boats are both read as "tomai" (土馬伊), making one character with three sounds. The village name "Kyan" (喜屋武) is read as the character "qiang" (腔), which is again three characters with one sound. Much of the national language is like this. In the speech of the country's people, there are very many cases where five or six characters are read as one or two characters. When they obtain Chinese books, they often use hooks and flourishes to mark alongside, reading each sentence in reverse. Substantive words (shi zi) are placed above, and function words (xu zi) are inverted below and read backwards. Speech is also like this. In the documents of this country, one or two Chinese characters are also used in combination, with national characters both above and below. At the end of the forty-seven characters, there is one character that makes a two-dot sound "ma." This is a separate character used as a marker to connect various sounds. Together they make forty-eight characters.

Tao Zongyi of the Yuan dynasty said: "When the Ryukyu kingdom presents tribute to China, the memorials they submit use wood to make tablets about eight inches high, three fen thick, and five fen wide, decorated with lacquer, fastened with tin, strung with leather, and carved with characters in horizontal lines. The character forms are in tadpole script." He also said: "Japan has its own national script with forty-seven letter-characters. If one can properly recognize them, one can understand their sound and meaning. Where they are joined together to form characters, they vaguely resemble Mongolian writing methods. When Chinese poetry and prose are written in their national script, though unreadable, the brush strokes move vertically and horizontally like flying dragons and snakes, truly bearing traces of the ancient masters." Now, all of Ryukyu's memorial documents use Chinese writing. What Tao described as horizontal carved characters in tadpole script was perhaps what the script looked like before communication with China was established. This cannot be verified today.
