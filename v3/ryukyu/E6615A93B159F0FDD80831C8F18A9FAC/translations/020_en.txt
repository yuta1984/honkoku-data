Writing Brushes

Writing brushes are made using deer hair, with short handles that are considerably shorter compared to Chinese brushes, measuring only slightly over four sun in length. The bamboo shaft resembles reed, chosen for its lightness. At the end of the shaft there is a small label with foreign characters. All brush caps have small openings. When the people of this country write, they do not lean against desks but stand or sit, rolling the end of the paper backward and gripping it with their left hand in their palm, writing with their wrist suspended in air. When dipping the brush in ink, they hold the brush horizontally and roll it while lying flat.

Paper

Paper is made from cocoons, and those that have grain and are firm and white are extremely fine. The yellow ones of loose quality are called "jiyi paper" and are all cut into square sheets for use, exactly the same as Korean cocoon paper. The thick quality ones are dyed purple and can be made into clothing, called "neiyong paper." Those with printed patterns are extremely lovely, like brocade.

Agricultural Tools

Hoes and plows all imitate those of China but are made lighter and smaller. The upland fields rely only on rain and dew, while the lowland fields are arranged in layers with springs channeled to irrigate from below. Those rivers and lakes that connect with tides are all saline and cannot be used for irrigation, so there are no tools like well-sweeps or water scoops.

Weaving Tools

The loom's shape has a narrow seating area that widens outward, with a height of one shaku five or six sun, and the low legs are only about three or four sun. A bamboo pole stands in front of the loom, with hanging cords pulled to operate the up and down motion. The shuttle is over four sun long and shaped like a gleditsia pod. The implements are light and small, convenient for sitting on mats. Every household has these, spinning banana fiber and weaving it together in various combinations.
