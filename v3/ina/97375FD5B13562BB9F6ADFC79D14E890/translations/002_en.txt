Representatives of eighty-three villages in Shinshu's Ina and Chikuma districts - Kubo Village, Oide Village, Amesawa within Minami-Ono Village, Kita-Ono Village, Matsushima Iwao Village, and Miyaki Village - became involved in a dispute with twenty-four villages regarding the transshipment of chūma cargo. The representatives of these twenty-four villages were Fujiike Village, Furuta Village, and Aida Village, along with wholesale merchants from three locations - Karishikihara, Aida, and Okada - and merchants from Matsumoto town also filed additional lawsuits. Upon examination of these cases, there was no credible evidence to be adopted, and previous rulings from Kanbun 13 (1673), Genroku 7 (1694), Hōei 3 (1706), and Hōreki 9 (1759) were difficult to apply. Since these were matters limited to specific locations and did not extend to general cases, yet parties adopted convenient interpretations, the disputes would not cease. Since chūma transport has been an ancient custom of Shinshu, Construction Magistrate Chief Yonekura Kōnai and Construction Magistrate Takahashi Hachijūhachi were dispatched to record in detail the villages with chūma operations and their transportation routes. They found that Matsumoto town and Iida town had freight wholesalers, and the route between them was called the Ina road. From Iida onward were routes to Owari Nagoya town and the two post stations of Okazaki and Yoshida in Mikawa, or from Matsumoto town via the Nakasendō to Nagoya and Kōfu, or from rural areas to Kuragano post station and other routes on the Hokkoku highway, where Shinshu products were shipped and return cargo horses
