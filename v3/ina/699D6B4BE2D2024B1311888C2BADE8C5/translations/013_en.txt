In exchange for the reward for his military merit, Masayuki and Yukimura's death sentences were commuted. Nobuyuki greatly rejoiced and informed them of this, so father and son left Ueda Castle and fled to Mount Koya. In Keichō 14 (1609), Awa-no-kami Masayuki became a monk and died at age 65. When the Osaka campaigns began, Saemon-no-suke Yukimura brought his son Daisuke into the castle, displayed unprecedented valor, and both father and son died in battle. {{Annotation: The father was 46 years old, the son was 16 years old}} Izu-no-kami Nobuyuki not only saved his father's and brother's lives, but was also granted extensive additional lands at Ueda Castle and ruled together with Numata Castle. {{Annotation: Numata 27,000 koku, Ueda 38,000 koku, additional increase 50,000 koku, totaling 115,000 koku}} When the Osaka campaigns erupted, Nobuyuki and his son served as the Shogun's vanguard to attack the castle, and when war broke out again, his sons and brothers

In Genna 8 (1622), Nobuyuki was transferred to Matsumoto Castle in Shinano Province. {{Annotation: 130,000 koku}} His rule over Numata continued as before. On the last day of the tenth month of Meireki 2 (1656), Nobuyuki retired. His heir Kawachi-no-kami Nobuyoshi died young, so he passed the house to his second son Naiki Nobumasa, {{Annotation: Matsumoto Castle 100,000 koku}} and divided Numata Castle to his eldest grandson Hyōbu-no-tayū. {{Annotation: 30,000 koku}} On the 16th day of the seventh month of Meireki 4 (1658), he became a monk with the name Ittōsai, and died on the 7th day of the tenth month of Kanbun 10 (1670) at age 93. Naiki Nobumasa was Nobuyuki's second son. At age 14, he participated in the Osaka campaigns, and when his elder brother Nobuyoshi died early, he inherited his father's house and ruled Matsumoto Castle.
