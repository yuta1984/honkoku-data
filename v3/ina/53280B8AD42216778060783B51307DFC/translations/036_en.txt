Soon after, his younger brother Hidenari was given Miki Castle and succeeded Hidemasa. In the fifth month of this year, Ōtomo Sahyōe-no-suke Yoshimune was charged with crimes and Bungo Province became a vacant domain, so it was divided among the retainers. Takeda Castle was given to Hidenari, who moved there. {{Marginal note: 74,100 koku}} Later he was granted court rank and made Shuri-dayū. {{Marginal note: His original name was Kohyōe}} In the same fourth year, he crossed to Korea and fought against Ming forces, defeating them. Though he himself was wounded, he distinguished himself and returned to Japan. The Taikō passed away and war broke out between east and west. Shuri-dayū Hidenari, as an ally of Lord Tokugawa,

When he heard that the western forces were defeated at the Battle of Sekigahara, he withdrew from Ōta's castle. Hidenari died at age forty-two on the fourteenth day of the eighth month of Keichō 17. His son Naizen-no-shō Hisamori {{Marginal note: Originally named Hidemasa}} succeeded the house. When the Osaka campaign began, he hurried to besiege the castle. When war arose again, there was no need for him to participate. He died at age sixty on the twenty-ninth day of the third month of Jōō 2. His son Yamashiro-no-kami Hisakiyo succeeded the house, retired on the last day of the fourth month of Kanbun 6, took the tonsure, and took the religious name Nyūzan. His son Sado-no-kami Hisatsune inherited his father's domain.
