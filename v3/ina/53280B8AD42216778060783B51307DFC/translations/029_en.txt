When Sukehei rushed to join Hideyoshi's camp and distinguished himself in battle, Hideyoshi was greatly moved and granted him a domain in Kawachi Province. In the same fifteenth year, when the Kanpaku subjugated the Shimazu, since Itō was a guide for that province, he was attached to Yamato Dainagon Hidernaga and led the vanguard toward Hyūga Province. Hereditary retainers of the Itō gathered from here and there, and before long the forces numbered several thousand men. Shimazu Hyōgo-no-kami Yoshihiro suffered defeat and retreated to Satsuma Province. The Kanpaku's

forces also subjugated Chikuzen and Chikugo, passed through Higo Province, and also attacked Satsuma Province, so the Shimazu brothers finally surrendered and submitted. The Kanpaku declared that the Itō forces were outstanding and immediately granted the original domain of Obi, Soi, Kiyotake and other lands in Hyūga Province, and [Sukehei] came to reside in Obi Castle. When the Korean affair arose and [Japanese forces] crossed over to that country to wage war for about seven years, after the Taikō passed away, on the 11th day of the 1st month of Keichō 4, [Sukehei] was appointed Bungo-no-kami. (According to genealogies, various records describing Sukehei show him sometimes as Yamato-no-kami or sometimes as Minbu-dayū. I think that since this was an ancestral court title, he was initially called Yamato-no-kami, but to avoid [conflict with] the Taikō's younger brother Yamato Dainagon Hidernaga, he used Minbu-dayū, and later became Bungo-no-kami.)
