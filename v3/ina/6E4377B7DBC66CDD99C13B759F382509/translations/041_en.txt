At this time, a dispute arose between Shibata (Katsuie) and Hashiba (Hideyoshi), and the two houses became antagonistic from this point on. Nagahide sided with Hideyoshi. Sanshichi-dono (Oda Nobutaka) had early perceived Hideyoshi's intentions to usurp control of his family's realm and conspired with Katsuie to eliminate Hideyoshi, raising an army. However, he was defeated by Hideyoshi and was ordered to make peace. Nagahide completely aligned himself with Hideyoshi's fortunes. Katsuie was in Echizen Province and became extremely angry when he heard that Hideyoshi was attacking the mansion in Mino, but the heavy snows of the northern provinces made it impossible to deploy troops. He first sent a letter to Nagahide proposing to attack Hideyoshi, but Nagahide did not comply. When dawn broke in the spring of Tenshō 11 (1583), Katsuie mobilized the forces of the Hokuriku region

and advanced into Ōmi Province. Nagahide was at Sakamoto in Ōmi Province at this time, but immediately departed Sakamoto, entered Shizugatake Castle, joined forces with Hideyoshi, and finally destroyed Shibata. Nobutaka had Gifu Castle taken by Hideyoshi and was forced to commit seppuku at Utsumi Bay in Owari. Hideyoshi personally seized control of the realm and, rewarding Nagahide for his meritorious service, granted him not only his original domain of Wakasa but also all of Echizen Province and one district of Kaga Province (said to be one million koku or 700,000 koku. It is said that Nagahide was later appointed Echizen-no-kami, though this does not appear in genealogies). Thereafter, Nagahide moved to reside in Kitanosho Castle in Echizen Province.
