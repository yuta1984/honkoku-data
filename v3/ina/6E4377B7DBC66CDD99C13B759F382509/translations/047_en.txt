To make such a fearsome person an ally without hostages, and then be shot with arrows from behind during battle would be unbearable," they said decisively, and the negotiations had already broken down. Since this situation could not continue, Toshinaga led a large force, pushed through Komatsu Castle, and conquered Daishōji Castle. At this time, it was reported that enemy soldiers had boarded ships and were rowing toward Noto Province, so Toshinaga, feeling uneasy about his home province, turned back from here and again approached Komatsu Castle. Toshinaga, hearing there was fighting in his rear guard, spurred his horse and galloped back. The battle had already ended with the enemy entering the castle. This castle was an ultimate stronghold that did not appear conquerable in a single assault. Moreover, being concerned about his home province, he suppressed his anger and withdrew. The report that the enemy was heading to Noto Province was false—the allies had won the battle in Mino Province, and Lord Tokugawa and his son were hastening to Sekigahara. Word came that Toshinaga should also hurry to join the campaign, so Toshinaga reconciled with Nagashige and they exchanged hostages
