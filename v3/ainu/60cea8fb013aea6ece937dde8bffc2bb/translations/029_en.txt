【Right Folio】
Oku-Ezo (the deeper Ezo lands) is the more remote region, and its shape resembles a half-opened fan. Let us now explain how to distinguish between "Ai" country and "Shamo" country. To distinguish Kuchi-Ezo, the narrowed, pinched part of the gourd shape is called the "Shifuwa Pass," which is a mountain crossing route. The area on the Matsumae side of this mountain pass is called Kuchi-Ezo, and the area farther from Matsumae is called Oku-Ezo. Furthermore, to distinguish "Ai" land from "Shamo" land, this too resembles the shape of a half-opened fan. The pivot (kaname) of the fan represents Matsumae; the exposed ribs of the fan represent "Shamo" land; and the paper surface of the fan (the spread portion) represents "Ai" land. The right corner (tip) corresponds to "Nonashi," and the left corner corresponds to "Sōya." Both of these shapes (the gourd and the fan) should be understood by referring to the illustrations.

【Left Folio】
The western side is called Nishi-Ezo (Western Ezo), and is also referred to as Kami-Ezo (Upper Ezo). The eastern side is called Higashi-Ezo (Eastern Ezo), and is also referred to as Shimo-Ezo (Lower Ezo). Throughout this land, west is universally referred to as "upper" and east as "lower."

　　　　　On the Shape of the Various Islands

The island of Matsumae (Hokkaido) has Ezo indigenous settlements and pathways along its perimeter, but as one ventures into the interior, the land remains undeveloped, and no one yet has detailed knowledge of its high mountains, vast plains, and rivers. Being such a large land, it is thought that there are no individuals who explore it privately. On the northwestern shore there is a place called "Sōya," and approximately ten ri offshore from there lies the island of "Karafuto." That island too is inhabited by indigenous people (Ainu).
