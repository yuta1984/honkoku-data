【Right Folio】
From among the brown bears raised in captivity at the homes of the village elders (*otona*) in each village, one that has grown into a fully mature bear is selected. The elder then faces the bear and explains to it the karmic bonds of cause and effect (*inga innen*), speaking thus: "What great fortune, O my bear—listen well. You are to be offered as a sacrifice to the tutelary deity (*ujigami*) of these people of the frontier. Surely in the next life you shall be reborn as a human being. Therefore, rejoice in this and become a sacrifice with a pure and willing heart." After thus speaking to it, they bind it with rope and lead it into a single dwelling, tying it at the front, back, left, and right. A great crowd gathers and they confine it securely, fitting it with fetters (*kase*), leg shackles (*ashikase*), and other restraints. They erect paper streamers (*nusa*) at its neck and adorn it with spears, swords, naginata, and other various long weapons. Thereafter, the village elder, along with his kinsmen and the respected elders of neighboring villages, gather together, and according to the old or new standing of each family, their places at the assembly are arranged with

【Left Folio】
a strict order of precedence, front and back, high and low, and each takes their designated seat without fail. Here a ceremony of ritual archery (*sharei*) is performed, with each person releasing an arrow in proper sequence—much in the manner of the *hikime* ceremonial archery rite. After this ceremony concludes, the great crowd closes in and beats the bear to death with sticks. Once the killing is complete, various offerings are laid before the bear, and food of a hundred flavors (*hyakumi no onjiki*) such as offered at Buddhist ceremonies is presented, resembling the manner of a *segaki* memorial service for the spirits of the dead. When this ritual is finished, the offerings are shared as a feast with the people of the surrounding villages and hamlets. Afterward, the decorated meat is boiled and roasted, and a great banquet is held with unrefined sake (*doburoku*), with feasting and revelry carrying on throughout the day and night. This is the ritual of the grand ceremony. The native people (*dojin*) call this "*Iyomante*"—which is to say, the Grand Festival (*daisairei*).
