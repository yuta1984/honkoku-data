After the fires at all three locations — Utetsu, Shirakami, and the castle — have been linked in succession, upon seeing the fire at Utetsu extinguished, both Shirakami and the castle simultaneously extinguish their fires. These signal fires exist, presumably owing to the friendly relations between the neighboring domains of Matsumae and Tsugaru, and the lord of Tsugaru domain also makes advance preparations for them. From the lord of Matsumae, the hunter Seihachi is awarded a reward of two *kanmon* in blue-tipped coins (*aosashi*).

　　　　　On the Matter of *Shidai* (the "Private Calendar Day")

　　In Matsumae, there is a custom called *shidai* ("private *ōmisoka*," or private year-end). The strait between Tsugaru's Sotogahama and Matsumae is battered by fierce winds throughout the winter, and crossings are frequently suspended. Being an island domain separated by a rough sea, and situated far from Edo, [it follows that]

[Left page]
　　even when the New Year arrives, the new calendar (*shinreki*) often fails to be delivered in time. It is thought that this custom arose from the difficulty ordinary people in Matsumae face in obtaining the new calendar with ease. In years when the twelfth month of the outgoing year is a "short month" (*shōnoki*) — that is, a month with only twenty-nine days — what is New Year's Day (*Gantan*) in other provinces is still the last day of the year (*ōmisoka*) in Matsumae. This day is locally called *shidai* ("private *ōmisoka*"). Now, in the first month of Tenmei 6, the *hinoe-uma* year [1786], when I set out from Matsumae and proceeded to Ezo territory, upon reaching a place called Mitsutani, I found a man residing there named Abeya Denkichi, a wealthy and prominent merchant of Matsumae. He asked me, saying: "On the afternoon of this year's New Year's Day, the sky suddenly became overcast and dark as night. Because of this, the Ainu (*dojin*, lit. 'native people') raised a great clamor..."
