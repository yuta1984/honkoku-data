This fish has never experienced a poor catch year after year. During the fishing season, they naturally come in schools without missing the proper season. Every year they arrive from about ten days after the spring equinox. If they come three times within approximately twenty days and a good catch is obtained, one's livelihood is abundantly secured until the following year. The arrival of herring is called "kukiru." During the fishing season, from samurai to all people of Matsumae - old and young, men and women, high and low - engage in this work, which is truly no different from the autumn rice harvest. Beginning from the spring equinox, in approximately twenty days they finish harvesting, dry the herring and herring roe respectively, load them as baled goods onto ships, and send them to Esashi. At this time, ships from the northern provinces enter Esashi, establish market prices for herring and herring roe, and

This processing takes until the end of the fourth month, and from the fifth month they turn to kelp harvesting, setting sail to various locations. Kelp does not exist in the western sea routes; kelp harvesting areas extend for forty to fifty ri from the outer seas of Hakodate along the eastern sea routes toward Ezo territory. What is mentioned in the Teikin Ōrai as "Uga kelp" is also harvested in the outer seas of Hakodate - this is specifically the name of that place. Altogether, harvesting kelp is an extremely easy occupation - simply cutting from the sea bottom and spreading it on the beach until it dries. This work ends by the end of the sixth month, and from the seventh month everyone rests together. During the Bon period, people of all ranks mingle together for otori festivals, and the otori is also conducted in the old style.
