From the eighth month, the cold becomes intense, and by the ninth month snow already falls, taking on a winter appearance. Accordingly, each household busies itself with preparations for winter, and until spring there is no work to be done, spending the days within the snow. The climate of the four seasons is not uniform. During spring the lingering cold is severe, and signs of spring are barely visible in the vegetation. Sea winds blow fiercely, even stirring up the snow on the mountains, and even in late spring, every time it rains, sleet-mixed snow falls, resembling the harsh lingering cold of early spring in Edo. From around mid-fourth month, plum and cherry blossoms finally begin to bloom, and in the fifth month hackberry trees, yamabuki, and other flowers all bloom together in unison,

finally taking on the appearance of spring. While samurai do not wear unlined summer garments, people from other places are suited to the season in lined or single-layer clothing. Even during the hottest period of summer, there are no signs of extreme heat, and although there are times in late seventh month when one feels increased heat, it is not unbearably hot all day long. The heat begins from past noon and by evening the cold becomes severe enough that one feels like wearing padded clothing. Even this heat does not last long and transitions directly to cold weather. Compared to the heat in Edo, the four seasons are not uniform. One could say this is a land of three seasons: spring, autumn, and winter.
