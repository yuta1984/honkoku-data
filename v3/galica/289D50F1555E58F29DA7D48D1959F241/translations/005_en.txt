Title: "Preface to the Illustrations of People from Forty-Two Countries"

Generally, those who hear the names of foreign countries should necessarily inquire in detail about what customs and preferences prevail in those lands. If they are good, one should record them to serve as laws for one's own conduct; if they are not good, one should also record them to serve as warnings for oneself. How can one merely ask casually about what the people of those countries are like, what their local products are, and what the geographical position, climate, size, and extent of their lands are, and then stop there?

This illustration originally derives from what the Dutch have transmitted. Presumably, they themselves engaged in trade, or were blown there by winds, or heard reliable accounts. Counting from China to the Land of Tall People, there are forty-one countries. But why do we now call this the "Illustrations of Forty-Two Countries"?
