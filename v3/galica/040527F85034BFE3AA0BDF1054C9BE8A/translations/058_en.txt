【Right Page】
【Heading, boxed section】Spring Orchid (Shunran)
Mountain Orchid (Sanran)
Secluded Orchid (Yūran)
National Fragrance (Kokukō)

【Outside the box】The flower has one bloom per stem. The flower is fragrant, and its fragrance is no different from that of the Cymbidium orchid (Keiran). The color is slightly yellow. The leaves are also the same as those of Keiran. The flower has distinct seed pods, and the color of the pods is white, pale yellow, and blue. The stem has a light blue-purple color. In the Zuo Zhuan (Confucian classic), there is mention of "National Fragrance," where someone dreams of giving an orchid to Yan Ji, a concubine of Duke Wen of Zheng. The orchid has a national fragrance that enchants people's eyes. Flowers bloom in the third month.

【Dividing line】

【Heading, boxed section】Spring Gentian (Haru-rindō) 《Also called Spring Bellflower》

【Outside the box】The flower shape resembles double bellflowers, with the back of the flower being white, pale yellow, with light red stars. The leaves are shorter and rounder than those of gentian, with pointed tips. The flower's appearance is no different from gentian. Flowers bloom in the third month.

【Dividing line】

【Heading, boxed section】Buddha Child Grass (Hotoke-kusa)
《Flowers bloom in the third month》

【Outside the box】The flower has six petals with no stamens or pistils. The flower color has a deep red base with blackness from below. The inner back is pale red with whiteness from the tip. The leaves resemble mandala flowers and are large. The flower's appearance resembles columbine grass.

【Dividing line】

【Heading, boxed section】Nine Luminaries Grass (Kuyōsō) 《Flowers in the third month》
Dog Tongue Grass (Kuzetsusō) 《bloom》

【Outside the box】The flower shape is like chrysanthemum, with the color originally yellow. The form blooms around the periphery. The leaves resemble those of bitter mallow and are small, attached below. There are single-petaled varieties. There are also thousand-petaled (multi-petaled) varieties. The single-petaled type is also called Sawa-moto-kō.
