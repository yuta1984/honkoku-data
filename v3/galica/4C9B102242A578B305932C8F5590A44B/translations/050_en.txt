The lords of Sanuki (Ikoma Sakon-no-taiyu Masatoshi), Tosa (Yamauchi Tosa-no-kami Tadayoshi), Nagato (Matsudaira Nagato-no-kami Hidekuni), Awa (Hachisuka Awa-no-kami Yoshishige), Iyo (Kato Sama-no-suke Yoshiaki), Higo (Kato Higo-no-kami Kiyomasa), Harima (Ikeda Sanzaemon Terumasa), Aki (Fukushima Saemon-no-taiyu Masanori), and Kii (Asano Kii-no-kami Yukinaga), with their combined domain holdings totaling 6,387,458 koku and 3 to, contributed their great forces for the construction. The five-story main keep was built entirely under the direction of Lord Higo Kiyomasa at his request. Rising several dozen jo in height, it is roofed with layers of bronze-green tiles, and golden shachihoko over one jo tall were placed at both ends of the uppermost story. To this day they tower into the sky and reflect the sunlight, astonishing all who see them. It is truly an unparalleled magnificent sight and Japan's greatest castle.

The old Nagoya Castle (existed before the current castle construction and had no lord from around the Koji era, eventually becoming abandoned. Its former site is in the area of the current castle's ni-no-maru. The history of this old castle goes back to the reign of Emperor Go-Kashiwabara, when there was a man named Okochi Bitchu-no-kami Sadatsuna, the land steward of Fusencho in Mikawa Province. Originally a retainer of the Kira family, in recent years he became independent and wielded power, fighting against Imagawa Shuri-no-taiyu Ujichika of Suruga. Okochi sought aid from Shiba Jibu-no-taiyu Yoshitatsu of Owari and fortified himself in Hikuma Castle in Totomi (present-day Hamamatsu).
