Mirror-maker

Setting aside matters of China, our Japan has the Three Sacred Treasures, and the imperial throne continues in unbroken succession, with the sacred mirror of the Naishidokoro shining purely. Even in the imperial poem by Emperor Go-Toba, it is sung "the mirrors that multiply in the seven shrines," and it is a treasure of Yamato, the land where gods reside. Particularly for women, it is the foremost tool for personal grooming, and the mirrors that reflect one's appearance in evening attire at the waiting chambers are

all incomparably beautiful to behold.

An ancient text tells us: Long ago, there was a married couple. They had to make an unavoidable separation, and the husband departed to a distant country. At that time, the woman grieved over the painful parting from her husband, took out a mirror she had used every morning and evening, broke it in two, and made a vow that the broken halves would be reunited.

Even after years passed, the husband did not return to his homeland. Though the woman could not forget her sorrow, her heart gradually grew distant, and when she finally made a pledge with another man, that mirror transformed into a magpie and flew to where her husband was. The husband learned of his wife's unfaithful heart. The custom of attaching a magpie design to the back of mirrors originates from this legend.
