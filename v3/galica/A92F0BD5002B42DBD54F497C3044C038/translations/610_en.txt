In the third year [of Eiwa, 1377], an envoy from the kingdom of Joseon (Korea), Jeong Mongju, came to Japan, met with the Tandai (regional deputy) Imagawa Ryōshun in Tsukushi (Kyushu), and returned home.

In the fourth year [1378], third month, Yoshimitsu held an inuōmono (dog-chasing mounted archery event). In the same month, Yoshimitsu moved into the new mansion in Muromachi, which was henceforth called the Muromachi-dono. Because many flowers were planted in the garden, it was also known as the Hana no Gosho (Flower Palace). In the same month, Yoshimitsu was appointed Dainagon (Major Counselor). In the fourth month, Uesugi Norinori of Kamakura died. His younger brother, Gyōbu no Taifu Noriharu, took over the administration of affairs. On the eighth day, Regent Tadamoto resigned from the position of Safu (Minister of the Left). Nijō Morotsugu was appointed Safu. Konoe Kanetsugu was appointed Ufu (Minister of the Right). Former Dainagon Fujiwara no Kimitada of Imadegawa was appointed Naifu (Minister of the Center). Yoshimitsu was appointed concurrently as Commander of the Right Imperial Guards (Ukon'e no Taishō).

In the eleventh month, Hashimoto Minbu and others of the Southern faction raised a revolt in Kishū (Kii Province), and Hosokawa Hyōbu (responded to the uprising).
