His son, Major Counselor Tadazane, was made to oversee the affairs of the Great Council of State. His grandfather Morozane was still alive and was called "Ōdono" (Great Lord). Politics were decided within the cloister. Moromichi used to be indignant and say, "In our country's precedents, is there such a manner as setting up carriages at the emperor's gate?" After Moromichi's death, the position of kampaku remained vacant for a while, and Cloistered Emperor Shirakawa increasingly governed as he pleased. From this time, affairs of the realm were conducted not through imperial edicts (senshō) and official documents (kanpu), but through cloistered emperor's edicts (inzen) and directives from the cloistered emperor's office (inchō no gebumi). People throughout the provinces respected these and none failed to fear them. Also, people of the rank of major or middle counselor or councillor were selected and given the title of "in no bettō" (superintendent of the cloister) to manage affairs within the cloister. Their authority was extremely strong. The hokumen no samurai (north-facing warriors) were also established for the first time to serve night duty within the cloister. In addition, the ceremonial practices of the cloister were all established from this time.
