A great amnesty was proclaimed.

In the second year of Hakuchi (651), an embroidered Buddhist image was made for the first time. Its height was one jō and six shaku. In addition, one thousand Buddha statues were carved. Moreover, over 2,100 monks and nuns were gathered in the imperial palace, made to recite the complete Buddhist scriptures, and over 2,700 lamps were lit.

In the fourth year of Hakuchi (653), Kishi no Osani and others were appointed as imperial envoys and a mission to Tang China was dispatched. They had an audience with Emperor Gaozong of Tang and returned to Japan. Following this imperial mission, Jōe, the founder of Tafunomine in Yamato Province, along with many other renowned monks, entered Tang China. Jōe was the son of Kamatari. During this period, Silla, Goguryeo, and Baekje presented tribute every year. Since the amounts were small, this was reformed and they were held accountable.
