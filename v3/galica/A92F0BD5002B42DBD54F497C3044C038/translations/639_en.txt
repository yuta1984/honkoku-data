On the sixth day of the fifth month, the former Sei-i Taishōgun, Daijō-daijin, Junior First Rank, and Junsangu, Minamoto no Yoshimitsu (dharma name: Dōgi), passed away at his residence in Kitayama. He was fifty-one years of age. He was posthumously titled "Rokuonji-dono" and referred to as "Tenzan." An imperial decree was issued to bestow upon him the honorary title of Daijō Tennō (Retired Emperor), but Yoshimochi declined and did not accept it. From the first year of Ōan to the first year of Ōei, he served in office for twenty-seven years. After abdicating in favor of Yoshimochi, he continued for another fourteen years, making his total reign forty-one years. Dōgi (Yoshimitsu) left no clear instruction as to whom he wished to succeed him in his legacy. Since his affection for Yoshitsugu surpassed that for Yoshimochi, it was thought that perhaps he had intended to pass things to Yoshitsugu. However, Yoshimochi was already established as head of the family, and the kanrei Shiba Yoshimasa (lay monk) wielded great influence and supported Yoshimochi.

In the eleventh month, Minamoto no Yoshimochi issued orders regarding the checkpoints (sekisho) throughout the provinces. Kanrei Shiba Yoshishige, the Director of the Right Division of Outer Palace Guards, affixed his seal, and Iio Tsunekado served as bugyō (magistrate/administrator). Yoshishige was the son of Yoshimasa.

In the twelfth month, the Yongle Emperor (Chengzu) of the Great Ming sent a letter to Yoshimochi, mourned Yoshimitsu's death, composed a memorial text, and bestowed upon him the posthumous name "Taikен-ō" (King Taikен).
