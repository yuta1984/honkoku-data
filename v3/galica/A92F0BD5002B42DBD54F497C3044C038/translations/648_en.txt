In the same month, Hosokawa Ukyō-no-daibu Mitsumoto resigned from the position of Kanrei (Deputy Shogun). In the eighth month, Hatakeyama Saemon-no-kami Mitsumasa, who had taken Buddhist vows under the name Dōtan, was reappointed as Kanrei.

In the twenty-ninth year, in the first month, Yoshimochi visited the quarters of Shōren'in monzeki Gien. Gien was Yoshimochi's younger brother. At this time, Gien had been appointed Archbishop (Daisōjō) in his capacity as the head of the Tendai sect (Tendai-zasu), and had received an imperial edict granting him the title of Jungo (honorary rank equivalent to that of a retired empress). Since the time of Yoshimitsu, the realm had been at peace and the capital was tranquil, and so Yoshimochi visited various places for leisure. He visited the residences of Kanrei Hatakeyama Mitsumasa, Shiba Uhyōe-no-kami Yoshiatsushi, Hosokawa Ukyō-no-daibu Mitsumoto, and various other daimyō, finding amusement and relaxation. He also paid visits to the houses of regent families (Sekke), monzeki temples, and families such as Saionji, Yanagihara, and Hino. These visits were referred to as "Onari." In the fourth month, Yoshimochi paid respects at the Retired Emperor's residence, and a performance of Sarugaku was held.

In the fifth month, Yoshimochi held the memorial service for Rokuon'in at Tōji-ji Temple. A Hokke Hakkō (Eight Lectures on the Lotus Sutra) ceremony was performed. Regent Kujō Mitsunori and others, along with court nobles and courtiers (ungaku), attended. In the eighth month, Yoshimochi's consort (midaidokoro) made a pilgrimage to the Ise Shrine. In the ninth month, Retired Emperor Go-Komatsu made a pilgrimage to the Hachiman Shrine.
