(Abdications, imperial accessions, and the appointment of Crown Princes all came to be arranged by the Kantō [shogunate].)

In the second month of Kenji 1 (1275), the Mongol envoy Du Shìzhōng and others crossed over to Japan. Koreans likewise accompanied them. They were examined at Dazaifu, and Du Shìzhōng and two others were sent on to Kamakura. They were not permitted to enter the capital (Kyoto). Although letters arrived, no reply was sent.

In the sixth month, Tadaie, the former Regent of the Kujō clan, passed away. He was forty-seven years of age. He was given the posthumous title Ichion'in.

In the eighth month, Michimasa, former Minister of the Right of the Kazanin clan, was appointed Grand Minister of State (Daijō-daijin).

In the tenth month, Ietsune of the Ichijō clan resigned as Regent and Left Minister of State. Kanehira, former Chancellor of the Takatsukasa clan, became Regent.

In the twelfth month, Morotada, Minister of the Right of the Nijō clan, was transferred to the position of Left Minister of State, and Tadanori, Major Counselor of the Kujō clan, was appointed Minister of the Right. Morotsugu of the Kazanin clan resigned as Inner Minister (Naidaijin). Iemoto of the Konoe clan was appointed Inner Minister. (Tadanori was the son of Tadaie; Iemoto was the son of Motohira.)

In the same month, Hōjō Tokikuni went up to the capital and assumed charge of the southern division of the Rokuhara [Tandai]. (He was the great-grandson of Tokifusa.)

This year, the monk Ippen founded the Jishū sect for the first time.
