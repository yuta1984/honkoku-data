(Continued from the twelfth month) Hōjō Takatoki placed his kinsman Tokiwa, Suruga no Kami Norisada, at Rokuhara, and appointed Hōjō Hidetoki as the Chinzei Tandai (Deputy for Kyushu). Around this time, Nagasaki Enki, the inner administrator (uchi-kanryō) of Takatoki, had become senile with old age, and so passed his office to his eldest son, Takasuke. Takasuke grew arrogant, made light of Takatoki, and wielded a tyrannical authority in defiance of his master.

In the second year (Genkō 2, 1322), in the first month, there was an imperial visit (gyōkō) to pay respects to the Retired Emperor (in holy orders), followed by a musical entertainment (kangen go-yū).

In the fifth month, a dispute arose between Andō Gorō and Matadaburō of Ōshū (Mutsu Province). As Nagasaki Takasuke had accepted bribes from both parties and shown partiality, the Andō clan rose in rebellion. Around the same time, the Watanabe of Sesshū (Settsu Province), the Yasuda of Kii, and the Ochi of Yamato, among others, turned against the warrior government (bakufu). It was from the Andō clan and their ilk that defiance of the Hōjō house's commands first began, more than a hundred years after the Jōkyū Disturbance (1221). The Emperor had long harbored resentment at the warrior government doing as it pleased. Upon hearing that Takatoki was given over to drink and debauchery, and that Takasuke's tyranny ran contrary to the will of the people, he began secretly plotting with his close retainers to bring about the destruction of Kamakura.
