In the second month of the first year of Ōan (1368), the Zen monks Chūshin and Myōsa were dispatched to the Great Ming (China). This year corresponded to the first year of Hongwu of the Taizu of the Great Ming dynasty. Chūshin took the religious name Zekkai, and Myōsa took the name Jorin. Both were monks of considerable literary talent.

In the third month, Emperor Gomurakami of the Southern Court passed away. His son Hiroshige ascended to the throne and was given the posthumous name Chōkei-in.

In the fourth month, Yoshimitsu underwent his coming-of-age ceremony (genpuku). Yoriyuki performed the role of placing the ceremonial cap (kakan), and Hosokawa Hyōbu no Taifu Nariuji performed the role of hairdresser.

In the sixth month, there was an order concerning the lands of the Imperial Palace, the Retired Emperor's Palace, as well as shrine and temple holdings, and the plundering by warriors was halted.

In the same month, acting as Yoshimitsu's representative, Yoriyuki made a pilgrimage to Iwashimizu (Hachimangū), and presented offerings of silver swords, sacred horses, and gold dust.

In the ninth month, Uesugi Noriaki, the executive administrator (shitsuji) of Kamakura, died. His son Yoshinori and his nephew Tomofusa jointly administered affairs, and they were referred to collectively as the "Two Uesugi" (Ryō-Uesugi).
