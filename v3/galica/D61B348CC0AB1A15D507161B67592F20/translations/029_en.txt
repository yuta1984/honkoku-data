On August 1st of Meiji 27 (1894), Japan notified all nations of its declaration of war against Qing China. On September 13th of the same year, His Imperial Majesty the Emperor, in his august capacity as commander-in-chief, advanced the Imperial Standard to Hiroshima, where an extraordinary session of the Imperial Diet was subsequently convened. Tracing the origins of the war with Qing China, it was rooted in the uprising of the Donghak Party in Korea.

The Donghak Party was a peasant uprising that arose from the people's inability to endure the severe oppression of local Korean officials, seeking to vent their long-pent-up grievances. Uprisings occurred every year across the various provinces of Korea and were not considered particularly unusual, yet the party's membership was said to have grown remarkably with each passing year. In the spring of this year (1894), when they rose up again in Jeolla Province, those long dissatisfied with the Min Yeong-jun clan and patriots incensed by the government's tyranny flocked together upon hearing the news, and their numbers swiftly reached well into the thousands. Under an appointed leader, they established their base across three localities in Jeolla Province — Gobu, Jeongeup, and Taein — setting up their main headquarters at Baeksan, situated between Jeonju (the provincial capital of Jeolla) and Taein. Relying on the natural defenses of the terrain to engage the government forces, they opened hostilities on April 22nd.

In this first battle, the Donghak forces were easily defeated by 800 government troops; however, on April 26th, they lured the advancing government army into an ambush, rising simultaneously from all four sides to attack. The government forces fell into great disarray, with casualties numbering over 200. Jeonju, where the headquarters of the punitive force was located, fell entirely into the hands of the Donghak Party. From this point, the Donghak Party spread their influence throughout the various provinces, and those who flocked to their support upon hearing of their success numbered nearly 20,000. Their momentum was fierce and unstoppable. They then assigned positions and began advancing toward various locations.

Prior to this, upon receiving news that the Donghak Party had risen in rebellion and was growing in power, the government in Seoul fell into panic. They finally dispatched Hong Gye-hun as the Punitive Commissioner, leading 800 elite troops of the Royal Guard Headquarters, and sent them immediately on their march.

Accordingly, Hong Gye-hun departed from Incheon by ship, arriving safely at Gunsan on May 1st.
