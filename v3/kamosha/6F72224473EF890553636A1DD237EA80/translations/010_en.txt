(Continuing from the previous page) With the formation of the encampments, since Lord Akechi was at his camp, and as he had long placed particular trust in us to make the rounds of courtesy visits to the various camps on behalf of our shrine, he stated that we were especially among the first he had expected to call upon. Having no alternative, we consented, and together with Lord Minbu-no-Shōyu, we traveled near Hashimoto. However, we found the road along the embankment was blocked, with no passage permitted day or night. We therefore made our way through Yawata and Naragatōge Pass, circled around via the Yamato Road, emerged in Kawachi, and paid our courtesy visits to the various camps. On the return journey, the road was again blocked, so we crossed via the Funasaka Pass, emerged at Nara and Saiky ō (western Kyoto), passed through Yamashiro, and returned home. We waited for Lord Akechi's return to camp, but it continued to be delayed.

Item: In an earlier matter, when there was a dispute with Ichiharano over Kifune Mountain, and Lord Kinoshita Tōkichirō was serving as the magistrate for Ichiharano — we submitted on our own authority that, according to precedent, both Ichiharano and Kifune were to be administered as a single jurisdiction. This was promptly understood and accepted. We proposed that it would be appropriate to submit this matter to Lord Tōkichi (rō) at this opportune moment. However, two or three people objected, saying: "Since we conceded defeat in years past, even if we were to raise the matter now, it would likely go poorly for us." While there seemed little choice in the matter, the defeat had been too damaging to our reputation, and so we urged firmly: "We will not say anything that would harm Kamo's interests; we will speak on our own authority." To this they all agreed, saying, "Then speak you shall." We accepted the task and, accompanied by the shrine administrator (雑掌) and Lord Iyo-no-kami, submitted our case: that according to precedent, the administration of both sites was indisputably unified, and that Lord Murai had ruled one-sidedly in favor of Futase. We further stated: "We have heard rumors that the people of Futase are about to come to this side (to receive the ruling), and we humbly request that you do not accept their petition." To this, Lord Tōkichirō replied: "Previously this matter was beyond my knowledge, but I have now heard and understood that your shrine has administered both as a single jurisdiction since ancient times. No matter who may come and petition hereafter, I shall not give my consent." We were most grateful for this. We then requested: "If it would not be too much to ask, might we receive this in writing on a folded document (折紙)?" He replied: "Ikuma, write out the document," and, while he himself remained standing, the document was being written...
