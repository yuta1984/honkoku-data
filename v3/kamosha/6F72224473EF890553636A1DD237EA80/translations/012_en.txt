Item: Regarding the Kamo Shrine estate of Kifune Mountain — an investigation was conducted into the matter of the village of Futase attempting to illegally claim the mountain, and it has been established beyond all doubt that this is rightfully Kamo's mountain. Therefore, in accordance with the previously issued order (御下知) of the Kubō (shogunal authority), the shrine shall continue to hold dominion over it. This document is issued accordingly.

　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Murai Nagato-no-kami　　[Seal]
　　　Tenshō 14 [1586], Twelfth Month, Twenty-Ninth Day　　Sadakatsu

　　　　　　　　To: The Entire Kamo Shrine Community
　　　　　　　　　　　　《Marginal note: Even a document such as this was obtained through our personal efforts, and we thus recovered Kifune Mountain.》

Item: Regarding the revenue from the felling and delivery of brushwood (firewood) from Kifune Mountain — in previous years, small sums had been paid to the shrine community from time to time, but as these were far too meager, we exerted ourselves and repeatedly managed to arrange matters, finally settling on fourteen koku per year for a period of five years, amounting to seventy koku in total, and this was established as a fixed arrangement to be maintained in perpetuity without dispute. However, on this occasion, the reduction of the mountain usage fee (山手) arranged through the intercession of the Lord of Iyo-no-kami is an utterly outrageous matter.

Item: We had repeatedly requested that a confirmatory vermillion-seal document (御継目御朱印) be obtained from Lord Ieyasu (Tokugawa Ieyasu), but the matter was repeatedly delayed. Around Keichō 11 and 12 (1606–1607), those responsible for accompanying documents submitted them each year, yet no progress was made regarding the confirmatory seal, and only other incidental documents were being processed. Therefore, on the nineteenth day of the second month of Keichō 16 (1611), we appeared before the senior and junior members of the shrine and said: "Lord Shogun (上様) is soon to make a visit to Kyoto — why have you not spoken to Lord Itakura Iga-no-kami?" They replied that even if the matter were conveyed to Lord Iga-no-kami, it would not be received. We then said: "If there has been absolutely no communication with Lord Iga-no-kami, shall we be the ones to raise the matter?" to which they responded: "If that is the case, then please do speak to Lord Iga-no-kami." We therefore immediately spoke to members of his inner household (御内衆), and made a prompt petition. Lord Iga-no-kami received us with great warmth and cordiality, and we brought along all the previous documents to present to him, conveying one by one the full details of the matters we had worked to arrange. The circumstances are too numerous to be fully described in writing.
