They moved to the toilet area, made one visit, proceeded to Unō-sha shrine, made offerings on the large altar, and recited prayers with double worship and bowing.
"We humbly address the sacred presence of Unō-sha shrine with great reverence. Today, as is customary each year, please peacefully receive this ceremony of presenting auspicious writings. May the prosperity of the shrine grounds, abundant harvests, and tranquility of the shrine families be eternally protected like eternal rocks, guarded and protected night and day, and may you grant us the blessing of good fortune," they prayed, clapped their hands, removed the offerings, and withdrew.
They moved the offerings from the altar and returned to the main seat. (However, there is also a theory that rather than offering at Unō-sha, they place items on a three-legged tray at that location and orient it toward Uwa-sha shrine.)

Next came the sake dedication ceremony. For the first offering presentation, they first placed five pounded chestnuts and five pieces of kelp on small stands, added chopsticks, placed them on three-legged trays, and set them before the head priest. The deputies and assistant deputies used flat serving trays, with the same items as before. Next, they placed three sake cups three times and three earthen vessels on three-legged trays before the head priest. For the sacred sake, cold pure sake was put in a serving bottle, the priest donned pure white robes to pour, and craftsmen wore formal attire to serve in their roles. First, the head priest passed the initial offering to the deputy, then to the assistant deputy, and after three offerings each, these were removed. The second offering was rice soup with vegetables (with rice cakes topped with daikon radish, taro, tied kelp, and skewered snake, served in rice bowls without side dishes. The head priest used three-legged trays, others used flat serving trays. Of course this replaced the first offering, but chopsticks were left in place and taken after the serving bottle was brought out). Sacred sake was warm sake in pewter vessels with lidded cups for three offerings each. The third offering was clear soup (with vegetables and pheasant served in earthen vessels three times. The head priest used three-legged trays, others used flat serving trays. This was exchanged like the first time, but at this point one lacquered cup was added. Chopsticks were taken after the serving bottle was brought out). Sacred sake was the same as before, served in lacquered cups for three offerings (each beginning with the head priest, but not limited to three offerings; separately, lacquered cups were placed on stands and brought out, with greetings repeated several times in flowing circulation). The ceremony of presenting three types of side dishes (plus carp for departure) was completed. The documents that had been set aside earlier were placed in their robes, they made one bow, and dispersed.

Eighth Day
1. This morning there was a ceremony for the beginning of rice field work at Hakken-tei pavilion, so the deputies and assistant deputies also went wearing pure white robes.
The number of people was ten from the rural areas, plus supervisory deputies, assistant deputies, minor record keepers, etc., totaling fourteen people.
Since there was no time notification for the deputies, assistant deputies, and minor record keepers, the deputy provided the arrangements.
Seating was according to seniority. The meal arrangements and other ceremonial aspects are detailed in separate records, so they are omitted here.
After sweets, tea, etc. were served, three people from the treasury were sent to the kitchen, followed by announcement matters and the distribution of response papers. The ceremony of response auspicious writings was completed, and everyone dispersed.
