One day, they saw Tartar military forces withdrawing from Nanjing, and also saw people from Nanjing who had come to Beijing to pay their respects. The Nanjing people had all shaved their heads in the same manner as the Tartars. A unit of Tartar soldiers was said to still remain in Nanjing. Nanjing was located about thirty days' journey south of Beijing, with many large rivers in between.

New Year ceremonies greatly resembled those of Japan: they erected kadomatsu (pine decorations), celebrated for three days, and exchanged New Year greetings with each other. During this time, all government officials paid their respects at the royal castle. The attire of the daimyo consisted of garments similar to Japanese kariginu (court hunting robes) worn over their usual new formal dress.

During the 14th, 15th, and 16th days of the seventh month, Buddhist ceremonies were conducted exactly as in Japan. In the temples, wooden statues were erected - some temples had as many as ten thousand statues, and even large temple complexes lacked sufficient space to house them, requiring the construction of temporary buildings for many statues. In general, both Tartary and the Ming state had very many temples, all with astonishing numbers of Buddhist scriptures stacked before the Buddha statues. The celebrations of Tango (Boys' Day) in the fifth month and mid-autumn in the eighth month were the same as in Japan. Even commoners celebrated birthdays, but

there were no seasonal festivals in the third and ninth months.

Initially, when they were in Tartary, they could not understand the language and had to communicate through gestures, with both sides trying to understand each other, though detailed comprehension was difficult. Later, they gradually became accustomed to hearing and learning the language, and communication between both sides became fluent, reaching a point where they felt no inconvenience. When they later traveled long distances with the Tartars, there was no daily conversation they could not understand. The fourteen-year-old sandal-bearer who had escaped death on the ship learned both countries' languages well, particularly mastering Beijing dialect, and became able to converse and interpret freely with people from both countries. Therefore, Ming officials and other people all called him "Achiki Toksou." "Achiki" means boy, and "Toksou" means interpreter. When their people wanted to go to government offices and passed through guard posts, or when they went into the city and were questioned by people, having this boy interpret would resolve any matter immediately.
