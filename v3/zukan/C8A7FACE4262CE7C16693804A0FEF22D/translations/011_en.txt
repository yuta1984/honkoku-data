Hakitoke
　Nunohiki
　　　　　Illustration recorded in Shin'yō Kinpu
　　　　　According to the same book, this mushroom
　　　　　reaches its peak when bush clover flowers bloom,
　　　　　hence the name. It also grows in clusters
　　　　　in mountains or under forests after autumn rains.
　　　　　Because it resembles stretched cloth,
　　　　　it is also called nunohiki (cloth-pulling). Both cap and
　　　　　stem are pale yellow in color, with good
　　　　　flavor. Sun-dried ones are also good. The sweetness
　　　　　is less than in fresh ones, it is said.

Nunohiki
　　Illustration recorded in Kinshi. According to the same book, it is pure white,
　　with thin caps, edges turning upward, and hair-like spines on
　　the underside. Resembling deer antler velvet, the stem is thin and hollow,
　　with a hard base. During summer and autumn, it grows in grassy
　　areas, among brushwood, and in tree shade. Multiple stems
　　connect continuously over several shaku. When viewed from afar, it looks like stretched
　　cloth, thus earning its name. There are also pale pink
　　varieties with slightly larger forms. People of Mikawa province
　　call this "shibamotokui," and also call it "shi-
　　bakuri." It is slightly bitter yet sweet and smooth,
　　most suitable for making into soup, it is said.
