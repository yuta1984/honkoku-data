(Continued from previous page) ...fishermen from the shore gather in great numbers to trade (in shells). Shells are called by names derived from the earlier *kasen* (shell-ranking charts), but those omitted from the *kasen* appear to have many locally assigned names, which are not particularly elegant.

When I visited Tajima Province (*Tanshū*), someone told me that there was a person called "*Shūbai*" (Shell Gatherer) who traveled on pilgrimage along the shores of various provinces, collecting and enjoying shells. What a remarkable aesthete he must be — surely a person for whom this passion has penetrated to the very marrow. I heard that he also comes to Tajima.

From the first day of the month to the fifteenth, the tides are low during the daytime, making it easy to collect shells in the afternoon. During the latter fifteen days (from the sixteenth to the last day), the low tide falls at night, making shell gathering difficult. In general, the period from morning until midday is best for visiting the shore. Toward evening, the tide tends to come in.

It is noted even in the section on the *Shako-gai* (giant clam, *Tridacna*) in the *Honzō* (natural history texts) that on the third day of the third month, an exceptionally large tidal ebb occurs.

**[Left page]**

In Japan as well, as is widely known, this is the day of the great tidal ebb. On this day, shell species can be gathered with particular ease and in great abundance.

In Shen Maoguan's (*Shin Bōkan*'s) *Kāboku-kō* (*Huamu Kao*, "Treatise on Flowers and Trees"), it is written that among *螺* (spiral shells / gastropods), there are many varieties: those with a white operculum and a fragrant smell are called *kōra* (fragrant spiral); those with a pointed and elongated shell are called *sanra* (boring spiral), whose flavor is next in rank; those with spines are called *shira* (spiny spiral); those with a pungent flavor are called *ratsura* (peppery spiral); and there are also varieties called *kenra* (fist spiral), *kenra* (sword spiral), *hanra* (spotted spiral), and *teira* (nail spiral), and so forth. From this, one can clearly understand the distinctions among gastropods — whether long, short, spined, and so on.
