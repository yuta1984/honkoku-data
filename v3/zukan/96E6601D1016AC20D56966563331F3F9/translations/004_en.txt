**[Left Page – Short Preface]**
*A Short Preface to Kaifu Ura no Nishiki*

"Lush and resplendent"—as praised in the *Shijing* (Book of Odes), and as sung in our own ancient Japanese poetry with verses such as "Shall I gather jewels? Shall I gather shells?"—shells have been cherished and admired by people since time immemorial. As spring breezes gently blow and waves wash ashore from the four seas in tranquil peace, the "brocade of the shore" (shells) has become a source of amusement and delight for children and women in this age of prosperity and contentment.

I have gathered these shells together, and, driven by my passion for the subject, I questioned the children of divers and the people of coastal villages for their names, visited connoisseurs to correct my errors, consulted ancient texts for reference, and recorded what I learned freely and as it came to me. Over the years, this grew into two volumes.
I named it *Kaifu* ("Shell Compendium") and stored it away in a box. However, thinking it might serve—if only as a trifle, much as a yellow leaf passed off as gold coin soothes a crying child—I have set down these circumstances at the beginning.

　　　　　　　　　　　Written by Ōeda Ryūhō himself
　An auspicious day in Koshin (the third month) of the year Kishi (己巳, 1749)
