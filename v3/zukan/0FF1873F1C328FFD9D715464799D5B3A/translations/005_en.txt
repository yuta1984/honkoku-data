Haji (Japanese Wax Tree)
Commonly called "Haze" [from Saijiki (seasonal almanac)]

Year of Kinoto-Tori (乙酉), mid 5th month, 9th day
Sketched from life [Seal] Various trees

From "Honzō Ikka-gen" (A Family's Words on Materia Medica):
Haji (Haze) - Mountain lacquer
There are two varieties: large-leaf lacquer and small-leaf lacquer

Baien's commentary:
The large-leaf lacquer tree grows wild and is called "nurude." The small-leaf variety is the haji. Both are used to extract wax and lacquer for civilian use, now called "urushi" (lacquer). Many haji trees have been planted in the vacant land in front of the Ofunagura (Imperial Boat Warehouse) in Fukagawa, Edo, and this place is called "Urushi-batake" (Lacquer Field). Haji trees for official use are also planted in vacant land outside Akasaka Gate and on the embankment along Furukawa Street in Azabu.

From "Yamato Honzō" (Japanese Materia Medica):
Yellow haji (Haji) - Its fruit is used to make candles
People cultivate it for profit. It is also used to make bows.
In "Kyūkō Honzō" (Materia Medica for Famine Relief), there is a tree called "kaikai-su," which might be the haji tree.

From "Wamyō Ruijūshō" (Japanese Names for Things):
Yellow haji (Hanshi) - According to the annotations in "Wenxuan," haji (pronounced like rakko, Japanese name hanishi) refers to today's yellow haji tree.
Yellow haji is used for dyeing yellow, and the emperor's robe is called "ōrozen" (yellow haji dye).

From "Shin Kokin Wakashū" (New Collection of Ancient and Modern Poems):
In the field where quails cry, the haji maples stand,
The autumn wind blows strong enough to scatter them.
