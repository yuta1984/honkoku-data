According to "Wakan Sansai Zue," soft and smooth category:
Bi (inu-endō) Wild pea Tarumi Large nest vegetable (commonly called "inu-endō")

The interpretation of bi is difficult to settle due to various theories. In the Zhao chapter of the Book of Songs: "Climbing that
southern mountain to gather bracken, climbing that southern mountain to gather bi"
(Zhu Xi's commentary: Bi resembles bracken but is somewhat larger, has awns and bitter taste, mountain people eat it) The commentary treats the two plants as the same type but
different species. The Wamyōshō treats the two characters bi-ketsu (Japanese name: warabi) as one plant, which is quite
wrong. Probably because bi and bracken form parallel phrases in poetry, they were mistakenly considered one
plant. The Erya and Shuowen also have similarities and differences, and Shizhen considers bi to be
wild pea. However, "Yamato Honzō" states that bi (inode) exists in deep mountains and secluded valleys, resembles bracken but is
larger, and that the bi of medicinal texts is a different plant. There are very many of this type - same name, different plants. A proverb says,
"Gyōyō (dog broad bean) and wild pea differ only in size - should we distinguish them? If gyōyō is considered
wild pea, then bi is also clearly wild pea." (I say) However, bi is
called large nest vegetable, which is the large variety of wild pea
and one species, thus
a different plant. Various theories about wild pea called kōbōketsu-mei
are mixed together, so I provide this collected explanation here only.
