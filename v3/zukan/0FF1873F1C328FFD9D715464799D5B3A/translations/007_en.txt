According to "Wakan Sansai Zue," Shrub section:
Bi (dog vetch), wild pea,垂水, large nest vegetable - commonly called inu-endō

The interpretation of "bi" has various theories and is difficult to establish definitively. In the "Book of Songs," Shaonan chapter: "Climbing that southern mountain, gathering its bracken. Climbing that southern mountain, gathering its bi."
(Zhu Xi's commentary: Bi resembles bracken but is slightly larger, has awns, tastes bitter, and mountain people eat it.) This commentary treats the two plants as the same family but different species. The "Wamyōshō" treats the two characters bi and bracken (Japanese name: warabi) as one plant, which is quite wrong. Presumably, because bi and bracken appear in parallel verses in the "Book of Songs," they were mistakenly considered one plant. The "Erya" and "Shuowen" also have similarities and differences. Shizhen considers bi to be wild pea, but in "Yamato Honzō," bi (holly fern) is found in deep mountains and valleys, resembles bracken but is larger, indicating that the bi in "Honzō" is a different plant. There are very many such cases of different plants with the same name. The saying goes "Gyōyō (dog broad bean) and wild pea are distinguished only by size. If gyōyō is wild pea, then bi is also wild pea" - this is an insightful observation. (I say) Not so. Bi is named ōsuna-na (large nest vegetable), which is the large variety of wild pea and one species, while the separate plant is called kōbōketsumei. Because the collected theories about wild pea are confused, I provide this collected explanation here.
