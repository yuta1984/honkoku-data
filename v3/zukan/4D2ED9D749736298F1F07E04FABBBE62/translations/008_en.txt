One might say this serves as a supplement. However, I do not presume to consider myself superior to the various venerable predecessors. I rely on these distinguished predecessors to advance and verify this work, so that later students may have no cause for doubt. I also do not know whether there are truly no errors, and without presuming to be correct, I humbly seek broad inquiry from those in the world who are versed in pharmacognosy, encouraging each other to achieve success in our endeavors. Here I write this preface.
Winter solstice day of Bunsei Boshi year, Kanzen Iwasaki Tsunemasa

General Principles
1. This book follows the Bencao Gangmu in its classification and begins by recording mountain herbs because among medicinal substances, plants are most numerous, and these were compiled first. As for illustrations of water, fire, metals and stones, birds, beasts, insects, fish, etc., these are not yet complete and await future work.

1. Chinese works that illustrate medicinal substances include the Beijiben Cao, Erya Tu, Sancai Tuhui, and Michuan Huajing, but these are crude and lacking in detail, unable to capture their true appearance. Japanese works such as Sanko Yasankusa Hanai and others seen in painting albums mostly lack color application, or even when colored, fail to capture the truth - none are reliable. This illustrated compendium [compares] forms from both [China and Japan]
