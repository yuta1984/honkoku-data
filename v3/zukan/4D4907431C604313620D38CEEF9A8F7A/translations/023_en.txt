## Welsh Onion / Spring Onion (Negi / Sō)

As stated in *Honzō Wage* (Japanese Commentary on Materia Medica):
　Onion (Sō) flowers — Winter onion — There is a variety called "Beturō-sō"
　　Alternative names: Kōsaihaku (芤菜伯)
　　　　　　　　　　 Kuwashisō (和事草)　Rokutai (鹿胎)
　　Japanese names: Hitomoji (比登毛志)　[supplemented by] Nebuka (祢不加)
　　　　　　　In the *Wamyō Ruijushō* (Japanese Glossary of Chinese Terms) it states:
　　　　　　　　Sō (葱) — As stated in *Tōin* (Tang Rhymes Dictionary), onion [pronounced "Tei"] is a pungent vegetable (kunsai).
　　　　　　　　Su Jing's annotation states: there are several varieties; the mountain onion is called "mei (茗)."

As stated in the *Bencao Gangmu* (Compendium of Materia Medica): The onion has leaves that are straight on the outside and hollow within. The newly sprouted shoot is called "sōshin (葱針, onion needle)"; the leaf is called "sōsei (葱青, onion green)"; the outer skin is called "sōhō (葱袍, onion robe)"; the stem is called "sōhaku (葱白, onion white)"; and the juice inside the leaf is called "sōzen (葱苒, onion fluid)." It may be grown from seed or transplanted as seedlings.

The winter onion (Fuyu-negi) [also known as: Ji-sō (慈葱), Tō-sō (凍葱), Ōmiya-negi (大宮葱)] flourishes in winter and weakens in summer.

The Han onion (Kan-negi / Honnegi) [also known as: Honnegi (本葱)] has a firm, solid stem; it does not flower in spring, and forms clusters of blue-white color.

The explanatory notes in *Wakan Sansai Zue* (Japanese-Chinese Illustrated Encyclopedia) state: Because the leaves are round and without branches, it is called "Hitomoji (一文字, single character)."

Essentially, the winter onion and the Han onion are originally the same plant: seeds are sown in the middle of the tenth month, and the leaves are cut and eaten from the first month onward. Since the leaves may be cut two or three times, it is commonly called "Karinegi (苅葱, harvest onion)." This is what is referred to as the Han onion. After being touched by frost, the flavor becomes exceptionally sweet and delicious; it warms the body and dispels cold — this is what is referred to as the winter onion. Thus it is a single plant that appears to be many varieties.
