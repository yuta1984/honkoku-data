## 【*Shikimi* (樒 / 莽草; Japanese Star Anise, *Illicium anisatum*)】

### According to the *Zoho Tashiki-hen* (増補多識編), in the section on Poisonous Plants (*Dokusōrui*):

　　*Mōsō* (莽草) [Japanese names]: *Shikimi* (志幾美). Also, upon investigation: *Netsumikoro-shi* (祢須美古呂志; "mouse-killer"). This plant is found in Suruga Province. One name: *Nabewari* (那倍和利).
　　[Alternative names]: *Somō* (鼠莽; "rat's bane"). [Additional alternative name from the *Zoho* supplement]: *Mōsō* (䒽草). *Bōsō* (芒草) [cited from the *Shanhai Jing* (山海経)].

> The *Wakan Sansai Zue* (和漢三才図会) lists two varieties — *Mōsō* (莽草) and *Mokumitsu* (木蜜) — but states that *Mōsō* bears neither flowers nor fruit. This deserves further investigation. Furthermore, the name "*Nabewari*" is uncertain. *Kōfun* (鉤吻; *Gelsemium elegans*, a distinct poisonous plant) is a different species entirely; see later volumes for details.

### According to the *Yamato Honzō* (大和本草):

**Flowers of *Shikimi* (莽草花 / シキミノハナ)**

The same book's section "Yakumei Bengī" (薬名弁疑; "Examination of Drug Names") states: "*Somō*" (鼠莽).

> The character 樒 (*shikimi*) has been read and transmitted as *shikimi* by popular custom in Japan.
> In the *Junwamyō* (順和名) it is likewise read as *shikimi*.
> In the *Imyō Bunruishō* (異名分類抄), it appears as "*Mōsō* (莽草)" with the reading *hanano-ki* (ハナノキ).
> In the *Wamyō Ruijushō* (和名類聚抄), it is given as *shikimi*.
> In the *Shinsen Jikyō* (新撰字鏡), it appears as *mohone* (モホネ).
> According to the *Wakan Sansai Zue* (和漢三才図会):
>
> 　　*Mokumitsu* (木蜜) [Japanese name: *Shikimi*; Tang Chinese reading: *Momitsu*]
> 　　　　Also known as: *Mikkō* (蜜香), *Mokkō* (没香), *Takōboku* (多香木), *A-* [character unclear].
> 　　　　[Pronunciation] According to the *Tang Yun* (唐韻): "It is a fragrant tree." Japanese name: *Shikimi* (之木美).
> 　　　　Furthermore, the *Kenpōnashi* (枳椇; Japanese Raisin Tree, *Hovenia dulcis*) is also called *Mokumitsu*, but it is not the same plant as this one.

---

　　　　　　By popular custom, this plant is offered as a flower (*butsu-ka*) before the Buddha.
　　　　　　It is also burned as powdered incense (*makkō*) before Buddhist altars.
　　　　　　Both the bark and the leaves are used.

　　　　　　Sketched from life on the tenth day of the final period of *Kyōshō* (夾鐘; second lunar month),
　　　　　　in the year *Kōshin* (甲申) [Bunsei 7, 1824].

### According to the *Wamyō Ruijushō* (和名類聚抄):

　　*Shikimi* (樒)　According to the *Tang Yun* (唐韻); also stated in the *Kangoshō* (漢語抄): "*Shikimi* (之岐美) is a fragrant tree."

The same book also states:

　　*Shikimi* (莽草)　According to the commentary on the *Shanhai Jing* (山海経注): "*Mōsō*" (莽草).
　　　　According to the *Wamyō Honzō* (和名本草): "*Shikimi*" (之木美).
　　　　It is a plant that can be used to poison fish.

---

　*Shikimi* is found in abundance in the provinces of Musashi, Izu, Awaji, Tamba, and Harima.
　Its branches are broken off and offered to the Buddha; the branches and leaves are called "flowers" (*hana*). The bark and leaves are gathered, dried, and burned as incense, which is known as *makkō* (抹香; powdered incense).

---

### A poem composed in Oki Province:

> *Sana kara ya / hotoke no hana ni / oresemaji /
> Shikimi ga eda ni / tsumoru shirayuki*
>
> "How could one bear to break it as an offering to the Buddha —
> the white snow piling upon the branches of the *shikimi*."
> — Retired Emperor Go-Toba (後鳥羽院)
