**According to Wakan Sansai Zue (和漢三才図会), section on Fragrant Trees:**

　Zuikō (Rinchōge) — Chinese pronunciation: Zuì Xiāng (ツイ・ヒャン)
　　Also: Bōkō (睦香) — [according to the *Wuzazu* (五雑爼)]
　　Commonly called: Shinteuge (沈丁花)
　　Erroneously referred to as: Rinchōge (里牟知也宇介)

According to the *Wuzazu* (五雑爼): It is said that a monk on Mount Lu (廬山) was napping beneath a mountain rock when he was awakened by an unusual fragrance. He followed the scent and found this flower. He therefore named it *Suikō* (睡香; "sleeping fragrance"). Later, a person of refined taste considered it an auspicious omen (祥瑞) and changed the name to *Zuikō* (瑞香; "auspicious fragrance").

**I (Baien) note:**

　The *Wakan Sansai Zue* lists Sanban (山礬) and Zuikō (瑞香) as two separate entries; however, most other books treat them as a single species without distinction. Sanban does not shed its leaves in winter, blooms with white flowers in the third month, and its abundant blossoms carry a rich fragrance — hence the name "Yun" (芸). Its leaves resemble those of the gardenia (*Gardenia jasminoides* / 梔子) and have serrated edges. The leaves of Zuikō are also similar to gardenia leaves, but without serration. I suspect that Sanban and Zuikō belong to the same category, differing only in that one bears white flowers and the other purple. Sanban flowers have six petals, while Zuikō flowers have four — a minor difference. Zuikō likewise does not shed its leaves in winter.

**I (Baien) note further:**

　Zuikō (瑞香) and Sanban (山礬) both seem to belong to the same category, and it appears there may even be a variety with red flowers.

　Kizuikō (黄瑞香; yellow Zuikō) — Mitsumata (*Edgeworthia chrysantha*)
　Shirozuikō (白瑞香; white Zuikō) — Ōnishibari (*Daphne pseudomezereum* / ヲニシバリ) and Natsuhōzu (ナツホウズ); additionally, there are two further distinct varieties.

　The Kizuikō differs greatly in its nature/character from the others. The Shirozuikō, while closely resembling (Zuikō), is nonetheless a separate species. Those that bloom in spring before their leaves emerge are eventually followed by their leaves — that is, the leaves come out after the flowers, in succession.
