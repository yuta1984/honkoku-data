**Sekiryūzui (石竜芮; Takarashi / Ushizeri)**　[From Chikinkishō]

According to Yamato Honzō:
　In the Kyoto dialect, it is called
　　"Takarashi" or alternatively
　　"Tazeri."
　In Tsukushi (Kyushu), it is called
　　"Ushizeri" or
　　"Ubazeri."
　In the Wamyō Ruijushō, it appears as
　　"Fukazumi."

In the Chikinkishō, this plant is referred to as "Tagarashi (多賀良之)," and there are very many varieties. [The author] has examined this personally and corrected the record accordingly.

Listed in the Water Plants (水草) section of the Yamato Honzō.
[Seal: Medicinal Herbs (薬草)]

Copied at the Ukōan (雨草庵) studio during the latter part of the third lunar month (Kōsen / Gosen), year Mizunoto-Hitsuji (癸未, 1823).

According to the Wamyō Ruijushō:
　Sekiryūzui (石竜芮) — read "Fukazumi"
　　The Bencao (Honzō) states: "Sekiryūzui"
　　[Pronunciation: read as "Ruì" (如鋭反)]
　　Japanese name: Fukazumi (布加豆三)

According to the Jiuhuang Bencao (救荒本草):
　Kaikai-san (回回蒜; "Muslim garlic")
　　One name: Mizu-koshō (水胡椒; "water pepper")
　　Another name: Sasorikogusa (蠍虎草; "scorpion lizard plant")

---

According to the Kyūkō Yafu (救荒野譜):
　"Yushakushaku (油灼灼; Takarashi)" grows along the water's edge, and its leaves are glossy. There is also "Koshōsai (胡椒菜; Takarashi)," also known as "Suikin (水菫)." The Bencao of Prince Zhōu-xiàn-wáng refers to it as such, but [in my view] its form differs considerably. The illustration of "Yushakushaku" does, however, agree with this plant [Sekiryūzui]. This is indeed the "Yushakushaku = Sekiryūzui" of the preceding volume. The alternative name "Koshō (胡椒; pepper)" derives from its flavor, while "Yushakushaku" derives from its color (glossy/oily appearance). In sum, these three items are one and the same species with three different names — [I] find this very persuasive. Both illustrations are included in the present volume.
