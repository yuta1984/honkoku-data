## Matsu (松 / Japanese Pine, *Pinus* spp.)

*Wakan Sansai Zue* (Encyclopedia of Japan and China), section on fragrant trees, states:
　*Matsu* (松, pine; Tang-dynasty reading: *Son*). Phonetic gloss: *shōyō-setsu* (祥容切).
　*Sho* (枩, alternate character, same meaning). *Yōboku* (容木, same). Japanese name: *Matsu* (万豆).
　*Shōkō* (松黄, "pine yellow"): commonly called *Midori* (美止利, "green").
　*Zui* (蕤; reading: *zui*/橤). *Zui* (甤, same) ■ — refers to the place where the stamens/pine catkins grow.

　　　The pine does not change color even when subjected to frost and snow;
　　　it is therefore compared to *teishin* (貞心, a steadfast, loyal heart)
　　　and called *teiboku* (貞木, "a tree of steadfast virtue").

*Wamyō Ruijushō* states:
　　　*Matsu* (松): The *Kanjo* (漢書, Book of Han) uses it in the sense of "planting green pines."
　　　　　The character is a *fanqie* spelling of "Shōyō" (祥容); also written as 榕, as seen
　　　　　in the *Tōin* (唐韻, Tang Rhymes dictionary). Japanese name: *Matsu* (万豆).

*Nōgyō Zensho* (Complete Book of Agriculture) states:
　The pine is the chief (長) among all trees; hence the character is written with the wood radical (木偏) and the character *kimi* (公, "lord/noble").
　There are two-needle, three-needle, and five-needle varieties, none of which change color through the four seasons.
　Both in Japan and China, the pine is greatly admired and treasured.
　Beneath a thousand-year-old pine, *bukuryō* (茯苓, *Poria cocos*, a medicinal fungus) is to be found;
　likewise on the pine grows *nenashikazura* (兎糸子, dodder, *Cuscuta japonica*);
　and in the roots, amber (琥珀) is said to be found. All of these are excellent medicines.
　Pine resin, when refined, is applied to bowstrings. Beyond that, it has countless uses in medicinal plasters and other preparations.
　The word "*Matsu*" carries the meaning of "*tamotsu*" (保つ, "to preserve/maintain"), with the first part abbreviated.
　Originally interchangeable with "*mato*," it is a tree that long preserves life.
　The *Shiki* (史記, Records of the Grand Historian), "Turtle Strategy" (*Kisakuden*) chapter, states: "Pine and cypress are the chiefs among all trees, and they guard the gates and lanes of homes."
　According to the *Shiki*, when the First Emperor of Qin climbed Mount Tai and a sudden violent storm arose, he took shelter beneath a certain tree.
　He thereupon conferred upon that tree the title of "Five Grand Masters" (*go-taifu*).
　The historical record did not initially specify what kind of tree it was;
　Ying Shao (応劭) was the first to state that it was a pine, and so
　(the pine) came to receive the rank of Grand Master.

*Yamato Honzō*:
　Pine (松)

*Shokumostu Honzō* (Food Materia Medica), commentary states:
　Pine flower (松花)
　Alternate name: Pine yellow (松黄)

## Sumire (スミレ / Violet, *Viola* spp.)

[Seal] Flowering plants

*Yamato Honzō* states:
　*Shika-jichō* (紫花地丁, "purple-flowered ground nail"); Japanese name: *Komahiki-gusa* (コマヒキクサ)
　Also: *Kin-ka* (菫花, *Sumiregusa*)
　　　　In Kyoto, called "*Sumōtori*" (相撲取り, "sumo wrestler")
　　　　In Tsukushi (Kyushu), called "*Tono-no-uma*" (殿の馬, "the lord's horse")

The author (Genju/Mōri Baien) reflects and states:
　The name *sumire* (菫) appears in various forms across different texts.
　The *Jiukuang Bencao* (*Kyūkō Honzō*, Famine Relief Materia Medica) records it as "*Kinkinsai*" (菫々菜) and "*Sentōsō*" (箭頭草, "arrowhead plant"). This is identified
　in the *Wakan Sansai Zue* as "*Kinkinsai*" (ヤハズソウ, arrowhead plant) — [having compared various accounts with "kin-ka" (菫花)]. The same work also records *sumire* (菫) as "*aoi*" (葵), "*ku-kin*" (苦菫), and "*ku*" (苦).
　*Yamato Honzō* treats *Shika-jichō* (*Sumire*, *Komahiki-gusa*) as a separate species with the alternate name *Kinkinsai*.
　The *Wakan Sansai Zue* regards *Shika-jichō*, *Kinkinsai*, and *Kin* (菫) as three distinct species.
　However, the name "Sentōsō" (箭頭草, arrowhead plant) also appears under *Shika-jichō*.
　These three are in fact all the same single species.
　As for "*Kinsai*" (菫菜), it is a separate plant entirely — it refers to *nozeri* (野芹, wild parsley/water dropwort, *Oenanthe javanica*).
　The various names for *sumire* recorded across different texts remain a matter of uncertainty and confusion.
