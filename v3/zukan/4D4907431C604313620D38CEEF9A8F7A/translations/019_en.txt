## 【AOKI (Japanese Laurel / Aucuba japonica)】

Zōho Tashiki-hen, Trees Section
　　　　　Aoki (阿於幾)

[Seal] Garden Trees

According to Yamato Honzō:
　　Ahaki/Aoki (檍)　also known as: Mannen-eda (Everlasting Branch)
　　　　　　　　　also: Tōsei (冬青, Winter Green)
　　　　　　　　Its flowers
　　　　　　　　　are called Mannen-hana (Everlasting Flower)
　　　　　　　　　　　and also Aoki (青木, Green Tree)

The Tōyō-sango (Peach-leaf coral, i.e., large-leafed Aoki), the small-leafed Aoki,
and the long-leafed Aoki are all of the same family. The Setsurisango (Yukizato-sango,
"snow coral") is popularly known as Shimofuri-aoki (frost-speckled Aoki). Its leaves are
mostly large, and it is not the variegated variety, but a naturally growing specimen.

　　Sixth day of Kisaragi (Yayoi month), Mizunoe-Uma year

According to Wamyō Ruijushō:
　　Ahaki (檍)　Pronounced "oku." The Nihon Shoki Shiki states: "Ahaki (阿波木)."
　　　　　　　　　Upon reflection, it also appears as an alternate name for the oak (Kashi).
　　　　　　　　　The commentary of the Erya states: "It belongs to the catalpa (Azusa) family."


According to Kihinkyō and Honzō Ikkagen:
　Tōyō-sango (Peach-leaf coral / Aoki)

## 【NAZUNA (Shepherd's Purse / Capsella bursa-pastoris)】

Zōho Tashiki-hen, Soft and Smooth Plants Section:
　　Nazuna (薺, Sai)　Japanese names: Natsu (那豆) / Na (那)
　　Alternative name: Gosei-sō (護生草, "Life-protecting herb")
　　The seed is called Sai (蒫)

　　　　　　■Natsuna (荖蔍) 〔Shinsen Jikyō〕　Nazuna (薺)　Japanese name: Nani (那耳)〔Kyōyaku Honzō〕


Kyūkō Yafu (救荒野譜):
　　Nazuna (薺菜児) 〔Chinese name〕
　　　　　　　　　Ninth day of Kisaragi (Yayoi month), Mizunoe-Uma year
　　Nazuna flower　Keimōzai (鶏毛菜)　〔Wamyōshō〕


[Seal] Vegetable Category
[Seal] Medicinal Herb

According to Wamyō Ruijushō:
　　Nazuna (薺)　In Cui Yüxi's Shokukyō (食経), it states: "Sai (薺)" — the character is a combination of the readings of "ji" (辞) and "kei" (啓), upper-tone reading. Japanese name: Natsuna (奈都那).

According to Wakan Sansai Zue:
Buddhist monks take its stems and use them as a tool for stirring up lamp flames,
and it can also repel mosquitoes and moths. For this reason it is called Gosei-sō (護生草, "Life-protecting herb").
Its taste is sweet. Shi Kuang (師曠) said:
"When the year is about to be prosperous, the sweet herb (甘草) grows first"
　[The sweet herb here refers to Nazuna.]
"When the year is about to bring hardship, the bitter herb (苦草) grows first"
　[The bitter herb here refers to Teireki (葶藶, field pennycress).]
Note: It is one of the seven vegetables used in the seven-herb rice porridge (nanakusa-gayu) eaten on the seventh day of the first month.
Its seedpods form a triangular shape, wider at the tip and narrow at the base,
resembling the plectrum (bachi) of a shamisen.
When children rub two of these pods together,
they make a sound. For this reason, it is also called Sansen-sō (三線草, "Three-string herb").
