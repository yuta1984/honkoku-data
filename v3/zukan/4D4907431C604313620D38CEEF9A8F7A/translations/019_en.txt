　　　　　　　　　　Shepherd's purse flower　Chicken-feather vegetable　<<glossed: Wamyō-shō>>
　　　　　　　　　　　　　<<glossed: Keimō-zai>>

According to Wakan Sansai Zue:
Buddhist practitioners take its stem and stand it as a lamp
staff. It repels mosquitoes and flies, hence named Goshō-sō
(Life-protecting grass). Its taste is sweet. Master Kuang said:
When the year is about to be abundant, sweet grass grows first <<glossed: sweet>>
<<glossed: grass refers to shepherd's purse>> When the year is about to be bitter, bitter grass grows
first <<glossed: bitter grass refers to draba>>
It is one of the seven herbs in the vegetable porridge of the seventh day of the first month.
The seeds it bears are triangular, large at the bottom and narrow at the top, resembling a
shamisen plectrum. When children rub two of them together,
they make a sound, hence called "three-string grass"
