【CHINESE MILK VETCH (Renge-so / Astragalus sinicus)】

*Phonetic reading: Saihesei / Renge-bana*

Children in the Kinai region call it "Renge-bana" (lotus flower).
In Tsukushi (Kyushu), it is called "Hōzō-hana."
In the common speech of Musashi Province, it is called "Renge-so" (lotus grass).
In the Saijiki (almanac), it is listed as "Gokei" (five shapes/forms).

According to Taihō Baryō-hō (Taihō Veterinary/Horse Treatment Manual):
　　Kiran-so (喜蘭草)
　　　Commonly known as Saihesei (砕米薺)
　　　　　Genge-hana
　　　　　Colloquially, Renge-so

Kaishi-en Gaden (Mustard Seed Garden Painting Manual):
　*Phonetic reading: Ziyunying (紫雲英) / Renge-so*
　End of Koshin (third month), Mizunoto-Hitsuji year
　Ninth day, copied directly at Matsubara with a portable inkstone

In the Jiuhuang Bencao ("Famine Relief Materia Medica"), "Saihesei" (Broken-rice Shepherd's Purse) does not refer to the plant's shape; rather, what is illustrated resembles what is today called the lotus flower (renge). In any case, the meaning of the name "Saihesei" has not yet been explained. In the Honzō Ikkagen, the plant "Purple-flowered Ground Violet" (Zihua Diding) is given the alternate name "Saihesei," and so on.

【GARLAND CHRYSANTHEMUM (Shungiku / Glebionis coronaria)】

According to Zoho Tashiki-hen, Pungent Vegetables category:
　　Dōkō (同蒿, garland chrysanthemum) [Japanese name: unclear]　[Additional alternate name:] Hōkō (蓬蒿)

According to Shokkyō (食競):
　　Tōkō (茼蒿)
　　　Kōrai-giku [popular name] (Korean chrysanthemum)
　　　Shungiku (spring chrysanthemum)　Mujin-so (inexhaustible grass) [historical anecdote omitted]
　　　Copied on the fifth day of mid-spring, Mizunoe-Uma year

[Seal] Vegetable category
[Seal] Medicinal herb

The author states:
In the Bencao (Materia Medica),
　it is written that "Nokiku (wild chrysanthemum), also called Gui (苦意),
　is the spring chrysanthemum, not the wild chrysanthemum of
　Saiho (薺蒿)," and so on. This theory is
　incorrect. The wild chrysanthemum opens small yellow
　flowers in autumn, while Dōkō opens its flowers in spring, resembling
　chrysanthemum—hence the name. The shapes of their flowers and
　leaves differ greatly.
