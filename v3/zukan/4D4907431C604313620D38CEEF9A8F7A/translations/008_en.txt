## Tsukushi / Sugina (Field Horsetail / Common Horsetail)

Spring of Kanoto-Mi (辛巳, 1821)　　[Seal: Vegetable category]

　　天花菜 (Tenkwasai) — Tsukutsukushi (horsetail sprout)
土筆 (Tsukushi / horsetail fertile shoot)

*From the appendix of "Yamato Honzō" (Yamato Materia Medica):*
　　It grows in mountain valleys and fields. In spring, it first produces a flower-spike resembling a brush (筆).
　　Separately, it produces stems and leaves, which are called Sugina (杉菜, "cedar grass"), as they resemble cedar. The Chinese name
　　is not yet clearly known. It has not been encountered in Chinese texts either. Some say
　　that in "Honzō Kōmoku" (Bencao Gangmu), under the entry for mokuzoku (wood horsetail), there is an entry for "Monkei" (問荊),
　　also called "Setsuzokusō" (接続草, "connecting grass"), and suggest this may be the Tsukushi.
　　　　　　　　(And so forth.)

*From "Honzō Ikka-gen" (Personal Views on Materia Medica):*
　蕒 (Sugina) — has eleven names: Gyūshin (牛脣), ■Dan, Sōshisō (相思草), Danchōsō (断腸草),
　　　　　Shūfusō (愁婦草), Sōsō (霜草), Ryōsamon (寮沙問), Ansokusō (按続草),
　　　水瀉花 (Suishaka), 筆頭菜 (Hittōsai). The name "Suisha" (水瀉) is said to be erroneous.
　　My (the author's) own assessment:
　　　The leaf-bearing plant is Sugina (杉菜), and the spore-bearing shoot is Tsukushi (土筆).
　　　Mōkō (麻黄, ephedra) is the male; Sugina is the female — so it is said.

## Hirado Tsutsuji (Hirado Azalea)

[Seal: Flowering tree]
　　　　Copied on a spring day of Kanoe-Tatsu (庚辰) year

*From "Wakan Sansai Zue" (Japanese-Chinese Illustrated Encyclopedia), section on poisonous plants:*
　　平戸躑躅 (Hirado Tsutsuji / Hirado Azalea)
　　　　琉球躑躅 (Ryūkyū Tsutsuji / Ryūkyū Azalea)
　　　On examination: Hirado is a place name in Kyushu.
　　　This plant originally came from the Ryūkyū Islands, hence the name.
　　　Those with white flowers are called "Ryūkyū," while those with purple
　　　flowers are called "Tsutsuji" (azalea).

## Kirishima Tsutsuji (Kirishima Azalea)

*From "Wakan Sansai Zue," section on poisonous plants:*
　　　　映山紅 (Yōsankō / "Mountain-reflecting red") — known as Kirishima
　　　　　紅躑躅 (Kō-tsutsuji / Red Azalea)
　　　　　　Commonly called "Kirishima" (岐利之末)
　　In "Honzō Kōmoku" (Bencao Gangmu), 山躑躅 (Mountain Azalea; also known as 山石榴 and 杜鵑花) and
　　映山紅 (紅躑躅, Red Azalea) are treated as one and the same plant.
　　Now [in this text] they are distinguished as two separate plants.

*From "Zōho Tashiki-hen" (Expanded Polyglot Encyclopedia), section on poisonous plants:*
　　山躑躅 (Santekiryoku / Mountain Azalea)　Japanese name: Tsutsuji　Expanded alternate name: 紅躑躅 (Kō-tsutsuji)
　　　山石榴 (Sansekiō) · 映山紅 (Yōsankō) · 杜鵑花 (Tōkenka / Azalea flower)

　　　　　My own assessment: "Honzō Kōmoku" treats 山躑躅 and 映山紅 as a single plant, and there is in fact some basis
　　　　　for considering them to be one, as can be understood from the "Tashiki-hen." 映山紅, whose flowers are
　　　　　deep crimson and which, when in bloom, color the entire mountain — could this be how the name arose? Perhaps it is a
　　　　　variety of 山躑躅, and a separate name was later assigned to it as an alias. This matter warrants further study.
