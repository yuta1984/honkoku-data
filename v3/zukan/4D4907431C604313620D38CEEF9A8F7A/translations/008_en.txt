【TSUKUSHI (HORSETAIL SHOOTS), SUGINA (FIELD HORSETAIL)】
Year of Metal Snake, Spring　Vegetable category

Heaven Flower Vegetable (Tenkwasai)　Tsukushi
Earth Brush

Yamato Honzo Appendix
Grows in mountain valleys and wild fields. In spring month, first produces flowers like a brush.
Separately produces stems and leaves, named sugina (field horsetail). Resembles cedar. Chinese name
not yet clear. Not yet seen in Chinese books. Some say
that in the Honzo Komoku appendix to mokuzoku (scouring rush), there is monkyo (field horsetail).
One name is setsuzoku-so (connecting grass), this might be tsukushi
And so forth

According to Honzo Ikkagen
Sen (Sugina) has eleven names: gyu-shin, ■-dan, soshi-so, dancho-so
shufu-so, shimo-so, ryosa-mon, ansoku-so
suisha-ka, hitto-sai. The name suisha is said to be incorrect.
My consideration:
The leaves are called sugina, the flowers are called tsukushi.
Mao (ephedra) is male, sugina is female, and so forth

【HIRADO AZALEA】
Flowering tree
Spring day of Metal Dragon year

According to Wakan Sansai Zue, Poisonous Plants section
Hirado azalea (Hirado tsutsuji)
Ryukyu azalea (Ryukyu tsutsuji)
Upon examination, Hirado is a place name in Kyushu
Originally came from Ryukyu, hence the name.
White-flowered ones are called Ryukyu, purple-
flowered ones are called tsutsuji

【KIRISHIMA AZALEA】
According to Wakan Sansai Zue, Poisonous Plants section
Eisanko (Kirishima)
Red azalea
Commonly called Kiri-no-mi

Honzo Komoku treats mountain azalea (mountain pomegranate, cuckoo flower)
and eisanko (red azalea) as one plant.
Now they are treated as two separate plants.

According to Zoho Tashiki-hen, Poisonous Plants section
Mountain azalea (Santekichoku)　Japanese name: Tsushi　Alternative name: Red azalea
Mountain pomegranate (Sekiryu)　Eisanko (Yosanko)　Cuckoo (Token) flower

My examination: The reason why mountain azalea and eisanko are treated as one plant in Honzo
is not without basis. That they are one plant can be known from Tashiki-hen. Eisanko has
deep red flowers, when flowering they cover the entire mountain. Perhaps this is the origin of the name. Whether it's
a separate variety of mountain azalea that later received a different name as an alternative designation requires further study of the literature.
