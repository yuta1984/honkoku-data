【Tennansho / The illustration may correspond to what is now called Urashimasō】

From Honzō Wage:
　Tennansho (天南星, *Arisaema* sp.)
　　Other names: Koshō (虎掌), Kōkotsu (虎膏), Kikonnyaku (鬼蒟蒻)
　　Japanese name: Oosоhi (於於曽比)
　◦ Yama-konnyaku (Mountain konjac)

　　In the Honzō [Bencao], it is called "Tiger's Palm" (虎掌); the smaller variety is called Yuhatsu (由跋).
　　Its form resembles konjac, and it grows from a perennial root in spring. It resembles Tennansho,
　　with stems producing two stalks and three leaves. The leaves resemble those of Hange (*Pinellia ternata*) but are larger. The flower resembles that of Nansho (南星), but differs in that
　　　　　　it is shaped like a stirrup (aburumi), hence the Japanese name Musashi-aburumi
　　　　　　(武蔵鐙, Musashi Stirrup). In autumn, the fruit ripens to a vermilion color, it is said.
　　　　　　This (Musashi-aburumi) is a separate plant. The present plant bears one flower
　　　　　　per stem, with leaves arranged like an open hand. It closely
　　　　　　resembles konjac. It is called Nansho (南星).

【Seal】Medicinal herb

Yamato Honzō says:
　　Li Shizhen states that the smaller form is called "Yuhatsu" (由跋), which constitutes a distinct variety.
　　Upon examination, there is a plant in the mountains that resembles Tennansho but is smaller;
　　this is likely Yuhatsu. It is clear that Yuhatsu
　　is not of the same category as Tennansho.
　◦ The Bencao Gangmu (本草綱目) states: "Tennansho blooms in the fifth month with flowers resembling a
　　snake's head, yellow in color." The remaining features—fruit and root—all match.
　　Tennansho is the same as the "Tiger's Palm" (虎掌) in the original Bencao. 《Interlinear note: The smaller form is called Yuhatsu》
　　In ancient prescriptions, Tiger's Palm was frequently used, and
　　Tennansho was not mentioned.
　　《Interlinear note: The name Tennansho began in the Tang dynasty》 The name "Tiger's Palm" derives from the shape of the leaves
　　resembling it, not from the root. The name "South Star" (南星)
　　was given because the root is round and white, resembling the
　　appearance of the "Old Man Star" (Canopus).
　◦ Genju [Mōri Baien] respectfully says: What is commonly called Tennansho in this country is in fact Yama-konnyaku
　　(Mountain Konjac, *Amorphophallus* sp.). True Tennansho does not produce a mouse-tail-like appendage from the center of the flower, but rather
　　produces a round, ball-like protrusion about the size of a little finger. The leaves and stems grow in two pairs,
　　and the flower emerges from their fork. Yama-konnyaku has one stem and one flower and is a separate plant.

　　Kinoe-Saru year, Koshitsu (third month), twenty-fifth day of the latter period
　　Painted from life using a branch cut in the mountains

From the Wakan Sansai Zue's commentary:
　　Tiger's Palm is the original name. The Wamyōshō also has Tiger's Palm
　　but not Tennansho. Today, on the contrary, there is no one who recognizes Tiger's Palm. It is suspected that this
　　is an anomalous variety arising from the seedlings of Tiger's Palm, and
　　that it was named Tennansho. 《Interlinear note: One substance with different varieties and names》 In recent times also,
　　further anomalous varieties have appeared. 《Interlinear note: Kankisō (歓喜草), Musashi-aburumi (武蔵鐙)》 and their kind are examples. Whereas Tiger's Palm and Tennansho resemble each other,
　　they are each separate. Furthermore, Nansho also has two varieties: one with pale blue flower spathes resembling a mouse's tail,
　　which does not exceed one shaku in height, and looks like a spoon (saji) and a Buddhist halo;
　　and another without a mouse's tail that grows to about two shaku in height.
　　These are naturally one category with two varieties. 《Interlinear note: The Sansai Zue and the Bencao invariably present two illustrations of Tiger's Palm and Tennansho, making clear that they are two separate plants》
