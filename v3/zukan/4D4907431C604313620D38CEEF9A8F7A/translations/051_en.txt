【Dog Mustard (Inunazuna)】
According to Kyūkō Honzō:
　　　Water radish (Niwataiko)

Explained in Honzō Wamyō:
　　　Teireki
　　　　　　Alternative names: Tei-reki, Tenkō, Ōmuro
　　　　　　　　　Ōteki, Kuyaku
　　　　　　Japanese name: Ashinazuna
　　　　　　　　　Inunazuna

According to Kyūkō Yafu:
　　　Kōsei (Dog mustard)

In Wamyō Ruijūshō it says:
　　　Teireki-shi (Hamatakana)
　　　Ashinazuna, Hamaseri

　　　In Honzō it says the Japanese name for teireki-shi is Hamitakana
　　　In one source it says Ashinazuna, also called Hamiseri

Kinoe-Saru year, Koshō month, twentieth
Jū-day, painted from life in the field

【Cercis (Hanazuō)】
According to Yamato Honzō:
　　　Shikei (Suōgi)
　　　　Hanasō

In Zōho Tashiki-hen, Shrub section, it says:
　　　Shikei (Japanese name unclear) Alternative name: Shishu (from Shūi) The bark is called Nikukō (from Kōmoku) Supplementary alternative name: Nikushō

Same year, same month, twenty-third day
Painted from life

In Wakan Sansai Zue, Shrub section, it says:
　　　Shikei (Suō-no-ki)
　　　　Commonly called Suhō-boku

It is commonly called suhō-boku, but it is not true sappanwood.
According to the explanations and examinations in Honzō Kōmoku, the shapes of leaves and
fruit match well.

　In Yamato Honzō it says: Suhō-boku (alternative name) Suboku (Japanese name) Sunpaū
　In Wamyō Ruijūshō it says:
　　　Suhō: In Su Jing's Honzō annotations it says suhō (in Tang rhymes written as "hō," pronounced the same as "hō")
　　　Commonly called suhō, used by people for dyeing. I say that according to some people, this does not exist in our country.
　　　The Japanese name suhō refers to shikei flowers. What is called shikei flower
　　　refers to the herbaceous genkwa (shikeishu). According to Yamato Honzō, what is now separately
　　　commonly called shikei tree is a small tree. This tree is not
　　　a shikei tree. It is recorded that shikei tree and genkwa are two different things.
