【Cypress and Oak Group (柏・槲 etc.)】

According to Zōho Tashikihen Fragrant Wood Category:
　　Cypress, haku (柏)　Japanese name: Kae (加恵); commonly called Sohatate (曽波多天) nowadays
　　　　　　　　　　　Alternative name: Sokuhaku (側柏, Chinese arborvitae)
　　　　　　　　　　　Additional alternative name: Kiku (椈)
　　[Author's note:]
　　　　Haku (柏) is not the same as kashiwa (oak). Its timber is called Asunaro (阿須奈呂, Thujopsis dolabrata).
　　Also called "Konote-gashiwa" (児手柏, Platycladus orientalis). Kashiwa (oak) is hō (枹). Haku (柏) is called "tōe" [a type of sawara cypress].
　　It belongs to the pine family and is also close to cedar. Recording them all as the same is an error.

　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　25th day of Koshō month, same season
　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Quick copy at the Usōan (Rain Hermitage)

According to Wakan Sansai Zue, Mountain Fruit Category:

[Author's own investigation:]
　　The explanations in various books are muddled and unclear. The tree now called Kashiwa (加之波) is this one.
　　The sokuhaku (側柏) in Yamato Honzō is also incorrect. The collected explanations in Sansai Zue correspond well.


◦ According to Honkō (Honzō Kōmoku):
　　Kunugi (檞, Quercus acutissima) has two types. One kind that grows in thickets and is large is called Kashiwa (枹), also named Large-leafed Oak (大葉檪), with alternative names Boku (樸) and Kashiwa (檞).

◦ The names Ge (檞), Sa (柞), Reki (檪), Hō (枹), and Nara (楢) are all confused across various sources; I therefore establish separate entries for each.
　　Ge (檞) [large acorn-bearing oak], Hō (枹) [kashiwa oak], and Nara (楢) [oak] all have similar leaves but differ in size.
　　Ge (檞) is intermediate in size between Hō (枹) and Nara (楢).
　　Sa/Hahaso (柞) belongs to the same group as these three but is a different tree; its leaves are similar but distinct.
　　Sa (柞) and Reki (檪) are two names for the same tree.
　　Maruguri (丸栗, round chestnut), Sa-Reki (柞檪), Hō (枹), Ge (檞), Nara (孛落) tree, and Buna (橅, beech) are of one group—their leaves all fall in winter.
◦ The claim of scholar Cao that "old and new leaves of Sa (柞) alternate [i.e., new leaves push out the old]" is worth further investigation.
