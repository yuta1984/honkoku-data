Faithfully copied (with original illustration) on the 3rd day below mid-month of Koshō, Mizunoto-Hitsuji year


From Zōho Tashikihen (Supplemented Tashikihen), Fragrant Grasses Category:
　　Snake Bed Plant　[Japanese name] Lists Hirumushiro (pondweed); I record it in the relevant entry. As the terms overlap, I omit it here.
　　According to Honzō Kōmoku (Bencao Gangmu / Compendium of Materia Medica): Snakes and vipers love to lie beneath this plant and eat its seeds, hence the name. [Et cetera]


From Zōho Tashikihen, Fragrant Woods Category:
　　Haku (柏, cypress)　[Japanese name] Kae; commonly called Sohatate today　[Alternative name] Sokuhaku (Side Cypress / Platycladus orientalis)
　　　[Additional alternative name] Kiku (椈)
　　　[My commentary]
　　　　　Haku is not Kashiwa (oak); its
　　　　　timber is called Asunaro (Thujopsis dolabrata)
　　Also, what is called "Chigaeshi-gashiwa" (児手柏): Kashiwa is Hobu (枹); Haku is called Toe (刀ヱ).
　　It is related to pine and close to cedar. To conflate and record all of these together is an error.

According to Wakan Sansai Zukai (Illustrated Encyclopedia of Japan and China), Mountain Fruit Category:
　Hobu / Kashiwa (枹) [phonetic character]　Large-leaved Oak (大葉檪)　Poku (樸)

　　In the Wamyō-shō, 蔛 is read as Kashiwa;
　　Some also use the character 柏, but both are incorrect.

[My investigation]
　The explanations in various texts are mixed and confused, and I cannot determine the truth with certainty. What is today called Kashiwa is this tree. The Yamato Honzō's identification with Sokuhaku (side cypress) is also wrong. The collected explanations of the Sansai Zukai fit well.


◦ According to Honkō (Bencao Gangmu), Kunugi (oak, Quercus acutissima) has two varieties. One variety, which grows in clusters and is large, is named Hobu (Kashiwa) and called Large-leaved Oak (大葉檪). One name: Poku (樸) / Kashiwa (檞)
◦ Kashiwa (檞)　Hahaso (柞)　Oak (檪)　Hobu (枹)　Nara (楢) — Various explanations are mixed together in the commentaries. I now set up individual entries to record each.
　　Kashiwa (檞) is [large-acorned oak / large Nara], Hobu (枹) is [Kashiwa], Nara (楢) is [Nara oak] — all have similar leaves but differ in size. Kashiwa (檞) is intermediate between Hobu (枹) and Nara (楢).
　　Hahaso (柞) belongs to the same family as these three but is a separate species; its leaves are similar yet different. Hahaso (柞) and Oak (檪) are two names for one tree. Marukuri, Hahaso, Oak, Hobu, Kashiwa, Nara tree, and Funa (橅, Fagus) are all one group, and their leaves fall in winter. ◦ Master Cao says "the old and new leaves of Hahaso alternate," which deserves further investigation.
