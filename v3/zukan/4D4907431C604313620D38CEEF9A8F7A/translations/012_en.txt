## 【FUKUJUSŌ (Pheasant's Eye / *Adonis amurensis*)】

Written on New Year's Day of the Bunsei Mizunoe-uma year (1822), as the first brushwork of the year.
Faithfully copied at the snow-lit window (Sessō).

From *Yamato Honzō*:
　Fukujusō — also called Fukuduku-gusa
　　　　　　 Also known as Ganjitsu-gusa (New Year's Day Grass)

　　　As recorded in various books: Hōshunkyoku (報春菊) — Chinese name
　　　　Alternative name: Saikyoku (歳菊, "Year's Chrysanthemum")
　　　　Also: Kensaikyoku (献歳菊, "Chrysanthemum offered at New Year") 〔*Taiwan Fuzhi*〕

According to *Xicheng Wenjian Lu*:
　　　Xue Lian (雪蓮, "Snow Lotus") — [refers to Fukujusō]

## 【UME (Japanese Plum / *Prunus mume*)】

As recorded in the Gokōka (Five Fragrant Fruits) section of *Wakan Sansai Zue*:
　Ume (Japanese reading), Tang Chinese reading: Mui　〔phonetic gloss: zhī〕　槑 〔archaic character〕
　　　Japanese name: Umetsu (宇女)
　　　Currently also written/read as: Mume (牟女)

According to *Sancai Tuhui*:
There are four things to prize in the plum: prize rarity, not abundance; prize age, not youth; prize leanness, not fatness; prize the bud, not the open bloom.

○ According to *Helin Yulu*: In antiquity, the plum was valued for its fruit, and the beauty and fragrance of its flower were not spoken of. It was only from the Song dynasty onward that it began to be celebrated in poetry and prose.

○ In our country (Japan), in ancient times, when people said "flower" (hana), they meant the plum. Since the mid-Heian period, when people say simply "flower," they mean the cherry blossom.

○ According to *Shoku Nihongi*: In the seventh month of the tenth year of Emperor Shōmu's Tenpyō reign (738), the Emperor pointed to the plum tree before the palace hall and said: "Since last spring, I have wished to appreciate this tree, but have not yet had the opportunity to enjoy it." He therefore commanded the men of letters each to compose a verse on the plum tree, and thirty men of letters all composed poems expressing the spirit of spring.

> *Haru no yo no / yami wa ayanashi / ume no hana*
> *Iro koso miene / ka ya wa kakururu*
>
> (The darkness of a spring night is meaningless — though the color of the plum blossom cannot be seen, can its fragrance truly be hidden?)

【Flowering Tree】

Wang Ren of Baekje referred to the plum as "this flower." The god Sugawara (Sugawara no Michizane) always cherished the plum, leaving behind the name "Tobiume" (Flying Plum). From the words of the *Jǐjū* of the Jin dynasty came the name "Kōbunboku" (Tree of Literary Refinement). From the poem of Tachibana no Naokane came the name "Ōshuku-bai" (Plum where the warbler lodges). In the poems of Saigyō appears the plum of "Come and find me" (*tomeko kashi*). Such examples are too numerous to enumerate.

According to *Yamato Honzō* 〔as recorded in the Baiko (Plum Section)〕:
　Those growing wild that have not yet undergone cultivation or grafting
　　are called "Kōbai" (江梅, river plum), and also
　　named "Chokkyaku-bai" (直脚梅, straight-legged plum).
　　Sometimes these are called simply "Nobai" (野梅, wild plum).

Wild Plum (Nobai)
　　Middle ten days of the intercalary first month, Mizunoe-uma year (copied)
