## 【KAIDŌ — Crabapple / Flowering Apple (Malus species)】

### From Yamato Honzō (Great Japanese Herbal):
**Kaidō (海棠)**

In Tang Dynasty poet Jia Dan's *Hundred Flowers Manual (百花譜)*,
it is called "the immortal spirit among flowers (花中の神仙),"
and the people of China (Kaka / 華夏) also call it
"the noble friend among flowers (花中の名友)."

[Seal] Flowering Tree

〇 According to the *Wakan Sansai Zue* (Illustrated Encyclopedia of Japan and China), Mountain Fruits section:
　Kaidō-nashi (海棠梨) — "Kaikō"
　Tang Chinese pronunciation: Haitanrī (ハイタンリイ)

　　　All flower or tree names containing the character "sea (海)"
　　　have come from overseas.

Copied on the fifth day after the first full moon of Yayoi (third month), Mizunoe-Uma year.

According to Shen Li's *Treatise on Kaidō (海棠の譜)*:
　Only the variety of purple-brocade color is truly called "kaidō (海棠)";
　the rest are merely the flowers of the tō-nashi (棠梨, wild pear).

---

The author (Baien) states:
　What the world calls the "kaidō flower" is the variety depicted here in the illustration.
　Its flowers are pale red (淡紅色), and the variety said to resemble the apple (林檎) is called
　"kaidō." Its stems are short and its flowers large. The variety whose flowers
　range from deep to pale red, with long red stems that hang downward,
　is called *Suishi-kaidō* (垂糸海棠, "Weeping-thread kaidō") and is considered
　the finest of all kaidō flowers.
　There is also a variety associated with Du Zimei (杜子美, the poet Du Fu),
　known popularly in Japan as "ko-ringo (small apple / 小林檎)."
　Its flowers are pale red or pink-white, and it bears a great abundance of fruit.
　There is yet another variety called *Seifu-kaidō* (西府海棠).
　Kaidō varieties that have been imported in recent times generally do not bear much fruit.

## 【KAEDE — Japanese Maple (Acer species)】

According to *Honzō Ikke-gen* (One School's Words on Materia Medica):
　*Ki* (榿) — alternate name: *Ki* (機)　 *Kaete* (訶恵底)　 Red Leaves (紅葉)

　　Keikanju (雞冠樹 / Maple)
　　　　According to *Tashiki* (多識):
　　　　　Japanese name: *Kaite* (加伊天)

　　Mizunoe-Uma year, Koshin (third month, archaic reading),
　　Copied on the sixth day from life.

---

*Ki* (榿, Maple) has several varieties. As for the *fūju* (楓樹, Chinese sweet gum/liquidambar):
　・ The Chinese maple (唐楓) is an entirely different species, with leaves quite unlike the others.
　The *fūju* is rare in Japan; there is a large tree on Mount Nikkō.
　In Edo, the nursery and plant dealer "Gunpōen (群芳園)" also has a large tree.
　Other than these, the author has not yet seen any elsewhere.

---

According to the *Wakan Sansai Zue*, Aromatic Trees section:
　《Keikanboku (雞冠木) — read *Kaete*; Tang Chinese pronunciation: *Momichi* (モミチ)》
　　Keikanju (雞冠樹)
　　　　In the Man'yōshū: Kaetemoku (蝦蟇手木, "Frog-hand tree")
　　　　Japanese name: *Kabete-no-ki* (加倍天乃木)　Alternate: *Kabirute-no-ki* (加比留提乃木)
　　　　　　Commonly known as *Momichi* (毛美知, i.e., Momiji / 紅葉)
　　According to the *Bencao Gangmu* (本草綱目, Compendium of Materia Medica):
　　Keikanboku has several varieties; its leaves have pointed lobes like a frog's hand.
　　They generally divide into seven or eight lobes, sometimes nine,
　　and those with thirteen leaves are called "jūni-hitoe (十二一重, twelve-layered)."

---

*Yamato Honzō* states:
　　*Kabete* (機木)
　　　　Keikanboku (鶏冠木)

　In Japan, the character "楓 (*fū*)" is mistakenly read as *kaete*.
　In the *Wamyō Ruijū-shō*, "雞冠木" is read as *kaete*,
　and it is also called *kaerute*. This is because its leaves resemble
　the hands of a frog (*kaeru* / 蛙).

According to the *Wamyō Ruijū-shō*:
　《Keikanboku — read: *Kaete-no-ki*》
　　Yōshi Kango-shō states: Keikanboku — *Kabete-no-ki* (加倍天乃木)
　　Benshoku-rissei states: Keitōju — *Kabirute-no-ki* (加比留提乃木)
　　The author's note: These are all names for the same tree.

From the *Man'yōshū* (Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves):
　　Each time I see in my garden the maple (*kaede*) turning crimson (momijiru),
　　　　　there is not a moment I do not think of you, my love, and yearn for you.
