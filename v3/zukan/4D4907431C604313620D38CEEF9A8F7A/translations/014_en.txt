## 【SAKURA (Cherry Blossom)】

**From the Wamyō Ruijushō (Dictionary of Japanese Names):**
Cherry (Sakura): According to the Monjishūryaku (文字集略), "cherry" [Japanese reading: Sakura]. The fruit is as large as that of the cypress, with specimens having red, white, and black coloration at the tips.

**From the Zōho Tashikiden (Expanded Encyclopedia of Names), Section on Trees:**
Cherry blossom (pronunciation: Ōei) — Japanese name: Sakura (左久羅)

In Tang China, it seems as though cherry blossoms do not exist, as they are not recorded in the herbals (honzō), the Sancai Tuhui (三才図会), or the Sōmoku Gafu (草木画譜), and Chinese poets likewise do not praise them.

[Seal] Flowering Tree

Written on the 27th day of the second month (February), in the year Jingo (壬午, 1822).

---

**A poem by Song Lian (宋景濂):**
The appreciation of cherry blossoms is far greater in Japan than in Tang China.
They are as if covered by the glory of both peony and crabapple combined.
It is likely even Zhao Chang would have difficulty painting them.
When the spring breeze barely stirs up the snow, it blows forth their fragrance.

**A poem by Wang Jing Gong (Wang Anshi, 王荊公) on the Mountain Cherry:**
The mountain cherry embraces the rocks, casting shade upon the pine branches.
Compared to other flowers, it blooms the latest of all.
Only the spring wind, disliking its solitude,
Blows its fragrance across the water to let people know it is there.

○ Looking at this, we can see that in China too, cherry trees are not entirely absent.

---

**Emperor Heizei composed the first poem on cherry blossoms:**
Long ago, dwelling beneath a secluded cliff,
Its radiant brilliance illuminated all four directions.
Suddenly encountered when someone reached to pluck it,
It smiled and spread throughout the three months of spring.
It sends forth its spirit in varying abundance,
Its shading branches long and short.
How is it that this single thing
Commands the entire stage of the long spring as it pleases?

---

**The Nihon Kōki (日本後記) states:**
In the second month of the third year of Kōnin (弘仁三年, 812 AD) of Emperor Saga's reign, there was an imperial excursion (miyuki) to the Shinsen'en Garden. The Emperor viewed the flowering trees and had men of letters compose poems. The tradition of the Flower Banquet (Hana no En) began from this occasion.

## 【KAWAZAKURA (Birch-bark Cherry)】

Kawazakura (樺桜) — Pronunciation: Wa; Japanese names: Kawa (加波) or, in another account, Kaniwa (加仁波)

This is what is known as the single-petaled cherry. Separately, there is a tree called "Kaba" (birch), which is distinct from the cherry. It is listed in the Yamato Honzō (大和本草) under miscellaneous trees.
The Zihui (字彙) states: "Kaba (樺) is the name of a tree whose bark is used for covering bows." Thus it is a separate species of tree. In the Wamyōshō (倭名抄) as well, it appears to be listed as a separate species. However, beyond the bark of the cherry tree, the author had not yet encountered anything that could be called "kaba."

---

**From the Yamato Honzō (大和本草):**
In the poem "Early Departure from Ding Shan" (早発定山) by Shen Xiuwen (沈休文) in the Wenxuan (文選), the line reads: "The mountain cherry blossoms open, as if about to burst into flame." The commentary states: "It is the name of a fruit tree; the flower's vermilion color is like fire about to ignite."
Wang Jing Gong's poem states: "The mountain cherry embraces the rocks, reflected in the pine branches." Sima Wen Gong's poem states: "The red cherry scatters, and the apricot blossoms open." This shows that what China calls "cherry" (sakura) refers to a red-flowered tree. The cherry known in Japan does not exist in China. During the Enpō era (1673–1681), a Chinese visitor came to Nagasaki, and He Qingfu stated: "If it existed in China, it would have been recorded in Chinese books and celebrated in poetry and prose. The statement that there is no such tree in China is likely to be the truth." It is said that the tree exists in Korea, where it is called "Naki" (奈木).
