## 【RINGO (Apple, Malus pumila / Malus asiatica)】

From the Zōho Tashikihen (Supplemented Encyclopedia), section on Mountain Fruits:
　Apple blossom (林檎花)　Japanese name: Ringō (利牟古宇); commonly also called "Ringo" (利牟古).
　Alternative name: Raikin (来禽, "incoming bird") [from the Hōjō (法帖, calligraphy copybooks)]
　Additional alternative name: Bunrinrōka (文林郎果, "Fruit of the Wenlin Scholar")

From the Wamyō Ruijūshō (Japanese Names Encyclopedia):
　Rinkō (林檎): The Bencao (Honzō) commentary states that the character 林檎 is read with the sound of 禽 (kin/qin), and its Japanese name is Riukōu (利宇古宇).

From the Saijiki (歳時記, Seasonal Record):
　This is the plant known in Japan as "kaidō" (海棠, crabapple/flowering apple).
　It is said that it was first found floating down a river, whereupon a person named "Bunrin-soku" retrieved it and planted the seeds. The fruit was therefore named after him (Bunrinrōka, "Fruit of the Wenlin Scholar"). [From the Wakan Sansai Zue]

　Transcribed faithfully on the 10th day of Kosen (3rd lunar month), Mizunoto-Hitsuji year (Bunsei 6, 1823).

## 【NASHI (Pear, Pyrus pyrifolia)】

From the Zōho Tashikihen, section on Mountain Fruits:
　Pear (梨, ri)　Japanese name: Nashi (那志)
　Alternative names: Kaika (快果, "refreshing fruit"), Kasō (果宗, "chief of fruits"), Gyokunyu (玉乳, "jade milk"), Mitsufu (蜜父, "honey father")

　Pear blossom [from the same work]: also called "Tōri-ka" (棠梨花, Pyrus calleryana blossom). [Note: Tōnashi (棠梨) has three-lobed leaves and shares the name with kaidō (海棠).]

　　　　　[From the Kokin Wakashū (古今和歌集, Collection of Ancient and Modern Japanese Poetry):]
　　　　　　At Afu no Ura, a pear tree spreading its single bough wide—
　　　　　　whether it bears fruit or not, let us lie beneath and lean upon it.
　　　　　　(Anonymous)

From the same work, Tashikihen, same category:
　　◦ Gari (鵞梨, "goose pear")　Japanese name, tentatively: Kokanashi (古加那志)　Alternative name: Menri (綿梨, "cotton pear")
　　◦ Kōsuiri (香水梨, "fragrant water pear")　Japanese name, tentatively: Tsunashi (豆那志)　Alternative name: Shōri (消梨, "melting pear")
　　◦ Kanshōri (含消梨, "melt-in-the-mouth pear")

Transcribed faithfully on the following morning of the same spring month, after washing the ink stone.
【Seal】Fruit and tree category

From the Wamyō Ruijūshō: Nashiko (梨子, nashi — pear)
From the Tōin (唐韻, Tang Rhymes dictionary): The character 梨 is formed by the fanqie (反切) combination of 刀 and 指, and its Japanese name is Nashi (奈之).
From the Kenmeien (兼名苑, Garden of Combined Names): Nashiko (梨子); alternative name: Kanshō (含消, "melt-in-the-mouth").

## 【HEBI-ICHIGO and related plants (Snake Strawberry, Duchesnea indica and related species)】

From the Jiuhuang Bencao (救荒本草, Famine Relief Herbal):
　Kekkanка (鶏冠果, Kuchinawa-ichigo — "snake strawberry"):
　　Alternative name: Noyōbai (野楊梅, "wild waxberry")

Snake strawberry (蛇苺, Jahō):
　From the Jikinsho (地錦鈔, Notes on Ground Brocade):
　　The flower of hebi-ichigo (snake strawberry) is also called "o-hebi-ichigo" (male snake strawberry). "Jakan" (蛇含, snake-embracing plant) shares the same name.

　From the Zōho Tashikihen, section on Climbing Plants (蔓草類):
　　Snake strawberry (蛇苺, jahō)　Japanese name, tentatively: Hebi-ichigo (倍比伊知古)
　　Additional alternative names: Jahō (蛇藨, snake bramble); Jiichigo (地苺, "ground strawberry") [from the Kaihен (会編, Compiled Encyclopedia)]; Sanpō (蚕苺, "silkworm strawberry")

Transcribed faithfully on the 25th day of Kosen (3rd lunar month), Mizunoto-Hitsuji year (Bunsei 6, 1823).
