## 【SUMIRE (Violet / *Viola mandshurica*)】

**Zǐhuā dīdīng** [Purple Flower Earth-nail] (Shikwachichō)　Japanese name: Sumire (Violet)　Alternative names: Dúxíng hǔ [Lone Tiger] (Tokukyōko) [from *Bencao Gangmu*]
　　Mǐ bùdài [Rice Cloth-bag] (Heihotei) [from *Zōho Imei*]　Jiàntóu cǎo [Arrow-head Grass] (Sentsu-sō) [from *Bencao Gangmu*]
　　Yángjiǎo zǐ [Goat Horn Seeds] (Yōkaku-shi) [from *Hiun/Hiinkun*]

According to *Jiuhuang Bencao* [Famine Herbs]:
　Jǐnjǐn (Sumire) cài [Violet Herb]
　　Also known as:
　　　Arrow-head Grass

Violet (Sumire) flower　— Alternative names recorded in the entry with the preceding illustration
　　Two varieties

From *Maoshi* [Book of Odes]:
　　Jǐn (Tsubosumire / Small Violet)

　　　Written on the thirteenth day of the third month (Gosen/Kōshin),
　　　　　Bunsei 7th year, Kinoe-Saru (1824)
　　　　　Drawn true-to-life from plants in my garden

【Flowering Herb】

According to *Wakan Sansai Zue* [Japanese-Chinese Illustrated Encyclopedia], Wetland Herbs section:
　　Jǐn (Sumire) [pronunciation: Qín]　Bitter Violet　Bitter Mallow　Japanese name: Sumire
　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Commonly called Sumōkusa [Sumo Grass]

According to Su Gong in *Bencao Gangmu*: "Jǐn cài" is precisely the Sumire. Liu Yuxi states: "The *Shuowen* says jǐn has roots like shepherd's purse and leaves slender as willow," etc. — what he describes as "bitter violet" is an inversion of ancient phrasing, just as licorice root (gancao) is called "great bitter" (dàkǔ). (My note: the passage about leaves resembling willow was copied earlier.)
Children play a game where two flower stalks are hooked together and pulled against each other; the side whose calyx breaks off loses (make). Hence the name Sumōgusa [Sumo Grass / Angle-force Grass]. Among the three plants — Gennoshōko (*Geranium thunbergii*), Sumire (Violet), and Tōsō (Sword Grass) — the name "Sumire" is the most widely known.

## 【HAKOBE (Chickweed / *Stellaria media*)】

According to *Shokukyō* [Food Competition]:
　　Arrow-head Grass

According to *Wamyō Ruijushō* [Japanese Names Classified Dictionary]:
　　Fánlóu [繁蔞] (Hakubera)　— As described in *Bencao* [Fánlóu, two pronunciations combining Fán and Lóu]
　　　　Japanese name: Hakubera (Hakubera)
　　　Táo Yǐnjū [Tao Hongjing] said: This is the same as Jīcháng cǎo [Chicken-intestine Herb]

　　　　First day of the third month (Yayoi), Mizunoto-Hitsuji year (1823)　【Medicinal Herb】
　　　　Sketched in a free-brush manner 　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　 【Vegetable Category】

According to *Zōho Tashikihen* [Expanded Identification Compendium], Soft and Smooth Plants section:
　　Fánlóu [繁■] (Hanro)　Japanese names: Hakubera (Hakubera), also called Hakobe
　　　　　　Expanded name: Tsuruhakobe [Creeping Chickweed]
　　　　　　Alternative names: Jí cǎo [Nourishing Herb] [from *Zōho Imei*]; Sōrō [繁縷] [from *Erya*]
　　　　　　Gāo [蔜] [pronunciation: Jiào]; Liáolóu [寮縷] [from *Guoya*]; Goose-intestine Herb (Gachōsai)

Recorded in *Engishiki* [Procedures of the Engi Era], *Ribu Ōki* [Prince Li's Record], *Chōmei Shiki Monogatari* [Chōmei's Four Seasons Tale], and various other works as one of the Seven Spring Herbs of Jinjitsu (the seventh day of the New Year).

## 【KAKIDŌSHI (Ground Ivy / *Glechoma hederacea*)】

From *Yamato Honzō* [Japanese Materia Medica]:
　　Jī xuě cǎo [Snow-accumulation Herb] (Kakitōshi)
　　[Shakusetsusō]
　　　In *Bencao*:
　　　Hú bòhé [Foreign Mint] (Ko-hakka)
　　　Tsuruhakka [Creeping Mint]

From *Kyūkō Yafu* [Wild Plants for Famine Relief]:
　　Dìqián'ér [Ground Coin Seedling] (Kakitōshi)　【Creeping/Climbing Herb】
　　〔Also known as〕 Jī xuě cǎo [Snow Herb]

Recorded in *Zōho Tashikihen*, Aromatic Herbs section:
　　Jī xuě cǎo [積雪草]　Japanese name: Tsubokusa (Tsubokusa); upon investigation, also Tsuruhaka
　　[Shakusetsu]　
　　　Alternative names: Kaiso [Sea Perilla] [from *Zōho Imei*]; Húbòhé [Foreign Mint] [from *Daihō-hō / Dàbǎofāng*]
　　　Dìqián cǎo [Ground Coin Herb] [from *Tang Bencao*]
　　　Liánqián cǎo [Linked Coin Herb] [from *Yaku-zu / Medicinal Illustrations*]

*Wakan Sansai Zue* states: Jī xuě cǎo is commonly called Kakidōshi [Hedge-Passer]. The illustration in *Bencao Xinxu* [Supplement to Bencao] showing branches is incorrect. The reading of Tsubokusa in the *Wamyō Shō* [Wamyō Ruijushō] is also incorrect. Tsubokusa [Pot Herb] is listed separately. It has not yet been found in *Bencao Gangmu*. The flowers remain a subject for further investigation.

According to *Wakan Sansai Zue*, Wetland Herbs section:
　　Jǐn (Sumire) ・ Sumōkusa [Sumo Grass]
　　Jǐn [pronunciation: Qín]　Bitter Violet　Bitter Mallow　Japanese name: Sumire
　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Commonly called Sumōkusa [Sumo Grass]

According to *Wamyō Ruijushō*:
　　Jī xuě cǎo (Tsubokusa)　— In the commentary to *Bencao*, Táo Yǐnjū [Tao Hongjing] states: "The Jī xuě cǎo
　　has the Japanese name Tsubokusa. It is named thus because of its cold nature." Sū Jǐng states: "Its leaves resemble coins, and therefore it is also called Liánqián cǎo [Linked-coin Herb]."
