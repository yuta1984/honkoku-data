## 【NIWAZAKURA / YUSURA-UME (Prunus tomentosa and related Rosaceae shrubs)】

From the **Wamyō Ruijūshō** (Japanese Names Encyclopedia):
　Shuo-ying (朱櫻, hahaka) [Niwazakura]
　The Honzō (Materia Medica) states: another name for sakuranashi (桜棑) is Shuo-ying (朱櫻).
　　Japanese name: "Hahaka (波々加)"; one source also gives "Niwazakura (迩波佐久良)."

　Cherry blossom flower (桜桃花): Niwazakura (仁和佐久羅)
　　In the Yamato Honzō, this character is read "Yutsura (ゆすら)."

From **Kankai Shokkei** (Dietary Compendium):
　One theory reads the name as "Yusura (由須良)"; the mountain cherry (山桜桃) is read "Niwazakura (仁和佐久羅)." Another reading gives "Niwa-ume (仁和宇女)." These three names refer to things that are broadly similar in form but slightly different. Nevertheless, their species are very closely related.

From the **Zōho Tashikihen** (Supplemented Encyclopedia), section on Mountain Fruits:
　Ōtō (桜桃, cherry)　Japanese name: Niwazakura (仁和左久羅)
　　Alternative names: Kantō (含桃, "containing peach") [from Yueling/Monthly Ordinances]；Kei-ka (荊花)
　　[Supplementary] alternative name: Ōtō (鸎桃, "oriole peach") [from annotations to the Rites of Zhou]
　The Sansai Zukai also lists these alternative names under the entry for "cherry (Yutsura)."

　　Transcribed on the 15th day (full moon) of the last spring month, Mizunoe-Uma year (Bunsei 5, 1822).
　　Copied with faithful intent.

From **Wakan Sansai Zukai** (Illustrated Encyclopedia of Japanese and Chinese Things), section on Mountain Fruits:
　Mountain Cherry (Niwazakura, 山桜桃)　— also: Rai-ō, Mugi-eidō, Sumomo-momo [Li Tao], "Niwa-zakura" [common name]
　[A separate variety known as "Niwa-ume (庭梅)" also exists; to be copied in a separate volume]

　Mountain cherry-pear (Niwazakura, 山桜棑) is listed,

## 【HIKIO KOSHI (Isodon japonicus — Japanese rabdosia / "life-extending herb")】

From the **Yamato Honzō**:
　Sainei (薺薴, hikio-koshi) — also called "Yama-hakka (山薄荷, mountain mint)."
　Enmei-sō (延命草, "life-extending herb") = hikio-koshi.

【Seal】Miscellaneous herbs

At the gate of Bian Que (扁鵲), the legendary physician of antiquity, the enmei-sō (life-extending herb) was planted. Those who saw it were said to uphold goodness, avoid evil, and thus enjoy a long extension of life. This plant corresponds to the sainei (薺薴), i.e., hikio-koshi.

　　Copied in the latter part of Kosen (3rd lunar month), Mizunoto-Hitsuji year (Bunsei 6, 1823).
