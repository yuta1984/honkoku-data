## 【Gumi (胡頽子; *Elaeagnus multiflora* / *Elaeagnus* spp.)】

**According to the *Zoho Tashikiden* (増補多識編; *Expanded Nomenclature Compendium*), in the section on Shrubs (*Kanboku-rui*):**

　　*Kotaishi* (胡頽子; "Elaeagnus")　[Japanese name:] On further investigation: *Kumi* (*Gumi*)　[Alternative names:] *Rotoshi* (盧都子) [source: *Bencao Gangmu*]; *Hanganshun* (半含春; "half-open spring") [same source]
　　　　　　　　　　　[Additional names in the expanded edition:] *Hotaishi* (蒲頽子) [source: *Bencao Gangmu*]; *Jakuniso* (雀児酥; "sparrow-child butter") [source: *Paozhi lun* (炮炙論)]

[Seal: Medicinal Wood (薬木)]

---

**According to the *Yamato Honzō* (大和本草; *Japanese Materia Medica*):**

　　*Kotaishi* (胡頽子) [*Gumi*] — in popular usage called *Higumi*.
　　　　The author states: This is truly the "Nawashiro-gumi" (苗代グミ; "nursery-bed *Elaeagnus*").
　　　　　　　The *Wamyō-shō* (和名抄) gives the name "Moronari."

---

**According to the *Honzō Wage* (本草和解; *Vernacular Explanation of Materia Medica*):**

　　*Sawa-gumi* (サハグミ; "marsh *Elaeagnus*")
　　　　Farmers observe the ripening of this plant's fruit to know the time of year for making rice seedling beds (*nawashiro*). For this reason, it is called "Nawashiro-*Kotaishi*" (苗代胡頽子; "seedling-bed *Elaeagnus*").

---

**The author's investigation states:**

　　The *Honzō* (本草; Materia Medica) also records, regarding *Sanshuyu* (山茱萸; Japanese cornelian cherry, *Cornus officinalis*), that "its fruit ripens at the time of making rice seedling beds," and people in China say "this is indeed the *Sanshuyu*." However, the *Chikinsho* (地錦鈔; a horticultural encyclopaedia) includes this illustration [i.e., the illustration of *Gumi*] and records it as "Nawashiro-gumi." Whether *Sanshuyu* also ripens its fruit at the time of *nawashiro* (seedling-bed preparation) — and thus whether this tree is truly one and the same — remains unclear. A certain expert in ornamental plants (*hanashoku no aruji*) has explained this matter.

　　The *Yamato Honzō* states regarding *Kotaishi* (*Higumi*): the tree is six or seven *shaku* (approximately 180–210 cm) in height; the branches are supple. The leaves resemble those of a pear tree, being long and narrow. The upper surface of the leaf is green; the underside is white. The flowers bloom in the first and second months. The flower shape resembles that of *chōkō* (丁香; clove), and at its peak the flowers hang drooping — this description corresponds well with the present tree [observed here]. Whether *Sanshuyu* is also referred to as "Nawashiro-gumi" should be further investigated.

---

**According to the *Wamyō Ruijushō* (和名類聚抄; *Japanese Names Classified and Collected*):**

　　*Kotaishi* (胡頽子; *Moronari*) — According to *Ba En*'s (馬琬) *Shokukyō* (食経; *Food Classic*), *Yōjō Hiyō* (養生秘要; *Secrets of Nourishing Life*), and other texts:
　　　　*Kotaishi* — Japanese name: *Kumi* (久美); one text gives "Moronari" (毛呂奈里).
　　　　　　[In the *Honchō-shiki* (本朝式; *Regulations of the Imperial Court*), the three characters 諸生子 (*Moronariko*) are used.]

　　　　　　　　　　Sketched from nature, the thirteenth day of *Kosen* (third lunar month), year Kinoe-Saru (甲申) [Bunsei 7, 1824].

---

　　One should be aware that the *Honzō Wage* records that *Sanshuyu* "has its fruit harvested in the ninth and tenth months." Though the record that it "ripens at the time of rice seedling beds" is noted by that name, the statement that it "closely resembles *Kotaishi*" suggests that they may be one and the same fruit.
