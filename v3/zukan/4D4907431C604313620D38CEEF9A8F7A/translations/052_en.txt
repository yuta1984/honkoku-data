## 【Sendaihagi (Thermopsis lupinoides / Lupine-like Thermopsis)】

According to herbalists: Wild Juémíng (Sendaihagi)
　　　Sendaihagi (Sendai bush clover)

According to *Yamato Honzō*:
　It is suggested that mùxù (alfalfa/Medicago) might be Sendaihagi, but this is incorrect.
　*Chikinsho* states: The illustration of mùxù is clearly a different plant; it resembles the plant called Yokusui-mono-kusa (a moisture-loving grass).

## 【Enishida (Scotch Broom / Cytisus scoparius)】

According to *Imyō Ruijushō*:
　Juéming [read as: Enisuta]
　*Chikinsho* gives the reading as Enisuta.
　*Gunsho* reads it as Enshita.

In *Gunsho*:
　Jīnquèhuā (金雀花) [read as: Enishida] — this should be investigated further.

*Yamato Honzō* also states that there are two varieties of juéming (決明).
　Is one variety this plant (Enisuta)? The two characters "決明" read as Enisuta — this is in fact ketsumei (a separate plant entirely). There is no book that records ketsumei (決明) as Enisuta. In the *Wamyō Ruijushō*, there is a Japanese name "Ebisukusa" (衣比須久佐). This is the ketsumei that blooms in autumn. This is a matter to be separately examined by later scholars. Furthermore, on reflection, there are far more than just two plants that bear the name "juéming/ketsumei" — they are exceedingly numerous. In this entry, the discussion has become too lengthy and cannot be exhaustively addressed here.

According to *Honzō Ikkagen* (Personal Remarks on Materia Medica):
　Enisuta (衛仁寸多): its flowers are said to resemble those of Redama (鷹爪/Genista) and Jīnquèhuā (金雀花/Kerria or Spartium).

　　The author remarks:
　　　Enisuta and Renju (連珠) belong to the same category by name,
　　　but it is difficult to distinguish them by their flowers alone.
