## 【*Itozakura* (糸桜 / 垂糸桜; Weeping Cherry)】

### According to the *Yamato Honzō* (大和本草):

　　*Suishi Kaidō* (垂糸海棠; "Weeping-Thread Crabapple") 　[Also known as: *Hikan-zakura*, *Itozakura*]

　　　　The Chinese call this plant "*Suishi Kaidō*" (垂糸海棠; "Weeping Crabapple").
　　　　However, there is another plant also called "*Suishi Kaidō*" — they share the same name but are different plants (*dōmei iibutsu*; homonymous but distinct species).

## 【*Hotoke-no-za* (仏の座; Henbit / Nipplewort)】

### According to the *Yamato Honzō Bekuroku* (大和本草別録):

　　*Hotoke-no-za* (仏の座; "Buddha's Seat")

　　　　The leaves of this plant resemble a lotus flower (*renge*),
　　　　and appear like a Buddha's lotus pedestal (*za*).
　　　　According to one view, "*Hotoke-no-za*" refers to the plant called *Tabirako* (田平子).

---

### According to the *Kenkai Shokkyō* (巻懐食競):

　　*Kōkasai* (黄瓜菜)　[Japanese reading: *Tabirako*]

　　　　In Japanese vernacular usage, it is read as *Tabirako* (多比良古), and is also called "*Hotoke-no-za*."
　　　　It is one of the seven spring herbs (*nanakusa*) of *Jinjitsu* (人日; the seventh day of the first month).
　　　　However, even though it bears the name "*Hotoke-no-za*," it is a distinct plant from the other plant of the same name.
　　　　[For further detail,] see Summer Volume, Book Two.

[Seal: Herbaceous Plants (*Kusarui*)]

---

### According to the *Gunpōreki* (群芳暦):

　　*Genpōsō* (元宝草; "Gold-Ingot Herb")　Japanese name: *Hotoke-no-za* (ホトケノ座)

　　　　In common usage it is also called *Kasumigusa* ("mist grass"), and in Kasai it is known as *Tonhigusa*.

　　According to the *Honzō Jūshin* (本草従新): It grows in marshy paddy fields and along waterways. A single stem rises straight upward; the leaves grow in opposite pairs resembling gold ingots (*genpō*), arranged in tiers facing upward — either three to four layers, or five to six layers, and so on.
