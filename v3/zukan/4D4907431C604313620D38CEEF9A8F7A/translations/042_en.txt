**Two varieties**

*Mokutōka* / *Seisetsuka* (Kibushi; *Stachyurus praecox*)

　　　　The sixteenth day of *Kosen* (姑洗; third lunar month), year Kinoe-Saru (甲申) [Bunsei 7, 1824]:
　　　　Sketched from nature after the style of the *Kagan-shū* (華萼集).

　　　　　　The author, Hisatoshi, states: *Seisetsuka* (旌節花) is in fact the original Chinese name for *Sakurasō* (桜草; *Primula sieboldii*, Japanese primrose).
　　　　　　The explanation for *Mokutōka* (木藤花) requires further investigation.

## 【Noseri (菫菜) / Murasaki Keman (藪苞荷; *Corydalis incisa*)】

[The illustration appears to depict Murasaki Keman (*Corydalis incisa*)]

**According to the *Yamato Honzō* (大和本草; *Japanese Materia Medica*):**

　Flower of *Kinkinsai* (菫菜; *Noseri*)
　　　　Also known as *Kankin* (旱芹; "dry celery/parsley").
　　　　It is incorrect to call this plant *Mamahikigusa* (ママ引草).

---

**According to the *Zoho Tashikiden* (増補多識編; *Expanded Nomenclature Compendium*), in the section on Pungent Vegetables (*Kunsai-rui*):**

　*Kin* (菫)　[Japanese name:] *Sumire* (須美礼); upon the author's reconsideration, [also:] *Noseri* (乃世利)　[Alternative name:] *Kukin* (苦菫; "bitter violet") [source: *Erya* (爾雅)]　[Supplementary alternative name:] *Ōki* (黄葵; "yellow mallow") [source: Tang Chinese herbal]
　　　　　　*Kankin* (旱芹; "dry parsley/celery") [source: *Bencao Gangmu* (綱目)]　[Note: The *Wakan Sansai Zue* (和漢三才図会) calls *Kukin* (苦菫) by the name *Sumire* (菫)]

---

**According to the *Wamyō Ruijushō* (和名類聚抄):**

　　*Kinkinsai* (菫菜; *Sumire*)

---

**According to the *Honzō* (本草; herbal compendium):**

　　*Kinkinsai* (菫菜): commonly called *Kinki* (菫葵). [The reading of 菫 is *kin* (謹). Japanese name: *Sumire* (須美礼).]

---

**The author, Hisatoshi, states:**

　　　In the *Wamyō-shō* (和名抄; *Wamyō Ruijushō*), *Kinkinsai* (菫菜; *Noseri*) is equated with *Sumire* (菫菜), but *Sumire* actually refers to *Shika Chitei* (紫花地丁; *Viola mandshurica*, the violet). [The plant here identified as *Noseri*] is called *Sumō-tori* (相撲取り) [by children]. This plant corresponds well to the *Kinkinsai* (菫菜) described in the *Yamato Honzō*. Its leaves resemble those of *Keman* [i.e., *Murasaki Keman*, *Corydalis incisa*] but are smaller, and resemble parsley leaves but are larger. Its flowers also resemble those of *Keman*, and it grows in thickets. The stems and leaves are soft, the flowers are purple, and it grows on dry ground. What is called *Kankin* (旱芹; "dry parsley") is indeed this plant. The reading of *Kinkinsai* (菫菜) as *Sumire* requires further investigation. This plant is also called *Yabu-geman* (藪苞荷; "thicket *keman*").

**According to the *Wakan Sansai Zue* (和漢三才図会; *Illustrated Sino-Japanese Encyclopedia*), in the section on Wetland Herbs (*Shitsusō-rui*):**

　　*Tōshu Jutsu* (桃朱術)　[Alternative name:] *Tōshu Jutsu* (陶朱術)

---

**According to the *Bukō Sanbutsu-shi* (武江産物志; *Record of Natural Products of Edo*):**

　　*Tōshu Jutsu* (*Yabu-geman*)

---

**The *Honkō* (本綱; i.e., *Bencao Gangmu*) states:**

　　*Tōshu Jutsu* (桃朱術) is a variety of *Seishō* (青葙; *Celosia argentea*) and grows in gardens. Its leaves are fine and parsley-like; its flowers are purple; its fruit forms a pod (角). If one holds a mirror next to it, the pods burst and scatter their seeds of their own accord. On the fifth day of the fifth month, one collects the seeds and wears them on one's person, and it is said that this causes a woman to be loved by her husband. One account says the flower is yellow [etc.].

---

**The author, Hisatoshi, states:**

　　The flowers, leaves, and fruit of *Tōshu Jutsu* accord well with the description in the herbal literature (*Honzō*). When a person touches the ripe pods, each bursts open, and the pods split (*saite*) like threads coiling into a ring (輪). The account that describes "the one with yellow flowers" refers to *Momochidori* (芹葉鉤吻; *Oenanthe javanica* or similar umbelliferous plant), which belongs to the same group but is a distinct species.
　　　　The description in the *Sansai Zue* (三才図会) of a plant "resembling a lily flower (百合花)" does not correspond to this plant.
