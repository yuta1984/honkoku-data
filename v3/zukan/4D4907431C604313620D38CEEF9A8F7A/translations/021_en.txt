## 【DANDELION (Taraxacum)】

Dandelion (Hōkōei / 蒲公英), According to Saijiki (almanac):
　　　○ Also called Tsutsumikusa (鞁草, "wrapping grass")
　　　○ The yellow-flowered variety is called Kō-hōtei (黄鞁釘) or Kōka-jichō (黄花地丁)
　　　The white-flowered variety is called Haku-yūtei (白鞁釘), referring to the white-flowered kind, as noted in Kyūkō Yafu

　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　26th day of the third month (Koshi), Mizunoto-Hitsuji year
　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Copied again from the original

According to Kyūkō Honzō (救荒本草):
　Tanpopo (孛孛丁菜)　Also called Kōka-byō (黄花苗, "yellow-flowering seedling")

According to Wamyō Ruijushō (和名類聚抄):
　Hōkō-sō (蒲公草)　According to the Honzō: Hōkō-sō, Japanese name: Fujina (不知奈), also called Tana (太奈)
　　　　　One name: Kōdoku-sō (耩耨草); the upper character read by fanqie of "kō" and "kō," the lower character by fanqie of "nu" and "dou"

According to Zōho Tashiki-hen, Soft and Smooth Herbs section (増補多識篇柔滑類):
　Dandelion (蒲公英, Hōkōei):
　　　Japanese names: Tana (多那); upon investigation, Tanpopo (多牟保々)
　　　Alternative names: Kinsankusa (金簪草) [from Bencao Gangmu]; Kōdoku-sō (耩耨草) [read as "Kō"]
　　　　　　Kōka-jichō (黄花地丁) [common name]; Fukōei (鳬公英) [from Qianjin Fang]; Hōkōei (僕公罌) [from Tujing]
　　　　　　Hokkō-ei (鵓鴣英) [from Yuce]; Hōkō-chō (蒲公丁); ○ Chitan-sō (地胆草)

According to Bencao Gangmu (本草綱目):
　Those bearing purple flowers are named Daichō-sō (大丁草)
　Those similar in form but bearing no flowers are named
　Chitan-sō (地胆草)

According to Nōsei Zensho (農政全書):
　It is called Tanpopo (孛孛丁菜)
　Its flowers open in the morning and wilt
　after noon each day;
　this happens daily, yet the plant
　endures for a long time

## 【CUDWEED (Gnaphalium / Pseudognaphalium)】

According to Kankai Shokkyō (巻懐食競):
　Cudweed (鼠麹草, Hahakogusa)　Read as Hahakogusa, also
　　　　called Gogyō-yomogi (五行蒿); it is one of the Seven Herbs of Jinjitsu (New Year's seventh day)

According to Imyō Bunruishō (異名分類抄):
　Upon examining the entry on Sōkiku (鼠麹) in the Bencao Gangmu,
　it lists "Butsuni" (仏耳) and "Soni" (鼠耳), and so forth — it is also called "Mimina" (ear-herb)
　The Wamyō-shō gives the name Ankoshi (菴蘆子, read as Hahako);
　the Honzō Ruihen names it Basenkkō (馬先蒿)
　It is also added to the Seven Herbs of Jinjitsu as Gogyō (御形), or Gogyō-yomogi (五行蒿)
　The author's note: Mimina (耳菜) is a separate species
　　　　To be transcribed later

According to Yamato Honzō (大和本草):
　Cudweed (鼠麹草, Gogyō)
　　One name: Butsunisō (仏耳草, "Buddha's ear plant")
　　Japanese name: Hahakogusa (母子草, "mother-and-child grass")
　Some identify it as
　　Kōkayomogi (黄花蒿, mugwort with yellow flowers)
　But identifying it as
　　Jakō (邪蒿, "false mugwort") is incorrect

According to Tashiki-hen, Wet Grass section (多識篇湿草類):
　Sōkiku-sō (鼠麹草):
　　Japanese name: Upon investigation, Hoohokusa (保於己久左)
　　Alternative names: Mushinsō (無心草) [Bieroku]; 
　　Additional alternative names: Beikiku (米麹) [Gangmu]; Soni (鼠耳) [Bieroku]; Butsuni-sō (仏耳草) [Hōshō]; Kōbō (香茅) [Shūi]; Kōhō (黄蒿) [Kaihен]; Jōni (茸母)
　　　　　　　※ There exists a poem on Jōni (茸母) by Emperor Huizong of Song

In ancient Japan, cudweed was used on the third day of the third month to make kusa-mochi (herb rice cakes);
hence the plant came to be called "Mochi-gusa" (餅草, "rice cake grass") in Japanese custom.
　　　　　　　　27th day of the third month of spring, Mizunoto-Hitsuji year — copied faithfully from the original
