## 【Niwatoko (Japanese Elderberry / Sambucus racemosa)】

**According to Yamato Honzō:**
　Flowers of Tatsunoki (接骨木, elder tree)
　　　　According to Chikinsho:
　　　　　Ganboku
　　　　　Niwatoko

**According to the Supplemented Tashiki-hen, section on Shrubs (灌木類):**
　Sekkotsuboku (接骨木)
　　Japanese name: Miyatsukoki (美也豆古幾); now called Ganboku (加牟保久)
　　Additional alternative names: Zokukotsuboku (続骨木, from Bencao Gangmu); Moku Sakuteki (木蒴藋)

Hastily copied at Uaan on the 25th day of Kosen (third lunar month), in the year Mizunoto-Hitsuji (癸未, 1823)
[Seal] Miscellaneous trees (雑木)

**According to Wamyō Ruijushō:**
　Sekkotsuboku (接骨木), read as Miyatsukoki

**According to Honzō (Bencao):**
　Sekkotsuboku
　　Japanese name: Miyatsukoki (美夜都古木)

**According to Chikinsho:**
Sakuchō (蒴藋) is the tree commonly known as Niwatoko. Its leaves resemble those of Rikuei (陸英, dwarf elder). Judging from the description of its flowers and fruits, it corresponds to this plant. According to the Bencao Gangmu: "The leaves resemble those of Rikuei; the tree reaches one or two jō in height, the wood is light, hollow, and without pith." It further states that Rikuei and Sakuchō are in fact the same species, but have been erroneously classified as two different species; the leaves are called Sakuchō and the flowers Rikuei — this interpretation passed down from Su Song to Li Shizhen is incorrect. Rikuei is an herb that grows from perennial roots in spring, whereas Sakuchō is a tree that grows larger year by year. △

△ [Note] Furthermore, the two illustrations in the present work accord well with each other. However, Yamato Honzō states: "The leaves of Sakuchō and Sekkotsuboku (Tatsunoki) are identical; the only difference is that one is an herb and the other a tree." For this reason it is also called "Kusa-Tatsu" (草タツ, herb-Tatsu). The Wamyō Ruijushō likewise lists Sekkotsuboku and Sakuchō as separate entries, causing considerable confusion. It is difficult to determine which interpretation is correct and which is in error. The Sancai Tuhui states: "This tree has the virtue of mending broken bones and reconnecting sinews, and it is from this property that it takes its name (接骨木, 'bone-joining tree')."

## 【Inu-Zansho / Shoku-shō (Sichuan Pepper varieties)】

**Flowers of Shoku-shō (蜀椒, Sichuan pepper / Zanthoxylum)**
　　Shokkyō:
　　　Shinshō (秦椒, Zanthoxylum bungeanum)
　　Japanese name: Fusahajikani

**According to Honzō Wage:**
　　Alternative names: Ha-shō (巴椒) · Kan-shō (漢椒) · Sen-shō (川椒) · Nan-shō (南椒) · Tōgi (蓎薿) · Ten-shō (点椒)
　　Japanese names: Naruhasikani (奈流波志加美)
　　　　　　Asamurasamu (安左久良佐武) [possibly Asamurasaki]
　　　　　　Shō (世宇)

Shinsen (秦川), Kan (漢), Ha (巴), and Shoku (蜀) are all names of regions. According to Yamato Honzō: "Ordinary sanshō in the Tanba region is called Binshō (ビンセウ). Asakura-sansho differs from ordinary sansho in its leaves, has no thorns, and is especially fragrant. Shoku-shō and Shin-shō are thought to be different plants because they grow in the countries of Shu (蜀) and Qin (秦), but they are in fact the same species, differing only in size." The varieties of sanshō are exceedingly numerous.

[Seal] Fruit trees (果木類)
Copied at Kanūtei in the same season (spring)

**According to Wamyō Ruijushō:**
　Shoku-shō (蜀椒) — According to Su Jing's commentary in Honzō: "The pronunciation of 蜀椒 is Shō (蕭)"
　　　　Japanese names: Naruhasikani (奈流波之加美)
　　　　　　　　　　　Fusahasikani (不佐波之加美)

**According to Jiuhuang Bencao (救荒本草):**
　Shōjū (椒樹, pepper tree) = Shoku-shō; also known as Nan-shō (南椒), Ha-shō (巴椒), and Tōgi (蓎藙)

**According to the Supplemented Tashiki-hen, section on Flavors (味類):**
　Shin-shō (秦椒)
　　Japanese name: Kawahi-Hasikani (加波比波志加美); nowadays in common parlance called Samu-shō (左牟世宇)
　　Alternative names: Daishō (大椒) — from Erya (尓雅); Kakashō (花椒); Kitsu-shō (橘椒) — from Wamyō
　　Note: Also referred to as Yusamu-shō (由左牟世宇).
　　The same book (Supplemented Tashiki-hen) lists Shin-shō (秦椒) and Shoku-shō (蜀椒) as separate entries; however, since the Japanese and alternative names for Shoku-shō are identical to those recorded in the preceding entry (Shin-shō), they are omitted here.
