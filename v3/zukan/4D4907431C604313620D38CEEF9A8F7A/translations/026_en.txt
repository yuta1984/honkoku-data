## 【FUKI (Japanese Butterbur / *Petasites japonicus*)】

From *Zōho Tashikihen* [Expanded Identification Compendium], Wetland Herbs section:

**Kuǎndōng huā** [Coltsfoot Flower]

Japanese name: Yamabuki (also called Ōhoba). Upon investigation, it is called Fuki. Further investigation reveals that, in the yellow-flowered varieties, there is the name Mizufuki; hence, the款冬 (coltsfoot) that grows on dry upland (kurochi) is called Yamabuki. There is also a separate species whose flowers resemble the tea rose (*Tūmí* / Naniwa-ibara), which is likewise called Yamafuki. These two plants share the same Japanese name but are different species. Ancient poets frequently composed verses about its flowers in the *Man'yōshū*, and on those occasions simply borrowed the Chinese character for their phonetic value; this was never intended to identify the tea-rose-like plant as coltsfoot. Borrowed characters in the *Man'yōshū* are by no means rare, yet later scholars have failed to distinguish this. The inclusion of coltsfoot in Fujiwara no Kintō's *Rōei* [Anthology of Recitation Poems] is already in error — how much more so the other poems that reference it.

　　Alternative names are recorded in the relevant entry and therefore not repeated here.

## 【SUGI (Japanese Cedar / *Cryptomeria japonica*)】

From *Zōho Tashikihen*, Fragrant Woods section:

**Shān** [Cedar] (San)　Japanese name: Suki (Sugi)

Alternative names: Shān (San) [pronunciation: Sugi]; Shā mù [Sand Wood] (Shamoku) [from *Bencao Gangmu*]
　　　　　　　　Qíng mù (Keimoku) [pronunciation: Jìng]; Dān zhì mù (Chokumoku) [pronunciation of 桎: Zhí]

---

From *Yamato Honzō* [Japanese Materia Medica]:

**Sugi flower** (Sugi no hana)
　　The wood grows straight (sugu), hence the name Sugi.
　　"Sugi" derives from "suku" (to be straight/upright).

---

From *Wamyō Ruijushō*:

　Sugi (Suki) — According to the phonetic commentary of the *Erya*: "Shān" is pronounced "shān" (sam), with an alternate reading of "xiān" (sen). Japanese name: Suki.
　　Found in the *Nihongi Shiki* [Private Commentary on the Chronicles of Japan]. Upon investigation, the common use of the character 榲 is incorrect; 榲 is pronounced with a fanqie spelling based on "sōn" (松/柗, an alternate form of 松) and "guì" (桂), as recorded in the *Táng Yùn* [Tang Rhyme Dictionary].

　　Resembles pine; grows in the Jiangnan region; suitable for use as timber for boat-building.

　　On the seventh day of the third month (Kōsen / Gosen), Kinoe-Saru year (Bunsei 7, 1824):
　　I broke a branch from the garden grove and drew it true-to-life, gazing at the single leaves in the rain.

---

From *Nánfāng Cǎomù Zhuàng* [Records of Plants of the South], Wood section:
　　Shān [Cedar] — also called Pīshān

---

Sugi (Japanese cedar) is a tree that grows perfectly straight. As a symbol of uprightness and integrity, it has long been revered as the sacred tree (shimboku) of this country. While other trees are also considered sacred, the sugi is by far the most common.

> *Sugi-fukaki / katayama-kage no / yūsuzumi*
> *yoso ni zo sugiru / yūdachi no sora*
> — By Gokyo-goku (Fujiwara no Yoshitsune)

*"Cooling oneself in the evening shade of the deep cedar grove on the hillside —
the summer shower passes by somewhere far off in the evening sky."*

【Seal: Garden Tree】

There are two varieties of sugi cultivated in residential gardens. *Kara-sugi* (Chinese cedar) has soft needles. *Edo-sugi* has small, hard needles, a pale color, and turns red in mid-winter (around the eleventh month). It greens again in the third month. Many households use it for garden fences (hedges).
