## Shijimibana / Kogomebana (笑靨花 — *Spiraea prunifolia*)

From the *Wakan Sansai Zue* (Illustrated Encyclopedia of Japan and China), in the section on shrubs:
　*Shōenka* (笑靨花, "dimple flower"), commonly called *Shijimibana* (蜆花, "clam-shell flower"). The *Dōsei Hassen* states: "The flowers of *Shōenka* are as fine and small as beans; along a single branch, a thousand flowers bloom, appearing as if snow had been pressed upon it."

　Written in spring of Kanoto-Mi, the fourth year of Bunsei (1821).

*Kogome-bana* (笑靨花) — as recorded in the *Yamato Honzō*
　*Kogome-bana* (糏花) — as recorded in the *Saijiki* (seasonal almanac)
　*Kogome-zakura* (小米桜) — also called *Hasezakura*
　　　Two varieties exist.
　*Gyokusetsu* (玉屑, "jade flakes") — another name for *Kogomebana*

## Karatachi / Trifoliate Orange (枳 — *Poncirus trifoliata*)

[Seal] Flowering trees

From the *Zōho Tashiki-hen* (Expanded Tashiki Encyclopedia), section on shrubs:
　*Ki* (枳)　Japanese name: Karatachi (加羅多知)　Alternative names recorded.
　Its fruit is called *Kishitsu* (枳実, *Zhishi*) [from the *Bencao* proper] or *Kikoku* (枳穀, *Zhiqiao*) [from the *Kaibao* materia medica].

From the *Wamyō Ruijushō* (Japanese Name Thesaurus):
　*Kikyo* (枳椇, *Hovenia dulcis*, Japanese raisin tree): The *Bencao* states,
　　*Kikyo* — read as "Shiku" in two syllables; Japanese name: Karatachi (加良太知).

The *Yupian* (Chinese character dictionary) states: *Ki* (枳) is a citrus that grows in a bent, twisted form. The *Seven-Volume Food Classic* (*Shichi-kan Shokukyō*) mentions *Kuen* (枸橼, citron); here, *ku* (枸) is the same character as *kyo* (椇).

The author's note: *Kikyo* is recorded in the *Yamato Honzō* as *Kenpō-nashi* (ケンポノナシ, *Hovenia dulcis*). It is unclear which identification is correct.

From the *Yamato Honzō*:
　*Kukitsu* / *Karatachibana* / *Karatachi* (枸橘)　Alternative name: *Shūkitsu* (臭橘, "malodorous citrus")
　Japanese name: Karatachi
　In Tsukushi (Kyushu), it is called *Gezu*.
　Since ancient times, it has been a common error in Japan to use this plant as *Kikoku* (枳殻) or *Kishitsu* (枳実) in medicine — which is incorrect.
　The *Sansai Zue* states: In Japan, if one plants a *mikan* (satsuma orange) in Ōshū (northeastern Japan), it transforms into *kikoku* — an unusual transformation of the same sort.
　The *Yamato Honzō* takes *Kukitsu* (枸橘, Gezu) to be *Karatachi*. Since *Karatachi* is in fact *Kikoku*, this accords with the illustrations and explanations in the *Sansai Zue*. The opinions of certain scholars warrant further examination.

The author Genjū (Mōri Baien) reflects:
　There are two kinds of *kikoku*. The *Karatachi* (狗橘) planted as hedges has smaller wood, leaves, and fruit than ordinary *Karatachi*. There is no doubt that this is the *ku-kitsu* (狗橘) described by Li Shizhen. The true *kikoku* has larger wood, leaves, and fruit, and is a distinct variety of the same genus. What is sold today in pharmacies as "small *kishitsu*" (小枳実) is the fruit of the hedge *Karatachi* (狗橘) and is not suitable for medicinal use.

## *Yamabuki* / Japanese Kerria (山吹・棣棠 — *Kerria japonica*)

*Tomi-bana* (荼靡花)
　Also called *Yamabuki*.
　Furthermore, *Tobi* (酴醿) is sometimes read as *Yamabuki*; both the *Man'yōshū* and the *Wakan Rōeishū* write 欵冬花 (*kantōka*) and compose verses about *Yamabuki* — but this is an error. *Kantō* (欵冬) refers to *Fukinotō* (ふきのとう, butterbur sprout). — As stated in the *Chikinsho*.

[Seal] Medicinal herbs

From the *Yamato Honzō*:
　*Yamabuki* (棣棠 — *Kerria japonica*)
　　Chinese names: *Kinwan* (金碗, "golden bowl") and *Kisui* (喜水, "water-loving")
　Reading *Tobi* (酴醿) as *Yamabuki* is incorrect.
　*Tobi* (酴醿) refers to *Goyōki* (五葉木 — a multi-petalled rose variety, likely *Rosa multiflora* or similar).
