## Raspberry / Strawberry (Ichigo / Fukubon-shi)

As stated in Zōho Tashiki-hen, Creeping Plants section:
　Fukubon-shi (覆盆子, *Rubus* species) — Japanese name: Kōfuri-ichigo; upon investigation: Kusa-ichigo
　Additional alternative names: Kei (茥) [Erya]; Ketsubon (鈌盆) [same]; Seikoku-sō (西国草, Western Province Grass) [Tujing (Illustrated Classics)]
　　　　　　　　Hirōka (畢楞伽) [same]; Taibakobo (大麦苺, Great-Barley Berry); Sōtenhō (挿田藨, Field-Insert Berry); Uhōshi (烏藨子, Crow Berry) [Bencao Gangmu]

In the *Bencao Gangmu* (Compendium of Materia Medica), berry-type plants are listed as five species in total: [Penglei (蓬蘽) / Fukubon-shi (覆盆子) / Biāo (藨) / Tree Berry (樹苺) / Snake Berry (蛇苺)]. Li Shizhen himself collected and compared specimens for the first time and arrived at accurate identifications. The differing theories from various scholars are numerous, and none can readily be trusted.
[Author Baien's note:] Even if one classifies berry-type plants into five species as Li Shizhen does, their actual varieties number over ten. In China (the Han lands), there may be five grades — this is uncertain. According to the theory of predecessors, "fukubon (覆盆)" is the name for the fruit of the berry plant.

In *Yamato Honzō* (Japanese Materia Medica), this plant is assigned to fukubon-shi.
In *Chikinsho* (Landscape Garden Manual), it is assigned to kenkōshi (懸鈎子); this warrants further investigation.
I (Baien) have depicted one species of fukubon-shi in my illustrated record.
This is based on the illustration in *Chikinsho*.
For this plant, I follow *Yamato Honzō*.
The illustration in *Wakan Sansai Zue* (Japanese-Chinese Illustrated Encyclopedia) differs greatly, though the accompanying description agrees.

## Japanese Yew (Ichiinoki) / Kyara-boku / Inugaya

As stated in *Yamato Honzō*:
　Ichiinoki (一位の木, *Taxus cuspidata*) — Kyara-boku (伽羅木)
　　Flowers are yellow-red in color, resembling a ball, clustered together in one place.
　　The fruit is [like a cluster], exceedingly small.

As stated in the Fragrant Woods section of *Wakan Sansai Zue*:
　Kyara-boku (伽羅木 / キヤラボク; also called Otsuko) [common name]
　　Otsuko (於豆古) [Ezo (Ainu) name]
The indigenous people of Ezo (Hokkaido) and Matsumae call this tree "Otsuko." Since its grain resembles that of kyara (奇楠, agarwood), it is commonly called "Kyara-boku."

Dosso-boku (土穌木)
　[Ichiinoki]
　[Sakunoki]

[As stated in Master Matsuoka Jokan's "Honzō Ikka-gen" (Personal Views on Materia Medica):]
　Abotssan (阿勃参) — Bebékaya (辺々榧); Inugaya (狗榧, *Cephalotaxus*)
　　　　　Kyara-boku; also: Ichiinoki

　In a Western (barbarian) language, this oil is called "Portuguese oil." It is also called "Aceite-una (アセトウナ)."

[As stated in Toyoda Yōkei's "Shabenhyoroku" (Notes on Materia Medica):]　Abotssan — [Bebékaya / Inugaya]

　　　　　　Wang's *Huiyuan Xiangzhu* (Wang's Annotated Garden of Terms) states: Abotssan comes from the country of Furin (払林国, likely the Byzantine Empire or a Western land). It grows over one *jō* (approx. 3m) tall; the bark is greenish-white; the leaves are fine and face each other in pairs, resembling turnip greens; the seeds are bright yellow; the fruit resembles pepper and is red. When the branches are ground, the juice is oil-like; applied to scabies and ringworm, there is not one case that does not heal. This oil is extremely precious and its price equals a thousand gold pieces.
　◦ It also appears in the *Kyokuseki Benran* (Reference Guide to Various Texts). The Bebékaya tree of Ōmi Province (江州, present-day Shiga Prefecture) is this plant. Bebékaya, also called Inugaya (*Cephalotaxus harringtonia*). It grows in various mountain locations. Mountain people press the oil from it, and it is used by surgeons (外科家).
