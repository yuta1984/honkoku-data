## 【TSUBAKI (Camellia)】

**According to Yamato Honzo (Great Japanese Herbalist):**
In the Nihon Shoki (Chronicles of Japan), it is recorded that in the third month of the thirteenth year of Emperor Tenmu's reign, a man from Yoshino named Umabe-no-Atai Yumi presented white camellia (tsubaki) as tribute. In the Engishiki (Procedures of the Engi Era), camellia is also written as "kaijuryū" (海石榴). The Junwa Myōshō (Wamyō Ruijushō) is the same. It is said the name derives from "Atsu-ba-no-ki" (thick-leaved tree), referring to the thickness of its leaves, and so forth.

**According to Wamyō Ruijushō:**
Tsubaki (椿) — The Tang rhyme dictionary states "椿" (fanqie reading: choku-rin)
Japanese name: Tsubaki (豆波木) — the name of a tree
The Yōshi Kango-shō states: kaijuryū (海石榴) = tsubaki
Japanese name as above. Used in the Honchō-shiki (Official Procedures of Japan) and other texts.

Written on the twenty-second day of the second month (kisaragi), Mizunoe-Uma year.
Painted from a cut branch taken from the garden.

**According to Wakan Sansai Zue (Illustrated Encyclopedia of Japan and China), Shrubs Section:**
Kaijuryū (海石榴, tsubaki) — Tsubaki (椿) in Japanese character
　The character 椿 refers not to this tree but to a type of tall timber tree — the ailanthus (樗) and Chinese cedar (椿) — and is entirely different from kaijuryū (tsubaki/camellia).

The Man'yōshū (Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves), Honchō-shiki, and Wamyōshō all use the character 椿 to write "kaijuryū" (海石榴), reading it as "tsubaki." This convention is of long standing.
