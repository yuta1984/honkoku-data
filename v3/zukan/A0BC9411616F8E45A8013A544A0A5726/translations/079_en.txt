Among the various colors and sounds of crickets and evening cicadas, the voice of the matsumushi that I endure goes "rin-rin, rin-rin" - the sound of autumn is vivid.

In the song of Nonomiya it says: "Whose matsumushi sound goes rin-rin, while the wind goes woo-woo through the night at Nonomiya shrine."

Lord Karasumaru Mitsuhiro's commentary on "Takuan Hyakushu" says: "Matsumushi is the sound of pine, suzumushi is the sound of ringing bells. However, in recent times this has been mistaken, as is also mentioned in renga poetry."

"Komado Zasshitsu" says: "A certain book says: Even interesting things become uninteresting if seen and heard carelessly. In the world, what makes the 'rin-rin' sound is called suzumushi, and suzumushi is called matsumushi - such is this biased way of listening. Like the pine wind that should make a 'chin-chiro-rin' sound, only the bell's 'rin' is hard to hear." 《Hirotaka says: According to these various theories and the renga poetry by Yūkō-ō and Tadamitsu cited in the poetry section, from the time of the Tale of Genji until the Tenshō period, it is clear that what made the 'chin-chiro-rin' sound was called suzumushi. In Kyoto, it is still this way today. Most provinces still use the old names, though rarely there are places that call them as in Kyoto》
