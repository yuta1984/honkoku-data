## The Buppōsō Bird (Broad-billed Roller / Oriental Scops Owl)

**[Editorial note: The illustration on the next page shows the Buppōsō (Broad-billed Roller, *Eurystomus orientalis*), but the calls described in this text are those of the Konohazuku (Oriental Scops Owl, *Otus sunia*).]*

The Buppōsō bird is also known by the name Sanbōchō ("Three Treasures Bird"). It inhabits the mountains of Kōya-san in Kii Province, as well as Nikkō, Mount Hiei, Matsunoo, Hōrai-ji Temple, and other mountain areas. The *Kairei-shū Hoketsu-shō* records a poem composed upon hearing the buppōsō call before dawn:

> Sitting alone in the quiet forest at the grass-thatched hall's dawn,
> I hear the voice of the Three Treasures from a single bird.
> One bird has voice, one person has heart—
> Voice, heart, clouds, water—all crystal clear.

The author's own note states that among the various records of Kongōbu-ji Temple on Kōya-san, there is a poem by Kōzan Undō composed in response to the above:

> The solitary moon, the solemn temple grove at dawn—
> The Three Treasures drift softly on the breeze, a single sacred bird.
> It cries out the true reality of the eternal present—
> In ten realms, voice upon voice, all crystal clear.

The *Nihon Kiryaku* records: In the eighth month of Engi 6 (906), while the Minister of the Right was performing the Lotus Sutra Eight-Lecture ritual, a buppōsō bird came and sang. It also records: On the thirteenth day of the eighth month of Engi 18 (918), cyclical day *kichū*, the Minister of the Right Tadahira lectured on the Lotus Sutra over five days and ten sessions at his residence on Fifth Avenue (Gojō), when a buppōsō bird came and sang from a tree, and men of letters were commanded to compose poems. It further records: On the night of the fourteenth day of the same month, cyclical day *kōin*, while a lecture was being held at the Fifth Avenue Empress's Palace, a buppōsō bird sang from the top of a pine tree, and the poets in attendance composed verses.

**[Marginal note: The postscript of the *Fusō Ryakki* states: "On the night of the fourteenth, a buppōsō bird sang in the pine grove of the Fifth Avenue Empress's Palace; the assembled people who heard it thought it wondrous. A lecture on the Lotus Sutra had been held since three days prior."]*

The *Michitsune-shū* records: On the thirteenth day of the eighth month of Engi 18, at the Minister of the Left's residence during the Eight Lectures, a bird called buppōsō sang, and the following poem was composed and presented:

> This bird that must dwell in rugged mountains—
> does it not sing exquisitely?
> Though there are many dense forests, many tall treetops,
> it beats its wings and flies past—
> though there are seasons of spring, summer, and winter,
> it is in your autumn alone that it has begun
> to let its cry be heard in the village,
> as the maple leaves fall in vivid crimson.

> Being a bird rarely heard in the mountain depths, it is in your autumn that it sings even in the village.
> You did not mark the day in advance—how could the bird have known ahead of time?

The lord's reply (conveyed through Saemon-no-kami Minamoto no Nakatada as messenger):

> The heart that thinks upon the dharma is deep indeed—
> even having entered [the mountains], the bird too must have had quick ears.

In Irinaka Masayoshi's *Kubo no Susabi* ("Idle Jottings from Kubo"), it is written: "Though one hears that the buppōsō bird rarely sings on Kōya-san, I have heard nothing about what its appearance is actually like. It is said that in ancient times this bird was occasionally kept in a cage and appreciated as a pet."

The *Ben-no-naishi Diary* records: In the second month of Kenkyū 2 (1191), a bird that sang "buppōsō" was sent from the Grand Minister of State to the palace, where it was placed for viewing in the regular living quarters. On days when rain fell, it sang especially well, and its name was heard quite clearly. Ben-no-naishi composed:

> In any case, since this is the reign of our august lord, even the bird of the Three Treasures would sing.

From the *Yamanome-ryōshū*, an imperial composition by Retired Emperor Go-Toba:

> Our land is a place where the dharma is strictly observed—even the birds cry "buppōsō."

From the *Nohō Fudoki*, a poem by Lord Taiko (Toyotomi) Hideyoshi:

> Even the bird handed down through tradition performs the august dharma—its voice is on Kōya-san beneath the lingering moon.

**[Marginal note: There are also poems about this bird in the *Fuboku Waka-shō*.]*

The male of this species resembles a dove in shape but is thin and small. The head is pale black, the feathers are green, the belly and back are a mixture of blue-green, the area beneath the throat is blue, the tips of the wings and tail are black, the beak is slender like a dove's, and both legs are red. Its call is said to be "buppōsō." When the male calls "buppō," the female answers with "sō-sō."

> Keichū's *Kawayashiro* states: The way the buppōsō bird calls is said to be: it calls "butsu-hō" (*buppō*) clearly two or three times, and then calls "sō" in a low voice afterward.

The female is of the same shape, but the entire body is pale black mixed with white feathers, with deep black speckles. The belly is white with black spots, the beak is pale green, and the legs are pale brown.

In Tamiya's *Kyōan Manpitsu* (second volume), it is written: "To classify it as a type of owl is incorrect. According to the theory of Master Ranzan [Ono Ranzan], it belongs to the dove family. The Chinese name is not clearly known."

In Ming scholar Shen Mao-guan's *Huayi Niaoshou Kao* ("Study of Birds and Beasts of China and Foreign Lands"), citing the *Jiuhua Shan Zhi* ("Gazetteer of Mount Jiuhua"): "The Nianfo Bird (*niànfó niǎo*, 'Buddha-chanting bird') is about the size of a dove; its plumage is a patterned mixture of yellow-brown and kingfisher-blue, and its tonal quality is clear and smooth, as if reciting the name of Buddha. It is also called the Nianfo-zi." In a poem by Tang poet Wei Chan: "I quietly listen to the nianfo bird flying through the forest; I carefully examine the painted horse on the roadside wall"—this nianfo bird exists here as well.

Xu's *Bijing*, and Fan Hong's *Dianji Bianlam* of the Ming dynasty, also contain this same text. In Qing scholar Wang Yuyang's *Chibe Oudan* there is a passage on the nianfo bird (abbreviated here). This nianfo bird should belong to the same group as the buppōsō. I [the author] have written about this in detail in the *Tōdō Ihitsu* ("Posthumous Notes from Peach Grotto").
