When examining the medicinal texts about honey, there are four types: stone honey, tree honey, earth honey, and domestic honey. For medicinal use, nothing surpasses the finest quality tree honey. Bees make their nests in hollow spaces near the roots of large trees, opening small holes in warm southern-facing locations for entry and exit. This is common in deep mountains where no human footprints are found. What people nowadays often raise in their homes originally came from taking these wild colonies. This is called domestic honey. In Kumano, Kii Province, many people raise bees as an industry. What is called mountain honey is of the highest quality. Other provinces also produce honey. That from Tosa Province is considered good quality. After aging for years, it solidifies like white sand and does not spoil, having a rich and sweet taste. Fresh honey has the fragrance of plum blossoms, with an aftertaste that overflows beyond words. Also, honey from Nabeshima and Omura in Hizen Province has a rich taste of exceptional quality, and is presented annually to the shogunate for official use. Generally, honey bees make honey from late spring to late autumn, going out to collect flower stamens on their legs and bringing back the essence of flowers, stuffing it into the honeycomb cells and fermenting it into honey, like brewing sake. In winter months, fearing wind and cold, they hibernate in their honeycomb chambers. During this time, they eat the honey they previously brewed and stored as sustenance. Honey is the bees' food supply. Therefore, when harvesting honey, one must leave food for them and not take everything. If everything is taken, the bees will starve to death.
