Sankambo (Wood sorrel)　I believe
　　　　　　　　　　Because of its sour taste, it was given this name as a local vernacular term.
　　　　　　　　　　Therefore it has the name "sukanbo."
　　　　　　　　　　The Wakan Sansai Zue records both "sanmo" (sukanbo) and
　　　　　　　　　　the Japanese name for rhubarb as "sushi," but they are
　　　　　　　　　　the same thing.

The Zōho Tashiki-hen, in the section on poisonous plants, states:
　　　Sanmo　Japanese name: Sushi (according to current investigation, commonly called "Suidaikusa")　Alternative name: Sankyōtei (Mountain dock)
　　　　Sandaiō　Bu　Sanbo　Sakusu
　　　　Tōyaku (Swertia)

　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Kinoto-tori year, Kosen month, 18th day
　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Painted from life

　　　　　　　　　The Yamato Honzō records both rinkin and marumeru as two varieties
　　　　　　　　　They are not the same thing nor of the same type

The Zōho Tashiki-hen, in the section on shrubs, states:
　　　Rinbai　Japanese name: Some say "rinkin," another theory says the current
　　　　　　name in Nanban language is "marumeru," and this is it.

　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Kinoto-tori year, Kosen month
　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　19th day, painted from a broken branch

Honzō Ikka-gen states:
　　　Rinbai (marumero, also) Oninajo tree

　　　　　　　　　The explanation of marumeru in Yamato Honzō matches well. In the pharmacopoeia there is
　　　　　　　　　rinbai (unbotsu), and some say this might be marumeru, but however, the
　　　　　　　　　rinbai in the pharmacopoeia has a very sweet taste and fragrant aroma. It also says the flowers are white and
　　　　　　　　　green. The pharmacopoeia's description mentions that the flowers are white and fragrant.
　　　　　　　　　Marumeru has pale red flowers, and both flowers and fruit are ineffective. The fruit tastes
　　　　　　　　　astringent and sour. It is not rinbai. Rinbai does not exist in Japan.
　　　　　　　　　Marumeru is probably a foreign word. It came from foreign countries and does not exist
　　　　　　　　　in China. ○Genju says that from ancient times, doubts should remain as doubts,
　　　　　　　　　and commonly used names should not be changed.
