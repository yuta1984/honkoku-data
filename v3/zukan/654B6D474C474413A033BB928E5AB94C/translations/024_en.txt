**Chestnut (栗, Kuri)**

From the *Wakan Sansai Zue* (Japanese-Chinese Illustrated Encyclopedia), Fruits section:
　　The flowers of the chestnut
　　　fall during the rainy season (梅雨) of the fifth month. Therefore, in common usage,
　　　"kuribana-chiru" (栗花落, "chestnut blossoms falling") has become a reading for "tsuyu" (梅雨, the rainy season).

From the *Wamyō Ruijushō*:
　　　Kuríko (栗子, chestnut)

From the *Kenmyōen*:
　　　The chestnut is "a companion of the sword's quality" (刀質の友); alternate name: *Senshi* (撰子)
　　　　Japanese reading: Kuri (久利)

---

　　　　　　　　　　　　An old text states:
　　　　　　　　　　　　In the fourth year of Emperor Keikō's reign, in the province of Ōmi (近江),
　　　　　　　　　　　　there stood a withered tree whose crown pierced the sky,
　　　　　　　　　　　　entering the clouds. When an elder of the province was asked
　　　　　　　　　　　　about this, he replied: "This is a chestnut tree from the Age of the Gods.
　　　　　　　　　　　　Because its branches stand level with the mountains,
　　　　　　　　　　　　it is called 'Naraeda (並枝) Mountain'; and because it
　　　　　　　　　　　　aligns with the high peaks, it is called 'Hirayama (並聯山).'
　　　　　　　　　　　　The fallen soil and everything within the earth is entirely chestnut leaves."
　　　　　　　　　　　　And so on. The name of Kurimoto District also perhaps
　　　　　　　　　　　　originates from this.

　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　【Tree Category】

---

　　　　　　　　　　There is a variety called Sawaguri (沢栗, "marsh chestnut"). This is a folk name;
　　　　　　　　　　it grows abundantly in the mountains of Kiso and along marshes.
　　　　　　　　　　Its fruit and leaves are no different from the ordinary chestnut.
　　　　　　　　　　Its wood is superior to that of the common chestnut, and is used for making utensils and implements.
　　　　　　　　　　The fruit is small, but it often grows into a large tree. It belongs to the category of club-like trees.
　　　　　　　　　　The term *Sasaguri* (栭栗, "small chestnut") refers to the small variety — it is simply a small chestnut.
　　　　　　　　　　It is also called *Shibaguri* (柴栗). The commentary of the *Erya* notes that
　　　　　　　　　　"in the Jiangdong region of China, small chestnuts are called 栭栗 (*yuritsu*)."
　　　　　　　　　　In Cui Yushi's *Shokukyō* (Food Classic), it is called *Chinshi* (枕子).
　　　　　　　　　　Shibaguri is sold as if it were large-fruited chestnut, but supply is limited;
　　　　　　　　　　most are used as *Kachiguri* (搗栗, pounded dried chestnuts) — a traditional food
　　　　　　　　　　offering for the New Year season, for which it is widely used.
　　　　　　　　　　The method of preparing Kachiguri is described in detail in the *Jitsupū*.
