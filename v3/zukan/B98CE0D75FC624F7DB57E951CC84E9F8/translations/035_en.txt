【Matsuba-ran (Whisk Fern / Psilotum nudum)】

From "Yamato Honzō"

Pine-orchid 《side note: now called》 Pine-leaf Orchid

　The Matsuba-ran (pine-leaf orchid) is considered the standard/authentic form of this plant. In recent years, further rare varieties have appeared on the market, their prices exchangeable for gold, and they are treasured as precious curiosities. Among those who cultivate them there are many enthusiasts, though I myself am not particularly fond of them. Nevertheless, friends who are devoted admirers of the pine-orchid have repeatedly urged me to make true-life illustrations of the rare varieties; thus I have compiled this illustrated catalogue of rare Matsuba-ran varieties.

【Tade-ai (Indigo / Polygonum tinctorium)】

From "Honzō" (Materia Medica) and "Mōshi Hinmotsu Zukō" (Illustrated Study of Plants in the Book of Odes)

　Ryoku-ran (Dye Knotweed / Indigo)
　　Ai-tade

There are five varieties of ai (indigo dye plants). The first is Shō-ran (Isatis tinctoria / woad), whose leaves resemble those of the white turnip (hakusai/hakusu). The second is Ba-ran (Strobilanthes), whose leaves resemble those of kuka (bitter herb). The third is Go-ran, which has long stems like mugwort (yomogi) and white flowers. The fourth is Moku-ran (Indigofera), with stems three to four feet tall, leaves resembling those of the Japanese pagoda tree (enju), and which opens pale pink flowers in the seventh month. The fifth is Ryoku-ran (dye knotweed, Polygonum tinctorium), which is precisely the plant shown here.

Dye knotweed is cultivated in large quantities and is of great benefit to the lives of common people. Jinba Village, in the neighboring area, produces large amounts of indigo. The method of producing indigo is as follows: first, the dye knotweed is dried in the shade until thoroughly dry, then soaked in water, steamed with hot water to soften it, pounded in a mortar, and the juice wrung out and used to dye paper, which is then removed. This is called ai-gami (indigo paper). The boiled liquid is then simmered down and hardened into ball-shaped lumps, which are called ai-dama (indigo balls). Ai-sumi (indigo ink) and ai-rō (indigo wax) are also produced by similar methods.
A female servant of mine is a native of Jinba Village and told me that she herself used to make this indigo at home.
