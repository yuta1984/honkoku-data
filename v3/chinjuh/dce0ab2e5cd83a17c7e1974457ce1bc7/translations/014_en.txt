Following the style of Muqi's paintings, only three parts - the head and hands - are painted in the six ken by three ken space. The names of adult worshippers are written and hung in the worship hall, including samurai from Kii and Owari domains, retainers of other feudal lords, and people from various districts of Edo and the Shinagawa area who make monthly pilgrimages. When I mentioned that this was my first visit today, they expressed surprise, saying some people come every month. After prostrating myself before the altar, I left through the gate and walked east a little, where I found the Iwatsuki Jion-ji temple road to the north. Walking along, I climbed up onto Tone Embankment. Here on the left side stands a stone marker carved with "Senior First Rank Handa Inari Daimyōjin."

They say if you go left along the top of the embankment, you'll reach Matsudo. Going south along the embankment for just a short distance, you reach Fuda-no-tsuji intersection. Here, if you descend the embankment westward, you'll reach Shinjuku. Continuing south along the embankment for about two chō, there is Katori Shrine on the eastern side of the embankment, on the bank of the Tone River. Coming out to this bank and looking north, the Matsudo area can be seen stretching into the distance - it must be about one ri away. Though no rain has fallen in Edo since the 19th of the fifth month, this river shows no signs of running dry and flows blue and clear, suggesting it must be raining upstream.

Going about half a ri from there, a stone marker reading "Edo Road" stands on the western side of the embankment. Descending the embankment toward Edo from here for two or three chō, on the south side of the road stands Kyōeisan Daikyō-ji temple where Taishaku-ten is enshrined. The hall is about six ken in size.
