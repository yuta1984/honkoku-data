Walking about one chō further, I reached Saikōji Temple in Shibue Village, of the Tendai sect. The main hall faces east. In the temple compound, about twenty to thirty ken to the southwest, where there are about three pine trees, there was originally an Inari shrine here, which has now been moved and enshrined in the main hall. This is called Kiyoshige Inari according to tradition. In the corner of the southern field within the temple grounds stands a five-ring pagoda (gorintō), said to be the grave of Kasai Saburō Kiyoshige. Whether this is true or not, behind that five-ring pagoda there is a blue stone grave marker carved with "a certain Zen monk, 14th day of the 11th month, Ōei 20" - this is separate from the gorintō. Whatever the case, the gorintō must be the mark of some appropriate person. Thinking it probably is not Kiyoshige's grave naturally dampens one's spirits. Having nothing to offer, I simply bow and depart. Leaving the temple gate and walking about two chō through the trees to the east,

I found Kyakujin Daigongen. The hall is a four-sided pavilion about nine shaku wide, with lattice screens and only a small zushi shrine inside. I wonder when this deity was first enshrined here. The Kyakujin deity originally moved from Hakusan in Echizen to Mount Hiei, and for this reason is called Maroudo no Daimyōjin, but why is it called Gongen? Since Masayasu's home province of Iwakuni village in Suō is where my ancestors lived for generations, and this very deity is the tutelary god of that place, I feel a personal connection and prostrate myself in worship. From here I crossed back over the original irrigation water bridge and returned along the road I had crossed this morning. Around the time the five o'clock bell rang, I arrived at my lodging.

Today, listening to a man who shared the boat at Yotsugi tell various stories, I heard about the world...
