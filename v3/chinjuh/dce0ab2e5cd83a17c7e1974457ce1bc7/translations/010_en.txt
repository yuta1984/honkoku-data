And so, here there are seven or eight houses that sell sake, sweets, rice, and such. Entering a path on the right side of the Ichikawa road, there is a Tendai sect temple called Saikōji, where it is said there is the grave of Kasai Saburō Kiyoshige. Planning to visit on the return journey, I glanced to the left while passing by and continued on.

Yotsugi【present-day Yotsugi】Here there are two teahouses, and also shops selling sake and rice, which are called "Niken Chaya" (Two-House Tea Shops). I rested here for a while. From this place, boats are floated on the waterway and pulled by rope for about 28 chō. This is called the Yotsugi hikifune (tow boat). Though it is a place well-known to many people, this was my first time coming here, so it seemed quite remarkable.

I boarded the boat from in front of the teahouse. There were four passengers in total, all travelers heading toward Mito.

From the 13th of last month, it has been extremely cold, far beyond merely cool. Yesterday when I went to the Gion Festival, I layered padded kimono and lined garments, but even so I could barely endure the cold. Today too I am wearing an additional lined kimono. However, the east wind blows coolly, and I do not feel any heat inside the boat. Since entering the doyō period on the 7th of this month, there has been no heat at all. Furthermore, no rain has fallen, the sky is clear without clouds, yet it is so cool that I wonder strangely what could be the cause.

At the place where the tow boat journey ends, there are also two teahouses, which are also called "Niken Chaya." Together with the previous two teahouses, there are seven boats each, making fourteen boats in total. The number of rope-pullers is also fixed, and they travel up and down between east and west. The boat fare is twenty-four sen per person...
