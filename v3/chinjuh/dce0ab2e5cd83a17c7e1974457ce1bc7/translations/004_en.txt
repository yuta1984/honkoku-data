Stele of Azuma Forest in Katsushika District, Shimōsa Province

The records state that Prince Yamato Takeru had a consort named Ototachibana-hime, daughter of Hozumi no Sukune Oshiyama. When the prince went on his eastern expedition, the princess also accompanied him. Proceeding from Suruga Province to Sagami Province, when they were about to cross the sea of Hashirimizu to Kazusa Province, winds arose and waves became violent, threatening to overturn their warships. The princess said, "This must surely be the wrath of the sea god," and threw herself into the waves as a substitute for the prince. The wind and waves then calmed, and the ships were able to reach shore safely. After the prince pacified the Emishi, he toured the fields of Hitachi, Kai, and Musashi, and when he climbed Usui Pass and gazed into the distance, he sighed and said, "Ah, my beloved wife!" The eastern provinces came to be called "Azuma" (my wife) from this beginning.

Thereafter, many shrines were built throughout the Inland Sea region, and in Katsushika of Shimōsa Province there is also Azuma Forest. Tradition says this is the tomb of Princess Tachibana. Though this is not recorded in the official histories, when oral tradition among the people is inscribed on this stele, how can one doubt it? Now we engrave it in stone to show it as increasingly imperishable. The princess's fierce spirit was truly like that of a great hero, and hearing of it, one cannot help but be moved. And who would not rejoice to record how the prince's single utterance became the enduring name for the eastern regions? That the divine descendants continued to be worshipped for ten generations until the present day is also recorded by shrine officials.

The inscription reads:
The land of Azuma, the forest of Azuma, the prince and princess of the realm, for a thousand ages
