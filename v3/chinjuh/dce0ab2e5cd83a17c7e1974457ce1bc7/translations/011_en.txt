When I asked where the forest was, I was told it was the woods behind the two teahouses on the opposite side. The view was indescribably magnificent.

After a while, the boat reached the far shore. Upon disembarking, I found myself on the west side of the Mito Highway. To the northwest, Senju was said to be one and a half ri from here. Walking five or six chō east from in front of the two teahouses, I reached the ferry crossing at Shinjuku. From here to Matsudo is one and a half ri [the actual half ri is not counted in the calculation, but the Tone River crossing is included as half a ri]. From Matsudo to Kogane is one and a half ri. The river at Shinjuku crossing is not very wide, and the current is gentle. The ferryman operated his boat, and upon reaching the opposite shore,

Shinjuku [the area by the river is called Kami-juku, then Naka-juku, then Shimo-juku. There are about two houses]. In Naka-juku stands the Sannō Gongen shrine. Today being a festival day, it was bustling with activity. Going up from the ferry, at the entrance to Kami-juku there are three inns. Among them, one called Nakagawaya has a distinctive gate structure, and even the rooms and furnishings are clean. I entered and had lunch. Perhaps due to the poor weather of the past two or three days, there was no fish at all. "It will surely be good in autumn. We'll serve sweetfish and carp caught in the river in front," said the male proprietor.

By the riverside, where the ferryman's hut stands, there is a stone marker. "Yūgao Kannon" is carved into it. It is said to be eighteen chō north along the river embankment. Going there and then visiting Handa Inari, Taishakuten, and other temples would not be inconvenient, he said. After finishing the meal, I left that place and headed along the riverbank.
