This man was not someone who made sand collection his profession, but since it was a day off from farming, he did it rather than doing nothing. When I asked if he would sell fish if people requested it, he said no, because even if people asked, it would be inconvenient when he couldn't catch fish. When the cool autumn wind rises, he baits the shallow parts of the riverbank here and lowers nets to catch many fish. For bait, he uses something called hie (barnyard millet), feeding it well as bait, scattering it in the shallows around evening, and when night falls, he targets the fish that come gathering and lowers his nets. He catches countless bass, catfish, carp and similar fish. Some people sell these, while others use them for their own meals. Being asked by people happens occasionally, he said.

For shark nets, unlike Edo nets which use fine thread, he makes his own with coarse mesh using thick thread because fine thread gets bitten through. For bass and carp nets, he said he uses ordinary fine thread.

After walking about half a ri along the ridge paths and looking back, I could see the Yūgao Kannon and the Fuji Asama forest in the distance. Walking a little further, I came out on the south side of Handa Inari shrine. This area is said to be part of Higashi Kasai domain, worth 30,000 koku. In front of the shrine there are vermillion-lacquered sacred fences and a tile-roofed gate. The shrine faces east, with a worship hall measuring six ken by three ken, an offering hall of about three ken, and the main shrine of about two ken. In the worship hall, a dragon is painted in ink wash, with very admirable brushwork and ink tones. Looking closely, I recognized it as the work of my acquaintance Katori Eikō.
