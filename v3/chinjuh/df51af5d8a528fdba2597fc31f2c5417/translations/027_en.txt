However, while water did not reach the outlying villages, water was being stolen from the irrigation channels here and there, which eventually led to disputes and fights among the participants. Villages that had stolen water were called derogatory names like "vinegar konjac" and refused to send laborers to the water wheel stations, leaving the outlying villages with nothing but wasted effort and causing great confusion. After that, no one attempted such projects, and with the limited power of just one or two villages, they helplessly watched good rice crops die, which I found truly pitiful. When I inquired in detail about the source of irrigation water, I learned that water was diverted to the reservoir at Kogō Village, about twenty-three ri from this area, drawing water from a place called Kawamata into Kogō Village's reservoir, which was then divided into thousands of branches.

The waterways were winding and extended about twenty ri. To try to draw water from twenty ri away when the rice fields had cracked and the soil had turned white was like Zhuangzi's parable of the dried fish - by the time the water arrived from so far away, the rice would have lost its vitality, which amounts to abandoning the advantages of the land. According to local stories, when Mount Asama in Shinano Province erupted and collapsed, its earth and sand were pushed into the Tone River system, causing the river to become more silted than in the past and the water to flow at a higher level. This seems likely to be true, as when viewing sailing boats from distant villages, they appear to be traveling above the embankments. This indicates that the river's water surface flows higher than the land within the embankments.
