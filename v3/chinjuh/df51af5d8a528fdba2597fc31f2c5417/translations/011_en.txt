The village headman says this stone is one that suffers damage from cold, and during winter it cracks and breaks here and there, but from when spring passes and warm weather begins to arrive, the broken places gradually swell and return to their original form. When I examined it closely, I saw that everywhere it had been damaged, it had swelled back to its original state, and during summer it completely returns to its original stone form. It truly deserves to be called a strange stone. The quality of the stone is very coarse, like a soft gray stone made mixed with sand. I believe it must be a "living resurrection stone" (kasshō-seki). I have not yet seen a genuine living resurrection stone myself, only heard of them in stories, but this can certainly be given that name. Living stones exist here and there throughout the provinces, and what is called the Kaname-ishi (keystone) of Kashima in Hitachi Province is also a living stone. It is commonly called a "rooted stone." No matter how many fathoms deep one digs, the stone has roots, and it seems as though it has grown up from the very foundation of the earth. This standing stone is also called a deep-rooted stone. If it is a stone that breaks off during winter and heals back to its original state during summer, it would not be unreasonable to call it a living resurrection stone.
