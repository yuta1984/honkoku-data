Since there are numerous plots of land along the Tone River called "townspeople's holdings," including reed sandbanks, rice fields, and mixed farmland, I asked the local people what circumstances led to such townspeople's holdings. They answered that this was for taking profit from the land, and in exchange, maintaining the river embankments. However, the official construction sites are scattered here and there, making it difficult to understand at a glance. The townspeople's land holdings became shares that were bought and sold, and until recently they were traded for one thousand ryō each. If so, there must be profits unknown to others. Now the ownership has passed through many hands and become joint ownership among about ten people. Even when the village urges repairs to damaged construction sites, progress is slow, and since it's not a single person's responsibility, expenses have doubled compared to before. Places that were once sold are now worth only about five hundred ryō. What circumstances originally led to these becoming townspeople's holdings with townspeople responsible for construction? Since it is said that competing for profit cannot match merchants, there must surely be some avenue that provides great benefit to the townspeople.
