Pursuing the benefits of land is not for the sake of desiring profit, but because it is the fundamental principle of receiving what Heaven has bestowed and utilizing it to sustain human life. Therefore, even if it is vast land, leaving places where the five grains should grow as wasteland is a principle that should be feared and avoided. Of course, Takamura being extensive with fallow fields is according to the benevolent governance of generations past, and is not something to be criticized. The wasteland illustrated here is land that could yield several hundred koku without much human labor, yet it has long been unable to obtain the benefits of the land and has become empty wilderness—something truly regrettable. When I stopped by and examined it closely, there were useless old embankments here and there in the wilderness. These appear to be remnants from a time when the river flowed widely through this area.

If this soil were used to build embankments for the Tone River, raising them about two shaku at the top, it would prevent most high waters, and the sites of the old embankments could become fields, combining both benefits and helping to save the people. In any province, building embankments along river courses involves complaints from villages along the upper reaches, and reasonable objections arise, making it difficult to obtain the benefits of that land, so there are often many places that must be abandoned out of necessity. This is because there are reasons for losing the benefits of old rice fields.
