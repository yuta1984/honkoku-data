[From here: Katsushika District Section - Part 2]
When I traveled through the villages of East Katsushika around mid-June, there had been no rain at all since a little rainfall in early May, and the drought was severe. When I observed the rice fields, more than half had no water at all, not even a drop, and in some places the soil had dried and cracked, turning white, with considerable damage to the rice plants. If there is no rain within the next four or five days, the rice will droop its leaves and fall over. In this area, the Naka River flows to the west and the Tone River (which the locals call the Edo River) flows to the east. Between these two rivers, the distance is about seventy to eighty chō in wide areas and fifty to sixty chō in narrow areas, and there is terrain where water can be drawn in multiple channels using reverse flow without exerting too much human effort. In my home province, there are similar places, and in drought years,

ten or twenty villages would consult together, dam the river water with embankments, dig ditches to draw it to higher ground, and use water wheels called "Yamato-guruma" arranged in three or five tiers, called "tsugi-guruma" (relay wheels), with multiple wheels lined up. Each village would establish regulations for taking turns, working day and night without negligence. In this way, rice fields of five thousand or ten thousand koku could have water drawn into them within a few days, and this moisture could sustain them for ten to twelve or thirteen days. During this time, they would receive rain and successfully harvest their rice crop. Since this is the custom in my province, I asked the locals why they don't draw water from the Tone River here in Katsushika as well. They replied that in this area too, in previous years, the villages had agreed together to draw water from the Tone River.
