It was built during the Wado era and remained temple grounds until around the Tenbun period. However, during the fall of Kokufudai Castle, a young monk from this temple was washing his loincloth in the river, tied it to the tip of a long bamboo pole, claimed it was a Genji white banner, and playfully imitating warfare, erected it within the temple grounds. The attacking forces of Kokubufu saw this from afar and thought that fallen warriors had raised their banner again at this temple, so they rushed there in great numbers and set fire to it from upwind. As a result, all the temple buildings burned down completely, and from then on it declined to its present state, as the story goes. Such things may well have happened.

These villages are located where the old Tone River flows around to the north and the water stagnates, so water disasters are frequent. In the flood of Hōei 1 (Year of the Monkey), the embankment of this area broke and overflowed, causing great floods from Nishi-Kasai to Honjo, with deaths numbering in the hundreds. The villages of Nigōhan were in a depression where water reached the eaves and were on the verge of being swept away. From the villages of Nishi-Kasai, many people gathered on the embankment to prevent the water and ran about to prevent the embankment from breaking, while from the other side of Nigōhan, people prayed that the Nishi-Kasai embankment would break. However, with the force of several hundred people using tatami mats and such to hold back the water, it narrowly avoided breaking. In a place called Togasaki Village in Nigōhan, there have been three lions since ancient times, and when I inquired about their form, they differ from the lions of today, being about the size of Echigo lion heads.
