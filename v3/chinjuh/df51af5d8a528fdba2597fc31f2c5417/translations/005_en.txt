This temple belongs to the Tendai sect, with Jōchi Nyorai as the principal deity, holding a vermillion seal grant of twenty-five koku of rice, and is generally a fine temple. Since the story of Umewaka-maru is well known to people of the world, I shall omit it. When I examined the temple's founding records, they were written in an interesting Japanese prose style and appear to have been authored in recent years. The matter of Umeka (plum lotus) does not appear in ancient texts, but the founding records state that he was the son of Yoshida Shōshō, whose father served three imperial courts - those of Emperor Murakami, Emperor Reizei, and Emperor En'yū - and that approximately eight hundred years have passed until this year. Upon reflection, that was an era dominated by court nobility, equivalent to feudal lords in today's world, and it is difficult to understand how a son or lady of such status could walk about alone, even in ancient times. Apart from what is revealed in the Noh play "Sumidagawa," we know nothing of Umewaka-maru's story, and like the two children of Sano Genzaemon and Iwaki Hangwan - Princess Anju and Zushiō-maru - he appears to have vanished from the world. Noh plays are like the jōruri books of that time, created for theatrical entertainment, and even before that period, there were numerous illustrated storybooks with fictional tales based on various stories for popular entertainment. The fact that this temple, being of the Tendai sect, now enshrines Umewaka-maru as Sannō Gongen is likely because it took the name Sannō in connection with Mount Hiei north of the capital. In any case, rather than being deceived by Buddhist priests into believing in fictitious foreign Buddhas that don't even exist in India, it would be closer to the true way to respect and honor the people of Japan.
