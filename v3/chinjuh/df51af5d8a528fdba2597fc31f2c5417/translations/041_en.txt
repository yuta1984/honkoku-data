They also gather small shrimp and seaweed, and during breaks from farming, they collect and use an abundant seaweed called "kugo" (note: a strong grass that is processed into rope, straw raincoats, and various other products). From the sea surface, fish are caught daily in enormous quantities, and therefore the village appears very prosperous. The seaweed called "Ukita nori" in this area is a famous local product, said to have a good reputation second only to Suizen nori from Higo Province (note: I have not eaten it myself, so I do not know the actual facts).

As they say, "when full, one learns propriety" - the village headmen in this area can read a little and have some appreciation for poetry. The middle and lower-class farmers favor courage, and there are stones for testing strength here and there. Farmers who live reasonably well seem to specialize in practicing martial arts. Among them, the village headman of Kuwagawa Village, though young, was sincere and had good customs, and I thought him a person who could serve as a model for others.

The borrowed newly reclaimed land is vast, with excellent soil quality, and when it becomes rice fields, it is difficult to measure the land plots. When I asked the local people why it was kept as dry fields, they answered that there were no irrigation channels. Looking carefully at the lay of the land, it's not that there are no waterways, but there are obstructions in the water channels, and because it would interfere with the main rice fields of other villages, it appears they have no choice but to keep it as dry fields.

Even without widening the irrigation ditch that draws water from the Yokogawa River, if they dug the ditch a little deeper and built a dam on the Yokogawa River as shown in the diagram to draw irrigation water, it would not interfere with other villages' main rice fields and could support rice fields worth two thousand koku.
