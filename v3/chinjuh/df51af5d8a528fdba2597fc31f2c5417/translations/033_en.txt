It is simply a matter of relocating the embankment and adding a little soil. Since it is not far from the sea, there are good drainage channels for bad water, and moreover the soil has good cohesion, so even thin embankments are stronger against water than soil from other places. Generally, the embankment width is only four or five ken, with narrow sections being four ken. Even when floodwaters overflow the top of the embankment during floods, it never breaks or collapses. When it occasionally does break, it is in places with many moles, where water overflows through these holes and causes breaks. Of course, being near the sea where the water's force is gentler, there are reasons why it doesn't collapse. Anyone should understand matters of this degree, yet the fact that it is left abandoned like this may have its reasons—being ignorant of economics, I cannot comprehend it.

Since it has not rained for a long time, during travel through the upper villages, the rice cultivation suffers everywhere one goes, with many rice fields showing white cracks in places. However, in this area the rice growth is extremely splendid, and all rice fields that are not flooded are completely full of water. When I inquired in detail, I learned that although not a drop of water comes down from the Ogai village irrigation channels, at high tide the Tone River water is let into the channels for draining bad water, and this is made to flow backward into the rice fields. Because of this, the local people say that judging from the current conditions, it will be a good harvest year.
