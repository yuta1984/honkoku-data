Behind the temple is a small pond called "Kagami no Chi" (Mirror Ground), where it is said the saint reflected his image to create the statue. There is also what is called the "Kesa-kake Matsu" (Surplice-hanging Pine), which has withered and fallen, leaving only the stump barely remaining. There is another fine hall containing a statue said to be carved by Prince Shotoku himself, a Bishamonten attributed to Master Jigaku, and one Kannon statue of unknown authorship - all excellent Buddhist images. They also treasure two Buddhist paintings said to be by the monk Eshin. All are superb works and paintings that astonish the eye. If they existed in today's world, they would surely become very famous. The head priest's boastful telling of the temple's origins seemed quite justified.

Nishi-Ukita Village [Ukida, Edogawa Ward]
In this village lived a peasant named Tora (Tiger), whose strength surpassed all others - he was thick-boned and tall. He could easily shoulder stones weighing over seventy-five kanme and walk carrying five bags of rice - such was his tremendous strength. Once, when he transported lumber by boat to Edo, there was a dispute at the timber landing. Some young men from Edo called "kiohi-gumi" came out and tried to prevent the lumber from being unloaded at the wharf. At this point, Tora swung a piece of lumber about two ken long, whirling it around like swinging a bamboo staff, making whooshing sounds as he leaped onto the wharf.
