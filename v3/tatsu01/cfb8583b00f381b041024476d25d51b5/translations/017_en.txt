Kasuga Grand Shrine Four Great Myōjin Deities (enshrined at the foot of Mount Mikasa, eleven ri from the imperial capital. Listed as the four Kasuga festival deities in the Engishiki Jinmyōchō)

From Shoku Kokin Collection - Poem by Kasuga Great Myōjin:
"Know me as Shakyamuni Buddha who appeared in this world to illuminate the world with the pure moon"

In the ancient age of the gods, when Emperor Jinmu first entered the Land of the Reed Plains (Japan), evil spirits opposed him. Therefore, Futsunushi-no-mikoto (Katori Myōjin) and Takemikazuchi-no-mikoto (Kashima Myōjin) were sent as subjugation envoys, bearing spears to govern the land. They opened the heavenly rock cave, illuminated the eternal darkness, and comforted the people's sorrows. Thus, Amaterasu Ōmikami and Ame-no-koyane-no-mikoto made a deep divine covenant, and like the unceasing flow of the Mimosuso River and the gentle spring breeze of Mount Mikasa, their manifestations brought peace to the nation and supported the royal law. Looking up at the shrine grounds, one sees the two-story gate tower, three corridors, the brilliant glow of night lanterns, and four shrine pavilions arranged in perfect order.

The first eastern shrine hall enshrines Takemikazuchi-no-kami (also called Takeikazuchi-no-kami, Tatebutsu-no-kami, or Toyobutsu-no-kami. This deity was born from the blood that dripped from the sword guard when Izanagi-no-mikoto slew the fire god Kagutsuchi - Nihon Shoki). Initially descended to Shiogama Bay in Mutsu Province, subdued evil spirits, and manifested at Kashima in Hitachi Province. On the 21st day of the 6th month of Jingo-Keiun 1, departed from Kashima, and arrived at Nakayama in Komofu on the 7th day of the 12th month of the same year. The accompanying retainers were Tokikaze and Hideyuki (Tokikaze's descendants became shrine administrators, Hideyuki's became construction administrators). The attendant was Otonomaru (ancestor of the shrine priests, Umenoki clan). At that time, when Tokikaze and Hideyuki offered provisions, they presented chestnuts that were nearby,

and the deity was pleased and granted them the surname Uekuri (Planted Chestnut). This is the origin of how the descendants of Tokikaze and Hideyuki came to bear the Uekuri surname under the Nakatomi name. On the 9th day of the 1st month of the 2nd year, moved to Mount Abe in Yamato Province, and on the 9th day of the 11th month of the same year, established presence at Mount Mikasa (from Kasuga Koki summary. Some parts differ from Kuji Kongen's account. Also, Seisui-ki states that the deity rode a white deer with a sakaki branch on the saddle, covered by five-colored clouds, with five divine mirrors appearing above the clouds, shining brilliantly as they moved to Mount Mikasa).

The second shrine hall is for Futsunushi-no-mikoto (also called Iwainushi-no-kami or Iwai-no-ushi. This deity was formed from the blood that flowed from the blade of the sword when Izanagi-no-mikoto cut the fire god. Descended to Isotasa no Ohama in Izumo Province and pacified the evil spirits of that land - Nihon Shoki). This is the deity of Katori Myōjin in Shimousa Province. Moved to Mount Mikasa in Jingo-Keiun 2 (Kasuga Koki).

The third shrine hall is for Ame-no-koyane-no-mikoto (ancestral deity of the Nakatomi clan, also called Tsuhayamusubi-no-mikoto, Koichichimusubi-no-mikoto, or Kokotomusubi-no-mikoto - Kojiki, also called child of Kokotomusubi-no-kami - Nihon Shoki. When Amaterasu Ōmikami shut herself in the heavenly rock cave and the four seas fell into eternal darkness, this deity together with Futodama-no-mikoto uprooted a sacred sakaki tree from Mount Amano-Kaguyama, hung yasakani jewels on the upper branches, the eight-span mirror on the middle branches, and blue and white cloth offerings on the lower branches. When all the deities consulted together in prayer, the rock cave was opened, the clouds of eternal darkness cleared, and day and night were distinguished - Nihon Shoki).

This is the deity of Hiraoka Myōjin in Kawachi Province. The enshrinement was on the tsuchinoe-saru day of the 11th month in the 4th year of Emperor Kōtoku (37th emperor), moving to Mount Mikasa before the other three deities (recorded in Kasuga Shaki. Kuji Kongen records that all four shrine deities were enshrined together in Jingo-Keiun 2).

The fourth shrine hall is for Hime-Ōmikami (also called Ōhirume-no-muchi or Amaterasu-Ōhirume-no-mikoto). This is Amaterasu Ōmikami of the Inner Shrine at Isuzu River in Ise Province (from Kasuga Shaki.
