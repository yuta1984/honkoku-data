【Right Page】
The capital of Heijō (Nara) was first established by Empress Genmei, the mother and dowager of Emperor Monmu, in the second year of Wadō (709 CE). 《Note: Nara was also written as Nara, Dakuraku, Neiraku, Nairaku, Hei, and Nara》 In the third year, the capital was moved there, and seven emperors 《Note: Genmei, Genshō, Shōmu, Kōken, the deposed emperor, Shōtoku, and Kōnin》 held their imperial residence in Nara. In the third year of Enryaku (784) under Emperor Kanmu, the capital was moved to Nagaoka Palace in Yamashiro Province, and in the thirteenth year (794), it was moved to Heian-kyō (Kyoto). The Left Capital refers to present-day Nanto (Nara), and the Right Capital refers to Saikyō (western capital).

《From the Man'yōshū》
Aoniyoshi Nara no miyako wa saku hana no niou ga gotoku ima sakari nari
(Blue-earth Nara, the capital, is now in full bloom like fragrant flowers)

《From the Shin-chokusen-shū》
Aoniyoshi Nara no miyako no kuroki mote tsukureru yado wa oredo akanu kamo - Emperor Shōmu
(In blue-earth Nara's capital, the dwelling made of dark wood never grows tiresome though I dwell there long)

《From the Shūi-shū》
Sora tobu ya kari no tsukai ni itsushika mo Nara no miyako ni kototsute yaran - Hitomaro
(Oh flying through the sky, wild geese messengers, when shall I send word to Nara's capital?)

The site of the imperial palace is not in present-day Nara town, but west of Kōfuku-ji temple, in a place called "Tsuiji no uchi" (within the earthen walls) in the southeast of the southern road of Nijō village, Chōshōji-gō. Even now no rice is cultivated there, and at this location there is a small shrine called "Dairi no miya" (Palace of the Imperial Residence).

《From the Kokin-shū》《Poem by the Emperor of Nara - according to commentaries, Emperor Heizei of the Daidō era》
Furusato to nari ni shi Nara no miyako ni mo iro wa kawarazu hana wa sakeri
(In Nara's capital that has become an old hometown, flowers still bloom with unchanged colors)

《From Hōan Hyakushu, Violets》
Sumire saku Nara no miyako no ato to te wa ishi sue nomi zo katami narikeru - Minamoto no Nakamasa
(Where violets bloom at Nara's capital ruins, only stone foundations remain as mementos)

《From Yomibito Shirazu Shūi》
Nara no ha no na ni ou miya no inishie ni furi ni shi koto kataran - Hōin Kōchō
(Let us speak of ancient things that have grown old in the palace famous for oak leaves)

【Left Page】
Kasuga Field extends from the Great Torii gate east to Kasuga Shrine. The reason this place is named Kasuga is that in the age of the gods, Amaterasu Ōmikami subdued 790 million evil deities and returned them to righteous hearts, bringing peace to all people. Because Amaterasu Ōmikami's heart was pure and peaceful like a tranquil spring day (haru no hi), she rejoiced and named it "Kasuga," and the village in that place was also named Kasuga.

Also, Koyane-no-Okina of Kotomusuhi-no-Mikoto was granted this Kasuga Forest and called it Kasuga Palace. At the base of Mount Mikasa's lower rocky foundations, the palace pillars were erected sturdily, and the four-pillar shrine was built magnificently. Worshippers constantly visit without interruption, and its divine efficacy is renewed daily.

《From Kokin-shū》
Kasuga no no yukima wo wakete oide kuru kusa no hatsuka ni mieshi kimi ka mo - Tadamine
(You appeared faintly like grass growing through snow gaps in Kasuga Field)

《From Shūi-shū》
Uguisu no nakitsuru nabe ni Kasuga no no kyō no mi yuki wo hana to koso mire - Tadafusa
(As the warbler sang, I saw today's snow in Kasuga Field as flowers)

《From Shin-kokin-shū》
Kasuga no no otoro no michi no umoremizu sue dani kami no shirushi arawase - Shunzei
(On Kasuga Field's hidden path, buried water, may the gods show their signs at least in the end)
