【Right page】

The imperial capital of Heijō (Nara) was first established in Nara in the second year of Wadō (709) by Empress Genmei, the mother of Emperor Monmu.
　※ (Note: "Nara" is also written as 那羅, 諾樂, 寧樂, 乃樂, 平, or 楢)
The capital was officially transferred there in the third year of Wadō, and seven successive emperors
　※ (Genmei, Genshō, Shōmu, Kōken, the Deposed Emperor [Junnin], Shōtoku, Kōnin)
held their imperial residence in Nara. Emperor Kanmu relocated the capital to Nagaoka Palace in Yamashiro Province in the third year of Enryaku (784), and in the thirteenth year (794) moved again to Heiankyō (Kyoto). The "Left Capital" refers to present-day Nanto (Nara), while the "Right Capital" refers to Nishikyō.

　【From the Man'yōshū】
　　Glorious Nara's capital — like the fragrance of blossoming flowers, it is now at its very peak.

　【From the Shinchokusen-shū】
　　The lodging built with dark timber in Nara's glorious capital — though old, I never tire of it.　　　　Emperor Shōmu

　【From the Shūi-shū】
　　Oh, could I send a message by the wild geese flying through the sky — how I wish to send word to the capital of Nara.　　　　Hitomaro (Kakinomoto no Hitomaro)

The site of the former imperial palace is not in present-day Nara town; rather, to the west of Kōfuku-ji, in the Chōshōji township, south of Nijō village, to the southeast (tatsumi) of the main road, there is a place called "Tsuiji no Uchi" (Within the Earthen Wall). To this day no fields are cultivated there, and at this site stands a small shrine called "Dairi no Miya" (Shrine of the Inner Palace).

　【From the Kokin-shū】　※ (A poem by the Emperor of Nara. A commentary notes: "Emperor Heijō, the Emperor of Daidō")
　　Though it has become a place of the past, in the old capital of Nara the flowers bloom unchanged in their colors.

　【From the Hōan Hyakushu, on the theme of violets】
　　In the old site of Nara's capital where violets bloom, only the placed stones remain as a keepsake of what once was.　　　　Minamoto no Nakamasa

　【From the Shūi-shū, anonymous】
　　O cuckoo, known by the name of the nara-leaf, tell of the ancient things of the palace passed down through the ages.　　　　Hōin Kōchō

【Left page】

Kasuga Field (Kasugano) refers to the area stretching from the Great Torii eastward to the Kasuga Shrine. The reason this place is called "Kasuga" (Spring Day) dates back to the Age of the Gods: the Great Deity Amaterasu subdued 790 million evil gods and returned the hearts of all people to righteousness, bringing peace and ease to the populace. Because of this, Amaterasu's heart was serene and harmonious — like the gentle warmth of a spring day — and in Her divine joy the deity named this feeling "Kasuga" (Spring Day), and so the village of this place also came to be called Kasuga.
　Furthermore, the deity Kototamusubi no Mikoto granted this Kasuga Forest to the ancestor deity Amenokoyane no Mikoto (progenitor of the Fujiwara clan), naming it "Kasuga-dono" (Spring Day Palace). At the foot of Mount Mikasa, the great pillars of the shrine were firmly planted, and the magnificent sanctuary of the four enshrined deities stands splendidly. Worshippers come in an unending stream, and miraculous efficacy (reigen) is renewed day by day.

　【From the Kokin-shū】
　　Parting the patches of snow on Kasuga Field, the young grass barely peeking through — is that you I glimpse?　　　　Tadamine

　【From the Shūi-shū】
　　As soon as the bush warbler cried, the snow of today on Kasuga Field was transformed into flowers before my eyes.　　　　Tadafusa

　【From the Shin-Kokin-shū】
　　On Kasuga Field, along the overgrown path, the buried spring water — may even its furthest reach reveal the divine sign.　　　　Shunzei

　【Nara Hakkei (Eight Views of Nara) — "Deer of Kasuga Field" (Chinese poem)】
　　No scent, no sound — the colors of the fields are beautiful;
　　One only watches the deer grazing on grass and sleeping.
　　As if combining Shun's deep mountains with the sacred garden of literature —
　　In this place, the holy divine has transformed the very land.
　　　　　　　　　　　　　Kajūji Sangi-Udaiben Tsuneshige

　【Same collection, waka poem】
　　The storm winds on the peaks of Kasuga Mountain must be cold — at the foot of the mountain, in the fields, the deer are crying.
　　　　　　　　　　　　　Shimizudani Gonchūnagon Kimitsu
