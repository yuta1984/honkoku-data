(Continued from previous page: One theory states that the fourth deity, Hime-Ōmikami, is the daughter of Takemikazuchi-no-mikoto and wife of Ame-no-koyane-no-mikoto, and therefore resides in the same shrine hall as Hiraoka Myōjin. According to Shoku Nihongi, in the 9th month of Kashō 3, Consultant Fujiwara no Tasuku was dispatched with an imperial decree. Takemikazuchi-no-mikoto and Futsunushi-no-mikoto were elevated to Senior First Rank, Ame-no-koyane-no-mikoto to Junior First Rank, and Hime-no-kami to Senior Fourth Rank Upper Grade. Given these court ranks, the theory of the conjugal relationship may indeed be plausible.)

**Six Small Shrines of the Middle Precinct** (the area outside the sacred fence is called the Middle Precinct)
- Iwamoto Shrine (located to the southwest of the main shrine; enshrines Sumiyoshi Myōjin)
- Jingo-ji Temple (located to the east)
- Aosakai Shrine (located south of Jingo-ji; associated with blue cloth offerings)
- Karasakaki Shrine (located south of Aosakai Shrine; associated with white cloth offerings)
- Anakuri Shrine (located south of Karasakaki Shrine; enshrines Anatsugu-no-kami)
- Ikuri Shrine (located south of Anakuri Shrine; enshrines Takamusubi-no-mikoto)

**Two Small Shrines of the Inner Precinct** (the area inside the sacred fence is called the Inner Precinct)
- Tajikarao-no-kami (southern shrine)
- Hirai Tenjin (northern shrine; Amenominakanushi-no-mikoto)

The Naoraiden (Hall of Direct Meetings) is where ceremonies for imperial envoys and senior court officials are conducted. It is also called the Hakkōya (Eight Lectures Hall), a name derived from the performance of the Lotus Sutra Eight Lectures ceremony there. (The Eight Lectures began in the first year of Tenryaku during Emperor Murakami's reign, held twice yearly. The chief official was Teishin-kō, and the administrator was the high priest Hyōgen. The "Ōnaohi" mentioned in the Kokinshū also refers to this place.)

The Heiden (Offering Hall) (situated alongside the Naoraiden; the place where imperial envoys present offerings on festival days).

The Maidono (Dance Hall) (adjacent to the Naoraiden; constructed in the first year of Jōgan).

Shishi-bashiri (Deer Run): (According to Kanbuniki records, located between the corridors. Each year on the night of the 5th day of the 2nd month, officials from the three schools of nō—Kongō, Kanze, and Hōshō—come to perform the Okina ritual. This is called Shishi-bashiri. At this time, sacred sake and offerings are brought out from the third shrine hall.)

Ringo-no-niwa (Apple Court): (Located in front of the Heiden; the place where offerings are presented on Kasuga Festival days.)

Mitarashi River: (Refers to the small stream flowing between the corridors. On the day of the Kasuga Festival, the imperial envoy performs hand-washing ablutions here.)

Ichii-bashi (First Rank Bridge): (Refers to the bridge inside the two-story gate.)
Nii-bashi (Second Rank Bridge): (Refers to the bridge in front of the two-story gate.)

From the Kasuga Koki, a poem composed at Ichii-bashi:
"Pray, people—for in the glory of this fleeting world, may the paths of good health, long life, and prosperity be yours."

First Torii Gate: (According to Hōeiki records, this gate is called the Nirvana Gate. It is also known as the Purple Gate.)

Hijiri-no-toko (Sage's Resting Place): (Located inside the corridors. According to Kanbuniki, this is where biwa-playing monks seclude themselves to recite the Jishinkyō sutra.)

【Left Page】

Utsushidono (Transfer Hall): (Located north of the Hakkōya. According to Kanbuniki, this is the place for temporary enshrinement during shrine reconstruction.)

South Gate: (Called the Rōmon, or Two-story Gate. According to Kanbuniki, in former times it was a torii gate with a plaque, but in the summer of Hōki 3 it was struck by lightning and burned. Thereafter it became a two-story gate. It is also said to have been built by Fujiwara no Mitsuyori.)

Yōgō-seki (Manifestation Stone): (Located south of the South Gate; this is the stone upon which the Red Child deity manifested.)

Nyoi-seki (Wish-fulfilling Stone): (Located south of the Manifestation Stone. In Kōhō 4, a bottomless pit opened at this spot. It is said that the shrine administrator Suketo buried a complete set of the Mahāprajna-pāramitā Sūtra and performed a purification ritual for ten thousand seats here.)

Fushō-no-hashi (Bridge of Fushō): (Also called the Oai-no-hashi, or Meeting Bridge.)

From the Kasuga Koki:
"How wondrous! Crossing over Fushō Bridge and hearing the sacred kagura music—what joy fills my heart!"

Sakadono (Sake Hall): (Located to the west of the corridors; the place where ceremonial sake is brewed. Contains two subsidiary shrines: the Sakadono Shrine and the Kama-dono Shrine.)

Gokusho (Offerings Preparation Room): (Located below the Naishi Gate to the west; constructed in the first year of Jōgan.)

Shunki Cherry Tree: (Located between the Naishi Gate and the Offerings Preparation Room. According to Kanbuniki, this is said to be an unrivaled famous tree under heaven.)

**Kasuga Wakamiya Shrine** enshrines Ame-no-oshikumo-no-mikoto, child of Ame-no-koyane-no-mikoto, as recorded in the Myōhō Yōshū. (Namely, the Wakamiya of Hiraoka Myōjin in Kawachi Province is also Ame-no-oshikumo-no-mikoto.) According to one record, the Yoshida family records identify the deity as Ninigi-no-mikoto. However, this is a secret teaching known only to the single lineage of the Wakamiya shrine administrators and is not disclosed to others. (Kasuga Shaki)

Regarding the enshrinement of the Wakamiya: On the 3rd day of the 3rd month of Chōhō 5 (1003), the deity appeared between the second and third shrine halls. Nakatomi Murajisadakoretada, the fifth-generation descendant of Tokikaze, transferred the deity to the third shrine hall and presented liturgical prayers. After 130 years had passed, on the 27th day of the 4th month of Chōshō 4 (1135), Sukefusa, the eighth-generation descendant of Tokikaze, separately constructed a shrine hall and enshrined the deity there. This is the present Wakamiya Daimyōjin.
