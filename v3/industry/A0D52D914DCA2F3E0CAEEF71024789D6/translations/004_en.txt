Various books mention the theory of "three risings and three sleepings" (sanki sanmen) for silkworms, but without being bound by such terminology, we use the common names "okiyo" and "tomi" as standard practice. Other terminology follows the same principle.

There are several varieties of mulberry: those with round leaves are called roson (Lu mulberry), and those with narrow leaves are called keison (thorn mulberry), as can be found in various books. However, for general convenience,

we call ordinary leaves "common leaves" or "Date mulberry."

Mulberry is mentioned in the "Complete Agricultural Manual" (Nōgyō Zensho) as one of the four trees (mulberry, paper mulberry, lacquer, and tea) and is said to be particularly precious. Among the many plants and trees, the one that produces silk thread from green leaves is truly a sacred tree, so it must be planted in addition to the five grains. Medical texts state that "mulberry fruit has a sweet taste, cool nature, is non-toxic, and helps with pain in the five organs and joints."
