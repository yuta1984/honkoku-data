There are also oral traditions, so one should learn well by listening to skilled practitioners.

1. Regarding the method of distinguishing female and male butterflies: When two are mating, know that the thinner and smaller one among them is the male butterfly and should be removed. Know that the plump, thick, and large one among them is the female butterfly and should be kept.
If both appear thick, separate the male butterflies to different paper and distinguish them. If there is a male butterfly among the females, it will restlessly shake its tail and rub against other insects. That insect is the male. Also, if there is a female butterfly among the males that were set aside, it will attach to the males. Female butterflies remain motionless.
One can know well by this method. In any case, female butterflies do not shake their tails and rub around

but remain in one place, as one should know.

1. Any kind of seed paper is acceptable. Having many seeds is considered good. Paper with eggs laid densely is good. When this paper is divided into portions of six to seven bu in weight, the seeds together with the paper amount to one mat's worth.
One full sheet weighs about seven monme and two to three bu together with the seeds. Of this, one monme and two to three bu accounts for the weight of the paper. However, this is the calculation for one thick Mino paper sheet. In any case, one should know that the seeds for one mat amount to about six bu net weight. However, this is the quantity for times of poor harvest. What is cut and divided with paper is bad. Originally, when the insects from that seed paper hatch and emerge, the portion swept away with wings should be measured to about six bu as mentioned above.
