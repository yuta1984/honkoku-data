The places that produce them are few while the places that consume them are many, and moreover, when these become export trade goods to foreign countries, there will be an even greater shortage for domestic use. For such items, we should endeavor to encourage and instruct the people, and if there are unused lands not under cultivation, we should have them plant mulberry trees, paper mulberry, and similar crops as much as possible, so that each of the seventy-odd provinces can produce silk thread and paper—this would not be difficult. Regarding sericulture, this has already been discussed in detail in many works by various authors, including the Sancai Tuhui, Yōsan Zusetsu, and Nōgyō Zensho, and

since many rural women and children also possess this knowledge, seeking this information in Western books now might seem like redundant discussion. However, considering that these have become the primary trade goods since the opening of ports in recent years, such products should be produced in ever greater quantities and should be of ever higher quality. Furthermore, since Western countries may have different methods of sericulture and silk production, we must learn about these.
