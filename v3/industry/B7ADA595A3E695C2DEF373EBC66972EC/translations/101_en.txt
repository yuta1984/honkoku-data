Section 3: On the Hatching of Eggs into Silkworms
If left to nature, they may lag behind or precede the budding of mulberry leaves, sometimes falling short or exceeding the proper timing. Therefore, observing the climate of that year and the condition of the mulberry, if budding is late, place the seeds in a cold place; if budding is early, place them in a warm place. Generally, eggs hatching into silkworms occurs slowly when cold and quickly when warm. If you want them to hatch quickly, burn charcoal to warm a room and hang the egg papers inside, and they will hatch rapidly. If you want them to hatch most quickly, wrap them in cotton and have a strong person wear them against their skin for two or three days, which will be even faster.

Section 4: Rearing silkworms that produce white cocoons is difficult, while those that produce yellow cocoons are easy. This is because yellow cocoon silkworms have a robust nature, while white cocoon silkworms have a delicate nature.

However, the temperature of the silkworm room should be at a level that feels comfortable when wearing lined or unlined garments during the young silkworm period. Cleaning of eggs is limited to noon. Spread the silkworms from one sheet of seed paper over an area of about 2 shaku 4-5 sun square, chop 24 monme of mulberry into fine pieces, and give it twice, at midday and evening. Particularly, observe the dry and wet conditions of the silkworm bed, and never neglect the care of increasing feed when dry and decreasing when moist. If there is insufficient mulberry when dry, or excess mulberry when moist, even if they do not become ill at the time, some will shrink and die as days pass, and others will become abnormally fat and eventually die after excreting white fluid.
