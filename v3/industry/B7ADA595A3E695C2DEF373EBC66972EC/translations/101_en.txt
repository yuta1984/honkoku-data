Section 3: Hatching of Eggs into Silkworms
If left to nature, they may lag behind or precede the budding of mulberry leaves, sometimes falling short or exceeding the proper timing. Therefore, observing the climate of that year and the condition of the mulberry trees, if budding is late, place the eggs in a cold place; if budding is early, place them in a warm place. Generally, when eggs hatch into silkworms, they hatch slowly in cold conditions and quickly in warm conditions. If you want them to hatch quickly, burn charcoal to warm one room and hang the egg papers in it for rapid hatching. For the fastest hatching, wrap them in cotton and place them against the skin of a robust person for two or three days, which is even faster.

Section 4: White Cocoon Silkworms are Difficult to Raise, Yellow Cocoon Silkworms are Easy
This is because yellow cocoon silkworms have a robust nature while white cocoon silkworms have a delicate nature.

However, the temperature of the silkworm room should be at a level that feels comfortable when wearing lined or unlined garments during the young silkworm period. Scattering should be limited to noon. Spread the silkworms from one sheet of egg paper over an area of about 2 shaku 4-5 sun square, chop 24 monme of mulberry into fine pieces, and feed twice a day, at noon and evening. It is particularly important never to neglect monitoring the dryness and moisture of the silkworm bed, increasing feed when dry and decreasing when moist. If the mulberry is insufficient when dry or excessive when moist, even if they do not become ill immediately, some will shrink and die as days pass. Others may become abnormally obese and eventually die by excreting white fluid. On the second day, give 39 monme in three portions.
