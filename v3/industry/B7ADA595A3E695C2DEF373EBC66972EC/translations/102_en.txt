Following the third day example. On the fifth day, divide into three sections. Give mulberry four times with the same amount. If warm, it's fine to give five times. Follow the above example until the eighth day. On this day, divide into six sections. After about nine days, sleeping silkworms will be visible. Once sleeping silkworms are already visible, reduce the amount of mulberry and feed frequently. When awakening silkworms begin to appear, do not feed immediately but wait until all have awakened, then feed little by little. This generally follows the principles of the hatching period. This is called "kuwatsuke" (mulberry attachment). On the second day of kuwatsuke, remove the silkworm bed. At this time, divide into six sections.

Section 6: It is not good to raise young silkworms with mulberry flowers. Mulberry wet with rain and dew should not be used for not only young silkworms but also mature and old silkworms.

Section 7: Nine days from first to second molt, six days from second to third molt, seven days from third to fourth molt, seven days from fourth molt to cocoon formation. From hatching to cocoon formation takes 36-37 days to 42-43 days. This is the general outline, but there are cold and warm years, different climatic conditions of mountains and rivers in different regions, and varying skill levels among sericulturists, so the frequency of mulberry feeding also changes accordingly. Therefore, it cannot be standardized uniformly. Silkworm beds should not be

piled too high. To dispose of this, until the third molt, use feather brooms to gather the silkworms, transfer them to separate containers and remove them. For the fourth and fifth molts, do the same by hand as before.

Section 8: Mounting procedure: Pick up mature silkworms whose bodies have become transparent, bind bamboo to both sides of mat-like or straw bag-like materials so they cannot bend, make continuous rings of straw inside, and store them from the ceiling. Put the picked mature silkworms into this device, and after cocoon formation is complete, scrape them off after four or five days.

Section 9: Days to transform into pupae: If warm, four days; if cold, extends to five or six days.

Section 10: The time from becoming pupae to transforming into moths varies in speed depending on the temperature of the season. Pupae that become maggots always have black spots. The appearance of black spots invariably leads to moths in about ten days, and the cause of becoming maggots depends entirely on the mulberry used in rearing. Mulberry from sandy, gravelly, thin soil along rivers produces many moths. High, dry, fertile land produces many maggots. It also depends on wet land, dry land, and droughts of that year.
