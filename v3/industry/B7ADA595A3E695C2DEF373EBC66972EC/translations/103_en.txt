Section 11: Method of collecting silkworm eggs on paper　First, move the mated male and female moths to another paper, then after about one hour, remove the male moth and place them on silkworm egg paper, surrounding the four sides with coating wood to prevent them from crawling out. Cold weather is not suitable for this timing; warm weather is good. Also, dark places are better than bright ones. Therefore, close the doors and windows and sweep them off the next morning.

Section 12: The period from silkworm hatching to cocoon formation is about forty days, and from cocoon to ant-stage silkworm is sixteen to seventeen days. However, there are variations in timing depending on the cold and warm weather of the year.

Section 13: Summer silkworms probably evolved from spring silkworms. The reason is that among spring silkworm eggs, there are what are called "re-emergence" eggs—three or four out of a hundred that hatch again. The eggs of these silkworms will hatch again the following year. This variety was distinguished and eventually given the name of summer silkworms.

　　This re-emergence is difficult to produce artificially and can only be left to nature. The production of summer silkworm eggs is several degrees easier compared to spring silkworm eggs.

Section 14: One sheet of silkworm egg paper (originally half a sheet) taken thickly contains several hundred silkworms, with about forty thousand silkworm eggs.

Section 15: To raise silkworms from one sheet of paper requires approximately 1,600 mulberry trees, or thirty horse-loads. This is for normal budding, and there are variations in density depending on the year's climate, so I only mention the general numbers here. The silkworm room should not be made too narrow—if narrow, it becomes stuffy. It should not be made too wide either—if wide, it becomes cold. Rather cold than stuffy is preferable.

Section 16: Although there are many varieties of mulberry trees, the best ones are generally limited to three or four types.
