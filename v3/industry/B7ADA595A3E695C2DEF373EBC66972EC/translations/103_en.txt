Section 11: Method of Collecting Silkworm Eggs on Paper
First, place previously mated male and female moths on another paper, then after about one hour, remove the male moth and place the female moth on the silkworm egg paper. Use painted wooden sticks to create barriers on all four sides to prevent escape. Cold weather is not suitable; warm weather is good. Also, darkness is better than brightness. Therefore, close doors and windows, leave until the next morning, then sweep off in the morning.

Section 12: The period from silkworm hatching to cocoon formation is around forty days, and from cocoon to ant silkworms is sixteen to seventeen days. However, there are variations in timing depending on the year's temperature.

Section 13: Summer silkworms probably evolved from spring silkworms. The reason is that among spring silkworm eggs, there are what are called "re-emergence" eggs - three or four out of every hundred eggs that hatch again. These silkworm eggs will re-hatch again the following year. This variety was treated specially and eventually given the name of summer silkworms.

This re-emergence is difficult to produce artificially and must be left to nature. The production of summer silkworm eggs is several levels easier compared to spring silkworm eggs.

Section 14: One sheet of silkworm egg paper (taking it thick, equivalent to half a sheet) contains several hundred silkworms, with approximately forty thousand silkworm eggs.

Section 15: To raise silkworms from one sheet of paper requires approximately 1,600 mulberry trees, equivalent to thirty horse loads.
This is for a normal sprouting year, and since there are variations depending on climate, I am only giving these rough numbers. The silkworm room layout should not be too narrow - if narrow, it becomes stuffy. It should not be too wide either - if wide, it becomes cold. Rather cold than stuffy is preferable.

Section 16: Although there are many varieties of mulberry trees, the best ones do not exceed roughly three or four types.
