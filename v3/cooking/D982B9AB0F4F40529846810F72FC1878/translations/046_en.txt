The general method of packing is as follows. However, each person should pack according to their own preferences. Just avoid packing in the same uniform pattern. Color coordination is of utmost importance. Similar colors should not be clustered in one place. Also, dark colors should be placed toward the center. Since most items in the boxes are odd-numbered...

In general, for box arrangements of nine, seven, five, or three varieties, it is best that neither the seasoning methods nor cutting styles are uniform. However, this does not mean cutting items into random shapes without reason. One should simply be mindful that sizes large and small, lengths long and short, and shapes round and square should not be uniform. Also, considering the dimensions of the box, one must plan and cut from the beginning to ensure convenient packing, otherwise irregularities will appear in various places when the packing is completed. One should plan the arrangement divisions and cut properly.

Using hijiki seaweed, bracken, or arame kelp on kaiseki paper is extremely vulgar and poor. It is better to have no kaiseki paper at all. However, packing is possible even without kaiseki paper. The packing method is as follows: first, place items that don't sit well wherever you intend to place them in the box, then pack items that stack well beside them, leaning them against each other—this way anything will work. Also, items that easily crumble should all be inserted into available spaces after finishing the packing of other items. However, one should consider this as extra space in the arrangement division and begin packing with this in mind from the start. After packing everything without exception, lightly pressing down evenly with a thin board will improve the appearance.
