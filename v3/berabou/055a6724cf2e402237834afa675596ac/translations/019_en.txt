The ink craftsman Matsui Izumi of Kobai-en devotes his heart to construction and achieves great precision. Among the guest items of the Mizunoe-uma year, he provides several dozen varieties. Here I append several types that his household now manufactures.

○Pine soot ink　Zong Shi said "One must use pine soot ink when adding to medicine," and Dongbi said "Superior ink uses pine soot, dissolved and mixed with ash tree bark juice to create it." In China, they exclusively use pine soot for manufacturing. In our country too, the ink made in ancient times at Fujishiro in Kii was made from pine soot, as recorded in the Kokon Chomonju, Volume 3. Lord Reizei Tameshige composed a poem: "Likening our meeting to pine, the famous ink of Fujishiro, precious letters on paper mulberry." Even now, pine soot is burned and extracted from the mountains of Fujishiro. Ink makers seek this to manufacture ink, naming it Taihei soot. If this is so, then the form of ancient Fujishiro ink would be the form of Taihei ink transmitted to the present world, as seen in Matsui Gentai's notes. Since the medieval period, in our country superior ink uses oil soot, while pine soot is used for inferior ink. Moreover, ash tree bark juice is not used, so its efficacy in treating eye diseases and the like is limited. For medicinal use, one should use superior Chinese-made pine soot ink. However, Kobai-en's method of using ash tree bark juice, glue and other materials differs not at all from Chinese manufacture. It is suitable for medicinal use. ○Chitose Pine　Considering Song dynasty Chao's Ink Classic and other various theories, this is ink made by collecting soot from thousand-year-old ancient pines in the mountains of Kumano. It is said there was an imperial decree bestowing the title "Chitose Pine." ○Imperial Ink　Matsui Gen
