Know that salt becomes medicine.

Water-sho: The "Bencao Gangmu" (Compendium of Materia Medica) distinguishes various types of sho (natron/soda compounds) with poku-sho-seki (crude natron stone) as the primary type. Upon current examination, there are homonymous but different substances among bōsho (mirabilite), kusho (bitter soda), and gasho (tooth soda), and there are many other explanations as well. Fearing such confusion, I now place water-sho and fire-sho at the head and attach the various sho beneath them. This differs from other examples.

○Poku-sho (crude natron): Also called shōseki-poku. This is what pharmacists today call hai-sama (ash-like) bōsho. It is the water-sho that forms on the ground surface, scraped and collected without yet being refined through boiling. When this is refined through boiling and crystallized, it becomes the three types: bōsho, eisho, and bonsho. Dongbi says, "What is refined by boiling, placed in basins, crystallized and settled at the bottom as crude material is called poku-sho." This confuses poku-sho and bon-sho as one, which is a great error. Poku is interchangeable with boku, referring to the base material of a vessel that has not yet been worked. Just as unpolished jade is called haku, unworked stone is called [illegible], and unrefined gold is called [illegible] - the meaning is the same. Mazhi says, "Poku means the principle of being unchanged. Therefore, bōsho and eisho all emerge from this, hence it is called shōseki-poku." He also says, "Using warm water to leach poku-sho, taking the liquid and refining it to reduce it by half, throwing it into a basin, after one night fine crystals grow, hence it is called bōsho." This explanation should serve as proof that poku-sho does not undergo boiling and refining. The theory of Zongshi and others that once-boiled material becomes poku-sho is quite wrong. The various families of materia medica are not detailed in distinguishing the various sho, and cases of confusing water-sho and fire-sho as one are too numerous to count. Dongbi says, "Since the Jin and Tang dynasties, all schools have clung to names regarding various sho while having no speculation whatsoever."
