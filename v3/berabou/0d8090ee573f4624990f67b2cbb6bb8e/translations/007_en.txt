Originally, even houses were rough and wild. They entered the darkness of the heavenly rock cave, and when their nose pillars knocked together with a bang, they came to be called the "two pillar deities." At this time, setting aside the matter of three futons, since it was an era when there were no Tentoku temples, the divine couple lay flat on the bare earth to pass their time. Because it was excessively damp, their retainers advised that they should wake up, saying it would be proper to do so. This was the origin of the term "damp" (shitsubuka) at that time. Then, using a bird called the wagtail (sekirei) as their master instructor, they began something called "heavenly curtain worship" and other such "shampukurin" activities, becoming the founding patriarch of Japanese horsemanship and the master of yin and yang.
