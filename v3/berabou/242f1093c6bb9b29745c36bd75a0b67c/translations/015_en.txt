"…that Yorimasa should be so named was too much to bear, and I could not help but laugh out loud — and it is for that offense that I find myself rebuked. That said, Yorimasa might at least be counted among the ranks of bowmen. Kiyomori, however, has neither martial prowess nor literary accomplishment — he merely enjoys the good fortune of basking in the imperial court's favor. If I say such things, it may sound as though I am praising my own household's Buddha, but my unworthy father, Tameyoshi, at the age of fourteen received a decree and went forth to defeat Minomae no Kami Yoshitsuna; and again at eighteen, when news arose that the monks of the Southern Capital (Nara) bore a grievance against the court and were said to be marching upon it, and the order came down to go and stop them — it being so sudden that his troops were not yet assembled — he rode with only seventeen horsemen to Mount Kurisu and drove back thousands of mounted monks. Now that he has grown old, who knows what he may be, but my elder brother Yoshitomo, at least, is an uncommonly formidable warrior when it comes to the bow." Hearing this, Lord Tameyoshi thought to himself: "That reckless boy is picking an idle quarrel and may bring down a terrible calamity upon us. I wish he would quickly humble himself and withdraw" — yet since the imperial seat was near, he could not bring himself to reprimand and dismiss him. Just at this moment, the Minister of the Left, Lord Yorinaga, had also arrived, and having quietly listened to this exchange, he sat with a faint smile upon his lips. At that, Shinzei leaned forward on his knees and said: "Now, now, Hachirō — that this old man shows partiality in his judgments toward those near and distant to him —

**[Left Page]**
— who said such a thing? Yorimasa, only recently, shot down a monstrous bird that appeared night after night above the Shishinden Hall, and his name has been fragrant with renown ever since. As for Kiyomori, when he was serving as Assistant Master of the Left Gate Guards (Saemon no Suke), there was a monstrous bird haunting the imperial palace, and he shot it down with ease. The bird then flew into the sleeve of Kiyomori's robe. He pulled it out at once, and it proved to be a great rat. He then had bamboo cut from the southern garden pavilion, sealed the rat inside a bamboo tube, and buried it on the hill of Kiyomizudera — this spot is now called Itchikutsuka ('One-Bamboo Mound'). Everyone marveled at how cleverly this had been managed. All of these are things that happened yesterday and today, and you yourself have seen and heard them directly. They are nothing like the deeds of Tameyoshi in former times — defeating his uncle Yoshitsuna, who would have collapsed of his own accord without being attacked, or driving back the monks of the Southern Capital who knew nothing of the martial arts. And as for Yoshitomo, I have yet to hear any renown of martial valor from him. Even given all this, do you still say my judgment is partial?" Tametomo laughed all the more mockingly and replied: "Even a common hunter can bring down a bird with an arrow, and a cat can catch a rat. Your excellence lies in matters of letters — how could you possibly know anything of the bow? When all is said and done, it is pointless to name this person or that. In this present age, when it comes to the bow…" *(continued)*
