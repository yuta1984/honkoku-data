This man was deeply learned and of great talent, versed in many fields of study. His wife was the wet nurse of Prince Masahito (none other than the Retired Emperor Go-Shirakawa himself), and so the Retired Emperor cherished him as a man without peer, and even entrusted him with the conduct of court governance. Although he appeared to bear his responsibilities well, he exclusively employed the methods of Shen Buhai and Han Fei (the strict Legalist approach to governance), making rewards light and punishments heavy, and often allowing partiality toward the near and distant to introduce bias into his judgments — and so he drew the resentment of many people. Though he boasted of such imperial favor, for reasons unknown, even after the relationship between the Retired Emperor and the New-Retired Emperor (Sutoku) had grown strained, he continued to visit Shirakawa Palace (the residence of the New-Retired Emperor) without interruption. Perhaps because the New-Retired Emperor had long harbored certain intentions, he had been quietly marking out warriors of the Genji and Heike clans. On that day, when the business at hand was concluded, he had Shinzei questioned on ancient precedents of civil and military matters, and in the course of this asked, "Who in this country's ancient past would be considered a master of the strong bow?" Shinzei replied: "Although our land is not lacking in such figures, none would surpass the two men Kibino Omi Okoshi and Tatehito no Sukune." When the Retired Emperor then asked, "And in the present age?" Shinzei answered, "The Governor of Aki, Kiyomori, and the Governor of Hyōgo, Yorimasa — both are men of notable repute." Upon hearing this, Tametomo involuntarily raised his voice and broke into a mocking laugh.

Shinzei turned sharply and said, "This is an act of disrespect. Who is that person?" For义 answered: "That is For义's eighth son, known as Kansha Tametomo. He is not of a rank that should be present at such a gathering, but since the New-Retired Emperor stepped down from the throne, perhaps finding himself longing for human company, he has been pleased to receive any visitor without exception. Though I have not received formal permission, I wished to let him hear today's lecture from outside, and so brought him along quietly." Shinzei immediately rose from his seat, approached the foot of the palace steps, and gazed intently at Tametomo's face for a moment, saying: "This young man has the 'double pupil' (重瞳, chōdō) — a mark of an extraordinary countenance. He cannot yet be fifteen years of age, and yet he appears remarkably mature. What cause had you to mock this old man so, without regard for the nearness of the imperial seat?" Tametomo, showing no sign of agitation, replied: "From what I hear people say, the monk Michinori (Shinzei's religious name) is a learned scholar, yet it is said he shows partiality in his judgments toward those near or distant to him — and it seems that is entirely true. To name Kiyomori and Yorimasa as the great bowmen of this age…"
