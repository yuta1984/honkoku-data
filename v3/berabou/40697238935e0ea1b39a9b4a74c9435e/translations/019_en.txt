Measuring distances means knowing
how many hundreds or tens of ken
there are from here to some
distant landmark,
calculated while remaining
right here. For example,
when asked how many ken there are
from the Great Gate to the fire
watchtower at Suitoshiri,
one answers: "110 ken."
According to the method, since Yoshiwara
is said to be two chō square,
the middle chō is set at 120 ken.
From this, subtract 10 ken
for the useless space behind the tower,
leaving 110 ken.
　The method of measuring distances
The above 110 ken has designations:
　The first 10 ken don't count
　　This is called "unserviced area"
　　or "no interior" etc.
　The next 10 ken is two ken
　The next 10 ken is (marginal note: ryankō|ryan)

From here we speak of rice cakes.
The thing called kagami-mochi (mirror cake)
is wasteful.
If an 8-sun mirror
cake comes from the other party,
you should send back
a 6-sun mirror cake in celebration.
If it's 6 sun, then 5 sun—
always arrange things so that
you gain advantage. Though 8 sun and 6 sun
seem like a small difference,
the small offerings and triple offerings
of rice cake are different. Look at this diagram
and understand by thinking about it.
