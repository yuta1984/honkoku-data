Women, regardless of beauty or ugliness, enter the palace and are envied; samurai, regardless of wisdom or foolishness, enter the court and are hated. If I may use birds as an analogy: peacocks, golden pheasants, and parrots and such—though people pay high prices to keep them as pets, they only look good on the outside. They catch no prey, tell no time, and are useless even as companions for leeks and burdock. And though crows may be unsightly in their masculine appearance,

they rise early in the morning to wake people up, know well the signs of fortune and misfortune, and give advance warning. One should be grateful for this, yet people hate them, saying 'the crow's cawing is inauspicious' or 'that detestable crow!' Seeing this, I'm reminded that good medicine tastes bitter to the mouth, and the nail that sticks up gets hammered down. Nevertheless, unreasonable though it may be, it's quite understandable. When rulers fail to be rulers and retainers fail to be retainers, like Hachiman Daimyō Tarō Kanja facing a fierce tiger, he has not a speck of true spirit within himself and turns his head whichever way the wind blows.
