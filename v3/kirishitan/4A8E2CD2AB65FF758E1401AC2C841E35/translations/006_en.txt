Nobunaga, through Inoko Hyōsuke, questioned Vulcan about his purpose for coming to Japan. When the interpreter conveyed messages between both parties, Vulcan replied, saying, "My visit this time is solely for the wish to propagate the Buddhist teaching, and there is no other reason beyond propagation of the faith." After Nobunaga concluded the audience, he had him lodged at Myōhōji temple, with Nakaizumi Tōzaemon serving as administrator to provide hospitality.

Regarding Vulcan's missionary activities, there was a council meeting at Azuchi where Bunkyōin Tōsen and others voiced opposition, but Nobunaga would not consent to their objections. He ordered Sugaya Kuemon to donate land measuring four chō square at Shijō-Bōmon in Kyoto, construct stone walls, build a temple, and name it Eiroku-ji.

Because of this, the monks of Mount Hiei became indignant, arguing that it was improper for any temple other than Enryaku-ji to use an era name as its temple name, and they appealed to the head priest, Priest Yōen. The priest said, "Although this matter concerns ancient law, now the imperial court has declined, the imperial position is treated lightly, and the power of Buddhism has also weakened. Even though Nobunaga relies on his authority to impose his will, when opposing him might instead bring harm to the mountain, it would be difficult to control this through imperial authority. Perhaps a gentle approach would be appropriate." However, the entire mountain rose up, gathered in the courtyard of the Great Lecture Hall, composed a petition for forceful appeal to the court, sent 130 monks down from the mountain, and submitted this petition.
