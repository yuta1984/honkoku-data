Village headmen as well as farmers, and for samurai the group leaders (monogashira), are subject to annual inspections without exception. Moreover, although there are supposed to be bulletin boards [prohibiting] hidden Christians in rural areas throughout the provinces, ten or twenty people at a time are continually discovered and subjected to various punishments—burning at the stake, crucifixion, drowning. The reasoning behind these ongoing punishments that continue to this day is difficult to question.

【Left Page】
　　　Regarding the Uprising of the Tsukushi Hizen Shimabara and Amakusa Rebellion

Around mid-November of Kanei 14 [1637], rebellions broke out frequently in Shimabara and Amakusa, and like pulling teeth from a comb, fast messengers and express horses crossed paths day and night without pause. Investigating the cause of this disorder: this was the territory under the rule of Matsukura Nagato-no-kami. Where [the domain] was supposed to be administered as 60,000 koku, land surveys assessed it at 120,000 koku, and [the lord] greedily extracted five or six times [the proper amount].
