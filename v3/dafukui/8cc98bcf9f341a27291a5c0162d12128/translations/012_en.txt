After leaving the Tōdō house and withdrawing to Yamato Province, he was summoned by Tajima-no-kami Naotomi-kō and appointed to the position of chief retainer (karō). Through this, he became known to the lord's family members, who granted him written documents that have been preserved to this day. However, when Toyohide was four years old in the spring, his father Kaga-uemon passed away. Although he was granted succession to the family headship, due to his young age, his relatives petitioned for leave, and he was taken in and raised by his uncle Imadate Rokū-emon Masatane, who was serving the house. After reaching maturity, he was summoned back to service. His title was changed and he became known as Imadate.

△Matsunami
Summoned in the 3rd year of Keian, year of the Metal Tiger (1650), during Lord Mitsumichi's reign
●Sakurai Den-uemon Masayoshi《Original province: Mikawa, Birth province: Settsu｜Surname: Fujiwara, Sakurai title from maternal side, Buddhist name: Jinyū》

His ancestor, six generations from Kawabe Sadaijin Uona, was the descendant of General Toshihito through seventeen generations - Matsunami Mikawa-no-kami Izuru■, who served during the reign of Emperor Go-Hanazono and had military achievements. His portrait exists at Shōkoku-ji Temple in Kyoto, and the inscription on the portrait records these accomplishments. Copies of this and genealogical records are preserved. Thereafter, generations ruled territories within Mikawa Province and served the Imperial Court. Five generations from Izuru■, Matsunami Gagaku-no-kami Mitsumasa served Lord Nobunaga. His son, Matsunami Hei-uemon Masaoki《Masayoshi's grandfather》served faithfully, and his descendants continue as hatamoto to this day. Masayoshi's father, Matsunami Den-uemon Masatsugu, was the second son of Masaoki, but due to poor health from a young age, he relied on his father Masaoki and his uncle Matsunami Gorō-uemon, who served as head deputy for the five provinces around Osaka in Settsu, until he passed away. After that, Masayoshi was summoned to service.
