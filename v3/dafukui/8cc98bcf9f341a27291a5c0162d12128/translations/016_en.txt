He exchanged words with the hatamoto messenger Honda San'ya to obtain proof, and quickly reported the details of his military service to the shogun. Afterward, he was summoned before both shoguns and granted a sake cup, received two letters of commendation, a horse, and gold. Furthermore, Gongen-sama (Ieyasu) stood up from his seat and gave kind words to the effect that "he engaged as first spear, particularly his performance on that battlefield, especially killing an enemy commander and taking his head, and his seven days of service were all incomparable." Honda San'ya reported these details to the shogun, who was greatly moved. At that time, Lord Doi Ōi-no-kami and Lord Aoyama Ōkura were waiting in the next room, and upon hearing these circumstances, they said "the present words of praise are no ordinary matter, befitting a warrior's divine favor" and expressed envy. It is said that Tomotaka requested and drank from the cup. His son, Hatta Kinemon Tomoaki, was the third son of the aforementioned Tomotaka. During the Kan'ei era, he served Ii Yukie-no-suke Naoshige, and when Naoshige went into seclusion at Hyakusaiji Temple in Ōmi Province, he served him throughout until Naoshige's death, after which he became a rōnin. He was summoned to service during Lord Mitsumichi's reign.

　　Hatta Kinemon Eiji (birthplace Ōmi, religious name Kinchū, actual father was Tomoaki's elder brother Hatta Sakon-emon Tomona, Buddhist name Suisei)
　Regarding the military achievements of ancestors, during Lord Mitsumichi's reign there was an inquiry to grandfather Kinemon Tomoaki, and a written report was submitted. Later, when there was an official inquiry to Lord Ii Kamon-no-kami about matters during the Osaka campaigns, written documents about ancestral military service were submitted by relatives of the same name residing in Hikone. The letters of commendation and other ancient proof items are preserved by the main family in Hikone, so only copies are possessed here.
