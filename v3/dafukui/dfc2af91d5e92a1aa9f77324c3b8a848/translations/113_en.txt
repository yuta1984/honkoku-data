The gist of the above was communicated during the Kyōhō and Kansei eras and on subsequent occasions as well. Yet over the years, society has become increasingly ostentatious. People fail to know their place and compete with one another in displaying finery. Furthermore, it is reported that some people, while not appearing conspicuous outwardly, are in fact buying and selling expensive goods indiscriminately. Even without the luxury of recreational items, the fact that regulations issued repeatedly are treated as though they were temporary matters, ultimately leading to negligence and violation of the law, is most improper. However, as for conduct up to this point, through special clemency, punishment has not been imposed, for which one should be grateful. Since orders have now been issued with great sincerity that customs should be reformed, this matter must not be taken lightly. Since there may be goods that have already been acquired, strict prohibition shall take effect from the coming year of the Tiger. If anyone is found to have violated the terms of this notice, officials shall be dispatched to conduct investigations, and punishment shall be imposed without mercy. However, suspicious inspections shall not be carried out, nor shall people traveling on roads be stopped and searched. Should such persons be found, they shall be detained and reported promptly. Henceforth, if there are those among the samurai class who order luxurious and expensive goods, they shall consult the magistrate's office and follow its instructions.

As the above has been communicated to the various neighborhoods, understand its intent well, and do not order ostentatious or expensive goods in general. Understand the intent of this order ever more deeply, and ensure that all matters are free from extravagance.
Tenth month

▲○ It has been reported that in various villages, during Shinto festivals and religious ceremonies, or under the names of "insect-sending" (mushiokuri) rituals or wind festivals for agricultural purposes, events similar to theatrical performances and shows are being organized, with costumes and props prepared and spectators assembled, wasting gold and silver. This is most reprehensible.
Not only those who make a livelihood of such things, but also travelers of poor conduct, riverbank people (kawaramono), and others of like character must by no means be permitted to enter villages. Since such people learn idle, weak, and undesirable habits, naturally become negligent in farming, lead to an increase in uncultivated fields, fall into poverty, and ultimately become the foundation of the dispersal and ruin of families, one must be mindful to understand these circumstances thoroughly.
Therefore, from now on, entertainment arts, kabuki, jōruri, dancing, and all such gatherings similar to theatrical performances are to be firmly prohibited. Even after issuing this notice as above, if such activities do not cease, strict punishment will be imposed without mercy.

As stated above, although this was communicated in the eleventh year of Kansei (1799), the year of the Sheep, it has been reported that such activities have recently become unruly again, which is most reprehensible. Henceforth, the terms of the notice must be strictly observed, all manner of public gatherings completely prohibited, idle and weak customs reformed, and farming attended to as the primary occupation. Should anyone violate this, they will be strictly punished after investigation.

The above shall be communicated without omission to: in government-administered lands (goryō), the deputy magistrates (daikan) and local magistrates; in entrusted lands (azukari-sho) and private domains (shiryō), the domain lords and local administrators (jitō); and including temple and shrine lands (jisha-ryō). Investigations shall be conducted without negligence. For small stipend areas (shōkyū-sho), nearby deputy magistrates shall also keep regular watch.
Eleventh month

○○ In recent times, there are many people who make hoods that conceal their faces and wear them in public. Since this is likely to cause confusion with fugitives sought by the magistrate's office, aside from the round hoods (maru-zukin) and square hoods (kaku-zukin) that have existed since before, the wearing of all other types of hoods is prohibited. Although this matter was communicated some years ago, it has recently been reported again that there are people wearing unusual hoods that conceal their faces, which is most improper. Such behavior must not occur.
