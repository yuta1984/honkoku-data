## The Guests: A Grand Assembly of Eight Million Deities
### The Feast: Kashima's Earthquake-themed Cuisine — A Grand Banquet of Grilled Catfish —

【Nishinomiya Ebisu】

Kashima Daimyōjin had traveled to the Grand Shrine of Izumo, where he was busy arranging marriages and bonds of fate (*en-musubi*) for his parishioners together with the assembled deities, when an urgent messenger arrived from his home province to inform him of the great disaster that had struck Edo.

Without a moment's delay, he used his divine powers to cover the hundreds of leagues back home in a single night. He immediately seized the great catfish and pinned it down, intending to carry out severe punishment as a warning to deter future mischief. Just then, the great and lesser deities of Japan's sixty-odd provinces, having wrapped up their matchmaking duties in haste, gathered together and came to Kashima to pay their respects.

Witnessing this scene, the assembled deities were deeply impressed by the swift action of the Kashima deity, and astonished by the immense power of the catfish — the commotion took quite some time to settle.

The host deity, Kashima Daimyōjin, expressed his heartfelt gratitude, saying, "I am deeply honored that you have traveled such a long distance to visit," and then considered how to entertain such distinguished guests. However, with so sudden and unexpected a gathering of no fewer than eight million deities, he was at a loss as to what to do. He consulted the two deities Ebisu and Sanuki Konpira, who suggested that rather than deliberating at length, they should grill the great catfish and serve it as a banquet dish — surely the finest of entertainments. Finding this an excellent idea, Kashima swiftly made preparations and, with his own hands, split open the great catfish and grilled it kabayaki-style, treating the eight million deities to a feast the likes of which had never been heard of since the age of Emperor Jimmu.

— These tales of "eight hundred lies" (*uso happyaku*) are told while tipsy from the sacred rice wine (*omiki*). —
