One of the lanterns hanging from the four sides of the hall at Tsukiji Monzeki was blown away and fell into the sea. The lantern was made of iron and suspended by iron chains. It was extremely heavy, and there should have been no concern about it being severed and flying away. Yet in one morning, this occurred. Moreover, that evening there was a south wind, yet the lantern fell into the sea against the wind. These are all matters that cannot be understood by reason.

Mamiya Rinzō said that before great winds, the ground generally trembles slightly at all times, but no one can perceive this. If one tests this with tall trees, it can be recognized. For tall trees move slightly at their roots, and the movement becomes somewhat more intense toward the treetops, so it can be distinguished.

Considering this statement, I recalled that earlier, rumors had spread among the people that Asakusa Kannon Hall was shaking by itself - moving slightly during the day and more severely at night. The bells on the four eaves struck against each other naturally, and there were sounds of something groaning beneath the hall. The torii gate beside the hall broke by itself. This was because the hall was so tall that it could detect the trembling of the earth. What was called "something groaning" was the sound of the hall moving. Could it be that before the great wind arose, the earth's energy had already begun to manifest?

North of Hanakawado, there was a private house. In the house lived only an old man and an old woman. They were usually prudent and discreet, devoted to good deeds. The neighbors loved and respected them. That evening, when wind and rain struck violently, the old couple awoke in alarm, supporting pillars and pushing against doors, exhausting all defensive measures. But the wind's force was like a blade, entering through every crack and stirring the interior of the room. The old couple, judging that it could not be stopped, fled to a neighbor's house. Just as they left their door and looked back, their house had already been pulverized. People considered this the reward for accumulated good deeds.

Outside the western walls, a farmer's wife was pregnant. That evening her husband happened to be away. When violent wind and sudden rain struck from all directions, she was terrified and helpless, rushing to take refuge in a neighbor's house. Suddenly the neighbor's house collapsed, and the master with his wife and children ran quickly and escaped. The farmer's wife, with her large belly making movement difficult, could not get out and was finally crushed to death. Yet her own house remained peaceful and unharmed.

At Lord Aizu's Shiba-kai villa, servants were gathered living in one large building. That evening the wind force was extremely fierce and the building was about to collapse. Everyone fled in fear. One person refused to leave. The others urged and reasoned with him repeatedly, and he finally began to want to leave, but still changed his clothes and put on his shoes, being self-important. Suddenly the building collapsed and crushed him, and his body became minced meat. Looking at it after the fact, the deaths of these two people seemed to have been brought upon themselves. In the end, it was truly Heaven that caused their deaths.
