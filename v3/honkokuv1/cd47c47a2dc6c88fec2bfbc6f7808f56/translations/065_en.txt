The Āgama Sūtras, the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra, and other texts describe various things that make it sound as though the entire earth moves — but that is not the case. As stated at the outset, earthquakes occur each in their own place, driven by their own qi, moving in their own way. I have prepared a diagram based on the Tenkyō wakumon ("Questions and Answers on the Heavens") to illustrate this point.

[Diagram of the Terrestrial Globe] (diagram omitted)

The circumference of the Earth is 90,000 ri. Converting this from Chinese ri (1 ri = 6 chō) into Japanese ri (1 ri = 36 chō), the circumference becomes 15,000 ri. In that case, the distance from the center of the Earth to its surface is approximately 2,500 ri. In this diagram, the distance between the black dots is approximately 1,500 ri. When the area affected by this recent earthquake is estimated at 200 ri square, it corresponds to only a small circle within the diagram. From this, one should appreciate how minute the area of seismic activity is, and how vast the Earth truly is.

○Upon my reflection, within heaven and earth, all of the workings of nature (zōka) have a root (hon) and branches (sue). The "root" is the foundation — the "heart" (shin/kokoro). The "heart" refers to the most violently shaking point of the earthquake. What spreads outward from this "heart" in all directions, gradually becoming gentler, is the "branches." Thus, it is not that the shaking comes from the east, nor that it travels from somewhere else. Rather, the shaking originates from the "heart" and spreads in all directions, dying away as faint tremors at its farthest reaches. In this recent earthquake, with the capital (Kyoto) as the "heart" (epicenter), it extended to neighboring provinces, and at its "branches" reached as far as Tōbu (Edo)...
