There is a theory that the large halo parhelia are produced by 90-degree prisms in a vertical position — that is, by ice columns having one edge of their base in a vertical position — but this is doubtful. The reason is that there is no rational basis for why ice columns should preferentially maintain this position over any other. The more reliable explanation is that this parhelia is produced when the same ray of light passes twice through a vertical 60-degree prism. In this case, the total deviation of the light ray equals the sum of two minimum deviations, i.e., 22° × 2 = 44°, which is very close to 46 degrees.

**Arc tangent to the small halo**　This arc is produced by horizontal 60-degree prisms. Among these prisms, light that has undergone the minimum deviation through those prisms perpendicular to the vertical plane passing through the Sun and the observer's eye produces two bright spots designated a and a (Figure 7). These constitute the vertical parhelia. Furthermore, when a horizontal prism tilts from the perpendicular position relative to this plane, light passes through its oblique cross-section. And as explained previously in the description of parhelia, an increase in the degree of tilt is equivalent to an increase in the apex angle of the prism; therefore, as the horizontal prism tilts away from this vertical plane, the minimum deviation grows progressively larger, and consequently a curve denoted aa is gradually generated.

**Arc tangent to the large halo**　Of these arcs, the upper arc is the most brilliant part of the entire halo phenomenon; the seven colors can be distinctly distinguished, and its two edges are sharply delineated. This arc appears even when the 46-degree halo itself is not present.
