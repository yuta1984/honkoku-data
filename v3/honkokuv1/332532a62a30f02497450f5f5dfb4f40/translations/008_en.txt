## Diagram of the Earth's Center (Chishin-zu)

[Figure: The character for "Heaven/Sky" (天) lines up around the outer edge; a slip of paper labels "Noon (午) / Zenith (天頂) / Ground Level (平地) / Earth's Center (地心) / Underground (地下) / Midnight (子)"]

As shown in the diagram, the point at the very center of the Earth is called the *chishin* (Earth's center). All people throughout the world stand with this Earth's center beneath their feet, and everything above their heads is "Heaven." One should know that when objects fall — drawn downward by the force that presses from above to below — they all fall toward this Earth's center.

## The Meridian Line (Shigo-sen)

To understand the principles of the Earth, one must first understand the meridian line. "Noon" (*go*, 午) refers to the line running north and south from the zenith — the point directly overhead — of wherever one stands. "Midnight" (*shi*, 子) refers to the north-south line directly below, underground. One should understand that this meridian line exists at the zenith and the nadir of one's own location, and is something that accompanies one's own person at all times.

For this reason, the twelve hours of day and night are assigned to the twelve Earthly Branches, and the time is determined by the position of the Sun. For example, if it is the Hour of the Ninth (around noon) in Japan, it corresponds to the Hour of the Eighth at night (around 2 a.m.) in Holland. Conversely, the Hour of the Fifth-and-a-half at night in Japan (around 9 a.m.) corresponds to the Hour of the Ninth in the daytime in Holland — which is midday there.

In this way, because the Sun revolves around the Earth at its center, the time differs from country to country. Therefore, one should understand that the meridian line is something belonging individually to each person wherever they stand.
