Explanation of the Acceleration and Deceleration of the Five Planets

The five planets - Jupiter, Mars, Saturn, Venus, and Mercury - sometimes move fast and sometimes move slowly as they orbit through the celestial sphere. Sometimes they stop, sometimes they become invisible, never maintaining a fixed position. Therefore, here I present a diagram of Venus's orbit to clarify this principle.

The degrees of heaven are like the paper of a fan, and one degree on earth extends to the high heavens with such vastness that it cannot be measured. However, Venus's orbit, regardless of its height or depth, completes one revolution around the sun as its axis in 583 days. Therefore, if it advances one degree per day in the lower heaven, it takes more than six days to advance just one degree in the higher heaven.

Since this orbit revolves around the sun as its axis, advancing one degree eastward each day is fixed. However, because Venus moves clockwise around this orbit, while it travels through the upper half of the orbit above the sun's heaven, the star advances eastward, but when it travels through the lower half, its movement is fast and the star retreats westward. Based on this, one should consider the principle of acceleration and deceleration by combining the sun's movement with the star's rotation.

For example, it's like watching a person working on a flowing raft - even when walking upstream on the raft, when viewed from outside, the body doesn't ascend at all, and when walking downstream, taking one step results in descending two steps. The orbit is like the raft, and the star is like the person. The acceleration and deceleration of all five planets should be understood through this principle.

Moreover, from the narrowest point of the orbit to the lower heaven, the degree by which the star separates from the sun actually increases significantly. During one complete orbit, there is conjunction in the higher heaven and conjunction in the lower heaven. The conjunction in the higher heaven lasts approximately seventy days, while the conjunction in the lower heaven lasts approximately fifteen days. Until reaching the high and low conjunctions, when the star is in the western half of the orbit, it appears in the morning, and when in the eastern half, it appears in the evening.
