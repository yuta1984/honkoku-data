(Continued from the previous page)...The daytime in this country (Japan) is nighttime in those countries (Europe and the Americas). The cold (winter) of those countries corresponds to the heat (summer) of this country. The lengths of day and night also follow this same principle.

Article: The midday (noon) of Tenjiku (India) corresponds to the lower quarter of the Hour of the Monkey (申, approximately 4:00 PM) in Japan. The midday of the Red-Hairs (紅毛, i.e., the Dutch/Europeans) corresponds to the sixth or seventh quarter of the Hour of the Boar (亥, approximately 10:00 PM) in Japan.

Article: Countries directly beneath the equator — India (印度), Bengal (榜葛刺), Tenjiku (天竺), Sumatra (蘇門荅臘), Batavia (咬𠺕吧, present-day Jakarta), Borneo (孛寧刺), and the like — experience two periods of intense heat per year and know no cold. Japan's spring and autumn equinoxes correspond to the periods of greatest heat in those countries. The Buddhist custom of baring the right shoulder (偏袒右肩, the gesture of respect) may also be understood in light of this climatic consideration.

Article: The Sun advances three degrees per day and completes one full circuit of the heavens along the ecliptic (黄道) from west to east in three hundred sixty-five days and twenty-five units of time (刻). This constitutes one year.

Article: The Moon advances an average of thirteen degrees and three minutes per day and completes one full circuit of the heavens along the lunar path (白道) from west to east in twenty-seven days and thirty-one units of time (刻).
