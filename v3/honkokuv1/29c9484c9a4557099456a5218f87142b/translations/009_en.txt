From there, after 18 or 19 days, it begins to appear in the east at dawn at about 7 or 8 degrees, gradually ascending for 50 days to 40 degrees where it remains visible for 60 days, then gradually descends, and after 130 days it conjuncts with the sun and becomes invisible again. From there, after 90 days, it appears in the west in the evening at about 7 or 8 degrees, following the same sequence as before.

Venus appears to remain stationary at 40 degrees for 60 days both at dawn and evening, but in reality it is not stationary. When we observe from Earth a star that moves forward or travels away, it appears to be stationary. It is like when we observe a lantern coming from afar and going away again - it appears to be stationary when viewed from here. Also, during these 60 days, Venus transits across the sky.
