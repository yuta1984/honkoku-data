【Upper Section】

In Kita-Aoyagi and other areas, approximately 200 people each received a ration of one gō (about 180ml) of unpolished rice per person per day. In Hikone, the price of rice rose to 14 sen per shō (about 1.8 liters), but thanks to the efforts of the Rice Exchange officials and brokers, from the 11th onwards, rice was sold at 11 sen and 8 rin per shō. However, no more than 3 shō would be sold to any one person. Takamiya suffered particularly severe flood damage: the Inugami River broke through its banks and the flow turned toward the town of Takamiya, causing 14 to 15 houses to be swept away. A telegram was sent to the governor requesting the dispatch of engineers and planning for flood defenses. On the night of the 11th, there was great panic that the entire village might be swept away. In Ōyabu Village, the embankment of the Inugami River collapsed on one side, allowing muddy floodwaters to pour in; then the embankment of the Seri River also broke, flooding the same village; and on yet another side, the waters of Lake Biwa gradually rose — the village was thus attacked from three directions. The school was half-destroyed and on the verge of complete collapse. Village office staff were temporarily unable to conduct rescues due to the ferocity of the floodwaters. The residents of Yasaka, Sukoshi, and Mitsuya evacuated their elderly and young to the neighboring villages of Hinatsu and Minami-Aoyagi first, but Hinatsu and Minami-Aoyagi also became flooded in succession, leaving no capacity to receive refugees. Those who had fled were asked to move on, so the residents of Yasaka and other areas withdrew once more toward Kawase, Kameyama, and other locations. Higashi-Kōra Village had 14 irrigation ponds, but when the mountain collapsed, 12 of these ponds were buried and became part of the mountain, and the roads were completely blocked and impassable. At the well-known temple of Saimyōji in this village, massive old cedar and cypress trees — some requiring two or three people's arm spans to encircle — fell against the main hall, and many precious treasures, robes, and Buddhist vestments were damaged or swept away; there is reportedly no prospect of recovery or preservation in the future. In Wakigauné and Seritani, there were many landslides. These areas are located in mountain valleys, where residents traditionally gathered firewood and brushwood to trade for rice in Hikone and other towns; due to the collapse of roads, they have nearly lost their livelihoods entirely. On the Inugami River, the two bridges of Inugami and Takamiya were swept away; on the Seri River, the two bridges of Ōhori and Seri were similarly lost. 【Lower Section】

In Imazu, Takashima District, out of more than 500 households in total, barely more than 20 households escaped flooding. The damage in this area was greatly magnified by the flooding on the 7th and the storm on the 11th. With the exception of a small stretch of about half a chō (approximately 55 meters) near the combined branch office of the district office and tax collection bureau, the entire town was flooded. Most residents of the town evacuated to higher ground. However, those who remained in their homes to protect their belongings and were living on the second floors were not only suffering from a lack of food, but reportedly the most acute source of distress was an unbearable thirst.
