Here, lying down and crying out "Hotchiji, Hotchiji" (the sound of volcanic eruption), they took their children to Kutsukake post station. Leaving their familiar hometown that was like the capital, wondering where they might find lodging today, when they approached houses they were turned away. With downcast eyes, from Mitsuya and Shiono, thinking "we might as well die," at Sakeguchi without even money for horse rental fees on the return journey, exhausted from walking with nowhere to turn. Oh Asama! We are caught in such hardship that tears well up in our chests like the great marsh. Here at the sandy plains and stone passes, stumbling on unsteady feet, at Hirahara's Yumie Hachiman shrine, they resolved firmly "not to be mistaken for thieves." Now blocked at Kashiwagi and Shiono, through Tsuba Mitsu Yotsuya and Kamashi, entering Ushirohira, Nishihara, and Mae with children before them, through Takihara - whatever happens to the well children, with their household goods from Tatetouchi, Shibata, and Takaramura, they stagger toward the castle town of Komoro, letting their feet carry them toward Mukaimachi. Truly this floating world is like dew and droplets on the pine trees of Karamatsu - though no rain falls, they wring their sleeves.
