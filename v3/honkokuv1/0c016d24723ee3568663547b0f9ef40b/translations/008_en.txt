(Continuing the quotation from Zhu Xi from the previous page:)
"[The pole star] does move. However, since it moves close to the North Celestial Pole (the center of heaven), its movement is imperceptible. It is like a spinning target board [used in a game]. The North Celestial Pole is the central stake, and the pole star is a point near that stake. [The pole star] also revolves with the board, but because it is close to the stake, its revolution is imperceptible." This is the essence of the passage.

From this explanation by Zhu Xi, one can understand that the North Pole has a slight movement. Later astronomers speak of this slight movement of the North Pole as if it were their own new discovery, unaware that Zhu Xi had already articulated this theory.

Now, the saying that "the North Pole rises 36 degrees above the horizon, and the South Pole descends 36 degrees below the horizon" applies to places in Japan such as Edo. Kyoto is slightly above 35 degrees, Kagoshima in Satsuma Province is 31 degrees, and Matsumae in Ōshū is 41 degrees.

From the difference in the elevation (altitude above the horizon) of the North Pole at different locations, one can determine the straight-line distance in ri between those places. For example, to find the straight-line distance from Edo to Matsumae: since Edo is at 36 degrees and Matsumae is at 41 degrees, subtracting 36 degrees from 41 degrees leaves 5 degrees. If one degree equals 30 ri, the distance would be 150 ri. However, it is said that even though one degree is reckoned as 30 ri, in practice it amounts to only about 15 ri. Therefore, one should understand the distance to be approximately half of 150 ri, or about 75 ri. This is the straight-line distance from Edo to Matsumae. The reason one degree is set at 30 ri or 15 ri is that traveling that many ri produces a difference of one degree [in the observed elevation of the pole star].
