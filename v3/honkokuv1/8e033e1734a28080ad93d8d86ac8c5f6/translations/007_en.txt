**Same district (Road District) — Teppōmizu (Cold Spring)**

A cold spring, welling up in the vicinity of Yuyama-chō. The elevation of this site is approximately 1,300 *shaku* above sea level.

The spring water is colorless and clear, but if left standing in an uncovered vessel for several days, a small amount of sediment (*odomi*) forms. The components of this sediment are notably iron hydroxide and ferrous oxide. When the water is poured out, it produces some bubbles within the water. It also shows an acidic reaction. When tasted, it has an acidic quality that stimulates the tongue, with no saltiness, yet carries a faint odor of hydrogen sulfide gas. ○ Foreign visitors who come to bathe here in summer all reportedly enjoy drinking this spring water as a refreshing beverage.

**Same district (Road District) — Tori-Jigoku ("Bird Hell")**

Located in Arima Jigoku-dani (Hell Valley). It is a small square dry well approximately three *shaku* deep. According to tradition, any bird that happens to approach it dies immediately — this is the reason for its name "Bird Hell." The site is somewhat higher than that of Teppōmizu and not very far from it. Because the bottom of the well is filled with rotted leaves and other decaying matter, it emits an unpleasant odor. The gas within the well was collected in two bottles and carefully analyzed, determining that it was filled with a substantial quantity of carbonic acid gas. It is likely that the cause of death among birds is suffocation due to the copious generation of carbonic acid. If this is indeed the case, the carcasses of birds ought to be found inside the dry well.

**Same district (Lower District) — Memeshi-yu ("Eye-Washing Spring")**

A cold spring. Located in Tani-no-machi, at a slightly lower elevation than the Arima hot spring. The water is slightly yellowish in color but odorless. It contains a large quantity of free carbonic acid. Reported to be efficacious against eye diseases.

**Same district (Road District) — Cold Spring near Shimizuan**

Shimizuan is a small temple at which foreign visitors are said to most frequently stay during summer. This cold spring wells up in a corner of a vegetable garden, forming the appearance of a garden pond. It has not yet come to be named and widely used in the manner of Teppōmizu. ○ This spring is located at approximately 1,260 *shaku* above sea level. Its nature is colorless, clear, and odorless; when poured out, it produces some bubbles and shows an acidic reaction. When tasted, it mildly stimulates the tongue, possessing an acidic quality without saltiness.

**Kawabe District (Lower District) — Tada-no-yu; also known as Hirano-yu**

Located in Tada-no-shō, more than one *ri* from Ikeda. As it is a lukewarm spring, the water is heated before bathing. The spring water is rich in iron and strongly salty in taste.

**Same district (Lower District) — Hitogura**

Located in Hitogura village, in the vicinity of Tada. Temperature and spring quality are the same as above.

**Muko District (Local) — Takarazuka-yama**

Located in Isoshi village. The spring water contains saline properties and is recommended for weeping skin sores (*shissō*).

**Ubara District (Local) — Magabuchi**

Kumauchi village. The spring water is rich in saline and acidic properties and is recommended for conditions of physical debility. A bathhouse has not yet been opened.

**Yabe District (Local) — Minatoyama**

Located at the foot of a mountain above the Minato River. The spring water contains both sulfur and alum properties.
