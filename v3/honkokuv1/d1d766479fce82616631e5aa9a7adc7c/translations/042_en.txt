(Continued from the previous page) The water remains similarly red and turbid, causing great difficulty. When I measured the turbidity, I found that while ordinary water weighs 500 monme per shō, during this period it weighs 35 monme more. That is to say, it is water with 35 monme worth of soil stirred into every shō. If you stir some of the red soil from your area into water and observe it, you will be able to imagine approximately what it looks like.

One matter: Regarding this earthquake, people in that area are saying that the shaking ran from the direction of Inu-I (north-northwest) toward Uma-Tora (south-southeast). Because of this, even in places only a short distance away, those locations that fell outside that direction of shaking had fewer collapsed houses, while others just slightly within that line suffered total collapse. Indeed, places like Nakanojo were exceedingly fortunate. Suzaka, though two or three ri distant from Zenkoji, suffered no fires, while Iiyama, eight ri away, had its castle and the entire town center totally collapsed and burned. Even places that had not collapsed by the 24th were later destroyed in subsequent aftershocks at various locations. In the town of Zenkoji, there was one storehouse (dozō) that survived without collapsing, and one household was found where the entire family survived in a house that escaped the fire. Among them, an elderly person had resigned themselves to death while sheltering inside the storehouse, but when the fire reached it, they felt only heat and survived without difficulty — a truly fortunate soul. In general, even in areas of post towns and villages that escaped the fire, all pots, cauldrons, and earthenware were broken and gone, causing great hardship.

One matter: This year, since rice prices are low in this area (Niigata), there has been more imported rice than ever since your visit to this place, and the town is thriving. Naturally, a large number of incoming ships only occurs when rice prices are high in other provinces and low in this one. Currently, premium rice (jōmai) is about 70 mon per shō, or roughly 9 to 4–5 shō per ryō. Around the time of your visit here, it was between 55–56 mon and nearly 60 mon, and since then it has been gradually rising.

The accounts of the earthquake above are reliable facts, and I have written them down and send them to you herewith.
