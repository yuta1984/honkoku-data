They laid bamboo swords and spears across the beams, and placed doors, tatami mats, and boards on top of them, barely managing to survive the night while waiting for time to pass. When Yaichiro visited in May to offer condolences, he was told that the water had risen eight to nine shaku above floor level. Particularly, people like Soga Shichibei had lost all their belongings to the flood. Looking from outside, most of the walls had collapsed, and the earth appeared cracked and warped. It was a sight too terrible to behold. The samurai residences on the eastern side across the river were in similar condition. As mentioned before, all the bridges had collapsed without exception, making passage to the other side impossible—a truly deplorable situation.

The samurai in the Kohinata area waited for rescue boats, and many were saved by boarding vessels from rooftops or second floors. These were unprecedented events. Some even pulled horses up onto roofs to save them. Due to the sudden nature of the flood, many people didn't think about their horses, and countless horses died while still tied in their stables. Some quickly cut the ropes to free them. The freed horses either swam through the water and survived, or drowned and died, according to rumors.
