When one falls ill and life is about to be severed, a few doses of medicine can restore life. Such benevolence is great. However, as stated in the preface to the "Nanjing" (Classic of Difficult Questions): "If there is even a hair's breadth of error, though the pulse may live, the medicine will kill." Life and death hang in the balance here. Therefore, even sages do not treat this matter lightly. If a physician's family has not practiced medicine for three generations, one should not take their medicine. Moreover, entrusting a parent's illness to a quack doctor is comparable to being merciless and unfilial. Already when the lord of Zhu died of illness, his heir apparent did not taste the medicine himself, and this was ultimately recorded in the "Spring and Autumn Annals" as regicide. This can be understood from the passage in the "Analects" where Confucius did not taste the medicine sent by Lord Kang. Since this is such a weighty and crucial matter, even if something is claimed to be a secret method of immortals or teachings of extraordinary people, one should examine it thoroughly before use. The gentleman does not value immortal arts and should not be led astray.

Postscript
This book was not originally intended for the perusal of great scholars. Therefore, I did not concern myself with using vulgar language and common expressions. What I have discussed is based on various books, but primarily relies on the "Tianjing Huowen" (Questions on Celestial Classics) by Master You of the Ming dynasty. Booksellers still have artists illustrate these works. What I have described here regarding the many principles of the universe is truly but a single drop in the great ocean. I hope that in the future, with leisure gained through diligent study, I will be able to write a sequel.
