In answer: If the ground of the burial site is damp and yin in nature, the fire burns because it combines with the fatty substances of the deceased. If the ground is dry, it will not burn. Therefore, even an ancient burial mound that normally burns will cease to burn if it is relocated to dry ground without moisture. This is in no way the work of the spirits of the dead. Those who have not fully reasoned through natural principles look at such mounds and say things like "it manifests the torments of the burning hell," but this has never been the meaning of the phenomenon.

Another question: There is a fire on the sea called "ryūtō" (dragon lantern). It never comes ashore, but is sometimes seen above the sea — what manner of thing is this?

In answer: Dragon lanterns traveling across the water are so common in my own village that there is hardly anyone who has not seen them. This is a yin fire of the water. Perhaps it may be fish that, having lived for many years, have come to possess yin fire and are swimming about. (Marginal note: The same principle as the yin fire said to burn from a fox's tail.)
