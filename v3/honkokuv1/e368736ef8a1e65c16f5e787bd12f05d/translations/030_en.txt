【Main Text on the Left Page】

○ Figure 12 illustrates that it is not only glass that refracts the line of sight (light rays). There are countless examples of this phenomenon among all things in the world. Yet people do not understand this principle. Therefore, we now demonstrate how water bends light rays. First, place a copper coin at the bottom of a bowl, then step back until the coin is no longer visible. When a person nearby pours water into the bowl until it is half full, half of the coin becomes visible. And when the water fills the bowl to the brim, the entire coin can be seen. This is because the surface of the water bends the line of sight, directing it down to the bottom of the bowl. This matter is also repeatedly discussed in Western books. To give another example: at around the Hour of Five in the morning (approximately 8 o'clock), place a bowl on flat ground. Because sunlight strikes at an oblique angle, it is blocked by the rim of the bowl and does not reach the bottom. When water is added to the bowl, however, sunlight is able to illuminate the bottom of the bowl directly. If one were to imagine oneself placed at the bottom of the bowl, one would see the morning sun at the Hour of Five as if it were as high in the sky as at the Hour of Four (approximately 10 o'clock).
