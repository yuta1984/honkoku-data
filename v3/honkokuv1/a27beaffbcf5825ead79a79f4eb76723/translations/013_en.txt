Great Earthquake in Edo. Eleven years later, in the first year of Kanbun under the 112nd Emperor Go-Sai, on the first day of the fifth month of Mizunoe-Tora (1662), there was an earthquake and the Toyokuni Shrine in Higashiyama was destroyed. Twenty-two years later, in the third year of Tenna under the 113rd Emperor Reigen, on the fifth day of the fourth month of Mizunoe-I (1683), there was an earthquake at Mount Nikko in Shimotsuke Province. At the same time, there was also a great earthquake in Edo. In the tenth month of the same year, there was an earthquake in Osumi Province where the sea became land. Twenty years later, in the sixteenth year of Genroku under the 114th Emperor Higashiyama, on the twenty-third day of the eleventh month of Mizunoe-Hitsuji (1703), there was a great earthquake in the Kanto region, and it is said that aftershocks continued for more than two hundred days thereafter. Three years later, in the fourth year of Hoei, Hinoto-I (1707), on the eighteenth day of the sixth month, the embankment at Sarugamata in Shimotsuke Province sank dozens of fathoms into the earth.

In the second year of Genryaku (that is, the first year of Bunji, 1185), Kinoto-Mi, on the ninth day of the seventh month in autumn at the hour of the horse, the earth split greatly and shook violently in Yamashiro Province. At this time, the octagonal nine-story pagoda of Hoshoji Temple in Okazaki and seventeen bays of Sanjusangendo collapsed from the tremors. This is recorded in the Tale of the Heike.
