In the same year, on the 4th day of the 10th month, a great earthquake struck Osaka. It is said that the crushed dead formed mountains. At this time, in the ports of Kishu, Mikawa, Totomi, and Ise, blue mud welled up and tsunamis occurred, with deaths numbering in the tens of thousands. On the 22nd day of the same month, Mount Fuji shook and ash fell on the neighboring provinces. Eighteen years later, during the reign of the 115th Emperor Nakamikado, on the 26th day of the 9th month of Kyoho 17, Nagasaki in Hizen experienced earthquakes more than 80 times day and night. In the same 11th year, on the night of the 19th day of the 3rd month of the year of Hinoe-uma, Benkeigadake in Katsuyama, Echizen, shook, and two rock masses measuring approximately 18 ken square flew a distance of 2 ri and 8 cho, damming a great river. The resulting flood killed countless people and livestock. At that time, two villages at the foot of the mountain, with over 300 houses, became pools of mud. Twenty-five years later, during the reign of the 117th Emperor Momozono, on the 29th day of the 2nd month of Horeki 1 (Kanoto-hitsuji), Kyoto experienced a great earthquake. The destruction was particularly severe, and tremors are said to have continued until the 7th month. On the 25th day of the 4th month of the same year, an earthquake struck Takada in Echigo Province. From the hour of the Rooster to the hour of the Ox, it shook more than 30 times. Mountains and houses collapsed, killing over 16,000 people. In the 6th year of the same era, at Umeda in Osaka (which is a burial ground), memorial services with ten thousand lanterns were held by various Buddhist sects for the 50th anniversary of those who died in Hoei 4. Twenty-one years later, during the reign of the 118th Empress Go-Sakuramachi, on the 28th day of the 1st month of Meiwa 3 (Hinoe-inu), a great earthquake in the Aomori area of Tsugaru in Oshu Province caused tsunamis, and people died.
