The sound of collapsing and breaking was like ten thousand thunderbolts falling on their heads. People on the streets crouched down on the main roads, and those in houses prostrated themselves on tatami mats, terrified that they might be crushed to death by roof beams and rafters, with everyone feeling as if they were not truly alive.

The violent shaking continued three times in succession, and when it finally became somewhat calmer after a while, each household threw their tatami mats out onto the main roads, and people fled together in groups, competing to escape onto these mats. Without anyone arranging it, in a short time throughout the capital and its outskirts, those remaining inside houses became rare, and people of all ages and genders, regardless of social rank, all sat together on the main roads - something unprecedented in the eighty years since the Hōreki period, setting aside the distant past.

《Marginal note: According to the Ruiju Kokushi, during Emperor Kōkō's reign in the third year of Gangyō, on the fifth day of the eighth month, which was the day of earthquake rituals, there was a great earthquake that night, and the people of the capital came out of their dwellings and stayed in the streets.》

Now, regarding the houses in Kyoto: some collapsed, others had their pillars warped and ceilings fallen, and those with damaged stoves were most numerous. Storehouses suffered particularly severe damage, with many collapsing immediately from the shaking. Additionally, walls on all four sides fell and large beams shattered, resulting in many injured people. Generally speaking, not a single storehouse in the capital remained intact, yet no one sought to repair them, nor did anyone think to clear them away. People simply prostrated themselves on the main roads, chanting the names of gods and Buddhas. Occasionally, someone would inquire about the safety of their master's house or nearby relatives' homes.
