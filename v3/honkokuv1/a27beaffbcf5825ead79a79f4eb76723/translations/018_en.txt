Although people went out to the main streets, they feared catching cold from the night air, so it was not like the previous night. However, everyone gathered near the edges of their houses, ready to flee to the main streets if severe earthquakes occurred. From this time on, earthquakes occurred frequently every day, and around the fourteenth or fifteenth day, there were fifteen to twenty occurrences in a single day and night. Now, among the houses in Kyoto, both large and small, none escaped damage, so people tried to repair them urgently. However, from carpenters and plasterers to assistant laborers, there were not nearly enough to serve all the houses. Even when summoned repeatedly, work could not be completed easily. Even when workers occasionally came,

if they came for one day, they would not come for two days; if it took two days, they would rest for five days, so repairs were incomplete. People finally used bamboo and lumber to create temporary supports, or tied things together with rope. Many places where no workers came at all were left as they were out of necessity. These included storehouses that originally appeared satisfactory and that people had boasted about to others, but which gradually sustained damage from the daily earthquakes and unexpectedly collapsed all at once, the sound disturbing the neighborhood.
