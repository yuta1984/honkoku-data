## Composition of the Earth

Geographers generally state the following: The interior of the earth is extremely hot, and all its materials exist in a molten state. The outer surface, however, has solidified into a crust as its heat has cooled.

Even the deepest mine shafts can barely reach a depth of one-third of one *ri* [a Japanese unit of distance], and therefore very few people have come to know the composition of the earth through direct experimentation. Nevertheless, by observing the uplifted layers of the earth's crust at or near the surface, it has become possible to understand in detail the composition of the earth to a depth of ten *ri* or less.

The earth's crust forms layer upon layer in a naturally ordered sequence, and the positions of these strata are never disturbed. These various layers are collectively referred to as *gan* (岩, rock/strata).

*Gan* — that is, the earth's crust — is divided into two categories. The first is called igneous rock (*kasei gan*), and the second is called sedimentary rock (*suisei gan*).

Igneous rock is formed by the thermal force of the earth's internal fire (*chishinка*) and the pressure of the upper layers of the earth's crust. It is ordinarily found below sedimentary rock. However, there are cases where it is recognized to exist above sedimentary rock. This occurs when, during a volcanic eruption, molten rock flows out from lower layers on top of sedimentary rock and then solidifies.
