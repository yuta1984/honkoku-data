The waterfall drops in two tiers from a height of over twenty jō, and the sound of the water is like thunder. To the right stands a peculiar rock formation known as "Rōsoku-iwa" (Candle Rock). In this Utsubo-sawa valley, a type of seaweed called fukuro-nori grows; it has a mild, light flavor and is well suited as a sake accompaniment when soaked in sanbaizu (a three-part vinegar dressing). Now, there was once a great temple on this Funao-zan, founded by the venerable Dengyō Daishi, but according to the founding legend of Ryūtaku-ji temple at Funao-zan in Yamakoda village, it was destroyed by Chiba-no-suke Tanemasa.

　　　○ A History of the Ikaho Hot Springs

According to the founding legend of Mizusawa-ji temple and oral traditions passed down by village elders, the hot springs of Ikaho in Nishi-Gunma District, Gunma Prefecture, were discovered during the reign of Emperor Suinin. With a sweeping view of Mount Akagi, Mount Komochi, Mount Mikasa, and other peaks, as well as the Tone River spread out before them, the scenery is supremely beautiful, and the therapeutic effects of the waters are likewise rare and extraordinary — and so the springs' fame soon became widely known throughout the land. The renga poet Sōgi himself bathed here once and bestowed upon the springs the name "Jinsen" (Benevolent Spring). [A note states: "In the household of the Chigira family, the preeminent proprietors of the bathing establishment, is preserved a record titled 'Jinsen-tei no Ki,' composed by Master Shibachū."] Furthermore, since Ikaho stands at an elevation of 2,563 shaku above sea level, air circulates well through the area, and the soil quality and climate are truly without peer — so stated the German physician Dr. Erwin Bälz in his treatise on Japanese mineral springs. Indeed, it is generally the case that hot springs emerge in remote mountain areas lacking in doctors and medicines; yet this is precisely the divine work ordained by the heavenly deities — Ōnamuji-no-Mikoto (also known as the Creator) and Sukunahikona-no-Mikoto — who established the springs for the salvation of the suffering people.
