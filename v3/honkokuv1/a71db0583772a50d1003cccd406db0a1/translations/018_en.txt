○ Tenjin Pass　This refers to the ascent from Ikaho Fuji toward the Haruna road. Because there was originally a stone shrine (*hokora*) to the heavenly deity (*tenjin*), the pass took its name from it; however, in recent years the stone shrine is said to have been lost.

○ Haruna Shrine　Descending eighteen *chō* along a cliff-side path from Tenjin Pass, one arrives at the shrine. Along the roadside stand curious rocks known as "Tsuzura-iwa (Winding Rock)" and "Gusoku-iwa (Armor Rock)." It is said that the Haruna Shrine enshrines the spirit of Prince Yamato Takeru, or alternatively that of Nanbu Saburō Mitsuyuki. The carvings adorning the shrine buildings are executed with consummate skill, and ancient records preserved in the Haruna Bunko (Haruna Archive) show that in former times the shrine held estates in several locations and even wielded military authority. Among its sacred treasures (*shinpō*) are numerous items, including a thunder mallet (*raitsui*), an ancient mirror (*kokyō*), the sacred sword of Prince Ōtōnomiya, and ancient documents.

Within the shrine precinct stands a single iron lantern (*tetsu-tōrō*) dated to the third year of Genkō (1323). The rituals of kagura dance and rain-prayer (*amagoi*) both follow ancient traditions that differ from the ordinary forms. There are also many unusual rock formations bearing names such as "Misugata-iwa (Sacred Figure Rock)," "Sodesuri-iwa (Sleeve-brushing Rock)," "Kurakake-iwa (Saddle-hanging Rock)," "Niten-iwa (Two Heavens Rock)," "Daikoku-iwa (Daikoku Rock)," "Nozoki-iwa (Peeping Rock)," "Kame-iwa (Turtle Rock)," and "Heiji-iwa (Ritual Vessel Rock)." There is also a valley where whetstone (*toishi*) is quarried. The ancient trees grow so thick and dense that not a ray of sunlight filters through; crystal-clear streams flow in abundance, purifying the six senses (*rokkon*) — it is truly a sacred place of manifest divine power. Within the mountains, rare plants grow, and chopsticks made from the wood of the *komekome* tree are celebrated as a local specialty.
