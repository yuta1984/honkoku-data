The following matters are recorded in order.

○ There are fourteen establishments called *Yumoto Ōya* (湯本大家, "Main Lodging Houses of the Spring Source"), along with several dozen other inns. All are built in two or three stories, constructed so as to afford fine views. Furthermore, the ancestors of the Yumoto Ōya families were all samurai of distinguished and unbroken lineage. During the Tenbun era (1532–1555), they served such lords as the Uesugi of Hirai, the Nagano of Minowa, the Takeda of Kōshū (Kai Province), and the Hōjō of Sōshū (Sagami Province). They later entered the service of the Tokugawa house, and during the era of the old shogunate, they guarded the *ura-sekisho* (back checkpoint) on the Mikuni Kaidō highway, and were families privileged to bear a surname and wear swords.

○ The mineral composition of the Ikaho hot spring includes: sulfate of soda (sodium sulfate), sulfate of potash (potassium sulfate), sulfate of magnesia (magnesium sulfate), sulfate of lime (calcium sulfate), chloride of sodium (sodium chloride), chloride of potassium (potassium chloride), bicarbonate of lime (calcium bicarbonate), bicarbonate of magnesia (magnesium bicarbonate), ferrous oxide, silicic acid, and others. The primary components are sodium sulfate and sodium chloride. As such, the waters are particularly efficacious for the following ailments:

○ Chronic and malignant rheumatism　○ Joint pain due to rheumatism　○ Lower back pain　○ Beriberi (*kakke*)　○ Bruising and contusions　○ Nervous disorders　○ Paralysis arising from mineral poisoning (iron toxicity)　○ Skin sores resulting from measles or smallpox　○ Anemia (deficiency of blood)　○ Menstrual irregularities　○ Weak stomach　○ Leukorrhea (*koshike*)　○ Uterine disease
