The "three" now appearing is the reduced representation of the opening division (right-forward opening: one chō); "four" is the reduced representation of the sought distance (kyūtei); and "five" is the reduced representation of the borrowed value (kashaku). (The opening division is the distance from the base station to the open-ground station; the sought distance is the distance from the base station to the target; the borrowed value is the distance from the open-ground station to the target. Follow this pattern in what follows.) Measure "three" against the opening division distance of one chō. ("Measuring against" means using the compass [konpatsu] to measure the reduced figure from end to end with neither excess nor deficiency. The method is: open the mouth of the compass and grip this "three" in one pinch [ikkyō], naming this "the measure of one chō." However, when measuring with the compass in any technique, one must not use just one pinch. When the opening in the reduced figure is wide, use five or ten pinches. It goes without saying that if you grip one chō's reduced figure in five pinches, you define that opening as a measure of twelve ken, and if you grip it in ten pinches, you define that opening as a measure of six ken.)

Using that measure (the "measure" refers to the opening produced by gripping "three" with the compass; the same applies hereafter), measure "four": there are three pinches (one chō per pinch). Three pinches equals three chō. This should be understood as the number of chō in the sought distance.
