Set the surveying board (ban) precisely level and properly oriented. ③ Then, find the location of a "small opening" (ko-hiraki, auxiliary survey point) directly ahead of the board, [thirty ken from the previous opening point], set a marker there [using measuring ropes and rods from the opening-ground marker to the small-opening marker], [the distance may be set to any number of ken as needed; generally, about one-half to one-third of the main opening distance should be taken as the standard], and sight through directly from the opening ground to this marker. ④ Next, move to the small-opening ground, set up the board, and re-sight the opening-ground marker [re-sighting with the opening-ground marker as the residual marker]; ⑤ then place the ruler on the board surface, look back toward the residual marker at the base station, and draw a line. At this point, the shape of the "seed" (tane) 3-4-5 triangle appears. The "3" is the reduced-scale representation of the small opening, and the "4" is the reduced-scale representation of the main opening (hon-hiraki). Measure the "3" against the actual distance of the small opening (3 chō), and using that scale [the scale of 3 chō for the small opening], measure "4": the result is 1 embrace (ikkyō) and one-third. [One embrace equals 3 chō; one-third equals 1 chō.] Therefore, the width of the obstacle (river, swamp, etc.) to be cleared—the "kaiyo" (開除)—is determined to be 4 chō. [This 4 chō is then used as the "3" reference for the next calculation.]

This is the number of ken of the opening-distance sought. Because the number of ken of the opening-distance is not directly measurable, this method is used to determine it. Once the number of ken of the opening-distance has been measured, this diagram figure is no longer used.
