The glass prism is designated as "C", the convex mirror as "D", and the focal point as "E". Light from Jupiter at position "A" enters the prism "C", refracts, passes through the convex mirror "D", and converges at point "E". At "E" there must be a small point of light, glowing brightly like a star. The method of positioning the prism "C" requires making the refracted light from "A" parallel to the line of sight to "B". Let "F" represent the human eye, which sees both "E" and "B". By moving "F" back and forth, the size of "E" is adjusted until the light intensity of "E" equals that of "B", at which point the measurement stops. This is the method for measuring the magnitude of that star.

In this technique, the brightness of Jupiter, which is much farther away than the star being measured, is brought optically to appear at the same location as the star currently being measured. A sixteen-grade scale is established for fixed stars, determining which star corresponds to which magnitude, and this is used as an instrument for measuring variable stars.

What is being discussed here differs slightly in its specifics but follows the same principle. First, the light from Jupiter at position "A" reflects on the upper surface of the triangular prism, the starlight refracts and exits through the side surface of the prism, and also reflects in the convex mirror. It passes through the convex mirror, reaches point "E" to form a small light point, and enters the human eye at "F".

What the human eye sees at that time is the star viewed laterally at point "E", as if unaware that this is the lower surface of the star actually located at position "A". The shadow of the laurel tree on the moon's lower surface reflects on the upper surface of the general wind-sphere, penetrates through the general wind-sphere, and reflects again on the upper surface of another wind-sphere. This refracts through the other wind-sphere and enters the human eye.

Therefore, even if the moon is at a distance of more than 360,810 yojanas and positioned obliquely, how could we see the lateral surface of the moon? This is because we see the shadow of the laurel tree on the moon's lower surface, which reflects on the upper surface of the wind-sphere and is refracted and diffracted through the atmosphere before reaching us. In this case, whether at rising, setting, or zenith, we only see the lower surface of the moon, so there is no reason for the laurel shadow to change.
