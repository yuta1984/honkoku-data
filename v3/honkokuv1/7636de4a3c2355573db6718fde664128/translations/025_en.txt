If one reaches an understanding of this principle, the solution becomes relatively easy to grasp. Nevertheless, unless one applies careful and thorough attention and thinks with great precision, it is difficult to arrive at this principle. For this reason, the Vinaya Master (Ritushi) also states: [Sumeru World Explanation] "In the calendrical systems of China, the Western countries, and others, the combined maximum angular separation between the ecliptic and equator (inner and outer degrees) is set at 4,700 parts. Therefore, the ecliptic-equatorial angular separation for the Sumeru world amounts to only 7 minutes, 23 seconds, and 34 micro-units. Comparing these, the ecliptic-equatorial angular separation of the Sumeru world is smaller than that of the Chinese and Western calendars by a factor of merely one six-hundred-and-forty-ninth." At first, when I (the Vinaya Master) examined this matter, I thought: even granting that this is the Buddhist calendar (Bonreki), with such a minute ecliptic-equatorial separation, how could one possibly measure and determine the twenty-four solar terms, and all the other calendrical elements such as the degrees of heliacal rising? I harbored this doubt. Later, upon gradually and carefully reflecting upon it, I came to understand how the "dense numbers" (mitsusuu, precise computational values) are generated from this figure of 7 minutes, 23 seconds and some remainder — which governs the advance and retreat of celestial bodies (the "waxing-waning and slow-fast" of their motion) — and thereby came to believe in the exquisite precision of the Buddhist calendar, he states. Thus it appears that the Vinaya Master truly agonized deeply over this particular point. He also states: [Reduced Celestial Sphere Explanation] "One knows but one aspect of this, and has never come to know that heaven contains the principle of the circular standard (enJun no ri)." Although he ultimately did not write this out in full, the "dense numbers" and the "principle of the circular standard" are precisely what derive from the distinction between the "true" (shin) and the "image" (zō), as I have repeatedly argued previously. In celestial phenomena there exist both the "true" and the "image." The explanation that the maximum solar distance at the solstices is 290 yojanas refers to the "true" value only. As for the "image" — that is, the celestial phenomenon as it appears before our eyes — in the interval of the maximum solstitial distance, the sun and moon can be lined up ninety-some diameters side by side, and it appears as 4,700 parts. It is by setting up calculations based on the celestial phenomena as they appear that one arrives at the calendar, and this is precisely the reason why the "dense numbers" are generated. One should understand that discussing this "image-phenomenon" constitutes what is called "the principle of the wind-wheel and the circular standard."
