From this, one can determine the climate of cold and warmth of each country. The pole star (used for measuring the north pole) cannot be seen from lands south of the equator. In the countries of the south, one instead observes the south pole star, and by knowing the degrees of each place's distance from the equator, one can assess the climate of cold and warmth of each country. Once the degrees of the north pole are measured and known, combining this with observations from southern lands, one can clearly determine these matters.

When China and Japan have the north pole rising above the horizon by thirty-six degrees, the south pole sinks below the horizon by thirty-six degrees as well. However, when both China and Japan are said to be at thirty-six degrees north polar elevation, this refers specifically to the central part of China's Henan Province and Japan's Musashi Province (Edo), which, though separated east to west, share the same north-south position. Hence both are said to be at thirty-six degrees.

[Marginal note at upper left of this page: "To be more precise, Musashi Province (Edo) is slightly less than thirty-six degrees."]

Not every country is at thirty-six degrees. Even within Japan's sixty-plus provinces, as they differ in north-south position, the polar degree is not the same. As China is a large country, there are great differences in the north polar elevation across its territory from south to north. The coastal regions of Guangdong in the south have a north polar elevation of eighteen to nineteen degrees, while the northern region of Beijing Province has over forty degrees. Similarly in Japan, the coastal areas of Satsuma Province in the south have a north polar elevation of thirty degrees, while Tsugaru in the north has forty degrees. Knowing the entry and exit of one pole, one can determine the opposite pole accordingly. One should know that the degrees by which a pole rises above the horizon and the degrees of distance from the equator are the same.
