　　Taisshoku (Eclipses at the Horizon) — This applies to both solar and lunar eclipses

When a solar or lunar eclipse is observed at the eastern or western horizon during the hours of mao or yū (i.e., at sunrise or sunset), it is called a "taisshoku" (帯食, "belt eclipse" or horizon eclipse). In a solar eclipse, the end of the eclipse may be seen in the morning on the eastern horizon, while the beginning of the eclipse may be seen in the evening on the western horizon. In a lunar eclipse, the end of the eclipse may be seen after dusk on the eastern horizon, while the beginning of the eclipse may be seen at dawn on the western horizon. In all cases, this refers to the situation where the beginning or end of the eclipse coincides with the time of the rising or setting of the sun or moon — that is, when the sun or moon rises or sets while still undergoing an eclipse.

　　Yōreki and Inreki (Outer and Inner Orbital Paths)

The region inside the ecliptic is called "inreki" (陰暦, inner path), and the region outside the ecliptic is called "yōreki" (陽暦, outer path). These are further subdivisions of the Nine Paths (九道) used to categorize lunar motion. During a solar eclipse: when it occurs under yōreki conditions, the southern portion of the solar disk is eclipsed — this is because the lunar disk is to the south. Under inreki conditions, the northern portion of the solar disk is eclipsed — this is because the lunar disk is to the north. In these cases, the eclipse begins from the west and ends toward the east. A lunar eclipse is the reverse: under yōreki, the northern portion is eclipsed, because the Earth's shadow is directed toward the north of the lunar body. Under inreki, the southern portion is eclipsed, because the Earth's shadow is directed to the south. In a lunar eclipse, the eclipse begins from the east and ends in the west. In sum, the two categories of yōreki and inreki simply distinguish whether the lunar disk is to the south or north of the ecliptic. When the moon is south of the ecliptic, it is outside the ecliptic and constitutes yōreki. When the moon is north of the ecliptic (it is inside and constitutes inreki).
