○Hatsushima Island: Located three ri by sea from Atami, this solitary island measures seven to eight chō east to west and four to five chō north to south (with an old rice field assessment of 93 koku, 3 to, 5 shō). This is the "small offshore island" referred to in the poem by the Right Minister of Kamakura (Minamoto no Sanetomo): "As I cross over the Hakone path, I see waves washing against a small island offshore in the Izu sea." The island has over forty households, most engaged in fishing. At the western end of the island stands Hatsuki Shrine, which enshrines Konohana-sakuya-hime. It is said that this goddess first discovered the hot springs (hashiriyu) of Izusan. There are stone torii gates and stone lanterns built during the Kyōhō and Genroku periods. There are also two Sōtō Zen temples: one called Tōmyōji, located among luxuriant pines and camphor trees at the eastern end of the island, and another called Jifukuji, situated south of Hatsuki Shrine, atop several stone steps. Both belong to the Zenshūin temple in Ajiro village as branch temples. Throughout this island, narcissus flowers grow abundantly with remarkably large leaves and flowers that astonish the eye. There are also many peach trees. Local products include tea, wheat, peaches, and sweet potatoes.

○Parrot Stone (Ōmu-seki): Located in an area about ten chō south of the road leading to Mishima Station, in Tanna village, Tagata district. About three ri from Atami. The stone is over one jō six shaku high and over six shaku wide. Human speech and all other sounds echo from this stone with no gap between the voice and its echo. According to a record by Chikkei (there is a text written by Chikkei Nishihara Akira during the Meiwa period), "Could this be the resonating stone of Nangaku Shinrō Peak mentioned in ancient records?" In recent years, it suffered damage from wildfire and is said to have greatly diminished in its resonating quality.

○One Cup of Water: Located in Taga village beside the Shimoda road, this is merely a small spring of about one shaku, but it is as clear and cold as crystal. According to tradition, when the Right General Yoritomo passed through this area, he cupped and drank from it when thirsty. Other sites include Komagata Hall (where Yoritomo's horse was buried), Kuromine (with a pond at its summit), Chōshiguchi, and Bin-no-sawa (all have Jizō halls tended by lay monks).
