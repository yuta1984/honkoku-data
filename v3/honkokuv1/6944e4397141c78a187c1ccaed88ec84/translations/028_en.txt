Stone Jizo statues are enshrined, with the distance markers recorded below them. Climbing eleven cho (counted from the Four-Sided Pagoda, same below), one reaches Tozawa Jizo Hall, which was founded by the great priest Gorin (who was also a disciple of Kukai). Lay monks guard it. Now the hall buildings are in ruins and not worth seeing. From here, the roadside is no longer obstructed by miscellaneous trees, and one climbs the winding serpentine path as if treading on clouds. After climbing forty cho, one reaches Higane Jizo. This mountain is said to have been founded by a hermit called Matsuba Sennin during the reign of Emperor Nintoku. It is not clear when the Jizo Hall was built. According to tradition, the Right General of Kamakura (Minamoto no Yoritomo) restored it and also granted it rice fields (Since Yoritomo established his military government, he regretted that he could not always climb this mountain, so he established a place for distant worship in his domain and called it Higane Jizo Hall. The old site still exists in Kamakura today). The bronze statue now enshrined was made by Hannyain Shosan during the Teikyo era, and the two attendant children are said to be the work of Kukai. In front of the hall are stone statues of the King of Hell and the River Hag of Life and Death, which are ancient works worth seeing. Among the lay monks who serve as guardians, there were originally six temples, but now there are only four: Genshūbō, Dōseibō, etc. According to tradition, mountain bandits from Hakone were converted by a certain monk from Hannyain, took religious vows, and came to live here. Behind the hall is the Hermit's Tomb, said to be the burial place of the three hermits: founder Matsuba, second-generation Randatsu, and third-generation Kinchi. From here, climbing another eight cho (fifty-eight cho in total from Atami), one reaches the summit of Maruyama, which is the Ten Provinces Pass. There is a stone with the following inscription: "From the summit of Mount Higane in Kamo District, Izu Province, one can observe ten provinces and five islands. From the direction of the Rat to the Rabbit: Sagami Province, Musashi Province, Awa Province, Kazusa Province, Shimōsa Province. From the direction of the Dragon to the Monkey: the five islands belonging to these provinces, and Tōtōmi Province. From the direction of the Rooster to the Boar: Suruga Province, Shinano Province, Kai Province. In the third year of Tenmei, the Eastern Capital hermit Rin, Morotori Izumo, Kōei Genseikō and others built this in response to the request of Watanabe Fusabō, village headman of Atami." After reading this and turning one's head to look around, the view opens in all four directions with not a shadow to obstruct the eye. To the west, Mount Fuji rises high into the middle sky, with Hakone and Ashigara extending to its north, while the mountains of Kai and Shinano appear and disappear between them in an admirable sight.
