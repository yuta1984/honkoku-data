○Reservoir Pool: Located north of Kami-juku, beside Kinomiya Shrine. This area is originally rich in hot springs but very poor in cold springs. My late friend Tanaka Heihachi frequently visited this place and, lamenting the great importance of drinking water for human health, donated 1,500 yen for the construction of new waterworks and proposed this project to the villagers. The villagers were greatly pleased and contributed additional funds. They installed pipes extending over fifteen chō from the foot of Mount Hikane, built a large pond here to store cold spring water, and used bamboo pipes to distribute it throughout the village. The water is clear and cold, truly sufficient to support the health of villagers and bathing guests from all directions.

○Yokoiso: At the eastern corner of Higashimachi beach, a wharf was built to create a small harbor. The view is magnificent, and it has also become a sea bathing and swimming area.

○Ito River: Flows from below Mount Hikane, with its upper reaches called Miya River. It passes in front of Onsen Temple, reaches Arajuku, and enters the sea.

○Hatsu River: Its upper reaches are called Iri River. It flows from Mount Takanosu, circles Mount Maruyama, passes through Minokuchi Village, and enters the sea.

○Wada River: Flows from Mount Wada. All other streams are small tributaries that flow south into the sea.

○Narihira Well: Located beside the road in Shinjuku. According to Reishūhō's "Atami Geographic Record," "The name Narihira is originally based on the poems of Lord Ariwara no Narihira. Men and women of the village would reflect their images in the well water morning and evening, considering it a sign for matrimonial connections." Today, such customs no longer exist, nor does anyone draw water from it. Only a stone well frame remains to preserve this historic site.

○Nasu-zawa Waterfall: Located several chō north of Mount Maruyama, at the end of a deep ravine. In recent years, people of refined taste have been clearing the undergrowth, planting flowering trees, and wish to make it a scenic spot.
