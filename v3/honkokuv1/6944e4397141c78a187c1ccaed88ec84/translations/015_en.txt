According to Walton's theory on hot springs, such hot springs must be located near the seashore with multiple outlets, one of which opens into the sea. During high tide, the spring mouth is pressed down by seawater and cannot flow out, so instead it flows back into its reservoir and takes another outlet to gush forth. These springs are called intermittent springs (geyser springs).

In ancient times, when the hot spring finished boiling, thousands of beetles would swarm and fly about, eventually mixing with the hot water and flowing away. However, during the Bunsei era, the monk Tokuhon (founder of Ichigyo-in Temple in Komagome, Tokyo) found this strange and pitiful sight troubling, so he practiced Buddhist law and built a Buddhist pagoda. Since then, these insects have never appeared again. The local people have passed down this story and regard it as a spiritual manifestation. (I have often been able to hear the facts from local elders who once witnessed these insects with their own eyes. The insects resembled what the locals call "iso-mushi" [shore insects], but no similar species can be found in either mountains or seas.) 

The stone pagoda still stands within the fence today, and several stone Buddha statues are installed beside it, said to have been erected by the monk Kan'yo (head priest of Shōtoku Temple in Asakusa, Tokyo) and others.

Also, British Minister Right Honorable Alcock bathed in this spring in July of the first year of Man'en (1860) and erected a stone monument with dozens of characters carved on it, including the phrase: "Out of love for the scenic wonders of mountains and seas, I erect this stone so that future generations may know that the British began visiting this place starting with us."
