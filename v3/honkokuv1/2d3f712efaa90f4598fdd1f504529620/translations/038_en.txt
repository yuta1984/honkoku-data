**Sixth Difficulty: Anomalous Eclipses**

The Western theory states: eclipses follow a fixed orbital pattern, occurring once every six months. This is called the "regular eclipse interval" (kō-jōdo). In this respect, the theories of the Indian (Brahmic) and Chinese traditions agree. However, the Indian and Chinese traditions also speak of anomalous eclipses beyond the regular interval, and regard them especially with awe and dread as reprimands and punishments from the divine spirits of heaven and earth. The Western barbarians have determined this to be the foolishness and delusion of the Indian and Chinese sages. Namely, in the *Hakubutsu Shinpen* (New Treatise on Natural Philosophy), folio 14, it states: "The people of the world do not understand astronomy... [abbreviated]... Solar eclipses have been taken as heavenly omens and warnings showing that the ruler has lost virtue, and from ancient times the court has used ritual offerings at shrines. All of this is utterly meaningless," and so on.

**Refutation:**

The regular eclipses occur at the points where the two orbital paths of the sun and moon cross obliquely—the two nodes of intersection between the positive and negative paths. These are called Rāhu and Ketu. These nodes always face each other in direct opposition. When the sun and moon arrive at these two points in their movements, an eclipse invariably occurs. From the first day the moon enters a nodal point, whether in the pattern of "six-enter-seven-exit" or "seven-enter-six-exit," it meets with the sun and an eclipse again necessarily follows. The interval amounts to one hundred and seventy-three days. These figures are established by the common measurement of the Indian, Chinese, and Western traditions, with no differences among them whatsoever. This is because the calendar systems of China and the Western lands were originally transmitted from the Indian calendar.
