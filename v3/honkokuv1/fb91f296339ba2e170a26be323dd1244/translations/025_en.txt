There were originally six temples, but now only four remain, including Genshū-bō and Dōsei-bō. According to tradition, mountain bandits from Hakone were converted by a certain monk from Hannya-in temple, took Buddhist vows, and settled here. Behind the hall is the Sennin-zuka (Hermit's Mound), which is said to be the burial place of three hermits: the founder Shōyō, the second-generation Randatsu, and the third-generation Konchi. From here, ascending another eight chō (58 chō total from Atami) brings one to the summit of Maruyama, which is the Jikkoku Pass. There is a stone inscribed with the following text: "From the summit of Mount Higane in Kamo District, Izu Province, one can observe ten provinces and five islands. From north to east: Sagami, Musashi, Awa, Kazusa, and Shimōsa provinces. From east-southeast to west-southwest: the five islands belonging to those provinces and Tōtōmi Province. From west to north-northwest: Suruga, Shinano, and Kai provinces. In the third year of Tenmei [1783], this was erected at the request of Watanabe-bō, village head of Atami, by Tōto Rin-koji, Morotori Izumo, Kōei Genseikō, and others."

After reading this and turning one's head to survey the vista, the view opens in all four directions with nothing to obstruct the eye. To the west, Mount Fuji rises high into the middle of the sky, with Hakone and Ashigara extending to its north, and the mountains of Kai and Shinano appearing and disappearing between them. Mount Aitaka stands majestically to the left, while the villages of Mishima and Numazu are scattered like go stones at its base. The pine trees along the official road and the flow of the Fuji River continue intermittently in the distance along the Suruga coastline. Directly ahead, across Shigedera Bay, one can see the mountains of Sanagi and Amagi, and turning further eastward, the seas of Izu and Sagami meet at the horizon. The scenic spots of Atami, Ajiro, Izusan, Manazuru, and Hatsushima gather closely before one's eyes, while the five islands (Ōshima, Toshima, Kozushima, etc.) and the mountains of Bōsō appear and disappear in the distance like clouds and mist. Miura, Enoshima, Ōiso and other places dot the scene, adding to its scenic charm. This is truly a vista that "the eye cannot fully take in and appreciate."
