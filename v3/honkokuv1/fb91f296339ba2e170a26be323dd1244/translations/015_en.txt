Based on European hot springs that were long experienced and found to be most similar to this one, the following therapeutic effects can be indicated: scrofula in children and the various symptoms arising from it; rheumatism and chronic gout (requiring the highest temperature the patient can tolerate, generally up to 104°F / 33°C); in cases of inflammatory exudation, it promotes the absorption of exudate after the inflammation has already subsided; in cases of extravascular fluid leakage and ordinary edema, it likewise has the effect of promoting absorption. Beriberi accompanied by edema and neuralgic skin conditions should all be treated by bathing in this spring. Furthermore, chronic gastric catarrh and chronic diarrhea, chronic throat and bronchial catarrh, chronic bladder catarrh, chronic vaginal and uterine catarrh (to be used in conjunction with bathing and vulvar injection), chronic bile duct catarrh, and depression arising from chronic gastric and intestinal catarrh — especially in cases where circulation is sluggish and the skin or mucous membranes are relaxed — require that this spring water also be used as a drinking cure.

Additionally, according to the theory of Nakashima Kuwata of Ōmi Province, this spring cures chronic rheumatism, scrofulous lymphadenitis (ruireki), beriberi, paralysis, syphilis, eye diseases, uterine diseases, skin diseases, and various ailments arising from skin debility. Furthermore, when taken internally, it induces mild diarrhea, promotes stomach function, aids in the digestion of food and drink, clears blockages in the liver, spleen, and mesentery, and at the same time invigorates the circulation of the blood.

In the travel account of Shaku Eton (Izumiya Gareki-shū), it is written: "Take a handful of flowers and bamboo leaves and immerse them in hot spring water, and they will wilt; but if soaked in [cold] water for the time it takes to drink a bowl of tea, they will immediately (revive)..."
