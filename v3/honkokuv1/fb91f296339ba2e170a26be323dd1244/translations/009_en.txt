○Yugawara Village was also once called Isoyama-no-sato (Village of Rocky Mountain). However, neither name is used today《Note: The name Yugawara Village existed until 350 years ago, as known from the roof tablet of the Yuzen Shrine construction in the 18th year of Eisho era. This tablet is currently in the possession of Fujiya Kiemon》. It is simply called Atami Village, with Wada and Minokuchi as its affiliated villages. This is precisely the source of the hot spring eruptions. The town is divided into eleven districts: Honcho, Hamacho, Shinjuku, Arashuku, Yokocho, Shin-yokocho, Higashi-sakacho, Kosawa, Niken-chaya, and Kamishuku《Note: There are also smaller place names such as Isohira, Ikeda, Fujisawa, and Okubo, totaling 53 districts》. Wada Village《Also called Katahira-no-sato》is located 7-8 cho south of the main village at the foot of Nenbutsu Mountain, with about 40 households where farmers and fishermen live together. 5-6 cho to its northwest, along the mountains, there are about 13-14 houses called Minokuchi Village. The residents all combine farming with forestry work. The main village has about 280 households where people engaged in agriculture, industry, commerce, fishing, and forestry live side by side, forming something of a small urban settlement.

○Hot Springs
The original sites of hot spring eruptions were formerly called the Seven Springs (Nana-yu): namely, Oyu, Seizaemon-yu, Hosai-yu, Furo-no-yu, Kawara-no-yu, and Sajiro-no-yu. Now new springs have emerged in various places: Mizu-no-yu, Naka-no-yu, Fukushimaya-no-yu, Kanbei-yu, Ioji-no-yu, Furuya-no-yu, Naka-no-yu, etc., totaling fifteen locations. Besides these, throughout the coastline and fields, wherever one digs even slightly deep, hot springs will surely emerge. This truly lives up to the name of Atami (Hot Ocean).
