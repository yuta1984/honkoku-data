### Hail

Question: Hail is a cold thing. Why does it not fall in cold seasons but falls in hot seasons? Some say it is spat out by lizards; others say it is stored within dragon scales. Is this correct or not?

Answer: Among the three atmospheric layers, the middle one is the cold layer. The lower layer is warm, being close to the earth, and the upper layer is hot, being close to fire. The place of extreme cold is within the middle of the cold layer. The rain of spring and autumn and the snow of the three winter months undergo transformation and fall as soon as they reach the outer edge of the cold layer. In winter months, the force by which qi rises is very slow and gentle; without the clouds spreading across the entire earth, it cannot gain sufficient momentum. Therefore, the base of the clouds spreads very wide, and the cloud bases of spring and autumn are also broad. Because clouds form slowly, the rain falls gently. All of this transforms at the initial, outer edge of the cold layer. In summer months, (qi) accumulates in dense concentration and bursts upward with full force. Its power is singular and its momentum swift. Therefore, the base of the clouds is narrow and concentrated, differing from field ridge to field ridge, with ditches and channels quickly filling up. Because of this singular, sharp force, the qi can directly reach the deeper part of the cold layer. The rising qi grows ever thicker, its upward surge ever faster, it penetrates ever deeper into the cold, the coalescence of (water droplets) becomes ever more rapid, and the resulting crystalline bodies grow ever larger. Surging suddenly into the zone of extreme cold, it rapidly congeals into hail. The size of hailstones further depends on the depth of penetration into the cold and the thickness of the cloud vapor.
