【Types of Thunder (continued from previous page)】

There are three types of thunder. The first is called "boring thunder" (鑽雷, sanrai): it is sharp and thin like a flame, boring through empty space and passing by. The second is called "surging thunder" (湃雷, hairai): when it encounters objects, it does not strike them apart but burns and scatters them. The third is called "burning thunder" (焼雷, shōrai): this arises from the pent-up and wrathful energy of yin and yang. When there is malevolent energy (悪気) in the earth and it happens to resonate and meet with this force, it causes a震動 (震動) that passes through, leaving traces of fire behind. Things that carry poison are stripped of their poison; things without poison have poison left in them. There are also cases where one poison is removed and another is created. All of these are caused by burning thunder.

《Yuxiong says: If the ground is close to yang energy, things can be born within the soil — beings of strange form, like birds or pigs — which, when encountered at the wrong time, can kill people. These are things generated by yang energy, which emerge by riding upon the yang. People can control them; they are not thunder.》

【Dust Rain (霾, Mai/Tsuchifuri)】

Question: Wind, clouds, rain, and dew are all products of earth's vapors rising and transforming. Sometimes something like ash falls as a kind of dust rain (霾). Sometimes millet or bean-like things fall. And sometimes various objects fall, each taking on a distinct shape. What are these phenomena?

Answer: The falling of dust rain (霾) occurs when fire energy is sparse and dry, carrying earth upward with it. The water energy is too faint and light to form rain, while earth energy is dense and thick, descending in a dull yellow haze — this is called mai (霾). If the fire energy is also strong, it is refined into a substance that may resemble the shapes of millet, beans, or various other objects. All of these follow the same principle as falling thunderbolts (落楔) and meteorites (隕石). The rising of earth's vapors takes the form of what is accumulated in its respective mountains and rivers; the various shapes of what falls are likewise determined by what the earth's energy produces, taking on forms accordingly.
