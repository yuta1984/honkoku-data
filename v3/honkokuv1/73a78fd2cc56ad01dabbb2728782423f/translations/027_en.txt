**[Continuation on Double Sun]**

...the light passes through, and is then struck by the sunlight. Behind it, there is yet another layer of cloud—dark and thick—which blocks the sunlight and causes it to pass back through the thin cloud. This is how the phenomenon of the "double sun" (multiple suns appearing) is created. If there are additional clouds on either side that align with the light-transmitting cloud, another solar image is formed once more. However, this occurs when the sun is to the east or west and observed along a horizontal plane; when the sun is at its zenith, this phenomenon does not occur. This is because looking upward strains the eye's power to perceive it, and at noon the yang energy is at its strongest, breaking through the cloud formations, and moreover the floating vapors at the zenith are even thinner than those to the east or west. The same holds true for the moon. These phenomena are seen two or three days before and after the full moon (望, *bō*). At the time of the full moon, the moon's light is at its greatest, and it illuminates the clouds well enough to produce such lunar images. In general, the doubling of the sun or moon is in all cases a sign of rain.
