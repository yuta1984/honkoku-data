Answer: Originally, the one qi divided into yin and yang, and the five elements became the pillars of heaven and earth, working together in a great manner. Wood fundamentally receives the qi of earth and water to grow, but since it grows by being nurtured by the fire of the sun, it must contain fire in its nature. Fire among the five elements is born from wood, and when wood is thrown into fire, the fire spreads even more. "Wood generates fire" is a natural principle. In the numbers of the River Chart, Heaven One descends to generate water in the north direction, and one is the beginning of all things from which the five elements emerge. Nothing in the world is more abundant than water. It benefits all things and circulates without stagnation. Therefore, sages and wise men are said to delight in water, and Laozi said "the highest good is like water." Water, fire, wood, metal, and earth are the generative numbers of the five elements of heaven and earth, while wood, fire, earth, metal, and water represent the sequential order in which the five elements circulate and generate. The reason these are called the five elements is that they carry out qi for heaven, as seen in the Baihu Tong. The five elements work with each other - for example, wood is born in earth and water, grows in fire, is overcome by metal, and receives saws and chisels to serve great purposes in the world. Metal is born in earth, but without being overcome by fire, it cannot fulfill its respective functions.

Both generation and overcoming serve great purposes. Due to "wood generates fire," seeing fire when cutting wood is a natural principle with inevitable reason. Moreover, the principle of the Supreme Ultimate, yin-yang, and five elements extends to all things without exception. There are five stars in heaven, five elements on earth, and five organs in humans. This applies to all matters and things. The ultimate theory on this appears widely in books, particularly in recent works such as Zhou Zi's "Explanation of the Diagram of the Supreme Ultimate" and the first volume of "Reflections on Things at Hand."
