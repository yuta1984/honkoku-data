○ Tides (Shio)

**Question:** The ebb and flow of the tides is like the breathing of the earth. Their fullness and emptiness wax and wane according to the moon. But when the tide comes in, from which country (direction) does it emerge, and when it ebbs away, where does it hide? Also, in common practice, people use a daily reckoning called "four-minute descent" (shi-bu sagari) to know the times of high and low tide. For short months (of twenty-nine days), when moving from the twenty-ninth day to the first day of the new month, there is a sudden shift of eight minutes, and since short months have no thirtieth day, it appears as though the tides also pause their coming and going for one day. What is the principle behind this? Moreover, it is curious that although all the waters of the world ultimately flow into the sea, it does not overflow even after many years. How can this be?

**Answer:** In general, the shape of the earth is spherical—like a walnut, so to speak. What is high are the mountains and peaks; what is somewhat high is the land; what is low and concave are the seas and rivers. Since the nature of the earth tends toward lowness, the waters of rivers and streams ultimately return to the sea. The sea, without rejecting even the smallest trickle, is thus able to achieve its great size. The *Yantie Lun* (Discourse on Salt and Iron) states: "Water accumulates to form rivers and seas." However, the principle of the creative transformation (zōka) of heaven and earth is such that if things were only created and not dissolved, the universe would become overfull; and if dissolved but not created, the ten thousand things would naturally perish. Thus, where there is life, there must necessarily be death; where there is gathering, there must also be dispersal. Zōka is the wondrous workings of the natural universe (kenkon). Within the earth, too, there is both water and fire, and water and fire, coming into contact with each other, are able to mutually transform. The relationship between the moon and water is like that of a magnet attracting a needle, or amber picking up dust: as the moon travels around the earth, the water also travels around the earth. It is not only the tides—all the water-energy (suiki) between heaven and earth follows the moon. Since the moon is fundamentally the essence (sei) of water, this is a necessary and inevitable principle. The tides have four cycles of ebb and flow during the day and night; when they rise at the time the moon is directly overhead, this is called "seissetsu" (direct contact); when they rise again at the point directly opposite the moon, this is called "hanssetsu" (reflected contact). The tides are always at their fullest at the new moon (saku) and full moon (bō), and at their lowest around the seventh or eighth day of the seventh month. The daily gradual shift of the tides is three *koku* and seventy-two minutes. The common reckoning of "four minutes late" is not the true figure in units of time; because the calculation is complex, it is averaged across long and short months as "four-minute descent," which, while not perfectly accurate, is not far off.
