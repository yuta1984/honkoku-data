One　Regarding the classifications and categories of surveying methods currently in circulation in the world, upon careful consideration, I have yet to find them satisfactory to my own thinking. Therefore, now setting aside my own narrow-mindedness, I have taken the liberty of revising them and newly established their headings and titles. However, as for the underlying principles and techniques themselves, the world's view and my own appear to be largely in agreement. If that is the case, then although disregarding the old methods and establishing new ideas may seem like self-attachment (stubborn self-opinion), "a name is but a guest to the substance" — that is, names should follow content. Why, then, should improper names be used? Furthermore, to impose upon others what one oneself does not desire would be contrary to the Way, and it is for this reason that I cannot but proceed as I have.

One　Among the various techniques, there are cases where following the fundamental theory causes confusion, while using the simplified method leads to a quicker understanding. For techniques of this kind, the fundamental theory is often set aside and the simplified method is used instead. Those with expertise should rely on the fundamental theory from the outset. Beginners should first learn the simplified method, and then, as the days and months pass, naturally come to comprehend and follow the fundamental theory. If one attempts to immediately reach a state of profound mastery without doing so, one will likely be unable to achieve it.

One　The instruments used in land surveying are divided among the Five Techniques (*go-jutsu*: Compass Technique [*Banshin-jutsu*], Surveying Board Technique [*Ryōban-jutsu*], Circumferentor Technique [*Konhatsu-jutsu*], Calculation Technique [*Sankan-jutsu*], and Conversion Technique [*Kiten-jutsu*] — together referred to as the Five Techniques), each having its own distinct forms and varieties. Both old and new items are exceedingly numerous in their kinds. Some use unusual forms under the name of a particular school's style, others add their own ideas under the name of innovation, so their manufacture is not standardized, and the items number in the dozens.
