(Continued from previous page) Therefore, if the juncture of the konhatsu (proportional compass) measures three spans against this scaled length, the distance is thirty ken. If four spans, the distance is forty ken. If two and a half spans, the distance is twenty-five ken. In general, the principle for measuring distances of any number of ri or tens of ri is the same. Moreover, measuring elevation differences, widths, and depths follows no different method from measuring distances. (Where there are minor variations in procedure, these are noted below each relevant technique.)

**On the Instruments and Tools of Kenpan-jutsu (Surveying Board Technique)**

Among the instruments used in kenpan-jutsu, there are those of old design and new manufacture, large and small, fine and rough, and their differences are varied. Removing the unnecessary items and selecting the useful ones, I illustrate them below. The three principal instruments — the kenpan (surveying board), the jōgi (straightedge/ruler), and the konhatsu (proportional compass) — are above all the most central of these. Furthermore, the chōkyoku (plumb ball), suishin (plumb needle), sumi-sashi (ink marker), fude (brush), tōmegane (telescope), shirushi-ki (surveying poles/staffs), ken-nawa (measuring rope), and ken-sao (measuring pole), etc., are the items regularly used in the field. There are also other items that students should know about, but as they are not urgently needed, they are recorded in later volumes. As for instruments related to banshinjutsu (compass surveying), konhatsu-jutsu (proportional compass technique), and sankan-jutsu (calculation technique), these are illustrated at the beginning of each respective volume, and where there are differences between old and new, these are discussed in the "Wakumon" (Questions and Answers) volume.

Among all these, the bundo no kaneru (protractor and compass instruments) are the sacred treasures of land surveying technique, and therefore, so as to inform beginning students in advance, I illustrate them below, even though they do not strictly fall under kenpan-jutsu.

**【Kenpan — The Surveying Board】**

The kenpan is an instrument by which the forms of near and far distances, widths, heights, and depths of terrain are scaled down and recorded on a board surface for measurement, and since it is the very instrument that gives this technique its name, there is no need to elaborate further on its importance as the primary instrument. Its construction is based on squareness and flatness. The board surface is made of hinoki (Japanese cypress). The supporting pillars and bracing posts (ushi) should be made of kashinoki (oak). In general, the larger version measures approximately one shaku four sun in vertical length and about one shaku in horizontal width. The smaller version measures approximately one shaku in vertical length and about seven sun in horizontal width. The height in either case is best set at one shaku four to five sun.
