For example, if you first measure from the base station to the target and find it to be three chō, you should clear six ken. If you measure it to be one and a half chō, you should clear three ken. This is the ratio of one-thirtieth.

However, even if there is a slight excess or deficiency, it does not cause great harm. Depending on the width, narrowness, difficulty, or ease of the land, when it cannot be helped, one should follow what is most suitable. If you do not repeatedly seek the cleared ground, and there are obstructions that hinder the back-sighting, or if the distance is much less than the ancient method's one-thirtieth (if it is more, it does not matter how much, as it causes no harm), or if the clearing marks or remaining marks are difficult to distinguish clearly, this may hinder the surveying technique. One should carefully consider the advantages and disadvantages, gains and losses.

Moreover, the method of clearing generally follows this pattern: when the right side of the base station is narrow, clear to the left; when the left side is cramped, clear to the right; when the rear ground is narrow, advance to the front ground; when the front ground is blocked, retreat to the rear ground. Or when the front, rear, left, and right are steep and narrow, one should know to use diagonal clearing in all directions. Furthermore, one should devise and deliberate on other methods.
