Therefore, since this ground revolves eastward every day, it cannot but appear as though the sun moves from east to west. This is not the sun moving; it is the earth on which we live that moves. And when the earth faces the sun is called day, and when it turns its back to the sun is called night. A certain person asks: If the earth revolves like this every day, then the land on which we live must, in the course of one day and night, at some point become the upper surface, then the side, and then the lower surface. When it becomes sideways or the lower surface, why does it not tilt and fall, with all things leaving the ground and falling away? The answer: This earth has something called "attractive force" (吸力), which absorbs and draws things on the surface toward it, so that even as the ground revolves and its orientation changes, things do not tilt and fall.

I argue: This theory is also greatly in error. In refuting it, there are two points. First, I shall refute the argument that the earth has an attractive force, and then I shall proceed to demolish the claim that the earth rotates and revolves. First, to refute the attractive force: The "Rokugō Sōdan," Volume 2, Part 2 (folio 13) states: "Within the sun there is an attractive force, which is therefore able to make the various planets revolve around it. Same text (folio 14): If the earth had one propulsive force moving it, it would necessarily travel along the straight line from point A to point B. Suppose there were no such propulsive force and there were only the sun's attractive force; then the earth would necessarily travel toward the sun. But the sun's attractive force and the propulsive force are always combined together. Therefore, a curvilinear (arc-line) path is produced. Since these two forces never cease, the arc-line path likewise never changes. And thus the earth's orbit is formed."
