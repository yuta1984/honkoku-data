This is six chō [approximately 654 meters]. [Note: Up to this point is the procedure of the first technique (shojutsu), which measures the right side of the target. These six chō serve as the basis for measuring the sought distance (kyūtei).] Then, set the board aside. [Note: This board was used only for measuring the right side of the target and is not used in subsequent techniques.]

[Middle] ➄ Now, place a new board squarely aligned with the pivot mark (kaname-jirushi), set diagonally as shown in the diagram. [Note: While it is acceptable to use the board from the first technique as it is, the ink marks can easily become confused, so a new board is used here.]

From the east of the board, sight directly toward the left side of the target. [Note: The left of the target is the right side of the home position (honza).] [Note: This sighting is not truly in the cardinal direction, but since the board is set diagonally, it is considered straight from the perspective of the current sighting direction.]

➅ From the west of the board, measure the correct opening distance (kaijo) to the right [Note: Right correct opening, four chō], plant the opening mark (kaiin), sight through it, ➆ proceed to the opening ground (kaichi), reconfirm the pivot mark [Note: This mark was also used in the previous technique], and set the board squarely. ➇ Use the southeast corner (ban-son/巽) of the board as the intersection point, look back toward the target [Note: referring to the left side of the target] and draw a line. This reveals a 3-4-5 shape (a right triangle). Measuring the "three" against the four chō opening distance, and measuring the "four" with the square (kane), there are two units [Note: one unit of four chō each]. Thus, the distance to the left side of the target is found to be eight chō. [Note: Up to here is the middle technique (chūjutsu), which measures the left side of the target. These eight chō serve as the basis for measuring the sought distance.]

Then set that board aside. [Note: This board was used only for measuring the left side of the target and is not used in subsequent techniques.] Having thus measured the distances to the left and right of the target,

[Final Stage] ➈ Using a new board, [Note: This board also need not necessarily be new — using the boards from the first or middle technique would suffice, but a new board is used here to make it easier for beginners to understand.] place it squarely aligned with the pivot mark, just as in the first technique. Sight directly from the east of the board toward the right side of the target. ➉ Using the northeast corner (ban-kon/艮) of the board as the pivot, look back diagonally toward the left of the target and draw a line. [Note: Up to here is the final technique (kōjutsu).]

[Final Integration ⊙] Now, using the senban method (剸盤法, board-cutting technique), align the line drawn in the look-back of the final technique against the distance to the left of the target of eight chō, which was measured and recorded in the middle technique. [Note: Using the divider (konhatsu), fit the look-back line of the final technique into ten equal divisions, and call each division one chō.] Using this scale, measure off six chō — the distance to the right of the target as measured and recorded in the first technique — along the east side of the board from the sighting direction of the final technique. [Note: Measure from the north side of the board toward the south side, and discard the remaining portion.] From this point where the six chō measurement ends, draw a slightly diagonal ruling line (kei) to the end of the eight chō line. This completes the surface of the board (banmen taisen).

The sighting line now visible represents the distance to the right side of the target. The look-back line represents the distance to the left side of the target. The slightly diagonal ruling line just drawn represents the width (hirosа) of the far face (the frontage of the opposite side). Measuring this ruling line with the same square used to measure the sighting and look-back lines, there are six units [Note: one unit of one chō each]. Six units equals six chō. This is the interval count (kensū) of the sought distance (kyūtei).
