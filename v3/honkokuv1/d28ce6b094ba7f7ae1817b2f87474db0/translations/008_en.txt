Rain falls during the kaichō (public display of a temple's hidden Buddha). Lords take up commerce, and members of the imperial court rent out their storehouses. Temple priests reek of fish and flesh; their brocade robes smell of milk. Townspeople take to the Noh stage, while daughters of proper two-sworded samurai families serve as attendants to female jōruri reciters. A senior retainer who rose from foot soldier to chief elder is dismissed. The wives of back-alley tenements and small shops wear half-coats with apron strings of crepe silk, yet go in and out wearing only a single-layered sash. The serving girls who imitate them are all the more keen to swap their belongings, and slip their chapped, cracked feet into clogs with velvet thong straps. Even a household gleaming with polished all-copper fittings is forever in financial ruin. They dress up in fine clothes for sightseeing and pleasure trips, only to return home flustered by the rental fees they owe. This is why all of Edo has been reduced to eating barley — heaven's admonishment, this famine. The repeated acts of charitable relief (distribution of aid) can barely keep up.
