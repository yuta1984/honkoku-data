If there is even a discrepancy of arc-minutes or arc-seconds, how could solar and lunar eclipses be accurately predicted? Therefore, one must carefully examine the degrees of the Moon's motion before establishing rules for practical application.

In general, the Moon does not follow the single line of the ecliptic, but travels its own orbit (游輪, "wandering wheel") over the course of one month. Its diameter is approximately twelve degrees, entering and exiting the inside and outside of the ecliptic, with a maximum separation of six degrees on each side. This orbit intersects the ecliptic like a small ring attached to and traveling along the band of the ecliptic. In addition to its own rotation (本旋), there is a mean motion of over thirteen degrees per day. However, this motion has four reference points (界):

The first reference point is where calculation begins from a certain degree and arc-minute within a particular mansion (宮次). This reference point is fixed and does not move.

The second reference point is the apogee (最高, highest point) of the Moon's own sphere (本天). This is not a fixed reference point; every day it advances rightward (westward) by slightly more than seven arc-minutes in accordance with the heavens. This means the Moon's daily motion relative to the apogee of its own sphere is 23 degrees and 3-plus arc-minutes. Therefore, in mean motion, the Moon completes one revolution in 27 days and 30-plus units of time, returning to the original degree of its mansion. It must then travel an additional 23-plus units of time, making 27 days and 53 units of time in total, before it can reach the apogee of its own sphere again. This is now called the Moon's "self-motion" (自行); in ancient times it was called the "revolution period" (転周). Completing one full revolution is called the "revolution terminus" (転終). The apogee itself makes a complete circuit of the heavens in slightly over eight years; this is called the "Moon's apogee" (月孛). [Note: 月孛 refers to the point where the Moon is at its greatest height and distance. Later scholars called it the "Moon's apogee star" (月孛星).]

The third reference point is the place where the ecliptic (黄道) and the lunar orbit (白道) intersect, called the "direct crossing" (正交) and "middle crossing" (中交). This reference point also has its own motion—specifically, retrograde motion. It retrogrades slightly more than three arc-minutes each day, so the Moon's mean daily motion relative to the direct crossing is 23 degrees and 13-plus arc-minutes. Reaching 27 days and 27 units of time, and subtracting 1 degree and 23 arc-minutes of nodal motion, one obtains 27 days and 15-plus units of time. The Moon then returns to its original reference point; this is called the "crossing terminus" (交終).

The fourth reference point concerns the Moon's angular separation from the Sun. The Sun advances approximately one degree per day, so the Moon falls behind relative to the Sun by 12 degrees and 10-plus arc-minutes per day. After 29 days and 53-plus units of time, the Moon finally catches up with the Sun and meets it again. This is called the "朔策" (shuò cè, the synodic period). In ancient times, it was said that the Moon travels nine paths (九道).
