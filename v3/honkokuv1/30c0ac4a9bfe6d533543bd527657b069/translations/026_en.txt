When the sun descends below the horizon, its light gradually diminishes. This is what is called "twilight" (chenghun/晨昏). The light of twilight is not equal throughout the four seasons, and the times at which stars become visible are also naturally unequal. The reason for this is that the sun descends below the horizon along the ecliptic to varying depths — sometimes ten degrees, sometimes fifteen degrees, or even thirty degrees — and these are inherently unequal. Moreover, when a star is south of the ecliptic, the angular distance between them is necessarily several degrees greater; when it is north of the ecliptic, the distance is necessarily several degrees less. Therefore, this limiting angle cannot be fixed rigidly.

In general, the three planets Saturn, Jupiter, and Mars move more slowly than the sun, traveling behind it, and therefore set in the evening (evening setting) and appear in the morning (morning rising). The two planets Venus and Mercury revolve eastward following heaven, moving faster than the sun and traveling ahead of it; they therefore set in the morning (morning setting) and appear in the evening (evening rising). When they retrograde and this is reversed, at their conjunction with the sun they again set in the evening and rise in the morning.

The moon moves even faster than the sun, setting in the morning and appearing in the evening. Regarding the latitude of Venus, it does not reach eight degrees. When Venus retrogrades and conjoins with the sun in the constellations Shouxing (the Gemini mansion, 辰宮) or Dahuo (the Scorpio mansion, 卯宮), and its latitude is again more than seven degrees north, even though it is in conjunction with the sun, its light does not disappear and it can be seen both morning and evening. The latitude of Mercury is only about four degrees. If its latitude is directed southward, after its conjunction with the sun in Shouxing (辰宮) and subsequent separation, it will necessarily not be visible in the evening. After its conjunction with the sun in Jianglou (戌宮, Aries mansion) and subsequent separation, it will necessarily not be visible in the morning. These are the differences in the occultations and appearances of the five planets.

In the case of Saturn, it completes one revolution of the heavens in slightly less than thirty years, moving in retrograde (westward). Evening setting (夕伏) and conjunction-setting (合伏) each last twenty days and forty ke (刻), during which it advances two degrees and forty minutes. [This uses a system of one hundred minutes per degree for the five planets.] It rises in the east in the morning, and with both fast and slow motion combined, over eighty-six days it advances seven degrees and sixty-five minutes. It then stations (留) for thirty days. Morning retrogradation and evening retrogradation each last fifty-two days, sixty-four ke, and fifty-eight minutes, during which it retrogrades three degrees, sixty-two minutes, and fifty-four and a half seconds. It stations again for thirty days. With slow and fast motion combined over eighty-six days, it advances seven degrees and sixty-five minutes. It then sets again in the evening and reappears, completing one cycle. Approximately every sixty years the pattern repeats, but the exact days and hours are not the same. [After sixty years, the patterns of slow/fast motion, setting, and retrogradation are the same, but the times of passing through the mansions differ; roughly it requires about three hours earlier.]
