By the morning of the fourth day, the degree of volcanic smoke gradually returned to normal. Regarding this matter, Dr. Kochibu Tadatsugu, an agricultural and commercial engineer and doctor of science who was staying in Fukui Prefecture at the time to inspect the earthquake disaster areas, sent the following letter to Police Chief Yoshida of Aichi Prefecture:

"Respectfully, yesterday in the Osaka Asahi Shimbun, it was reported that Mount Asama in Shinshū has been dropping ash since the 31st and volcanic smoke has been increasing. Since this matter has great relevance to the earthquake regions, I inquired with the governor of Nagano Prefecture after arriving in Tsuruga last night. According to his return telegram just received, the mountain in question has been ejecting flames since the 31st and has not stopped even today, but there are no particular abnormalities. I believe this will reduce the seismic forces that have been stimulating the Nōbi region by releasing the sharp energy of the tremors. Therefore, I believe that severe earthquakes will no longer recur in the affected disaster areas. I report this for your peace of mind, so please handle this appropriately and share this information with the prefectural secretary and others as well. In haste."

●Violent Waves in Ise Bay: On the day following the great earthquake, namely October 29th, the waves in Ise Bay were exceptionally high. The forty-ri stretch from Shima Toba to Katsuura experienced rough seas, particularly severe, to the extent that small steamships that had departed from Yokkaichi were forced to return to the same port.

The editor notes: I inquired about the conditions of these rough seas at Yokkaichi, and according to a boatman, on the day of the rough seas, it felt as if the seabed was rumbling, and the turbulent wave areas and the relatively calm wave areas appeared to be distinctly separated. Regarding this account, Dr. Kochibu theorized that this rough sea was also a phenomenon of the earthquake, suggesting that a major collapse occurred on the seabed of Ise Bay, and the seawater suddenly rushed to fill this void, causing these undulations.

●Natural Gas Eruption: In the Shidabe area near Fujieda in Sunshū, there had previously been natural coal gas eruptions. Using simple equipment, this gas was stored and sent through iron pipes to houses, where it was lit in bedrooms, bathrooms, and other rooms as a substitute for lamp oil. However, the quantity was very scarce, and by around 9 PM, the supply would be exhausted and lighting was no longer possible. Since the earthquake of October 28th, the pond where this gas erupted has continuously made sounds like small torpedoes exploding, and in severe cases, water has been shot up six or seven feet into the air, displaying unusual phenomena. This is entirely due to the sudden increase in the amount of gas eruption, urgently seeking an outlet for escape. Since then, the quantity has increased dramatically and is no longer like before. If this continues to erupt at this rate, it could not only provide lighting for each household but also serve as a substitute for firewood. The local residents were greatly pleased, considering it entirely a gift from the earthquake.
