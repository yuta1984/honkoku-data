(Continued from previous page) Those who had witnessed the great Ansei Earthquake firsthand were terrified and fled outside, creating quite an uproar for a time. As a result of this earthquake, the Ryōunkaku tower in Asakusa shook considerably and sustained minor damage in several places. Notably, the chimney of the Kotsukappara Prison Factory collapsed suddenly from a point about four ken (approximately 7.3 meters) up, apparently because the mortar was not sufficiently solid. Furthermore, throughout the city, it was exceedingly common for objects to fall from shelves and for gutters to topple, and pottery shops and lamp stores in particular suffered a great number of damaged goods.

●The Earthquake in Yokohama　Yokohama also experienced an earthquake on the same day at the same time. The entity that suffered extraordinary damage was the Yokohama Joint Electric Light Company, whose chimney, standing one hundred and six shaku (approximately 32 meters) tall, crumbled down with a tremendous crash from its midpoint all at once, sending bricks tumbling into the coal room and the engine room. Inevitably, the roofs of those two rooms were torn apart with a great cracking sound, and the machinery below was destroyed, resulting in a loss of nearly ten thousand yen for the company. It was fortunate, however, that not a single person was injured. Within the city, there were areas that experienced stronger and lighter tremors alike. As the foreign settlement generally had many brick buildings, the noise during the quake was terrifyingly loud, and afterward, cracks were found in various walls. The licensed quarter in Makanecho, built on reclaimed land whose ground had not yet fully settled, shook with particular violence, and half-asleep courtesans in their disheveled nightgowns rushed one after another to flee outside, creating considerable uproar. It is said that two houses in the city were completely destroyed.

　Editor's Note: The collapse of the Yokohama Electric Light Company's chimney immediately became a topic of discussion in the architectural community. In response to the still-unresolved academic question of how much weight can withstand vibration, it was argued that if this chimney had indeed collapsed due to vibration, it would represent a major discovery in the field of architectural science. Eager to investigate the matter promptly, foreign engineers residing in Japan, along with Ichinosuke Fujioka, Chief Engineer of the Tokyo Electric Light Company, Yoshiaki Yasunaga, Engineer of the Army Ministry, and several other individuals, each made their way to Yokohama to personally visit the site and conduct detailed inquiries with Kobori, the Chief Engineer of the company. According to the investigation carried out by Engineer Yasunaga, the chimney had been designed by the former Chief Engineer of the company, Mr. Takeo Iwata, at a cost of over four thousand eight hundred yen. It was a circular brick structure standing one hundred and six shaku tall. The foundation buried underground measured five ken and four shaku, with the upper portion measuring thirteen shaku square; the circumference at the widest point at the base was eight shaku, and at the highest point — the flue opening — six shaku. While its external appearance seemed quite solid, an examination of its construction and materials revealed the following faults: first, the selection of bricks was inappropriate; second, the laying of the bricks was incomplete; third, the mortar was of poor quality; and fourth, perforated bricks had been used at the mid-section of the chimney.
