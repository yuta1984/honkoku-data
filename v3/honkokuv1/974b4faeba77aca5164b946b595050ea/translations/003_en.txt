The fierce demonic wind threatened to tear apart his three robes, but when he chanted the divine incantation for subduing demons, the four directions immediately cleared, white sunlight illuminated the surroundings, and he saw the thrown vajra (ritual implement) resting upon a rock. Thinking that such a place of karmic connection must surely hold wonders, he opened the rock, and a fragrant hot spring bubbled forth abundantly. When he tasted it as a test, he found that it truly harmonized yin and yang and warmed the afflicted heart and soul.

Realizing that this had been hidden here for the complete benefit of people, and that Heaven had commanded him to open it so that the time had now come to remove the defilements of sickness and karmic obstacles from many people, he arranged small rocks to contain the spring and with sincere heart performed esoteric Buddhist rituals over it.

From then on, he went around to places where people lived and demonstrated such benefits, so that even shepherd boys and fishermen, guided by the Great Master, came to know the miraculous effects of the hot spring for the first time. Eventually it spread throughout the world, and thatched houses came to stand in rows. By the Kamakura period, the Shogun's family and various retainers frequently bathed there, so the bathhouses and inns were magnificently renovated. However, due to the Kaba-dono incident and later the accident involving Lord Yoriie, this place became almost desolate and declined.

Just as it was beginning to prosper again, it became a battlefield once more, and the bathhouses and residences were lost to fires and storms, with even the hot spring flowing with a cold, lonely aspect. But through the abundant blessings of imperial governance, this became a blessed land where people prospered and multiplied - surely this is the effect of the sacred spring blessed through the Great Master's adamantine power.

This account, both preceding and following parts, is based on the posthumous writings of the venerable Gitō Kikuchi. The spiritual mysteries of Buddhism should not be carelessly doubted through ordinary reasoning.
