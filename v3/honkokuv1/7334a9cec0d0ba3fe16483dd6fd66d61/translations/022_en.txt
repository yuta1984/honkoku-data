It was constructed like bamboo fencing on all sides, with straw stuffed into the earthen spaces, and the ceiling had logs laid horizontally with mats spread on top and earth placed over them. Now, an earthquake like this recent one created tremors so violent that even the streets and flat ground cracked and split, so how could anything dug deep into the earth be safe? Therefore, among the collapses caused by the shaking, I record below the most severe case.

Here in Hongo Shinmachi lived a man called Mikawaya Hikobei, whose miso business was extremely prosperous. His wife Toyo-onna had put the entire household to bed on the night of the second, and went down to the garden to have a young boy relieve himself when suddenly a great earthquake struck. Startled with an "ah!", she tried to return to her original position, but the earth's tremors were so strong that the koji fermentation room split open and collapsed into a pit. Toyo-onna, still holding the child, let out a single scream and fell in.

Shocked by this, her husband Hikobei and even the servants were thrown into commotion trying to rescue them, but during this time the earth on all sides collapsed, the foundation pillars loosened, the large house tumbled down, and various goods fell and piled up, so regrettably there was no way to rescue them. They could only watch for a while, but then fires broke out on all sides above the collapsed house, making the chaos even worse, with even their own lives in extreme danger. While dealing with this situation, night turned to day and the fires were extinguished, but aftershocks continued intermittently. Unable to abandon them, they removed the scattered debris and dug into the earth, where they found Toyo-onna still grasping the child's leg, both mother and child with faces changed in death. Not only Hikobei, but even the seemingly heartless servants were choked with tears of grief, and finally held a funeral. Natural disasters are truly beyond the limits of lamentation.
