### Volcanoes

**[2]** A volcano is a conical mountain that emits smoke and flames, flows with molten rock called "lava," and showers down red-hot stones and ash. The opening through which such materials are ejected is called a crater (from the Greek word "krater," meaning a large cup or bowl, named so because of its shape).

**[Figure caption]** Figure 12: Cross-section of a Volcano

Figure 12 shows the cross-section of a volcano. A indicates the crater; B indicates the vertical channel connecting to the Earth's interior; and C indicates the conical mountain formed from ejected materials. ○ Ejected lava is initially a viscous liquid resembling resin, but as it cools and solidifies, it becomes a black, porous mineral. The particularly light and porous form of lava is called "scoria." In addition to lava, volcanoes also emit steam and gases (particularly carbon dioxide), sometimes releasing such large quantities as to be capable of killing animals living within a certain radius. In 1730, on one of the Canary Islands, all domestic animals were killed by such a flow of gas. The famous Valley of Death (Upas Valley) in Java is the crater of an active volcano, where any living creature that enters has its breathing obstructed. Consequently, the ground surface is strewn with the bones of birds, animals, and humans that have perished from the constantly emitted gases.

**[3]** Craters — Craters vary greatly in size. For example, the crater of Vesuvius is 2,000 feet in diameter; that of Popocatépetl in Mexico is 5,000 feet; and the crater of Kīlauea on Hawaiʻi, one of the Sandwich Islands, is 18,000 feet. (For these volcanoes, refer to the Second Atlas Map.)
