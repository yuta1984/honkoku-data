[Section 22] [Volcanic Series of the Atlantic] Various volcanic chains exist along the Atlantic Ocean and its associated gulfs and bays. The most notable among these are, in the east, the chains of Iceland and southern Italy, and in the west, the Lesser Antilles chain which encompasses the volcanoes of St. Vincent, Guadeloupe, Martinique, and others.

[Caption for illustration above: The crater of Krafla volcano in Iceland]

The mountains of Iceland all show signs of potential volcanic activity in various places. Eight of its volcanoes were active during the previous century. Among them, Mount Hekla is the most famous. This mountain has had 43 eruptions since 900 AD, five of which occurred simultaneously with eruptions of Vesuvius, or if not exactly simultaneous, then nearly so. Additionally, four of them occurred simultaneously with eruptions of Mount Etna. During an eruption of Skaftárjökull in the southeastern part of this island, lava flows continued unabated for two years, forming two deep rivers. One was 50 miles long and 12 miles wide, while the other was 40 miles long and 7 miles wide. It is said that the total volume of material ejected during this time amounted to 40 billion tons. Among the mountain ranges of Iceland, there is another volcano called Mount Krafla, whose violent eruptions continued consecutively from 1724 to 1730. The lava that emerged from its crater flowed and poured into a lake six miles away, causing it to almost completely dry up. The volcanic chain of southern Italy includes the previously described Mount Etna and Vesuvius. Vesuvius is a solitary mountain that rises majestically from the Campanian plateau, with a circumference of 30 miles around its base.

[Section 23] [Antarctic Volcanoes] Even in the ice fields discovered in the Antarctic Ocean, volcanoes exist. Mount Erebus in Victoria Land is located at 77°30' south latitude and has a height of 12,400 feet.
