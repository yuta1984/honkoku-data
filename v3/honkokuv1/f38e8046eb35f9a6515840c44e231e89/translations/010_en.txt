**Section 22**

**Volcanic Series of the Atlantic Ocean**

Adjoining the Atlantic Ocean and the various bays belonging to it, there are volcanic series of various kinds. The most notable among them are, on the eastern side, the volcanic series of **Iceland** and **Southern Italy**, and on the western side, the series of the **Lesser Antilles**, which encompasses volcanoes such as **Saint Vincent**, **Guadeloupe**, and **Martinique**.

[Figure caption: The crater of Mount Krafla in Iceland]

The mountains of Iceland show signs of volcanic activity in many places. Eight of its volcanoes were active during the past century, among which **Mount Hekla** is the most famous. The forty-three eruptions of this mountain have occurred since the year 900 AD, and five of them coincided — or at least nearly coincided — with eruptions of **Vesuvius**. Four others were simultaneous with eruptions of **Etna**. In the eruption of **Skaptá Jökull** in the southeastern part of the island, lava flows continued unabated for two years, forming two deep river channels: one measuring fifty miles in length and twelve miles in width, and the other forty miles in length and seven miles in width. The total volume of material ejected at this time is said to have amounted to forty billion tons. Among the mountain ranges of Iceland, there is also a volcano called **Krafla**, whose violent eruptions continued intermittently from 1724 to 1730. The lava discharged from its crater flowed outward and poured into a lake six miles away, causing it to almost completely dry up. The volcanic series of Southern Italy includes **Etna** and **Vesuvius**, already described in previous sections. Vesuvius is a solitary peak rising majestically from the **Campanian** plateau, with a circumference at its base of thirty miles.

**Section 23**

**Volcanoes of the Antarctic Ocean**

Even in the ice fields of the Antarctic Ocean, which were once discovered, volcanoes exist. **Mount Erebus** in **Victoria Land** is situated at twelve and a half degrees from the South Pole, and its height is 12,400 feet.
