…Could there be no angular difference in the direction (of the sunset)? [Marginal note: The difficulties concerning the directions of rising and setting, the poles, and day and night are discussed below. Read in conjunction with that section.]

Moreover, the shape of the general wind-wheel (sōfūrin) is convex and dome-like, with Mount Sumeru at its center. Therefore, when one looks toward the Western Continent (Aparagodānīya) from the Southern Continent (Jambudvīpa), there naturally exists the arc of a curved surface standing at an oblique angle. [Marginal note: Refer especially to Figure 17 in the Hidden Chapter (隠篇) in conjunction with this.] In addition to this, when there is a separate wind-wheel (betsufūrin), would the position of the setting sun not appear to shift to the south and become invisible in the west? (That is, it would certainly appear in the west.) [Marginal note: The principles of the general wind-wheel and the separate wind-wheel are the same; there is only a difference in scale.]

If the direction of the sunset, with its angular difference, appears in the west, then could the direction in which the crescent moon opens also be anything other than westward? (That is, it must certainly face west.) Refer to the diagram.
