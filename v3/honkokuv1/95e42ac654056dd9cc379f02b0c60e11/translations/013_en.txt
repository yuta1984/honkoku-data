●Report on Damage to Postal and Telegraph Lines in Miyagi Prefecture

A telegram sent by the Sendai Postmaster at 2:15 p.m. on June 26th, which reached the Ministry of Communications the previous day, reads as follows:

The post offices destroyed and washed away in the affected areas of the prefecture were the Ōya and Okachi-hama offices. Deaths included: four family members of the Ōya postmaster, one mail carrier of the same office, and one telegraph worker. One telegraph worker was injured. Washed-away postal items numbered approximately ninety pieces from the Ōya office, while the Okachi-hama office is still under investigation. Destroyed telegraph poles: 53 poles between Shizugawa and Kesennuma (2 ri 10 chō of telegraph line destroyed); 68 poles between Kesennuma and Sakari-machi (1.5 ri); 51 poles between Kamaishi, Ōtsuchi, and Yamada (1 ri 11 chō); 33 poles between Yamada and Miyako (2.5 ri); 32 poles near Kuji (1.5 ri) — totaling 279 poles, with a total of 10 ri 20 chō of telegraph line destroyed. The above is hereby reported.

Telegraph offices that suffered damage are as follows:

●Deaths and Injuries Among Elementary School Students in Miyagi Prefecture

According to a survey conducted by the Miyagi Prefectural Office on June 24th, the number of elementary school students in the disaster areas who were killed or injured by the tsunami is as follows:

Deaths: 372　　Injuries: 54

The above figures represent the combined total of male and female students. While a complete breakdown by gender has not yet been fully surveyed, in any case, the number of male students accounts for no less than approximately two-thirds of the total. Among the schools, Migita Elementary School, for example, had as many as 44 male students and 23 female students killed.

【Lower Section】

◎Pitiful Orphans

In Funakoshi-hama, there are five orphans who lost their parents and younger sibling. According to what little they were able to recount: when the tsunami first struck, their parents had the six children — brothers and sisters — hold each other's hands and cling to the lintel of the house, desperately waiting for the floodwaters to recede. Then one of the youngest children let go and was about to be swept outside. The mother, trying to save the child, dived into the water — at which moment a violent wave struck, separating mother and child, and both drowned. The remaining five children clung desperately to the lintel. When a piece of drifting lumber threatened to strike them, the father tried to push it away and was himself swept off by the backwash. Thus, all five children lost their parents before their very eyes. Their anguish was beyond words, yet they continued to cling to the lintel together, and fortunately, when the waters receded, their lives were spared. Now, with no one to depend upon, they are at a complete loss — young children left utterly without direction.

○ Detailed Report on the Tsunami Damage at the Okachi Branch Station

The detailed report on tsunami damage at the Okachi Branch Station, submitted on June 17th by Koizumi, Warden of the Miyagi Prison, to Home Minister Itagaki, reads as follows:

Regarding the matter of our Okachi-hama Branch Station having been washed away by the great tsunami, an urgent telegram was sent on the 16th of this month. The warden himself immediately traveled to the site to conduct an on-the-ground investigation, the results of which are as follows:

Item: The number of inmates dispatched to said location was 195, with 34 guards.

Item: The branch station faces the bay, and the barracks in particular were situated closest to it, making them the full target of the violent waves. When a tremendous roaring sound erupted, the 16 guards on rest duty that night sensed that something extraordinary was happening; they gathered together, went outside, and were in the very act of preparing to follow the orders of their superiors when they were engulfed by the violent waves and raging surf. Of these, eight barely escaped with their lives from what seemed like certain death, while the rest remained unaccounted for.
