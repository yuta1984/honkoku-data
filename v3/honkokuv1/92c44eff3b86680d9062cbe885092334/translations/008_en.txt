He was praised as a leader. During the Hōreki era (1751-1764), he served in Karatsu through Confucian learning and greatly enriched scholarship there. He was troubled that later Confucian scholars each established their own family schools, dividing like distinguishing plants and trees, thereby going against Confucius's teachings. He exhausted his mind and spirit to write "Hi-Shu Kitsumotsu" (Critique of Zhu Xi). Although it was in the tradition of the Fuji school (Fujiwara Seikwa), there were many points to doubt, so he wrote "Gitō" (Doubts about Tō). However, he did not completely reject it, probably because he could not bear to turn against his former teachers. This sufficiently shows what an upright person he was. Eventually, he established his own school separately and called it the learning of Confucius. After his death, his friend Akutsu Genshō composed his grave inscription, in which everyone praised his virtue, broad learning, good guidance, and teaching ability, saying that the praise of many scholars in Karatsu was largely due to his influence. Now, Tōgai was a great Confucian scholar of his time, and what he praised must be worth testing. Akutsu was also a renowned Confucian scholar, so his words were not necessarily empty. Moreover, there are very many writings now preserved in Keichū's house. He once wrote one volume called "Onsen Shōgen" (Brief Words on Hot Springs) based on various items of natural history.
