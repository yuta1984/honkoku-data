It is also said that where there is arsenic stone, there are hot springs as well. I do not know the details of whether this is true or false. In recent times, Kagawa Taichū of Kyoto has firmly relied on the theory of Ina Jakusui, asserting that there are two veins in the earth - water and fire - and that hot springs are formed when these intersect and undergo transformation. His discourse is truly excellent. However, when establishing theories, there are not a few who are bound by one-sided views and advocate their own positions too forcefully. How is it that no suitable person corrects these errors and spreads proper views? Hara Kōyō of Heian once wrote a volume called "Onsen Shōgen" (Brief Words on Hot Springs). His insight was solid and his arguments precise; he refuted Taichū's crude discussions and returned to orthodox views. This also dissolved my long-standing doubts - was this not truly refreshing? Moreover, the book uses vernacular script (hiragana) to facilitate reading by farmers and common folk, which can truly be called a manifestation of compassionate heart. If those in the world who carry illness to the mountains and valleys [for hot spring cures] were to discard this volume, what would they rely upon?
