…corresponds to 3,440,000 tsubo (approximately 1,157 chō), equivalent to **an area of less than one ri square**. Compared to the total area of Tosa Province today, which is approximately 400 square ri, this amounts to roughly one four-hundredth of the province — not a figure that should cause particular astonishment. Nevertheless, if a flat expanse of farmland covering an area of one ri square were to sink to the ocean floor in a single earthquake, it would by any measure constitute an extraordinary natural catastrophe.

It was therefore not at all hasty for the provincial governor of Tosa to report this in alarm to the Imperial Court, or for the court historians to record it in the official history. I have thus come to believe that the accounts of the ancients are, surprisingly, quite reliable and ought not to be dismissed lightly. I am firmly convinced that the account of 500,000 *kei* (頃) of farmland sinking during the great Tosa earthquake is no mere rhetorical exaggeration of the disaster, but an entirely factual account admitting of no doubt.

**(Section D) The Tsunami Following the Earthquake**

Now, it is a well-established principle of geology that after such a great earthquake, until the ground regains stability, considerable strong tremors and major tsunamis continue for days or even months thereafter, even if they be aftershocks. Indeed, in the following month after the great earthquake — that is, in the entry for the eleventh month of Hakuhō 13 in the *Nihon Shoki* — the following record appears:

> *Kōjutsu* (庚戌): The provincial governor of Tosa reported that the tides had risen greatly, the sea had flooded and surged, and many tribute ships were lost.

The minor tremors and high tides following a great earthquake do not subside easily; they gradually settle over the course of months and years. It is therefore likely that such events were encountered daily, but given the antiquity of the times and the difficulty of communication, they were not recorded in written sources one by one. However, this report from the provincial governor, submitted one month after the event, certainly serves as proof of these facts, and must be regarded as a most interesting historical document.

**(Section E) Meteorological Changes Following the Great Earthquake**

After the great Tosa earthquake of Hakuhō 13, minor earthquakes and meteorological disturbances throughout the country did not settle for some time. For reference, I will here excerpt records from the subsequent three years from the *Nihon Shoki*.

1. Hakuhō 13, 11th month, *Bōshin* day: At dusk, seven stars all streamed toward the northeast and then fell.
2. Same month, *Kōgo* day: At sunset, a star fell in the eastern sky, as large as a jug (*kame*). By the Hour of the Dog (approximately 8 p.m.), all the stars in the sky were blurred, and stars fell like rain. On this day, a comet appeared in the center of the sky, traveling alongside the Pleiades (*Subaru*), then disappearing at the end of the month.
3. Hakuhō 14, 3rd month: This month, ash fell over Shinano Province and all the plants and trees withered.
4. Same year, 4th month, *Kibō* day: The provincial governor of Kii reported that the hot spring at Muro had sunk and ceased to flow.
5. Same year, 12th month, *Shinshi* day: An earthquake originating from the west.
6. Shuchō 1st year, 3rd month, *Kōjutsu* day: Snow fell.
7. Same year, 11th month, *Ki* day: An earthquake occurred.

Examining the situation during the three years following the great Hakuhō earthquake in this manner, one can see that it was truly an ill-fated period of natural disasters and geological upheaval, with minor earthquakes and volcanic eruptions occurring throughout the country as the earth's crust sought to regain stability — a continuous succession of calamities so relentless as to leave virtually no respite.
