Ise Yokkaichi：　　Deaths: 800
　Iga Ueno：　　　　Deaths: 170
　Yamato Kasuga Shrine：　Stone lanterns toppled: 1,700

Furthermore, on November 4th of the same year, a great earthquake and tsunami struck the entire Kantō region, causing enormous damage. According to the records, the situation was roughly as follows:

　Izu Shimoda Port: A great tsunami struck, causing over 80 deaths by drowning. A Russian naval vessel anchored in the harbor was destroyed. The Russians petitioned the Bakufu (shogunate), and from that same month began constructing a replacement vessel at Toda-ura. In the third month of the following year, Ansei 2, they departed on that ship for Kamchatka.
　　　　　　The tsunami from this event transmitted its wave as far as the state of "California" in the United States, twenty-four hours later.

　Izu Ōshima: A great earthquake and waves struck; many islanders drowned.
　Shima Province, Toba Port: A great earthquake and waves struck; over 1,000 drowned.
　Osaka Dōtonbori: A great earthquake and waves struck; over 700 drowned.

The above lists the most notable incidents from both days. The full statistical figures for all deaths, injuries, and damages across the board cannot be enumerated here in their entirety.

Upon reflection, the earthquake and tsunami of November 4th, Ansei 1, was itself a powerful event and is regarded as one of the most significant examples of natural disaster in the history of our country. However, when compared in practice to the earthquake and tsunami of the following day, November 5th, it appears to be naturally somewhat lesser in terms of the scale of seismic waves and the extent of damage. That is to say, according to the records:

　November 4th:
　　　Earthquake affected: over 16 provinces
　　　Tsunami affected: over 14 provinces
　November 5th:
　　　Earthquake affected: over 30 provinces
　　　Tsunami: (no record given)

Therefore, judging by common sense, the earthquake of the 4th was "very strong" and that of the 5th was "maximum strength." To put it another way, **it appears that the earthquake of the 4th served as a foreshock, and that of the 5th was the main shock.** And it was indeed the great earthquake of the 5th that Tosa Province felt most acutely.

Now, the great earthquake of November 4th was felt primarily in the Kantō region, while the western provinces felt it comparatively little. The situation as observed in Tosa Province on that same day was roughly as follows:

　Vicinity of Kōchi:
　　November 4th, 9:00 a.m. (the time designation has been converted to modern terminology): A strong earthquake was felt.
　　　　　　　Between 8:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m., the sea retreated and advanced three times. (Note: 8:00 a.m. on this day was high tide…)
