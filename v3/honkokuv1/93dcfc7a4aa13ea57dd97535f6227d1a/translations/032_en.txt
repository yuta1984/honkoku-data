They were given the strategy to infiltrate and destroy, taking the Yokogawa checkpoint with a small force while the main army would provide adequate defense. They would not attack beyond the pass. In the meantime, if they could break through from the rear attack (karamete), all the main forces would retreat as well. With this command, they hastened the Agatsuma forces. When the seventh day dawned, from early morning they beat the war drums, and the Asama forces fought so fiercely without pause that the Kantō forces gradually fled and retreated. Even the deity of Usui tried to stand firm and

fought, but was surrounded by the Asama forces and barely escaped, falling back among the Kantō forces. The survival of both mountains of Asama depended on this single action. If they did not decide victory or defeat in this one battle, when else could they expect to do so? With their righteousness hardened like steel and iron, they attacked without looking aside. The Kantō forces gradually fell into disadvantage, and by the evening of the seventh day, they broke through the pass, hurling sand and stones to destroy Hakuun Mountain, breathing fire and flame, with arrows and firearms echoing in the cloudy sky, shaking heaven and earth.
