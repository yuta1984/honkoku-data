He took it home and was very pleased, but when he said "one" and shook the dice, six came up, and when he said "six" and threw it, five came up, or three came up - nothing came up as called, so he went to the man's place again to exchange it. But the man had placed thorns and brambles around his house so no one could approach. So thinking "I'll bring something he dislikes to frighten him," he bought kinako mochi (soybean flour rice cakes) and threw them, but the man said "What a treat!" and picked them up as they were thrown and ate them. Therefore he could not get the dice back and had no choice but to keep them.

From then on, gambling was transmitted. There is such a history, and those who speak of it in various ways do so because they do not know the facts, he said. Then Sanjakubō said, "While I swing my three-shaku staff, I travel around all of Japan. Because of my swift pace, I don't know the details, but long ago when I passed through Mount Hōrai, there was a connection where those dice were received, and now there is a place called 'Morai-ga-take' (Receiving Peak)."
