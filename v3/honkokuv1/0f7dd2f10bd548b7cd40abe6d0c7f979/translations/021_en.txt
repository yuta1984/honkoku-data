The subterranean fire (fire pit) is compressed throughout the entire earth and remains in a very calm state, but whenever air manages to pass through even slightly, the fiery vapor is activated by this and causes the earth to tremble. In severe cases, it shakes and collapses even mountains, spews forth sand and rocks, destroys people's homes, causes harm to many people, and goes so far as to transform the landscape of mountains, rivers, hills, and valleys. A distant example is that of a city in Italy, which was buried underground, with its people, vegetation, and livestock completely annihilated. More recently, in our own country, the earthquakes and tsunamis in Echigo, Shinano, the Kinai region, Kii, Ise, Iga, Izu, Suruga, and other areas are examples of this. Since fire veins branch from one channel into many, lands adjacent to neighboring provinces tend to suffer greater damage. This depends on whether or not they are struck by the fire vapor. The reason why shrines and temples suffer less damage is that the spacing of their foundation stones and the heights of their ridgepoles and beams differ from those of ordinary houses.
