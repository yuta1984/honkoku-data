He was highly valued, and after traveling through India, he returned to his country and said: China has virtuous people and fertile land, and even without trading with foreign countries, the nation lacks nothing. The capital city "Kubisai" (probably a corruption of "Kyoshi" meaning capital) is so vast and prosperous that it exceeds human imagination—it is the greatest city in the world. He also described the abundance of Chinese products. Therefore, Europe deeply admired China and wished to establish friendship and trade relations, but this was not possible. When trade routes were first opened, they reached Macao (called Xiangshan'ao in Chinese) and requested permission to conduct trade and establish trading posts, but China firmly refused to grant permission. After several years, they gradually obtained permission from the Chinese government (according to the Ming History, foreign trade was permitted only after the 9th year of Jiajing. The Ming term "Fojiqi" refers to the Portuguese, which I have explained in detail in my revised and expanded "Yakwaranigen"). They built residences and fortifications in Macao, lived there, and traded with the Chinese. Subsequently, Dutch and English people also came to Guangdong or Fujian in China to trade. The goods exported from China to other countries included gold, jade, pearls, musk, rhubarb, smilax, cotton, silk, gold thread, porcelain, sugar, rice, and grain. In 1519 (Japan's Eisho 16th year, Ming's Zhengde 14th year), the Spanish conquistador "Hernando Cortés" (written as Ge'erdesi in Chinese) led an army to Mexico in North America.
