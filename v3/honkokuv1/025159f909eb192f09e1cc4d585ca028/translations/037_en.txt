In 1564 (the 7th year of Eiroku in Japan; the 43rd year of Jiajing in Ming China), the Spanish reached the Philippines in the South Sea, and in 1572 (the 3rd year of Genki in Japan; the 6th year of Longqing in Ming China), they finally annexed the large island of Luzon among them (this matter is recorded in the first and second volumes). They made this their base in the South Sea, and also opened the Ladrones Islands, located directly south of Japan (these are what appear on maps as the "Islands of Thieves"), stationing a garrison there. Furthermore, they opened the Solomon Islands to the east of this location, established a residence there, and used it to facilitate maritime traffic between Luzon and the various states of the Americas. In 1583 (the 11th year of Tenshō in Japan; the 11th year of Wanli in Ming China), they also opened up the great land to the northwest of Mexico, took possession of it, and built fortresses in various places. This was named "New Mexico." This land produces much silver, crystal, and gemstones. Around this time, the English, Dutch, and Danish also each set sail, and the English opened up lands such as "New England," "Virginia," and "Maryland" in North America, took possession of them, built fortresses, and stationed garrisons. After this, Dutch navigation grew increasingly vigorous day by day. In 1612 (the 17th year of Keichō in Japan; the 40th year of Wanli in Ming China), they reached the Moluccas, and in 1619 (the 5th year of Genna in Japan; the 47th year of Wanli in Ming China), they used the land of Jayakarta in the country of Java as their base, built a great fortress, named it "Batavia," stationed a garrison, and equipped it with soldiers and many ships. They also opened the surrounding territories, built fortresses, reinforced their defenses, and made this the fundamental base for trade with the various countries of Asia (to this day, all ships coming to Japan depart from this place).
